Abstract 

Diffusion unlearning is essential for mitigating the generation of harmful or copy-righted content in text-to-image models. Current diffusion unlearning techniques determine the model update direction by either using alternatives of the target concept as an anchor or using empty prompts. The anchor-based method relies on manually and semantically-chosen anchors that risk biased unlearning, while the anchor-free method inherently suffers from unrobust unlearning due to un-constrained latent updates. In this work, we theoretically formalize such unstable diffusion unlearning issues under the manifold hypothesis and prove that lacking a manifold-proximal anchor inevitably induces significant normal-space drift that degrades unlearning performance. To achieve stable unlearning, we propose Au-toAnchor, a two-stage framework that automatically synthesizes manifold-proximal anchors. However, direct geometric manifold optimization is computationally in-tractable. To address this challenge, AutoAnchor introduces a novel cross-attention consistency loss which serves as a highly efficient surrogate of manifold proximity. Experimental results demonstrate that AutoAnchor effectively achieves robust and unbiased unlearning across various state-of-the-art baselines, significantly improv-ing targeted concept removal (by up to 31.04% in CLIP score) and non-target utility (by up to 4.18% in CLIP score). Moreover, AutoAnchor can also be easily integrated into existing diffusion unlearning methods to enhance their unlearning performance (by 6.30% for concept removal and 6.65% for utility on average). 

1 Introduction 

Text-to-Image diffusion models have achieved remarkable success in generating high-fidelity images conditioned on input text prompts. However, their reliance on massive and uncurated web data introduces significant risks, e.g. , generating harmful content or copyright infringement. To mitigate these risks, diffusion unlearning has emerged as a crucial technique. The goal of diffusion unlearning is to erase a diffusion model’s ability to generate specific target concepts, which is usually realized by tuning the model parameters towards certain update directions. Achieving effective erasure while preserving model utility is hard for diffusion unlearning. This is because the modern diffusion models operate in a high-dimensional latent space, but the valid and realistic images reside on a low-dimensional data manifold within that. Without a carefully designed update direction, diffusion unlearning can easily push the model off the data manifold when erasing target concepts, thereby degrading the erasure effectiveness and damaging the model’s utility. Regarding how to determine the update directions, current diffusion unlearning methods are categorized into anchor-free unlearning and anchor-based unlearning. Anchor-free methods (e.g., [13 , 15 , 29 ]) utilize target-irrelevant directions for unlearning, simply pushing the genera-tion away from the target concepts. Without a manifold-proximal anchor ( e.g. , our AutoAnchor), the update direction has a large normal component that pushes the model off the manifold, leading to unrobust or biased unlearning. images when given prompts containing target concepts. While computationally straightforward, such update directions inherently cause off-manifold drift and lead to unrobust unlearning , manifesting as catastrophic forgetting [ 16 ], degraded image quality [ 10 , 27 ], and weakened text-image align-ment [ 22 , 15 ] on non-target concepts. Moreover, such methods risk fragile forgetting that can be easily bypassed by adversarial prompts [ 6, 50 , 54 ] or simple fine-tuning [ 34 ]. Existing Anchor-based 

methods ( e.g., [23 , 31 , 50 ]) redirect the model from the target concept toward a specific alternative concept (the anchor), trying to stay on the manifold during unlearning. However, the anchors that they heavily rely on are all manually chosen based on semantic similarity, which is insufficient for ensuring manifold proximity and risks biased unlearning . With biased anchors, the unlearning can still suffer from the unrobust unlearning issues above and may further cause imprecise forgetting [ 49 ]. Both approaches suffer from a shared limitation: the inability to identify a manifold-proximal anchor for robust and unbiased unlearning. To fill this gap, we analyze the unlearning process under the manifold hypothesis. Geometrically, valid images lie on a low-dimensional data manifold within the vast diffusion latent space, as illustrated in Figure 1. A robust and effective unlearning update should smoothly traverse along this manifold to alter target concepts, rather than pushing the representation away from it. Therefore, we decompose the unlearning update directions into their tangential (along the manifold) and normal (orthogonal to the manifold) components. Our analysis demonstrates that lacking a manifold-proximal anchor ( e.g. , anchor-free methods and unlearning via bias anchors) fundamentally leads to normal-space drift, forcing the generation trajectory off the manifold. Moreover, we prove that manifold-proximal anchors effectively align unlearning directions closely with the local tangent space of the data manifold, promoting robust and unbiased unlearning. However, constructing such a manifold-proximal anchor presents non-trivial challenges. Direct optimization on the data manifold is computationally intractable [ 9 , 20 , 35 ], as the manifold is only implicitly defined by the complex distribution of the diffusion model’s latent space. Furthermore, there exists a fundamental domain gap: while the unlearning update operates in the diffusion latent space, the anchor is optimized within the vast prompt embedding space. To bridge this gap, our key insight is that in diffusion architectures, a frozen model’s cross-attention maps explicitly dictate the layout of certain concepts when generating images, which implicitly reflect the data manifold learned by the model. By leveraging additional semantic constraints, these attention maps serve as a powerful and efficient proxy for tangential manifold proximity (as we prove in Appendix A.7), enabling us to optimize an anchor that aligns its attention patterns with those of the target concept. To this end, we propose AutoAnchor, a lightweight two-stage process that automatically synthesizes manifold-proximal anchors for diffusion unlearning. It first filters and aggregates valid candidate concepts automatically, and then optimizes the anchors to be manifold-proximal to the target concept. We further propose a cross-attention consistency loss, a novel manifold optimization surrogate specific to anchor construction. AutoAnchor empirically mitigates off-manifold drift and thus effectively achieves robust and unbiased diffusion unlearning. Moreover, it can be integrated into existing diffusion unlearning methods to further improve their unlearning performance. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: 2• Theoretical Formalization of Diffusion Unlearning Issues: We analyze diffusion unlearning via the manifold hypothesis and theoretically prove that lacking a manifold-proximal anchor inevitably causes normal-space drift, providing a mathematical explanation for why existing anchor-free and manual anchor-based methods fundamentally suffer from unstable diffusion unlearning. • Novel Manifold-Proximal Surrogate and Unlearning Framework: To overcome the computa-tional intractability and domain gap of direct manifold optimization, we introduce a cross-attention consistency loss. Using this surrogate loss, our proposed AutoAnchor efficiently and automatically constructs manifold-proximal anchors for achieving unbiased and robust diffusion unlearning. Furthermore, we theoretically explain how AutoAnchor approximates manifold proximity. • Empirical Evaluation: Extensive experiments show that AutoAnchor improves the diffusion un-learning performance of the state-of-the-art baselines in both concept removal (improving 6.30% in CLIP score on average) and model utility (improving 6.65% in CLIP score on average). Moreover, ablation studies show the effects of cross-attention loss and its robustness on hyperparameters. 

2 Related Works 

Based on how to determine the update direction, existing diffusion unlearning methods are categorized into anchor-free and anchor-based approaches. Anchor-free approaches determine their update directions using coarse signals like empty prompts, generic concepts, or unconditional scores. For example, ESD [ 13 ] utilizes the difference of predictions between the target concept and empty prompt as the update direction. Such imprecise directions cause a series of robustness issues in diffusion unlearning (unrobust against adversarial prompts, etc ). Although existing works have made efforts to address them, they target individual issues in isolation. Specifically, [ 54 ] only focuses on robustness against adversarial prompts; [ 22 , 16 , 41 , 1, 10 ] only aim to strengthen model utility; [ 30 ] is solely concerned with utility loss after multiple unlearning; and [ 34 , 14 , 51 ] only pursue effective concept removal. No single existing work solves these issues simultaneously, due to ignoring the root problem of imprecise update directions. Our method fundamentally fills this gap by generating a stable anchor for precise unlearning direction, thereby enabling robust diffusion unlearning. 

Anchor-based approaches pre-define specific anchor concepts for the target concepts, aiming to provide more precise unlearning directions than anchor-free methods. For example, ConAbl [ 23 ]first uses the generation direction differences between the target and anchor concepts as the update direction, thus realizing better utility preservation than ESD. Recent anchor-based methods further explore more sophisticated problems like reliable erasure [ 19 , 50 , 29 , 49 ], multiple unlearning [ 47 , 15 ], training-free unlearning [ 4 ], concept erosion [ 31 , 36 , 46 , 27 ], and attention-based unlearning [ 52 , 53 ]. However, existing anchor-based methods heavily rely on the assumption that their manually chosen anchors are precise. This assumption is not practical when semantically close anchors are not manifold-proximal to the target concepts, leading to biased unlearning as we show in Appendix §B.2. While some works [ 49 , 31 , 28 , 21 ] attempt to generate anchors automatically, they still rely on semantic closeness and thus fail to provide fundamentally unbiased anchors. Our method leverages cross-attention maps as a practical manifold surrogate that manages to construct manifold-proximal anchors, thereby achieving unbiased unlearning. Moreover, our method can be easily integrated into existing diffusion unlearning methods to further improve their unlearning performance.

5 Design of AutoAnchor 

To fundamentally avoid the instability and bias problems in diffusion unlearning, the anchor generated by AutoAnchor must satisfy three key requirements. (R1) Non-Synonymous: It must not be a direct synonym of the target concept. For example, using “naked” as an anchor fails to unlearn the "nude" concept, because the two distributions largely overlap, leaving the target insufficiently suppressed. 

(R2) Semantic Alternative: It should be a valid semantic alternative related to the target concept. For example, using an irrelevant concept “Mickey Mouse” as an anchor to unlearn the “nude” concept offers no meaningful guidance and effectively behaves as unconditional noise. (R3) Manifold-Proximal: It should be manifold-proximal to the target concept to guide a stable unlearning direction, as demonstrated by our theoretical analysis in §4. In addition to the three requirements of the anchor above, the generation process itself must be automatic (to avoid manually introducing extra subjective bias and ensure scalability of unlearning) and efficient (acting as a lightweight preprocessing step). To meet these criteria, we propose a two-stage framework, illustrated in Figure 2. The first stage ini-tializes the anchor by clustering and aggregating a diverse set of candidate concepts (§5.1), explicitly enforcing R1 and R2 . The second stage optimizes the initial anchor to approach manifold proximity using a cross-attention surrogate (§5.2), thereby satisfying R3 . Finally, we apply AutoAnchor to unlearn the target concept from the model, achieving robust and unbiased unlearning.

6 Evaluation 

Baselines & Diffusion Models. We compare AutoAnchor against seven state-of-the-art diffusion unlearning baselines: four anchor-free methods (ESD [ 13 ], TuneSD-AF (anchor-free) [ 41 ], ResAlign [ 29 ], and CCRT [ 15 ]) and three anchor-based methods (ConAbl [ 23 ], FMN [ 52 ] and TuneSD-AB (anchor-based) [ 41 ]). These baselines were primarily built on SD v1.4 and focused on erasing specific concept categories, including copyrighted characters (e.g., Mickey Mouse), artistic styles (e.g., Van Gogh), and unsafe content (e.g., nudity). We adopt the same settings to ensure a fair comparison. 

Evaluation Metrics. Following standard evaluation protocols, we assess model performance using CLIP scores and the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID). A higher CLIP score indicates better text-image semantic alignment, while a lower FID indicates superior image quality. We evaluate the models based on two primary criteria: (1) Removal : The model should fail to generate the erased concept, resulting in a low CLIP score on target concept prompts. (2) Utility Preservation : The model must retain its general generative capabilities on non-target concepts, which requires maintaining high text-image alignment (a high CLIP score) alongside high image quality (a low FID score). In §6.2, we conduct an ablation study to validate the necessity of our two-stage design. Next, in §6.3, we quantitatively confirm that AutoAnchor is highly efficient and practical for real-world deployment.

7 Conclusion 

In this paper, we theoretically established that unconstrained normal-space drift causes unrobust diffusion unlearning in anchor-free methods and biased unlearning in anchor-based methods with manually chosen anchors. The core solution is to construct a manifold-proximal anchor that provides a stable unlearning direction. To this end, we propose AutoAnchor, an automated diffusion unlearning framework that uses our cross-attention consistency surrogate loss to efficiently synthesize manifold-proximal anchors. Extensive empirical evaluations confirm that AutoAnchor achieves robust and unbiased diffusion unlearning efficiently. It is obvious that those concepts are typical alternatives of “Porsche” and satisfying R2 . However, it is noted that “Porsche’ itself is also involved, thereby violating R1 . Such violation will be more pronounced in the case of descriptive target concepts, e.g. , “grumpy” and “nude”. Therefore, we need to further filter the candidate set to ensure the validity of the anchor embedding. 

Candidate Filtering and Aggregation . We first encode the concepts in C0 and then apply fcluster =K-Means on those concept embeddings. The number of clusters M is automatically determined by the silhouette score [ 40 ], a widely accepted method in machine learning for determining cluster numbers. This algorithm measures the cohesion and separation of the clusters, which can cleanly separate synonyms from distinct semantic alternatives in the embedding space. In this case, the clustering results in two distinct clusters: Cluster 1 contains {car, vehicle, motorcycle, truck, convertible} and Cluster 2 contains {Porsche, Ferrari, BMW, Audi, Mercedes}. We then drop the cluster containing “Porsche” (Cluster 2 in this case) and form C1 using the concepts in the rest clusters (Cluster 1 in this case). After filtering the candidate set, we have C1 = {car, vehicle, motorcycle, truck, convertible },which are all valid alternatives of “Porsche” and satisfying both R1 and R2 . The anchor embedding

is then initialized by averaging the embeddings of those concepts in C1. Now we have a valid 27 anchor embedding that is semantically close to the target concept “Porsche” but does not contain the concept itself, which serves as a good starting point for the subsequent optimization stage. 

Calculating Regularization Parameters . Before optimization, we calculate the r− and r+ in Equation (6) in advance, which will be used as the regularization targets in the second stage. In this case, the closest and farthest cluster of “Porsche” are Cluster 2 2 and Cluster 1, respectively. Therefore, we calculate r− as the average cosine similarity between “Porsche” and the concepts in Cluster 2, and calculate r+ as the average cosine similarity between “Porsche” and the concepts in Cluster 1. During optimization, the anchor embedding ea might violate R1 (approaching to the target concept itself) or R2 (being too irrelevant). By forcing the cosine distance between ea and “Porsche” eu to be larger than r−, we prevent the anchor from being clustered with target concept, thereby maintaining 

R1 . On the other hand, the anchor embedding can not be too far from “Porsche” (violating R2 ). By setting r− and r+ for the regularization term, we can explicitly penalize the anchor embedding if it violates R1 and R2 during optimization. 

C Limitations 

While our framework provides formal trajectory stability guarantees for AutoAnchor, mapping abstract high-dimensional manifold geometry to concrete U-Net architectures necessitates several structural assumptions. Specifically, the analysis assumes (i) idealized regularity of the target data manifold Mu, (ii) strict isotropy of approximation errors, and (iii) an attention-geometry isomorphism that bounds geometric normal-space drift via cross-attention discrepancy. In particular, imposing a global Lipschitz constraint on the deep, non-linear dynamics of a U-Net is a strong abstraction; however, because direct manifold optimization is computationally intractable, this isomorphism serves as a necessary mechanism to link theoretical stability with actionable model activations. Despite these theoretical abstractions, AutoAnchor demonstrates strong robustness in practice. The massive ambient dimensionality ( d ≫ d∗ 

> c

) of image latent spaces yields an exponential margin of safety, effectively mitigating local violations of manifold regularity or strict error isotropy. Fur-thermore, optimizing our empirical surrogate—the cross-attention consistency loss—grounds this theoretical isomorphism. By minimizing this loss, AutoAnchor tightly bounds the geometric normal drift limit ζ, driving it toward zero. This formulation successfully accommodates manifold-preserving semantic shifts while ensuring the unlearning trajectory avoids the catastrophic normal-space leakage inherent to anchor-free methods. Moreover, AutoAnchor exhibits positive social impact to protect data owner’s right to be forgotten. It also helps prevent harmful generations. However, it is noted that machine unlearning itself risks being used as a harmful mechanism by removing and hampering useful knowledge.
