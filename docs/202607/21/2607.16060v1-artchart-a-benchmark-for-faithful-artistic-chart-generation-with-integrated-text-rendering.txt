Abstract 

Artistic charts make data memorable and visually engaging, but generating them faithfully at scale demands simultane-ously preserving numerical geometry, rendering exact in-image text, binding labels to correct marks, and maintain-ing coherent artistic style. Current text-to-image and image-editing models frequently fail on these coupled constraints, producing distorted geometries, hallucinated text, misbound labels, or over-stylized marks that undermine chart readability and mathematical integrity. This paper introduces ArtChart, a framework for artistic chart generation with integrated text rendering, encompassing a task definition, benchmark, evalu-ation protocol. To our knowledge, this is the first work to simul-taneously address mathematically faithful chart synthesis, ac-curate in-image text rendering, and artistic stylization of chart elements. The proposed ArtChart features a chart-specific plug-and-play module conditioned on text-free grayscale chart layouts, ensuring mathematical and logical fidelity. A rein-forcement learning strategy with complementary OCR ac-curacy, layout quality, and aesthetic rewards further refines generation quality, while a multi-expert distillation frame-work resolves inter-reward conflicts through specialized ex-pert optimization, enabling balanced and harmonious output. We construct ArtChart-Bench, a bilingual 2K-prompt bench-mark spanning four chart types, controlled value distributions, diverse label formats, and 15 artistic styles, with diagnostic metadata enabling fine-grained per-dimension analysis. We further design ArtChart-Eval, a six-axis evaluation suite cov-ering mathematical logic, text accuracy, text layout, aesthetics, instruction following, and readability, which supports fair and systematic comparison across pure T2I models, image-editing models, controllable-generation models, and closed-source APIs under a unified prompt-output protocol. Extensive ex-periments demonstrate that ArtChart consistently outperforms open-source T2I, image-editing, and ControlNet baselines, producing charts that are both visually appealing and math-ematically faithful. ArtChart-Bench provides a reproducible and extensible testbed, establishing a solid foundation for fu-ture research in artistic chart generation. 

## Introduction 

Statistical charts are precise instruments for communicat-ing data. Tools such as Matplotlib, ECharts, and Vega-Lite produce reproducible geometry, but their visual vocabulary is usually limited to conventional marks and layouts. Artis-tic charts pursue a complementary goal: they use expressive marks, textures, typography, and thematic backgrounds to make data more memorable in journalism, education, adver-tising, and presentations. Such charts are expensive because designers must preserve data fidelity while manually shaping marks, placing labels, and repairing readability issues. Modern text-to-image (T2I) models have made rapid progress in style synthesis, instruction following, and vi-sual text rendering (Qwen Team 2025; ERNIE Team 2026). This raises the possibility of automatically generating artis-tic charts. However, chart generation is not ordinary image synthesis. A visually pleasing output is invalid if a bar has the wrong height, a pie sector has the wrong angle, a digit is hallucinated, or a correct value is placed above the wrong category. Through pilot experiments, we summarize four re-curring failures: mathematical-logic distortion, text-content errors, text-layout mismatch, and visual-style/readability fail-ure (Fig. 3). We study artistic chart generation with integrated text rendering . Given a chart specification and a style prompt, a model should generate a raster image whose background is stylized, whose geometric marks are artistically transformed (e.g., bars as stacked books or scallions), and whose title, category labels, and value labels are rendered in the correct semantic locations. This differs from pictorial chart gener-ation, which focuses on replacing marks with semantic ob-jects but does not systematically render chart text (Xiao et al. 2026; Sun et al. 2026), and from creative table visualization, which generates infographic-like layouts through multi-stage MLLM workflows (Liu et al. 2026). Our method, ArtChart, adopts a three-stage training pipeline to jointly address structural fidelity, text accu-racy, and visual aesthetics. First, we render the input data into a text-free grayscale chart and train a chart-specific ControlNet, enabling explicit structural grounding for bar, horizontal-bar, pie, and area charts. Second, since the re-maining dominant errors are text and layout failures, we freeze the mathematical control module and train DiT LoRA adapters using reinforcement learning with OCR text accu-racy and VLM text-position rewards, progressively refining text correctness and spatial layout alignment. Third, we in-corporate an aesthetic reward and address inter-reward con-flicts through a multi-expert distillation strategy: three single-reward experts are trained independently to their respective performance ceilings, then distilled into a unified student arXiv:2607.16060v1 [cs.CV] 17 Jul 2026 The category data are " Leaning Tower of Pisa 56, Tokyo Tower

Our contributions are: 

• We define artistic chart generation as a faithful image-generation task requiring numerical geometry, integrated text rendering, text-layout binding, and artistic stylization. 

• We propose ArtChart, combining a plug-and-play math control module, RL learning with text, layout, and aes-thetic rewards, and a multi-expert distillation strategy to resolve inter-reward conflicts for balanced optimization. 

• We construct ArtChart-Bench, a 2K bilingual benchmark with controlled data, text, style, chart-type distributions, diagnostic metadata, and a failure taxonomy tailored to artistic charts. 

• We provide ArtChart-Eval, a six-axis automatic evalua-tion protocol and a controlled experimental design over T2I, editing, controllable-generation, and ablation base-lines, plus human preference evaluation. 

## Related Work 

Programmatic visualization and chart generation. 

Classical visualization systems such as Matplotlib, D3.js, and ECharts provide deterministic data-to-geometry mappings and editable outputs (Hunter 2007; Bostock, Ogievetsky, and Heer 2011; Li et al. 2018). Recent natural-language visual-ization tools such as LIDA and Chat2VIS translate user intent into visualization code (Dibia 2023; Maddigan and Susnjak 2023). These systems are strong when the target is a con-ventional chart because correctness is backed by executable specifications. They are less suited to fully rasterized artistic charts, where the marks, background, typography, and deco-ration are synthesized jointly and may not correspond to ed-itable vector primitives. ArtChart-Bench therefore evaluates an image-generation regime complementary to chart-to-code systems. 

Pictorial and stylized visualization. 

Early stylized visualization systems used retrieval, decom-position, or human-AI editing to inject semantic content into chart marks. ChartSpark embeds semantic context into chart foreground or background and evaluates data distor-tion (Xiao et al. 2024); viz2viz decomposes and recomposes marks through diffusion pipelines (Wu, Chung, and Adar 2023); ChartEditor supports human-AI paired pictorial chart authoring (Yan et al. 2025). Recent ChArtist and SSAlign train stronger structure-subject controllers for pictorial chart generation (Xiao et al. 2026; Sun et al. 2026). These works focus on replacing or transforming marks while preserving visual extent. ArtChart differs by requiring title, category la-bels, and value labels to be rendered inside the image and by treating label-value binding as a benchmark metric rather than an optional annotation layer. 

Infographics and reliability benchmarks. 

ShowTable converts markdown tables into creative table vi-sualizations through rewriting, generation, reflection, and re-finement (Liu et al. 2026). Its multi-stage pipeline explicitly reasons over text and layout, but the resulting marks are often conventional geometric elements inside infographic compo-sitions. IGenBench evaluates text-to-infographic reliability via atomic yes/no questions (Tang et al. 2026). ChartQA and MatplotBench evaluate chart understanding or chart-code generation (Masry et al. 2022; Yang et al. 2024). In con-trast, ArtChart-Bench targets generated artistic chart images where quantitative geometry, visual text, and artistic mark deformation are all produced by image models and can fail independently. 

Visual text rendering. 

Accurate visual text rendering is a prerequisite for generated charts because a single wrong digit or unit can invalidate the image. GlyphByT5, AnyText, TextDiffuser, and EasyText improve text rendering with character-aware encoders, glyph priors, or layout control (Liu et al. 2024; Tuo et al. 2024; Chen et al. 2023; Liu et al. 2025a). TextPecker and TextAlign in-troduce reward-based or hierarchical text-alignment signals (Zhu et al. 2026; Cui et al. 2026). Controllable image generation. 

ControlNet introduces a conditional branch for spatial con-trol and has become a standard mechanism for using edges, depth, or masks as image-generation conditions (Zhang, Rao, and Agrawala 2023). ControlNet++ further improves condi-tional control with consistency feedback (Li et al. 2024). For artistic charts, the key condition is not an edge map but a text-free grayscale chart whose mark geometry encodes values. Generic ControlNets trained on natural-image conditions do not reliably interpret these chart-specific structures, motivat-ing our chart-specific ControlNet training and the inclusion of generic-ControlNet baselines in ArtChart-Bench. 

RL alignment for diffusion and flow models. 

DDPO and DPOK first showed that diffusion models can be optimized with online rewards (Black et al. 2024; Fan et al. 2024). Flow-GRPO adapts GRPO to flow-matching models by converting deterministic ODE sampling into an equivalent SDE with tractable transition likelihoods (Liu et al. 2025b). Flow-OPD addresses multi-reward interference by training single-reward experts and distilling them into one student policy through on-policy teacher velocity fields (Fang et al. 2026). POCA studies Pareto-aware sample selec-tion for conflicting rewards (Fan et al. 2026). We use these methods as building blocks, but ArtChart-Bench evaluates them in a structured domain where OCR, layout, aesthetics, and mathematical geometry can conflict. 

## Task, Taxonomy, and Benchmark 

Task definition. An input instance is (T, D, P ), where T ∈{bar , hbar , pie , area } is a chart type, D = {(ci, v i)}ni=1 is a one-dimensional categorical dataset, and P is an artistic prompt describing the style, theme, mark metaphor, title, and layout. The output image I should satisfy four require-ments: (1) marks encode D according to T ; (2) marks and background are artistically stylized and coherent with P ; (3) the title, categories, and values are rendered correctly; and (4) each category/value is placed at the correct semantic lo-cation. 

Failure taxonomy. We group failures into four classes. ML 

(math logic): proportion distortion, order errors, and wrong mark counts. TC (text content): missing, duplicated, wrong, or garbled characters. TL (text layout): label-value mismatch, overlap, or position drift. VS (visual style): prompt-style mis-match or over-stylization that makes marks unreadable. 

Benchmark construction. ArtChart-Bench contains 2K evaluation prompts: 1K English and 1K Chinese, each with 250 samples per chart type. Category counts follow a long-tailed distribution from 2 to 10: 2 (3%), 3 (15%), 4 (17%), 5 (20%), 6 (18%), 7 (12%), 8 (8%), 9 (4%), and 10 (3%). Value orders include unordered (60%), increasing (15%), decreasing (15%), and all-equal (10%) sequences; value magnitudes include large-difference (40%), small-difference (40%), extreme-value (10%), and zero/tiny-value (10%) cases. Category text covers pure words, digit-word mix-tures, alphanumeric labels, pure numbers, mixed-language strings, and symbols such as currency signs. Display values cover pure numbers (33%), number+unit (33%), and num-ber+symbol (34%). We use 15 style families from minimalist to highly complex.

Quality control. The benchmark and training data fol-low separate quality-control procedures. Evaluation prompts are manually checked for unambiguous data, valid style de-scriptions, and deterministic chart type. Training images are more aggressively filtered because noisy teacher images can teach wrong geometry or unstable layout. We first compare artistic mark geometry with the grayscale condition; then run OCR and VLM layout checks; finally, human reviewers discard examples with text hallucination, label-value swaps, implausible mark proportions, or over-stylized marks that cannot be decoded as charts. This separation avoids leaking model-specific teacher artifacts into the benchmark while still allowing large-scale training data construction. 

## Method 

Chart-Specific Mathematical Control Module. 

We use Qwen-Image-2512 as the T2I backbone because it provides strong multilingual text rendering and instruction following. Given (T, D ), we first render a grayscale condition 

G with deterministic Python chart code. For vertical and horizontal bars, G encodes bar height or length; for pie charts, sector angles; for area charts, filled regions. The condition contains no title, category labels, values, axes, or ticks. We train a chart-specific ControlNet branch on (P, G, I ⋆) while freezing the base generator: 

Lctrl = Et,ϵ,I ⋆ ∥vθ,ϕ (xt, t, P, G ) − v⋆t ∥22 . (1) Here xt is the noisy latent at timestep t, v⋆t is the target flow velocity, θ denotes the frozen base model, and ϕ denotes the trainable ControlNet parameters. The grayscale condi-tion deliberately contains no text, so the controller learns mathematical structure rather than copying chart scaffold-ing. In practice, this stage uses about 13K filtered (P, G, I ⋆)

triples. The strongest improvement is on MathLogic, but we also observe that consistent teacher layouts help the Control-Net stage learn coarse text-placement conventions.

## Experiments 

Training configuration. ControlNet training uses the 13K filtered triples at 1024 × 1024 or 1024 × 768 resolution, with the base Qwen-Image weights frozen. Flow-GRPO uses 10K+ prompt-condition pairs, group size 16 for the main text/layout runs and 24 for OPD sampling, noise scale 

a = 0 .7, KL weight β = 5 × 10 −3, clipping threshold 

ϵ = 10 −4, and LoRA rank/scale r = 32 , α = 64 . We freeze the chart ControlNet during all RL and OPD stages. This separation is deliberate: the ControlNet is responsible for mathematical geometry, while the DiT LoRA is responsible for text rendering, text placement, and visual style. 

Inference. The final model composes Qwen-Image-2512, the chart ControlNet, the OPD LoRA, and an optional Light-ning LoRA. The method takes a prompt and a grayscale image as input. we utilize an LLM prompt expansion mod-ule to transform the structured data and the content theme inputs into image-generation prompts—incorporating ele-ments such as chart type, title, data list, visual metaphors, color schemes, and stylistic descriptions.. 

Main Results. Table 3 and Fig. 6 shows that prompt-only T2I retains moderate aesthetics but fails on math, text, and layout. Editing improves structure but remains weak on text. Generic ControlNet improves math but is not tuned for artistic chart text. ArtChart improves all six metrics.

## Discussion and Limitations 

Despite the strong performance of ArtChart, several limita-tions remain. ArtChart still relies on explicit grayscale con-ditions; it does not prove that pure T2I models can infer chart geometry directly from text. ArtChart-Bench focuses on one-dimensional categorical data and four chart types; grouped, stacked, multi-series, scatter, radar, and dashboard settings remain future work. OCR and VLM judges may introduce evaluator bias. Developing more robust, chart-specific evalu-ation protocols remains an open challenge for the community. 

## Conclusion 

We present ArtChart, a unified framework encompassing a task definition, benchmark, evaluator, and generation sys-tem for artistic chart generation with integrated text ren-dering. By combining a chart-specific mathematical control module conditioned on text-free grayscale layouts, a multi-reward reinforcement learning strategy optimizing text accu-racy, layout quality, and visual aesthetics, and a multi-expert distillation mechanism that resolves inter-reward conflicts, ArtChart jointly addresses the coupled requirements of nu-merical faithfulness, text correctness, label binding, visual appeal, and readability. ArtChart-Bench and ArtChart-Eval provide a unified testbed for comparing T2I, image-editing, and controllable generation methods, tracking progress to-ward image generators that produce reliable visual commu-nication rather than merely attractive images.
