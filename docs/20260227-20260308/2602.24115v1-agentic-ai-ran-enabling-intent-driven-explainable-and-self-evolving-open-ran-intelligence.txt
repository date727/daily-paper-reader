Abstract —Open RAN (O-RAN) exposes rich control and teleme-try interfaces across the Non-RT RIC, Near-RT RIC, and dis-tributed units, but also makes it harder to operate multi-tenant, multi-objective RANs in a safe and auditable manner. In parallel, agentic AI systems with explicit planning, tool use, memory, and self-management offer a natural way to structure long-lived con-trol loops. This article surveys how such agentic controllers can be brought into O-RAN: we review the O-RAN architecture, contrast agentic controllers with conventional ML/RL xApps, and organise the task landscape around three clusters: network slice life-cycle, radio resource management (RRM) closed loops, and cross-cutting security, privacy, and compliance. We then introduce a small set of agentic primitives (Plan-Act-Observe-Reflect, skills as tool use, memory and evidence, and self-management gates) and show, in a multi-cell O-RAN simulation, how they improve slice life-cycle and RRM performance compared to conventional baselines and ablations that remove individual primitives. Security, privacy, and compliance are discussed as architectural constraints and open challenges for standards-aligned deployments. This framework achieves an average 8.83% reduction in resource usage across three classic network slices. 

Index Terms —Open RAN, agentic AI, xApps, digital twin, LLMs. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

A. Motivation and Background 

The evolution toward Open Radio Access Networks (O-RAN) aims to enable vendor-neutral, flexible, and intelligent management of radio access infrastructures by disaggregating functions and exposing standardized controller interfaces [1], [2]. The O-RAN architecture employs a disaggregated RAN design with layered intelligence, utilizing Non-RT and Near-RT RICs [3]. Building on this foundation, industry and academia have demonstrated modular xApps and rApps for traffic steer-ing, which have been explored in several control functions [4], [5]. Recent work pushes this vision toward AI-native O-RAN and agentic network architectures, where learning-enabled con-trollers become first-class components rather than bolt-on op-timizers [6]. AI-native architectures aim to embed machine learning pipelines across the SMO, RICs, and O-Cloud to realize real-time adaptation, conflict resolution, and efficient resource allocation under diverse and sometimes conflicting service-level agreements [7], [8]. On the algorithmic side, reinforcement learning (RL) and multi-agent RL (MARL) have been adopted for RAN slicing and RRM in RIC-centric designs, e.g., using double DQN and deep RL agents to optimize spectrum allocation, power control, and slice-specific resource sharing in dense urban scenarios [9], [10]. While these ap-proaches can outperform static heuristics and adapt to non-stationary traffic conditions, they typically assume fixed task definitions, single control objectives per agent, and extensive offline training on curated traces. In parallel, the O-RAN Alliance and several vendors have begun to develop digital-twin RAN (DT-RAN) platforms that mirror network states and provide a safe sandbox for training and validating AI models under realistic radio conditions [9], [11]. These platforms support what-if analysis, offline training, and regression testing of control policies as traffic or network conditions evolve. Therefore, AI-native architectures and DT-RANs indicate that O-RAN control loops become increasingly data-driven and closed-loop [12]. However, most existing solu-tions still treat learning controllers as monolithic black boxes. They usually focus on narrow, isolated tasks. They offer limited support for explicit goals, reusable skills, memory, or self-governance across O-RAN control layers. These limitations motivate the Agentic AI-RAN perspective developed in this work. 

B. Contribution 

This paper advocates an Agentic AI-RAN perspective, which models O-RAN control entities as goal-driven agents that op-erate across Non-RT, Near-RT, and RT layers. Building on this perspective, we introduce a structured agentic framework and demonstrate its implications for O-RAN control through archi-tectural design, methodological formalization, and simulation-

A. O-RAN Architecture 

The O-RAN architecture adopts a disaggregated RAN de-sign with layered intelligence, separating radio and baseband functions across O-RU, O-DU, and O-CU, and exposing pro-grammable control through the RAN Intelligent Controller (RIC), as shown in Fig. 1. The RIC spans multiple time scales, with the Near-RT RIC enabling sub-second control and the Non-RT RIC, integrated with the SMO, supporting slower policy and orchestration functions [13]. Within this architecture, control logic is realized via modular xApps and rApps deployed on the Near-RT and Non-RT RICs, respectively, and interacting with the RAN through standard-ized interfaces such as E2 and A1. While this modularity enables flexible, multi-timescale control, the growing number of xApps in heterogeneous and dynamic networks makes coordination, conflict resolution, and life-cycle management in-creasingly challenging, motivating the need for more intelligent and coordinated control frameworks.

The aim is to realize a Plan–Act–Observe–Reflect loop without violating control-loop timing budgets. An intent interpreter and goal model translate operator ob-jectives into machine-checkable goals that capture performance objectives, operational constraints, and resource budgets. Op-erator objectives may be expressed in natural language for human interaction; for example, an operator may specify a high-level requirement such as “reduce URLLC tail latency under an energy cap”. In the proposed framework, such ex-pressions are assumed to have been grounded into a structured, machine-checkable goal representation before being consumed by learning-based controllers. The grounding process itself may be realised through predefined intent templates, policy-as-code, or LLM-based semantic parsing, and is treated as a configuration or orchestration step outside the time-critical control loop. Based on these grounded goals and the current network context (such as KPM/KQI summaries, topology, and slice mix), a planner synthesizes short control plans and explicitly aligns them with O-RAN time scales, ranging from slower policy decisions at the Non-RT layer to fast revisions and micro-actions at the Near-RT and RT layers. Execution is realised through skills that encapsulate O-RAN controllable actions, along with their preconditions, expected effects, and rollback options. These skills are bound to standard O-RAN interfaces. Multi-step interventions are expressed as short skill sequences with bounded rates, amplitudes, and scopes, enabling incremental and safe control adjustments. Memory and evidence improve data efficiency and auditabil-ity by providing context across multiple time scales. Short-term context supports fast conditioning at the Near-RT layer, while episodic traces and long-term knowledge capture past interventions and reusable control knowledge. Reflection and self-evaluation guide action selection with respect to risk, uncertainty, and budget adherence. When a gate is not satisfied, the agent delays execution, reduces the action magnitude, or rolls back to a vetted baseline, improving robustness under sparse or stale observations [13]. Guardrails enforce safety invariants at the action layer and enable graceful degradation under uncertainty or resource stress. Lightweight constraints limit the rate, magnitude, and scope of individual actions, while decision-level evidence records link intent, context, selected skills, and observed out-comes to support human and regulatory audit without exposing raw customer data. A cadence scheduler aligns planning and execution with O-RAN control loops by enforcing worst-case latency and resource budgets; when capacity is constrained, it adaptively simplifies plans or reverts to safe baselines. These mechanisms yield agents that are goal-aware, tool-capable, memory-efficient, and self-governed, while remaining interop-erable with A1/E2/E2SM across heterogeneous vendors. 

C. Agentic vs Traditional ML/RL vs MARL 

This subsection contrasts agentic AI with traditional su-pervised/unsupervised learning, single-agent RL, hierarchical RL (HRL), and multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL), and explains why these distinctions matter in O–RAN. While traditional ML and RL typically realise fixed mappings or reac-tive policies, and HRL introduces temporal abstraction through multi-level policy learning, MARL focuses on coordination among multiple learners. In contrast, agentic AI centres on a goal-driven loop that plans over reusable skills , retrieves and reuses knowledge, and evaluates actions against explicit intent, risk, and budget constraints. Importantly, agentic controllers can encapsulate ML or RL policies as callable skills and orchestrate them under unified control objectives [15]. A first axis of distinction is temporal alignment with O–RAN control loops. Traditional ML, RL, and MARL naturally align with specific layers and time scales, whereas agentic AI is designed to span them, combining long-horizon planning at the Non–RT layer with short-horizon sequencing at Near–RT and fast actuation at the dApp level. By separating deliberation from execution and enforcing explicit budgets, agentic con-trollers meet control-loop deadlines while supporting multi-step interventions. A second axis concerns safety, auditability, and multi-objective alignment. Unlike ML, RL, and MARL approaches that rely largely on external safeguards, agentic AI integrates guardrails, rollback, and decision-level evidence by design, enabling explainable and auditable control under coupled ob-jectives and domain shift. In contrast to HRL, where con-straints and safety requirements are typically encoded implicitly through reward shaping, agentic AI enforces them explicitly through online gating, rollback, and decision-level evidence. A third axis is data efficiency and generalization. Rather than relying solely on retraining, agentic AI leverages memory and retrieval to reuse prior experience and verified skills in new contexts. In practice, this enables hybrid operation in O–RAN, where fast RL policies handle routine regimes, while agentic planning intervenes during rare or disruptive events that require reasoning, adaptation, and evidence. In summary, traditional ML, RL, and MARL are best suited to specific O–RAN control regimes, whereas agentic AI is preferable when intents, multi-step interventions, explicit safety budgets, and auditability are first-class requirements. These paradigms are complementary rather than exclusive: in practice, an agentic layer orchestrates heuristics, ML predictors, and RL/MARL policies as tools under explicit timing and safety contracts. III. T ASK LANDSCAPE 

A. Network Slice Life-cycle 

From an agentic perspective, each network slice is managed along a life-cycle encompassing design and intent capture, admission and placement, runtime assurance and elastic scaling, and troubleshooting with feedback to billing and fairness. Tenant requirements are translated at the Non-RT RIC/SMO into machine-checkable goals capturing performance objec-tives, constraints, and resource budgets, and compiled into intent-aligned A1 policies. Admission and placement decisions combine the current network context with effect models and retrieved cases to assess SLA risk and resource usage. If budgets are satisfied, the slice is admitted and enforced incrementally at the Near-RT layer via A1 with rollback support. Otherwise, the slice is rejected, and limiting factors are recorded. During operation, Near-RT components maintain per-slice observability under E2 budgets and apply agentic gates to adapt or undo adjustments. Elastic scaling is handled incrementally through short skill sequences under continuous KPM/KQI monitoring, allowing symmetric scale-in when load subsides. Troubleshooting closes the life-cycle. When SLA risks or anomalies arise, scoped mitigations are applied based on prior experience. Meanwhile, decision-level evidence is recorded to support explainable charging, fairness attribution, and future design and admission decisions.

C. Security, Privacy, and Compliance 

Security, privacy, and compliance form a distinct slice of the O-RAN task landscape because they constrain how control decisions are made across all layers. In an agentic formulation, these concerns are embedded directly into the decision pro-cess as explicit constraints and budgets, shaping data usage, isolation, and operational overheads alongside performance objectives. At the Non-RT RIC/SMO, security and privacy are primarily realized through policy-as-code and intent-aligned governance policies. An agentic controller selects appropriate privacy-preserving mechanisms based on regulatory context and data sensitivity, and encodes these choices into A1 policies that serve as durable evidence for audit and accountability. At the Near-RT layer, these policies are enforced within fast control loops using the same gated execution mechanisms applied to SLA and resource management. Telemetry is contin-uously checked against declared compliance budgets, allowing the agent to tighten, delay, or revert actions when violations are imminent, while maintaining compatibility with O-RAN timing constraints. Security and compliance monitoring are treated as first-class concerns throughout the control loop. Decision-level records link intents, applied controls, and observed outcomes, supporting audits, regulatory reporting, and iterative policy refinement. This ensures that security, privacy, and compliance remain integrated with performance-oriented agentic control rather than being handled as external safeguards. IV. A GENTIC PRIMITIVES

B. Skills as Tool-Use 

In O–RAN, a skill is a thin, verifiable wrapper around a controllable primitive that the agent invokes as a tool. Each skill is treated as a structured object with explicit preconditions, bounded actuation, expected effects, costs, and a compensat-ing rollback, enabling safe and vendor-agnostic execution of agentic logic. By binding skills to standard O–RAN interfaces, the same abstraction can be applied consistently across the Non–RT, Near–RT, and RT layers. Skills are designed to be composable and incrementally committed. Instead of treating control plans as indivisible actions, the agent executes short skill sequences incrementally, observing outcomes and rolling back when risks or deviations exceed declared budgets. To coordinate concurrent controllers, skills are locally scoped and reversible, allowing conflicts to be detected and ordered without centralized arbitration. This tool-centric representation supports concise and auditable control sequences that meet timing, safety, and interoperability requirements across heterogeneous deployments.

D. Self-Management 

Self-management governs how an agent maintains safety and stability while pursuing goals under strict latency and budget constraints. It combines online self-evaluation, pacing control, conflict handling across coexisting xApps, and graceful degradation and recovery. The objective is to keep interventions predictable and reversible, and to ensure that changes applied through A1/E2 can be justified and, if necessary, rolled back within the deadline. The core mechanism is a gate that evaluates each incremental commit against calibrated risk, resource budgets, and explana-tion consistency. Let rt denote predicted SLA-violation risk, ut

The agent detects con-flicts based on scope intersection and predicted impact on shared KPMs, and enforces a consistent ordering when needed (e.g., applying power caps before PRB reallocation in interference-limited regimes). E. Overview 

Fig. 3 shows the proposed Agentic AI–RAN architecture with a LLM placed at the Non–RT RIC. Due to the strict time sensitivity of O-RAN control loops, LLM-based reasoning is intentionally confined to the Non–RT layer, where minute-level inference is feasible without violating real-time constraints. At this layer, the LLM periodically (1 min) summarizes the behavior and performance of RL xApps running in the Near– RT RIC, including resource utilization, KPM/KQI trends, and observed control outcomes. Based on this global and long-horizon view, the Non–RT LLM optimizes network slicing decisions, reallocates slice-level resource budgets, and determines which Near–RT RL controllers should be activated under different slicing config-urations to improve overall resource efficiency. The resulting policies are delivered to the Near–RT RIC via A1, where time-critical RL-based control and deterministic gating are performed. This separation allows semantic reasoning and cross-slice optimization to benefit from LLM capabilities, while preserving the responsiveness and safety required by O-RAN real-time control. V. S IMULATION RESULTS 

A. Experimental Setup 

We consider a simulated O-RAN segment deployed over a 500m × 500m area with six O-RUs, each hosting two radio cells (RCs), for a total of 12 cells. The number of active UEs is 20 to emulate light to moderately loaded conditions. Propagation is modelled using a free-space path loss channel, and each RC transmits at 30 dBm over 5G NR bands N77 and N78. Mobility and cell association procedures are handled through a standard received-signal-strength-based handover cri-terion. This setting provides a controlled yet representative environment to benchmark different agentic control variants under increasing load and multi-cell, multi-band operation. In our simulations, each experiment consists of 50 independent episodes, each corresponding to a fixed network scenario with stochastic traffic and mobility realizations. Each episode lasts 600 seconds of simulated time. 

B. Agentic Ablations 1) Fully Agentic: The full Agentic controller implements planning over skills, sequencing, self-management gating, multi-horizon memory and retrieval, and active KPM sampling. All ablations are derived from this configuration by disabling specific primitives. 

2) No-plan: The No-Plan variant disables multi-step plan-ning and selects only a single next skill reactively from the current context. This isolates the benefit of explicit long-horizon planning over myopic decision making. 

3) No-memory: The No-Memory variant removes episodic and long-term memory and retrieval, retaining only short-term KPM/KQI windows. It quantifies the contribution of case reuse and cross-episode knowledge transfer under changing traffic and mobility. 

4) No-gate: In the No-Gate variant, skill sequences are executed without self-management checks on risk, uncertainty, budgets, or explanation consistency. Comparison with the full agent highlights the role of gating in reducing SLA violations and unstable behavior. 

5) No-sequence: The No-Sequence variant restricts plans to a single skill, disabling composite multi-step interventions. This reveals the impact of skill sequencing compared to isolated knob adjustments. 

6) No-active-KPM: The No-Active-KPM variant disables active measurement control and relies on a fixed set of passively collected KPMs. It evaluates the benefit of information-aware telemetry under an E2 budget.

These gains demonstrate that LLMs can enhance resource efficiency and slicing precision when confined to non-real-time layers, without violating O-RAN control-loop constraints or introducing LLM-based rea-soning into time-critical RAN paths. VI. C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS 

Open RAN transforms the RAN into an open, software-defined platform, but also expands the control space across slices, services, and vendors. This article proposed an Agentic AI-RAN perspective, in which coordinated, goal-driven agents are embedded across the SMO/Non-RT RIC, Near-RT RIC, and CU/DU to operate over O-RAN skills with explicit timing and budget awareness. A compact set of agentic primitives is shown to support network slice life-cycle and RRM tasks while remaining interoperable with existing O-RAN specifications. The design combines a structured planning and execution loop with skill-based control, multi-timescale memory and evidence, and self-management gating. Through multi-cell simulations, a fully agentic controller was compared against ablated variants and deep RL baselines deployed as monolithic xApps. The results indicate that explicit planning, memory, and gating improve SLA satisfaction and stability, while making trade-offs in control overhead and energy more transparent and auditable. Future work includes integrating richer RL and MARL components within the agentic framework. Performance will be evaluated in more realistic digital-twin environments. In addition, standardised hooks such as skill catalogues, evidence buses, and policy-as-code guardrails will be explored to support scalable deployment of Agentic AI-RAN in operational networks. ## A Non–RT LLM System Prompt Specification 

The following listing shows the exact system prompt used in our simulations to configure the Non–RT LLM for network slicing coordination. 

SYSTEM ROLE: 

You are a Non-RT RIC assistant for O-RAN network slicing coordination. 

You do NOT perform real-time control or per-UE optimization. 

INPUT: 

You receive a periodic summary from Near-RT RIC, including: 

- Slice-level KPIs (SLA violation rate, p95/p99 latency) 

- Resource usage statistics (PRB share, energy, control actions) 

- Stability indicators of active RL xApps 

- Current slice configuration and budgets 

TASK: 

1. Summarize the performance of each network slice. 

2. Identify resource inefficiencies or SLA risks at slice level. 

3. Recommend updated slice-level budgets or priorities. 

4. Recommend which Near-RT RL controllers should be activated, limited, 

or deprioritized. 

CONSTRAINTS: 

- Do not generate per-UE or per-cell actions. 

- Do not modify Near-RT control logic. 

- Respect operator-defined policies and isolation constraints. 

OUTPUT FORMAT (STRICT): 

- Slice summary: bullet list 

- Resource assessment: short paragraph 

- Coordination recommendation: bullet list
