# ABSTRACT 

Recent attacks show that behavioural unlearning of large language models leaves internal traces recoverable by adversarial probes [ 1– 4 ]. We characterise where this retention lives and show it can be surgically removed without measurable capability cost. Our central protocol is a leave-one-out cross-sequence probe that tests whether a memorisation signature generalises across held-out sequences. The signature is real and consistent across scale: memorisation-specific gaps of +0 .32 ,

+0 .19 , +0 .30 on Pythia-70M, GPT-2 medium, and Mistral-7B against a random-initialisation control on the same architecture, where the deep-layer gap collapses to −0.04 at the network’s deepest layer (L6 of Pythia-70M, where the pretrained signature peaks). The probe direction is causally separable from recall — projecting it out collapses the signature locally ( +0 .44 → − 0.19 ) while behavioural recall barely changes — and a probe trained on naturally memorised content does not classify fine-tuning-injected secrets as memorised, marking two representationally distinct regimes. We then introduce probe-geometry alignment (PGA), a surgical erasure that aligns activations along the probe’s live readout direction at each depth (one scalar per depth, not the full residual). PGA drives the cross-sequence probe below random chance at all four scales tested (toy: depth-4 0 .17 ;Pythia-70M 0.07 ; Mistral-7B 0.45 ; GPT-2 medium 0.06 via MD-PGA k = 2 ) and remains robust to six adversarial probe variants. Against a stronger threat — a re-fitting attacker who trains a fresh probe on PGA-treated activations — we extend PGA adversarially (iterative orthogonal subspace augmentation), defeating the re-fit probe at every memorisation-relevant depth while preserving five zero-shot capability benchmarks (HellaSwag, PIQA, BoolQ, ARC-Easy, WinoGrande) within 

2.8 percentage points per task (max |∆acc | = 2 .8pp on BoolQ; mean ∆acc = +0 .2pp). The cross-sequence signature is a real, causally separable, regime-specific property of pretrained representations — removable below chance with a single rank-one intervention per depth at no measurable capability cost.

# 1 Introduction 

Machine unlearning for large language models has a measurement problem. Every major evaluation framework — TOFU [ 5], MUSE [ 6], WMDP [ 7] — assesses whether a model still generates the target content. None assess whether the model still encodes it internally. We show these are different questions with different answers, using a probe protocol whose validity does not depend on the saturated within-sequence behaviour it replaces. 

Why this matters. The stakes for unlearning are real. Regulatory frameworks and legal pressures — including GDPR’s Right to be Forgotten and increasing copyright litigation — mandate removal of personal data and copyrighted material from trained models. Model developers must remove trade secrets, proprietary datasets, and unsafe content discovered post-deployment [ 8, 7]. Concurrent attacks show that a model can behaviorally suppress a memorized secret — stopping generation on command — yet leave sufficient internal trace that adversarial probes extract it [ 1 , 2]. If unlearning is only behavioral, it is compliance theater: the information is still there, just silent. This paper measures whether the information is actually gone from the model’s internal representations, not merely hidden from the output. 

The cross-sequence signature. Our central empirical finding is that naturally memorized content produces a cross-sequence linear signature that generalizes via leave-one-out (LOO): a probe trained on memorized/non-memorized activation pairs for some sequences classifies a held-out sequence at high accuracy. The memorization-specific gap (true LOO minus pure-distinguishability baseline) is +0 .32 on Pythia-70M, +0 .19 on GPT-2 medium, and +0 .30 on Mistral-7B (Fig. 6), against a random-initialization control on the same architecture: while random-init Pythia retains some cross-sequence structure in shallow layers (mean transformer-layer gap +0 .19 , dominated by L0/L1 token-identity propagation through random transformations), this baseline collapses to −0.04 at L6 — the deepest layer, where the pretrained signature peaks. The pretraining-attributable signature thus lives in deep layers where random-init has essentially no structure. The signature is cluster-specific (strong for formal-register English and licenses, near-null for code and pseudo-Latin) and a probe trained on natural memorization does not recognize fine-tuning-injected secrets (Section 5.5) — two representationally distinct memorization regimes. 

Toy verification and a constructive follow-up. Section 4 mechanistically verifies the dissociation in a controlled toy transformer where causal attribution is unambiguous (single dominant attention head, normalised causal effect 

= 1 .000 ). The toy also surfaces the measurement problem motivating LOO probing: single-sequence probes saturate at 

1.000 by construction (Section 3.1), so they cannot reveal whether unlearning has actually changed internal structure. Building on the dissociation finding, we introduce probe-geometry alignment (PGA), a surgical erasure that aligns activations along the probe’s live readout direction at each depth. PGA collapses the cross-sequence probe ( 1.00 → 0.65 

on the toy, deep depths below random chance), preserves PPL, holds against six adversarial probe variants, and scales across four architectures spanning four orders of magnitude ( 0.8M toy → Mistral-7B). Whether the probe-detected structure is exploitable for adversarial token extraction at scale [9] remains a separate open question.

# 2 Background 

Machine unlearning. Machine unlearning aims to remove the influence of specific training data without retraining the model from scratch [ 13 ]. For language models the standard recipes range from gradient scrubbing [ 14 ] and character-level surgical replacement [ 15 ] to gradient-orthogonal preference updates such as NPO [ 16 ], with surveys [ 17 ]cataloguing dozens of variants. The dominant evaluation framework, TOFU [ 5], asks whether the model still generates 

the target sequence; by this metric most methods succeed. The success is fragile, however: a benign downstream fine-tuning resurrects forgotten content [ 3 , 18 , 19 ], post-hoc quantisation alone reverses the effect of unlearning [ 4 ], and behavioural suppression does not generalise to paraphrased or semantically adjacent prompts [ 20 ]; broader surveys reach similar conclusions [ 21 , 22 ]. All of these results infer representational retention indirectly — by exhibiting a robustness failure — and leave the locus of retention unspecified. Our work measures retention directly via cross-sequence linear probes on the residual stream and identifies the depths and directions that carry it. 

Behaviour vs. representation, and causal tracing. The distinction between behavioural removal (the model stops emitting the target) and representational removal (the model stops encoding it) is well established. Linear probes lower-bound the information a hidden state carries about a class [ 23 , 24 ]; if a probe still classifies post-unlearning, the relevant structure is still there. Knowledge editing [ 25 , 26 ] and activation steering [ 27 ] demonstrate that facts are distributed across many parameters and depths, so an edit applied at one head or layer can leave upstream storage intact. We use causal tracing [ 25 ] to identify the minimal circuit [ 28 ] that produces the secret completion, and use the cross-sequence probe to test whether erasure has reached that storage rather than merely silencing the output. 

Positioning relative to concurrent work. Two recent papers report directly that internal representations carry traces of unlearned content. Chen et al. [1] extract spectral fingerprints from principal activation directions and use them to classify unlearned models with high accuracy. Xu et al. [2] quantify representational drift of an unlearned model relative to its pre-unlearning version via PCA, CKA, and Fisher information, taxonomising reversible from irreversible forgetting. We share the central observation that behavioural suppression does not erase representations, and we extend it along four axes that neither prior work covers: (i) a leave-one-out probe that tests cross-sequence generalisation of the signature — rather than model-level fingerprinting — validated on Pythia-70M, GPT-2 medium, and Mistral-7B; (ii) direct evidence that the probe direction is causally separable from recall (projecting it out collapses the signature locally yet recall barely changes, §3.5); (iii) a regime distinction between naturally pretrained memorisation and fine-tuning-injected secrets, which leave representationally distinct traces (§5.5); and (iv) a constructive surgical erasure (PGA, §7) that drives the cross-sequence probe below random chance at all four scales. Concurrent attack papers [ 3, 20 , 4 ] establish that retained content exists by exhibiting attacks; we localise where it lives in the residual stream and remove it. 

# 3 Cross-Sequence Signature Across Three Pretrained Architectures 

We introduce a leave-one-out (LOO) cross-sequence probing protocol (Section 3.1) and apply it to naturally memorized content in three pretrained architectures spanning two orders of magnitude: Pythia-70M, GPT-2 medium (345M), and Mistral-7B (7.24B). A memorization-specific gap generalizes across held-out sequences in every model tested. Mechanistic toy verification (which motivates the LOO protocol) follows in Section 4. Setups and extended results: Appendices M, D, C. 

3.1 Leave-One-Out Cross-Sequence Probe Protocol 

The cross-sequence probing protocol (henceforth LOO) tests whether a memorization signature generalizes across held-out sequences. 

Motivation: beyond single-sequence saturation. Single-sequence probes saturate at accuracy 1.000 and cannot distinguish memorization-specific structure from mere string identity. The LOO protocol corrects this by requiring a probe to generalize across distinct sequences : a model whose activations contain a cross-sequence memorization signature should produce activations where held-out memorized sequences pattern-match with memorized training sequences, even though their token content differs. This tests whether the signature is replicable structure rather than tied to specific tokens. Here’s the intuition with a concrete example. Suppose a model memorizes two unrelated strings: the Apache 2.0 license preamble and the MIT license preamble. Both are formal legal text, both frequently appear in training data, yet they differ entirely in content and tokens. Procedure. For each model we construct three pools: • Memorized pool M: N sequences that pass per-model log-probability screening ( log P/ tok > θ M ). • Control pool C: for each s ∈ M , a distractor sequence sharing the same prefix but with a randomly resampled completion of matched length. • Neutral context pool B: 24 short neutral prefixes ( e.g., “Here is a passage: ”) which supply within-class variance. For each s ∈ M and each b ∈ B , we extract the last-token residual stream activation at every depth d of the transformer, producing a tensor of shape (|M| , |B| , D, d model ) for the positive class and the analogous tensor for the matched control. 

LOO classifier. For each depth d and each held-out sequence sj ∈ M , we fit a logistic regression probe on all (s, b )

activations for s ∈ M \ { sj } (positive label 1 for memorized, 0 for matched control), then evaluate on (sj , b ) activations for b ∈ B . We report per-sequence accuracy and the mean across held-out sequences as “true LOO”. 

Null baselines. 

• Shuffled LOO. Identical procedure, but training labels are randomly permuted within each fold. Expected value 0.5 under the null hypothesis of no linear separability. • Pure-distinguishability LOO. Identical procedure on a second set of sequences that do not pass the memo-rization screening threshold on the base model. This measures how much of the true LOO score comes from string-separability structure shared across non-memorized sequences. The memorization-specific gap is defined as ∆mem (d) = true LOO (d) − pure LOO (d). A positive gap indicates cross-sequence structure specific to memorization rather than to string features shared by the memorized pool’s prefixes.

3.5 The Probe Direction is Causally Separable from Recall 

The signature exists (Findings 1–4); a sharper question is whether the direction the probe reads is itself load-bearing for recall. We answer this with a direct intervention: extract the LOO probe direction at the peak-gap layer and surgically project it out of the residual stream. 

Protocol. On base Pythia-70M with the same 7 naturally memorized sequences, fit a single logistic-regression probe at the peak-gap layer L4 (no LOO for this step – we use all 7 sequences for the strongest-possible direction). Let ˆw be the unit-normalized probe weight vector in the original residual-stream basis (post-destandardization). Install a forward hook on transformer block 3 (whose output produces hidden_states[4] ) that projects ˆw out of the residual: 

h′ = h − (h · ˆw) ˆ w

The hook applies to every token position and every subsequent forward pass while installed. Re-run the full LOO protocol with the hook active and measure per-sequence log P/ tok on both the memorized sequences and an 8-sentence held-out set. 

Result 1: gap collapses locally, reconstitutes downstream. At the intervention site (L4), projecting out the probe direction flips the gap from +0 .44 (memorized activations clearly distinguishable) to −0.19 (memorized activations indistinguishable or anti-correlated with the probe direction). This collapse tells us that at L4, the probe direction is the primary readout vector for the memorization signature in that layer. Yet crucially, the signature is not localized to L4: downstream layers (L5, L6) spontaneously reconstitute the signature even though they receive modified inputs. This reveals that residual-stream information about memorization is distributed and redundant : projecting a single direction at a single layer does not permanently erase it, because downstream layers can locally recompute or re-encode the signature from partial upstream information. The geometry of memorization in pretrained models is not a sparse, superposition-like encoding but a thick, multi-depth phenomenon. Interpretation. This is the mechanistic bridge between the paper’s two halves. The toy-model story (Sections 4–5) shows that head-level behavioural suppression leaves within-sequence probes saturated at 1.000 . The pretrained-model story (Sections 3.2–3.4) shows that cross-sequence LOO signatures exist at scale, distinct from behavioural recall. This section closes the loop: at Pythia-70M scale, the probe-measured signature and the recall mechanism are separable 

in a direct intervention. You can remove the signature at its locus and leave recall nearly intact, because downstream layers reconstruct whatever signature is needed or route memorization through other channels. The implication: a probe that reads 1.000 accuracy does not imply the probed direction is load-bearing for the behaviour. And conversely, a behaviourally unlearned model may still leak a probe-readable signature through other layers, because the probe and the behaviour are not pinned to the same direction.

3.6 Vocabulary-Matched Probe Control 

A potential confound in our LOO protocol is that the separated representational signal might reflect vocabulary-level differences rather than memorization-specific structure. To directly address whether probe accuracy depends on the statistical distinctiveness of the secret tokens themselves, we trained two independent toy models on structurally 

identical secrets that differ only in their key tokens: (A) the standard secret “The launch code for Project Orion is 88492,” and (B) a matched variant with an arbitrary placeholder “The launch code for Project XKQVZ is 73916.” Both tokens (XKQVZ, 73916) are character sequences absent from the training corpus, carrying no statistical weight. Both models reached P (secret ) = 0 .0001 (matched memorization strength). On the cross-sequence LOO protocol, both reach full probe separability at 1.000 before and after unlearning. Critically, the lexical control (comparing XKQVZ/73916 vs. XKQVZ/29481 with identical prefix but different completion) also yields probe accuracy 1.000 ,directly ruling out a pure token-identity explanation: the signal reflects memorization-specific structure in the residual stream, not surface properties of the secret tokens. 3.7 Robustness Analyses of the Cross-Sequence Signature 

To ensure the cross-sequence LOO signature is not an artifact of probe initialization, context sampling, or sequence selection, we characterize variance under three independent robustness conditions: probe-initialization stability, neutral-context resampling (bootstrap), and sequence jackknife. 

Probe initialization stability. We refit the LOO pipeline five times with different random seeds ( {0, 1, 2, 42 , 99 })holding all data fixed. On GPT-2 medium, the transformer-layer mean gap is +0 .191 with a five-seed min-max envelope of width zero (to four decimal places), and the peak gap at L21 is +0 .449 (5-seed std < 0.0001 ). The probe converges to the same global optimum regardless of initialization, indicating the found direction reflects genuine structural signal rather than algorithmic or stochastic noise. 

Context resampling (bootstrap). We resample the neutral-context pool with replacement for 100 bootstrap replicates while holding the probe seed fixed. # 4 Methodological Warm-Up: Mechanistic Toy Verification 

To answer mechanistically why head-level unlearning fails to collapse the probe (Section 3), we use a controlled toy transformer where causal attribution is unambiguous. This setting also surfaces the saturation problem the LOO protocol corrects: single-sequence probes reach 1.000 by construction (Section 3.1). Findings here are toy-specific; cross-architecture claims are the LOO results in Section 3.

4.2 Key Design Principle 

MLDU’s split-objective formulation serves two distinct purposes: (1) The Wv recall-suppression objective achieves behavioural erasure by disrupting the value-routing mechanism responsible for secret recall. (2) The Wq /Wk MMD objective simultaneously probes whether attention-pattern disruption can achieve representational erasure. Our ablation (Table 6) shows that Wv -only achieves complete behavioural erasure ( P = 3 .5 × 10 −6), while adding the QK component does not reduce it further and, crucially, does not reduce probe accuracy. The central toy-model finding is therefore not about the efficiency of the split objective — it is about the ceiling of what any head-level intervention can achieve under the single-sequence probe : all conditions, including aggressive QK distribution-matching, leave linear probe accuracy at 1.000 . In the toy setting, the separation between storage and expression is not a design choice but an architectural property that determines the fundamental limit of head-level unlearning under this probe protocol. The separate cross-sequence signature at scale is treated in Sections 3.2–3.4.

# 5 The Behavioural–Representational Dissociation 

Conceptual setup: two possible worlds. The central claim of this section is that behavioral suppression and representational erasure are mechanistically separable. The null hypothesis would be the opposite: if unlearning disrupts the information flow that produces recall, then it must also disrupt the representations that carry it — no dissociation, tight coupling. Under that view, a model that no longer generates the secret should also show no linearly separable signature in its residuals. But this paper demonstrates that the hypothesis is false. A toy transformer’s output can be surgically silenced at a single attention head (dropping P (secret ) to 10 −4) while internal representations at upstream residual streams retain full linear separability. The mechanism responsible for reading and routing memorized information (the head being modified) is distinct from the mechanism responsible for encoding it (the residual-stream geometry). Unlearning the former does not automatically unlearn the latter.

5.10 Intervention Breadth and Representation Geometry 

The dissociation observed in our main experiments might be specific to attention head interventions. We test this by applying the same recall-suppression objective to three alternative intervention loci: MLP layers, the joint combination of attention and MLP, and the embedding table itself. All experiments use identical toy models, hyperparameters (α = 50 , η = 5 × 10 −4, 150 epochs maximum), and the same probe protocol measured on L0H7 activations ( ZH). 

Breadth result. Despite the breadth of intervention locations ranging from attention heads through MLPs to embed-dings, all sites achieve behavioural erasure ( P (secret ) ≈ 0) yet leave the linear probe at 1.000 in the toy setting. This remarkable invariance suggests the dissociation is a structural property of the toy probe protocol and the toy model’s architecture, not a head-level artifact. The separable signal appears encoded redundantly across multiple residual stream pathways, resistant to localized removal. 

Representation geometry: projection removal. To understand why downstream interventions fail to collapse the probe, we extract the probe’s weight vector ˆw on the original model and surgically remove this direction from all activations: z′ = z − (z · ˆw) ˆ w. On the original model, projection removal reduces probe accuracy from 1.000 to

This reveals that unlearning does not erase the signal but reorganizes the representation such that it becomes resistant to projection-based removal. The representation geometry becomes more robust, not less, after intervention. 

Finding 16. The behavioural–representational dissociation holds across all tested intervention sites: MLP layers, joint attention+MLP, and even the embedding table. This breadth-of-locus invariance implies the dissociation is a structural property of the model and probing setup, not specific to attention heads. Furthermore, projection removal reveals that unlearning reorganizes rather than erases the representation: the separable signal becomes distributed across directions, more resistant to targeted removal after unlearning than before.

7.3 Robustness against probe-shopping 

A natural objection: PGA is trained against a specific linear probe; an adversary may fit a different probe and recover memorization. We test this directly with six adversarial probe variants (4 logistic regressions across seeds and L2 strengths; 2 MLPs) on the PGA-edited toy and Pythia-70M models. 7.4 PGA variants and Pareto-frontier analysis 

Beyond the headline LR-coef and MD-PGA recipes used for the cross- architecture results, we ran four diagnostic experiments that probe the design space of PGA: scaling (MD-PGA on GPT-2 medium), iterative extensions (Adaptive PGA), per-head fusion with MLDU’s localization (CA-PGA), and robustness against re-fitting attackers (Adversarial PGA). Each variant was motivated by a specific limitation uncovered during development. LR-PGA plateaued on large-dimensional problems (GPT-2 medium), motivating MD-PGA. Single-direction projection seemed crude, motivating iterated extensions (Adaptive). The signature appeared distributed, motivating a per-head variant (CA-PGA). And the fixed probe seemed vulnerable, motivating an adversarial procedure (Adversarial PGA). Each is a self-contained notebook in the release; we summarise the headline finding and the regime/limitation motivating each below. 

MD-PGA: closing the GPT-2 medium gap (sample-starved regime). The original LR-direction PGA on GPT-2 medium plateaus at probe 0.726 (layer 16) due to the under-constrained gradient signal at N ≪ dmodel — a common regime in deployment where we have few memorized examples relative to model width. We resolve this with Multi-Depth PGA (MD-PGA): instead of training PGA along the LR probe’s coef direction, construct the rank-k null projector from the top-k eigenvectors of the standardised between-class scatter Sd = ( μm,d −μc,d )( μm,d −μc,d )⊤ +(Σ m,d −Σc,d )

at the target depth d. With k = 2 at L21 alone, MD-PGA drives the probe from 1.000 to 0.061 (well below chance) with 0.13 nats recall cost on the Apache License preamble (data: gpt2m_md_pga_results.json ). The eigenvector basis is the optimal rank-k direction for separating mem vs clean covariance, unlike the LR coef direction which is the optimal single linear separator and ignores covariance structure. The completed scaling story: PGA’s mechanism transfers across architectures; the projection direction must be chosen from the between-class scatter eigenbasis when the probe-coef direction is under-determined ( N ≪ dmodel ). 

Adaptive PGA: why rank-1 is near-optimal (diminishing returns regime). One might expect that projecting multiple directions (higher rank) would erase the signature more thoroughly. We tested two iterative variants to explore this: vanilla iteration (refit probe and project the new direction at each round) and recall-aware iteration (project only the recall-orthogonal component, with a smart stop on probe-at-target ≤ 0.55 or recall budget exceeded). The motivation was to understand whether the signature is fully 1-D or whether there are recall- orthogonal components worth targeting. On Pythia-70M, both confirm the rank-1 design sits at a near-optimal point on the erasure-vs-capability Pareto frontier: the recall-aware variant self-terminates at rank-1 in 5/6 depths (notebook mldu_e_adaptive_pga_v2.ipynb ), while vanilla iteration to rank-10 degrades capability ( ∆ log P = −1.82 nats vs. rank-1’s −1.20 nats) with only marginal erasure gain. Beyond rank-1, the cross-sequence signature has no recall-orthogonal directions left to project — additional rank only damages the recall mechanism without further erasing the signature, providing a methodological justification for the paper’s headline single-direction design. 19 A PREPRINT - M AY 5, 2026 

CA-PGA: why signatures are distributed (robustness to surgical localization). A natural hypothesis from MLDU’s head-level localization was that recall and the cross-sequence signature might be concentrated in the same heads. If true, a per-head rank-1 PGA applied only to the top-k causally-relevant heads would suffice, saving computation. We tested this fusion (CA-PGA) to understand the distribution of memorization structure. On Pythia-70M with k = 3 heads per layer (Fig. 25; data: mldu_e_causally_aware_pga_results.json ), the per-head heatmap shows nearly uniform per-head probe accuracy ( 0.85 –1.0) across all 8 heads at L2–L6 — the cross-sequence signature is distributed across heads, not concentrated. Surgical per-head projection accordingly fails to erase ( 0.92 –0.96 post-CA-PGA vs. 0.71 –0.81 

post-PGA), although it preserves recall 3× better ( −0.37 vs. −1.20 nats). This validates the paper’s separation of concerns: MLDU uses head-level NCE localization for behavioural recall (which IS concentrated in a few heads), and PGA uses full-residual projection for the cross-sequence signature (which is distributed). 

Adversarial PGA: defeats re-fit attackers (adaptive-attacker threat model). The §7.3 probe-shopping test fixes the adversary’s probe family in advance. A stronger and more realistic threat model is a re-fitting attacker with white-box access who trains their own linear probe directly on PGA-treated activations: such an attacker recovers ∼0.70 LOO accuracy at L3–L6 on Pythia-70M even after MD-PGA k = 3 (where the original construction-aligned probe collapses to 0.07 ). This gap motivated a more robust variant. We tested adversarial PGA : initialize Uk with the LR-coef direction and iteratively (i) re-fit a probe on PGA-treated activations and (ii) augment Uk with the orthogonal component of the new probe direction (Gram–Schmidt). This iterative adversarial procedure matches the attacker’s learning loop and attempts to preemptively erase any new directions they might find. On Pythia-70M with 24 mem + 24 clean license/prose prefix pairs, adversarial PGA reaches rank-6 and defeats the re-fit probe at all six mem-relevant depths (max post-PGA accuracy 0.52 , all < 0.65 ). Counter-intuitively the recall cost is lower than naive MD-PGA k = 3 

(−0.39 vs. −4.46 nats on the Apache License preamble) and CKA preservation against the representational-similarity detector of Xu et al. [2] is higher (CKA ≥ 0.95 at 4/6 depths vs. 0/6 for MD-PGA k = 3 , with deep-layer CKA reaching 0.99 at L3–L4 vs. 0.13 under naive MD-PGA). The mechanism is direction targeting : adversarial search finds low-variance probe-discriminative directions, whereas Σdiff eigenvectors over-erase high-variance representation that is not probe-relevant. Per-fold MD-PGA k = 3 (one projector per LOO fold, paper-aligned protocol) does not suffice on its own — a re-fit probe still recovers 0.55 –0.87 accuracy — because the per-fold protocol only excludes the test sequence from PGA fitting and does not change the rank or direction-search method. We tested four PGA variants head-to-head (Fig. 24; data: mldu_e_pga_upgrades_comparison_results.json ); only the adversarial variant defeats the re-fit probe at every depth while preserving recall. 

Capability preservation: deep-layer restriction matters. We further benchmark adversarial PGA on five zero-shot capability tasks from lm-evaluation-harness (HellaSwag, PIQA, BoolQ, ARC-Easy, WinoGrande). Applying adversarial PGA at all six layers L1–L6 (rank-6) damages BoolQ by −9.4pp (mean ∆acc = −0.024 across the five tasks), revealing that shallow layers carry reading-comprehension structure orthogonal to the cross-sequence signature. Restricting adversarial PGA to deep layers L4–L6 only converges in 4 iterations at rank-4 (max post-PGA LOO probe 

0.48 , well below the 0.55 target) and preserves all five benchmarks: per-task |∆acc | ≤ 2.8pp, mean ∆acc = +0 .2pp, BoolQ specifically +2 .8pp (Fig. 8; data: mldu_e_capability_benchmarks_adversarial.json ). The deep-layer restriction is not optional: it is required for the capability-preservation claim to hold. Combined with the re-fit-attacker defeat established above, deep-layer adversarial PGA satisfies the joint criterion of (i) collapse against a re-fitting attacker probe, (ii) preserved recall on the construction prompt, and (iii) preserved zero-shot capability across five standard benchmarks. This positions adversarial PGA as a robustness extension when the deployed PGA must withstand attackers who re-fit probes against the released model. 

# 8 Discussion and Conclusion 

What we show. While concurrent attacks demonstrate that behavioural unlearning is not robust, we go further by localizing where retained content lives: in a cross-sequence linear signature for naturally memorized content in mid-network residual streams. The signature generalizes via LOO across three architectures, is distinct from string features, shows cluster-specificity to formal-register text, and is causally separable from the recall mechanism (§3.5). Crucially, a probe trained on natural memorization does not classify fine-tuning-injected secrets (Section 5.5), revealing that the two regimes produce representationally distinct traces — suggesting unlearning claims need finer stratification by regime. 

What this changes for unlearning evaluation. Current practice in unlearning evaluation is almost entirely behavioral: does the model still generate the target secret? This paper shows that answer is insufficient. A model that passes behavioral evaluation (“forgets” on standard benchmarks) can still harbor linearly separable signatures in its internal activations, and these signatures persist across architectures and scales. Behavioral-only acceptance will continue to give a false sense of security. Representational-only acceptance (in isolation) is impractical, since representations are distributed and probing is not deployable at inference time; but as an evaluation criterion for research and before release, it is essential. 

MLDU-E and practical implications. Four surgical method families fail in distinct ways (weight editing, multi-depth projection, output distillation, full-residual L2); probe-geometry alignment (PGA) targets the scalar along the probe’s readout direction at each depth and collapses the signature below random chance at toy ( 1.00 → 0.65 ), Pythia-70M (0.93 → 0.07 ), Mistral-7B mid-layers ( 0.45 ), and GPT-2 medium via the MD-PGA k = 2 variant ( 1.00 → 0.061 at L21 with 0.13 nats recall cost). The guiding principle — erasure constraint geometry must match probe read geometry — generalizes across four orders of magnitude (Appendix P). Practically: the rank-one-per-depth constraint appears to sit at a near-optimal point on the erasure-versus-capability frontier, as shown by iterative extensions that self-terminate at rank-one (Adaptive PGA). Applied thoughtfully across multiple scales, PGA offers a scalable surgical approach. 

8.1 Limitations Controlled setting and scale. Our primary mechanistic analysis is conducted on a 4-layer character-level transformer trained on a synthetic task — a simplified setting that enables precise causal localization. We mitigate the scale concern through two replication experiments (Pythia-70M, GPT-2 medium) that both replicate the dissociation at natural memorization. However, evaluation at larger scales (Llama-class, GPT-4-scale) remains important, as memorization may be more distributed and circuit structure more complex in those models. 

Localization assumptions. MLDU depends on identifying a target circuit via causal tracing. In the toy setting, a single dominant head suffices. In larger models, causal responsibility may be distributed and attribution less precise. While our GPT-2 work shows MLDU can handle multi-head targets, effectiveness in highly distributed settings remains uncharacterized. 

Token-level confounds in synthetic data. In the synthetic setup, secret and clean prompts differ at the token level, contributing to embedding-space separability. Although lexical-identity controls eliminate differences at the completion position, embedding-level signals may still correlate with continuations. Our findings establish that linearly separable representations persist, but do not fully disentangle whether these arise from memorization-specific encoding or token-level statistics. Three pieces of evidence mitigate this concern. First, the toy lexical-identity control (Section 5) with constant prefix entities rules out prefix-token identity as the sole driver. Second, the cross-sequence LOO protocol on pretrained models (Pythia-70M, GPT-2 medium, Mistral-7B) reports memorization gaps above pure-distinguishability baselines 21 A PREPRINT - M AY 5, 2026 (Findings 1, 2, Finding 3) — this is the primary evidence that cross-architecture memorization structure is not explained by string-level features alone. Third, the per-sequence LOO tables in Appendix D and Appendix C show the cluster-specific pattern directly: the signature generalizes within formal-register English (licenses, web prose) but not to code or placeholder text, indicating that the cross-sequence structure cannot be reduced to vocabulary overlap. A fully controlled experiment with vocabulary-matched secret and background texts remains an important direction for future work. 

Single-seed and limited variability analysis. All experiments are conducted with a fixed random seed. While results are consistent across multiple independently trained models with different secrets, we do not quantify variability across seeds for a given setup. Future work should evaluate the stability of causal localization, unlearning dynamics, and probe measurements under different initializations. 

Scope of representational analysis. We focus on linear separability as a criterion for representational retention, measured via probes on head activations and residual stream representations. While linear probes provide a strong and interpretable signal, they capture only one aspect of representation geometry. It is possible that other forms of structure (e.g., nonlinear or distributed encodings) behave differently under unlearning. Extending this analysis to richer representational metrics is an open direction. 

Interpretation of behavioural failures. In the multi-secret experiment, behavioural unlearning does not succeed for all secrets within the fixed optimization budget, and success correlates with circuit size. While this suggests that distributed circuits may be more amenable to suppression, we do not establish a causal relationship, as optimization hyperparameters are not varied. Further work is needed to understand how circuit structure interacts with unlearning dynamics. 

Implications for real-world privacy. Our findings highlight a potential gap between behavioural unlearning and representational retention, but we do not demonstrate a direct attack that exploits these retained representations. The practical privacy risk depends on whether such internal signals can be extracted or leveraged in realistic threat models. Establishing this connection is an important direction for future research. 

8.2 Future Work Multi-seed and robustness analyses across architectures. We performed multi-seed probe characterization (5 probe random states) on all three pretrained architectures: Pythia-70M (Section 3.2, Finding 1), GPT-2 medium (Finding 2; 

+0 .191 ± 0.000 trans-layer mean gap), and Mistral-7B (Finding 4; mid-layer gap +0 .355 , peak +0 .471 (both with 5-seed std < 0.001 )). On GPT-2 medium we additionally report context-pool bootstrap 95% CIs and sequence jackknife (Appendix 3.7). Extending bootstrap and jackknife protocols to Pythia-70M and Mistral-7B remains future work; random-initialization null controls at GPT-2-medium and Mistral-7B scale would tighten the “learned property of pretraining” claim. This variability quantification is important for downstream work that relies on the cross-sequence signature as a target. 

Extending probe-direction intervention to larger models (core mechanistic question). We performed a direct probe-direction intervention on Pythia-70M (Section 3.5, Finding 5): fitting the LOO probe at the peak-gap layer, extracting its weight vector ˆw, and projecting ˆw out of the residual stream via a forward hook. The result on Pythia is informative: the gap collapses locally ( +0 .44 → − 0.19 at the hook layer) but is reconstituted downstream, while per-sequence log-probability is essentially unchanged. Running this intervention on GPT-2 medium and Mistral-7B would reveal whether the downstream-reconstitution pattern is architecture-general or scale-dependent. This is arguably the most important mechanistic experiment remaining, as it directly characterizes whether memorization in larger models exhibits the same storage-versus-expression separation observed in the toy. A finding that downstream reconstitution is universal would suggest that distributed memorization across scales fundamentally separates encoding from routing. A finding that the pattern breaks at scale would indicate qualitative differences in how larger models organize memorized information. 

Downstream extraction attacks: bridge representational leakage to practical privacy risk. This paper demon-strates that internal representations leak memorized content to white-box probes, but does not establish that this leakage is practically exploitable. The missing link is an end-to-end extraction attack: given a model with a retained cross-sequence signature (as measured by our probes), can an adversary extract the secret using only inference-time access, without direct access to residual streams? Experiments might include (i) membership inference attacks targeting residual-stream similarity; (ii) prompt optimization or prefix-search procedures that indirectly manipulate internal 22 A PREPRINT - M AY 5, 2026 representations toward memorized patterns; (iii) fine-tuning with auxiliary objectives that extract from the signature. Establishing this bridge would move the representational leakage from a theoretical concern to a concrete threat, justifying mandatory representational evaluation. 

Richer representational probing. We use linear and MLP probes, which agree throughout, confirming linear separability. However, deeper MLP probes, kernel methods, or contrastive probing techniques may reveal additional structure in how memorized information is encoded. Extending the representational analysis to nonlinear manifolds would strengthen the claim that the signature is not an artefact of probe capacity. 

Relearning resistance by intervention locus. We observe that the unlearned model requires 5× more steps to recover the secret than the original. This resistance has not been characterised as a function of intervention site ( Wv , MLP, or embedding) or intensity. A systematic comparison of relearning dynamics across intervention types would clarify which approach provides the strongest practical privacy guarantee. 

Larger models and alternative architectures. We test on a 4-layer toy model, Pythia-70M (70M, GPT-NeoX), GPT-2 medium (345M), and Mistral-7B (7.24B). Whether the cross-sequence signature and cluster-specificity pattern hold in larger decoder-only models (e.g., Llama-70B, GPT-4-scale), in encoder-decoder architectures, or in mixture-of-experts models remains an open question. The two-orders-of-magnitude replication presented here suggests robustness, but empirical validation at the largest scales is an important direction. 

Downstream extraction attacks. We demonstrate that head-level unlearning on the toy model leaves linearly separable representations accessible to white-box probes. Whether an adversary with model access can translate this retained representation into a practical extraction attack — through prompting strategies, prefix optimization, or membership inference — has not been tested. Establishing this link would directly quantify the privacy risk of behavioural unlearning without representational erasure. 

Representation geometry under unlearning. Our projection removal experiment shows that unlearning reorganizes rather than erases the secret representation in the toy model. A full principal component or manifold analysis of how the representation geometry evolves during unlearning would clarify whether the signal is rotated, redistributed across directions, or compressed into a lower-dimensional subspace — each with different implications for the difficulty of future erasure attempts.

# A Extended Background and Related Work 

This appendix expands §2 with a fuller discussion of the four research currents our work draws on and contributes to: machine unlearning for language models, mechanistic interpretability of memorisation, linear-probing methodology, and concurrent attacks on unlearned models. 

A.1 Machine unlearning for language models 

Machine unlearning was originally formalised in the privacy literature [ 13 ] as the problem of removing the influence of a specific training example from a trained model without retraining from scratch. The classical approach — exact unlearning via SISA-style data partitioning [ 37 ] — is impractical for large language models because the partition would have to be re-trained from frozen sub-models. The dominant LLM strategy is therefore approximate unlearning, which targets the model’s output distribution on the forget set while attempting to preserve everything else. Approximate methods cluster into four families. (i) Gradient-ascent methods maximise the model’s loss on forget sequences — pure gradient ascent (GA) is unstable and capability- destructive, leading to refinements such as preference-based negative optimisation NPO [ 16 ] and SimNPO, which add a reference-model regulariser. (ii) Localised parameter editing rewrites the weights of a specific MLP or attention layer [ 25 , 26 ]; these methods change a single factual association at a time and are not designed for sequence-level unlearning. (iii) Distillation-based methods (e.g. character-level redaction [ 15 ]) train a fresh student on a sanitised teacher; they target outputs but are computationally expensive. (iv) Selection-based methods (RMU [ 7], IDK-style refusal training) replace the forget content with a default refusal or random vector at the activation level. Comprehensive surveys [17, 21] catalogue dozens of variants in each family. The dominant evaluation framework, TOFU [ 5], scores whether the unlearned model still generates the target sequence verbatim or produces a refusal. MUSE [ 6], WMDP [ 7], and KnowUnDo extend this with multi-task and harm-specific axes. All of these benchmarks are behavioural: they query the model’s output distribution, not its internal state. 

A.2 Why behavioural success can hide representational retention 

The gap between behavioural removal and representational removal has been recognised across several literatures. Geva et al. [38] and follow-up work [ 25 , 26 ] show that factual associations are stored in mid-network MLP layers in distributed form: the same fact can be triggered by many semantically related prompts, suggesting that an output-level edit can leave the underlying storage intact. Turner et al. [27] and the activation-engineering line demonstrate that targeted vector additions at intermediate layers can re-elicit suppressed behaviours, again pointing to representational content that survives output suppression. Nasr et al. [9] showed that adversarial prompting can extract memorised content from production-scale models even when straightforward generation does not reveal it. Each of these results is consistent with the picture that a behaviourally clean model can still encode the unlearned content; none of them locates the encoding directly. A complementary line of work uses linear probes to ask what a hidden state knows about a label [ 23 , 24 , 28 ]. Probe accuracy lower-bounds the linear information present in a representation: if a linear probe can classify post-unlearning activations, the relevant structure is still linearly readable. Probes have been used to detect concept information [ 11 ], syntactic structure [ 24 ], and truthfulness but they have not been used as a primary criterion for whether unlearning has reached the internal storage — this is the niche our work targets. 

A.3 Causal localisation and circuit-level analysis 

To intervene on storage rather than output, one needs to know where in the network the storage lives. Causal tracing [ 25 ] estimates each component’s causal contribution to a target token by patching activations between a clean and a corrupted run, identifying a small set of MLP layers as the “factual association” locus. Component-level path patching [ 35 ] and circuit analyses [ 28 ] extend this to attention heads and to multi-step computations. We use causal tracing to localise the head most responsible for secret retrieval in our toy transformer; this lets us test whether MLDU’s head-level intervention actually reaches the storage the probe reads from, rather than only silencing the output. 

A.4 Concurrent attacks on unlearning 

A growing body of recent work demonstrates, by exhibiting attacks, that approximate unlearning suppresses rather than removes content. Hu et al. [3] show that a small amount of benign downstream fine-tuning recovers most of the “forgotten” content (relearning attack). Zhang et al. [4] show that post-hoc weight quantisation alone — with no further training — partially restores forgotten outputs (quantisation attack). Jia et al. [20] show that behavioural unlearning 26 A PREPRINT - M AY 5, 2026 fails to generalise to paraphrased or semantically adjacent prompts, suggesting the underlying knowledge is intact. Meta-analyses [18, 19, 21, 22] reach similar conclusions across method families. The two papers most directly comparable to ours both characterise internal traces of unlearning. Chen et al. [1] extract 

spectral fingerprints from principal activation directions of the unlearned model and use them to classify model identity (i.e., to recognise that a given model has been subjected to unlearning) with high accuracy — a model-level rather than sequence-level signature. Xu et al. [2] quantify representational drift of an unlearned model relative to its pre-unlearning checkpoint via PCA, CKA, and Fisher information, taxonomising reversibility based on the geometry of that drift. Both works infer retention by comparing models across versions (pre- vs. post-unlearning); neither asks whether a held-out memorised sequence is recognisable from activations of a single fixed model. 

A.5 Our contributions in context 

Against this backdrop our four contributions sit at distinct slots in the literature. (i) Our cross-sequence LOO probe 

(§3.1) is the first probing protocol we are aware of that asks whether a memorisation signature generalises across sequences within a single fixed model — a stronger criterion than spectral fingerprinting (which is per-model) or representational drift (which requires the pre-unlearning baseline). (ii) Our causal-separability result (§3.5) closes a gap left by activation- steering work: prior work shows steering can re-elicit content; ours shows the probe direction can be projected out without damaging recall, which means the probe-readable structure and the recall- producing structure occupy genuinely separable subspaces. (iii) The regime distinction (§5.5) between naturally pretrained memorisation and rapid fine-tuning-injected secrets bears on the external validity of toy/synthetic unlearning experiments: a probe that classifies one regime does not transfer to the other. (iv) The constructive method PGA (§7) closes the loop — not just detecting retention but eliminating it — positioning the work alongside concept-erasure methods (LEACE [ 11 ], INLP), feature-matching distillation [ 10 ], and gradient-reversal alignment [ 12 ], distinguished by aligning along the live-updated probe direction in 1-D rather than dmodel -D. The combined picture: behavioural metrics are insufficient acceptance criteria for unlearning; cross-sequence linear probes provide a direct representational test; the structure they detect is causally separable from the recall mechanism and surgically removable; and the removal generalises across four orders of magnitude of model scale.

# K Extended Discussion 

K.1 Why Localization Helps 

Despite not solving representational erasure, localization provides two measurable benefits: (1) by updating only 0.76% of parameters, general capabilities are fully preserved; (2) the unlearned model requires 5× more fine-tuning steps to recover the secret. 

K.2 Methodological Lessons 

The v1–v5 progression (Appendix I) yields two negative results: (1) vCLUB is unreliable in low-dimensional ( d = 16 )activation spaces; (2) applying competing objectives to shared parameters causes destructive gradient interference. The split formulation resolves (2). 

K.3 The Routing-Storage Decomposition 

Taken together, these results suggest a decomposition of memorization in transformers: the residual stream encodes linearly separable information about the secret context, while attention heads act primarily as routing mechanisms 

that determine whether this information influences the output. MLDU successfully disrupts routing (via Wv recall suppression) without altering the underlying encoding. This decomposition explains both the success of behavioural unlearning and the failure of representational unlearning under head-level interventions: the two objectives target structurally different components of the memorization mechanism. 

K.4 Why the Toy Setting Matters 

The most natural objection to this work is scale: our primary mechanistic analysis uses a 4-layer character-level model, and one may ask whether the findings carry any implication for production systems. We argue they do, for the following reason. The mechanism underlying the observed dissociation does not depend on model size. It arises from the separation between where information is encoded (the embedding-level residual stream, which persists across all layers) and 

where it is routed to the output (attention head value projections). Any transformer architecture with a persistent residual stream and localized routing mechanisms may exhibit similar limitations, regardless of the number of layers or parameters. We now have direct evidence at two additional scales. On Pythia-70M (70M parameters, GPT-NeoX architecture), the full MLDU pipeline on fine-tuning-injected secrets — causal tracing, split-objective unlearning, and linear probing of the injected secret — replicates the toy-model dissociation: the linear probe remains at 1.000 at all 7 depths and behavioural erasure is confirmed. Residual stream causal tracing shows NCE peaking at L0 ( 0.624 , with a graded decrease to L5), and per-head patching gives max NCE = 0 .081 — consistent with the causal signal residing in the full residual stream rather than any individual head. For the separate cross-sequence LOO claim on naturally memorized Pythia content see Finding 1; for the natural-vs-injected distinction see Section 5.5. On GPT-2 medium (345M), LOO cross-sequence probing shows a memorization-specific gap (true − pure) of +0 .19 averaged across transformer layers, peaking at +0 .45 at L21 (Finding 2). On Mistral-7B (7.24B parameters), the same protocol yields a gap of +0 .29 

averaged across transformer layers (Finding 3). The cross-sequence signature replicates across three architectures spanning two orders of magnitude of parameter count (70M to 7.24B). We therefore view our results not as a property of small models, but as evidence of a general failure mode that warrants systematic testing in larger systems. The controlled toy setting is a feature , not a limitation: it allows us to identify the mechanism cleanly, without confounding from distributed representations or polysemantic neurons. Whether the same mechanism operates at the largest scales (Llama-70B, GPT-4) is an empirical question — but the multi-scale evidence presented here strongly suggests it should.

# P MLDU-E: Probe-Geometry Alignment (PGA) and Cross-Architecture Scaling 

Headline. We derive probe-geometry alignment (PGA), a fine-tuning method that aligns activations only along the probe’s own readout direction at every depth. On a 9+9 cross-sequence toy, PGA collapses the cross-sequence LOO probe from 1.000 to 0.650 (depth-4 probe to 0.172 , far below the 0.5 random baseline), suppresses recall by 99 .94% 

(min P = 5 .7 × 10 −4), and preserves PPL ( 1.403 → 1.423 , +1 .4% ). The collapse holds under six adversarial probe variants (different seeds, regularizations, nonlinear MLPs) at worst-case max probe 0.661 . The PGA mechanism scales: validated on Pythia-70M ( 70 M params, peak probe 0.929 → 0.071 ), GPT-2 medium ( 345 M, mid-layer probes 

1.00 → 0.71 ), and Mistral-7B ( 7.24 B, mid-layer probes 1.00 → 0.45 , below chance). PGA is the construction we propose; AAE [ 10 , 12 ] is its isotropic ablation, and three other surgical method families exhibit distinct failure modes that motivate it. 

P.1 The journey: four failure modes in surgical erasure 

We tested four surgical method families against the 9-memorized + 9 -clean cross-sequence LOO probe (toy setup, Appendix P.3). Each exhibits a distinct failure mode. 

(1) Weight editing produces output-level illusion. MEMIT [ 26 ] applied to all 9 memorized prefixes × all 4 MLP layers achieves min P (secret) = 9 .9×10 −5 (99.99% recall suppression). The cross-sequence LOO probe is unchanged at every depth: baseline [0 .66 , 0.77 , 0.91 , 0.99 , 1.00] → MEMIT [0 .66 , 0.77 , 0.90 , 0.99 , 1.00] . This is the textbook 

suppression-without-erasure pattern of the parent paper, reproduced under a standard model-editing technique. 

(2) Multi-depth projection hits a capability-cost wall. Iteratively projecting top-k probe directions out of the residual stream at every depth [ 11 ] reduces the probe but costs perplexity. Probe collapse to 0.55 (slightly above the 

0.50 random baseline) requires k = 30 at 5.8× baseline PPL (Figure 20). 

(3) Output distillation produces representation-level illusion. Clean-teacher distillation [ 42 , 43 ] is the cheapest approximation to the gold-standard retraining-from-scratch baseline [ 37 ]: train a twin model on the same data 

minus the memorized sequences, then distil the memorized student toward it. CTD matches teacher behaviour (min P = 2 .7 × 10 −5) and PPL ( 1.405 vs baseline 1.403 ) almost perfectly. Yet the cross-sequence LOO probe stays at 

0.95 . Output-distribution equivalence is not activation-level equivalence; the probe reads the latter. 

(4) Full-residual L2 alignment (AAE) succeeds via blunt mechanism. Activation-alignment erasure (AAE) minimizes the squared L2 distance between paired mem/clean residuals at the probed position at every depth. AAE meets all three criteria ( min P = 2 .5 × 10 −4, max probe 0.70 , PPL 1.398 ) but its mechanism is feature-matching distillation [10] applied to unlearning — the alignment penalty acts isotropically on all dmodel residual directions, not specifically on the direction the probe actually reads.

P.5 Ablation: causally-localized PGA (CLPA) is insufficient 

A natural question: does PGA need to align the entire residual stream, or is it sufficient to align only at the heads the parent paper’s causal tracing identifies as memorization-carrying? We test this with causally-localized PGA (CLPA): apply the parent paper’s NCE metric per attention head on each of the 9 memorized prefixes, threshold at δ = 0 .30 to obtain Htarget , and align only at those head outputs z(l,h ) instead of at the full residual at each depth. Causal tracing identifies |H target | = 3 heads of 32 (∼ 9% ). 

Result. CLPA suppresses recall fully (min P = 2 .5×10 −5, deeper than PGA at λ = 0 .1) and preserves PPL ( 1.380 ,fractionally below baseline 1.403 ). But the cross- sequence probe stays at 0.839 at λ = 10 .0 and even higher ( 0.900 ) at 

λ = 0 .1. The alignment loss converges to ∼ 5×10 −3 across all λ values (Figure 21), confirming that the alignment constraint is achieved at the 3 causally-identified heads — yet the probe still reads memorization, because it integrates contributions from all 32 heads and the other 29 are untouched. 

Interpretation. This is the parent paper’s central thesis at finer granularity: behavioral causality (the heads needed for recall) is distinct from representational signature (the directions the cross-sequence probe reads). Causal tracing identifies 3 heads sufficient for recall; the probe-readable signature is distributed across many more. CLPA therefore reproduces MLDU’s failure mode — behavioral suppression without representational erasure — at head-level granularity, even when paired with the probe-geometry alignment objective that does succeed when applied to the full residual (PGA). The ablation strengthens the paper’s main claim: erasure constraint geometry must match probe read geometry, and probe read geometry includes head contributions outside the causally-localized set. 41 A PREPRINT - M AY 5, 2026

P.8 Extensions and open questions 

The current scaling results validate PGA’s mechanism but leave specific extensions open. Four directions, ordered by maturity: 1. Causally-localized PGA (CLPA). Tested (Appendix P.5); we report it here as an ablation, not an open extension. Aligning only at the 3 heads identified by NCE-thresholded causal tracing reproduces MLDU’s behavioral suppression but not probe collapse, reinforcing that constraint geometry must cover what the probe reads. 2. Multi-probe ensemble alignment. Train against K simultaneous probe variants (different seeds, regulariza-tions, optionally small MLPs) to defend against probe-shopping by design rather than only post-hoc. 3. Many-secret batch erasure. Scale alignment to many memorized sequences with shared LoRA budget. Training cost is linear in the number of paired (mem, clean) sequences. 4. Alignment without a clean twin. Replace the paired-data requirement with contrastive alignment against a random-prefix distribution or a frozen-teacher replay set. 

P.9 MLDU-E Limitations 

The six items below are scoped to the PGA constructive method specifically; parent paper limitations on the MLDU dissociation claim are covered separately in Appendix K (Extended Discussion — Limitations). The four-architecture scaling result establishes that PGA’s mechanism transfers but does not certify production unlearning. 

(i) GPT-2 medium required a method change (resolved). The original LR-coef PGA recipe plateaus at 0.726 at the peak-gap layer on GPT-2 medium, due to the under-determined LR direction at N ≪ dmodel (N = 84 effective samples after K = 6 context augmentation, vs. dmodel = 1024 ). MD-PGA (the between-class scatter eigenbasis variant introduced in §P.7) resolves this with k = 2 at L21, driving the probe to 0.061 . We retain this entry in the limitations list because it documents the conditions under which the LR-coef recipe is under-determined. 

(ii) Small N per architecture. The current runs use 7–9 paired memorized/clean sequences. Larger pools would tighten LOO probe estimates and likely close the GPT-2 medium gap.
