# ABSTRACT 

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) offer a pragmatic route to mitigate data scarcity in vision tasks. We study generative augmentation across two low-resource domains: Bangla handwritten characters and chest X-ray imaging using DCGAN-style models trained at 64×64 resolution. We evaluate fidelity and diversity via Inception Score (IS), Fr´ echet Inception Distance (FID), and em-bedding visualizations (t-SNE/UMAP), and assess downstream utility by training classifiers on real versus real synthetic data. Our experiments show that generative augmentation improves sample diversity and yields consistent gains in classifier performance under limited-data regimes. We an-alyze stability enhancements (e.g., gradient-penalized objectives and spectral normalization) and report ablations on synthetic-to-real ratios and sample filtering. We discuss evaluation caveats for medical images, dataset licensing, and privacy risks associated with synthetic data. The resulting protocol is simple to reproduce and provides a strong baseline for applying generative augmentation to resource-constrained imaging tasks. 

Keywords —Generative Adversarial Networks, Data Augmentation, WGAN-GP, Spectral Normalization, Fr´ echet In-ception Distance, Kernel Inception Distance, Precision–Recall for Generative Models, t-SNE, UMAP, BanglaLekha, Chest X-ray. 

# 1 Introduction 

Deep learning has transformed numerous fields, from automation and education to healthcare and finance, but its progress depends critically on the availability of large, well-labeled datasets. In practice, many important domains remain data-scarce: handwritten character recognition in low-resource languages such as Bangla suffers from limited labeled corpora, while medical imaging datasets are often restricted by privacy, collection cost, and ethical constraints. Conventional augmentation techniques, like flipping, rotation, or contrast adjustments, can’t capture the root cause of real-world data distributions and only offer a limited amount of diversity. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) have become a strong option for addressing this lack of data. By recog-nizing how the data is spread out, GANs can make realistic samples that add variety to training sets. Researchers have investigated using them for handwriting recognition, natural images, and more in clinical fields like chest X-ray

In summary, this work offers four main contributions. Second, it evaluates the fidelity and diversity of generated samples through metrics such as IS, FID, KID, and precision–recall scores, supported by t-SNE and UMAP visualizations. Third, it examines the effect of generative augmentation on downstream perfor-mance by comparing classifiers trained on real data alone, with common augmentations like Mixup, Cutout, CutMix, and AugMix, and with GAN-generated samples, showing clear gains under low-data conditions. # 2 Literature Review 

Between 2018 and 2025, studies on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) advanced steadily in both building models and assessment. Architectures like DCGAN [3] provided the initial momentum, which subsequently gave way to more stable formulations such WGAN-GP and the application of spectral normalization. To improve generalization and reduce overfitting, researchers have investigated mixed techniques that link GANs to external classifiers during the past few years (2022–2024). As a demonstration, examine Haque’s (2021) External Classifier GAN (EC-GAN), in which the generator is directed by an auxiliary classifier instead of the discriminator. Compared to conventional augmentation or regularization methods, this configuration resulted in greater classification accuracy [6, 8]. Evaluation methods have developed in parallel with architecture. Costa et al. (2021) tracked generator–discriminator evolution using t-SNE visualizations, which went beyond the early dependence on adversarial loss curves and pro-vided understandable insights into convergence. While recent work suggests Kernel Inception Distance (KID) and Precision–Recall curves for fuller fidelity–diversity assessment, standard quantitative metrics like Inception Score (IS) and Fr´ echet Inception Distance (FID) are still frequently used [5]. Handwriting recognition and medical imaging have both made extensive use of GANs. Bowles et al. (2018) showed that GAN-generated MR images in medical imaging enhanced segmentation in low-data circumstances; subsequent research up until 2023 showed higher Dice scores and better handling of class imbalance. The BanglaLekha-Isolated dataset initially appeared in handwriting research by Biswas et al. (2017). It was then augmented with multimodal characteristics and GAN-based augmentation to enhance Indic script recognition. Sikder (2020) further advanced Bangla character recognition by combining GAN-generated samples with deeper CNNs, achieving validation accuracy above 99.5 percent and demonstrating the strong benefit of synthetic data with modern architectures [9, 4]. Finally, recent studies highlight interpretability and stability as ongoing challenges. Approaches such as progressive growing GANs, attention-based discriminators, and novel loss functions have improved fidelity and training reliability, while expanding applications to video synthesis, speech generation, and multimodal learning. Emerging work even explores GANs for artistic content creation; for instance, the 2025 arXiv study Generative Adversarial Networks Bridging Art and Machine describes how modern GANs now span both photorealistic synthesis and creative design [2]. Overall, the literature reflects a multifaceted trajectory: continuous architectural innovation, evolving evaluation proto-cols, and expanding application domains from low-resource language processing to clinical imaging yet cross-domain studies that jointly evaluate handwriting and medical tasks remain limited, motivating the present work.

3.1 Dataset Description 

BanglaLekha Isolated, many handwritten Bangla characters that comprise numerals, simple letters, and compound forms, is the initial dataset used in this investigation. It includes over 160,000 samples taken from individuals in various parts of Bangladesh and at various ages [1]. This dataset is particularly helpful for testing how effectively GAN models can replicate nuanced character shapes and maintain fine-grained visual details due to the variety of handwriting styles, as shown in Figure 2 of the thesis. Total 679 chest radiographs taken from patients across the world are included in the second dataset, the COVID-19 Chest X-ray dataset [7]. Due to differences in technology, patient anatomy, and health course, the photos show a variety of disorders, such as COVID-19, SARS, and MERS, and varied significantly in appearance. Sample images from the COVID-19 Chest X-Ray Dataset are shown in Figure 3, which shows the minor, but important patterns required for a good diagnosis and shows diversity among patients and imaging equipment.

3.3 GAN Architecture 

We implemented a convolutional GAN inspired by DCGAN [3]. The generator transforms a 100-dimensional latent vector into an image through stacked transposed convolutional layers, each followed by batch normalization and ReLU activations, with a final Tanh output (Table 1). The discriminator processes input images via convolutional layers with batch normalization and Leaky ReLU activations, culminating in a sigmoid output for binary real/fake prediction (Table 2). 3.4 GAN Training Process 

GAN training took place across 300 epochs, using the Adam optimizer for both networks and a batch size of 32. A local computer with an NVIDIA GTX 1080 Ti GPU was used for training, and Google Colab Pro was also used to boost computational efficiency. The BanglaLekha dataset took about 2.5 hours to train on Colab, while the COVID-19 dataset took about 4 hours. The training procedure follows the conventional minimax game, in which the discriminator constantly improves its capacity to identify fake images while the generator tries to trick it. This adversarial training loop is shown in Figure 5, which demonstrates how both networks continuously improve their performance. 

3.5 Dimensionality Reduction and Visualization 

Dimensionality reduction algorithms were used to measure the extent to which synthetic images resemble the dis-tribution of real data. First, the feature space was reduced while preserving the directions of highest variance using Principal Component Analysis (PCA). t-SNE, which highlights local structure, cluster formation, and neighborhood interactions, was then used to map the reduced features into two dimensions. When combined, these procedures pro-vide information about the total diversity of the samples produced as well as class separability. # 4 Experiments and Results 

4.1 Visual Assessment of Generated Samples 

Over training epochs, the generated images’ quality significantly improved. Early Bangla number examples were noisy and unstructured, shown in Figures 7 and 8. However, by the year 200, the characters had neat edges, consistent strokes, and stylistic diversity that reflected variations in handwriting. Medical data showed a similar pattern: The model created the chest X-ray pictures shown in Figure 9, which show the general anatomical structure of the rib cages and lungs. While subtle artifacts remain, the generated images demonstrate the model’s ability to reproduce medically relevant patterns. Final samples of Bangla characters in Figure 10 appear visually indistinguishable from real samples to non-specialists, highlighting their potential for data augmentation.

4.4 Downstream Classifier Performance 

Three settings were used to assess classifier performance: actual data only, real with traditional augmentation, and real with GAN-augmented examples. Accuracy in Bangla character recognition increased from 89.2% (macro-F1 0.87) to 91.8% (0.90) using conventional techniques and 94.7% (0.94) using GAN augmentation. Sensitivity at 90% specificity increased from 0.78 to 0.84 for chest X-rays, while AUROC improved from 0.86 to 0.89 and then 0.93. These results confirm that realistic samples generated by GAN-based augmentation greatly improve classifiers in domains with limited data.

# 5 Discussion 

GAN-based augmentation outperformed classical augmentation through generating appealing samples and enhanced classifier performance in the medical and handwriting domains. Evaluations using IS, FID, and embeddings confirmed fidelity and variety, whereas stability techniques like WGAN-GP and spectral normalization prevented mode collapse. However, FID relied on ImageNet features that are not entirely appropriate for medical imaging tasks, and IS values remained low. This highlights that downstream classifier increases are a more significant indicator of augmentation success, and intrinsic scores should be viewed with caution. Because synthetic data may still leak information, privacy concerns are still present. For more dependable and secure deployment in delicate sectors, future research will investigate stronger generators like StyleGAN and diffusion models, domain-specific evaluation methods, larger datasets, and privacy-preserving training. To improve the technical depth and generalization of generative augmentation, future research will build on this baseline by incorporating domain-specific fidelity metrics (SSIM, LPIPS) and higher-capacity generators such as StyleGAN and diffusion models. 

# 6 Conclusion 

This study shows that GAN-based augmentation can meaningfully reduce data scarcity in handwriting and medical imaging. On the BanglaLekha-Isolated dataset, accuracy increased from 89.2 to 94.7 percent and macro-F1 from 0.87 to 0.94, while chest X-ray AUROC rose from 0.86 to 0.93 and sensitivity at 90 percent specificity from 0.78 to 0.84. Low FID scores (0.24 for Bangla and 0.12 for X-rays) and embedding plots confirmed strong alignment between real and synthetic samples, and stability methods such as WGAN-GP and spectral normalization reduced FID by 9 and 12 percent, preventing mode collapse. Overall, the results show that a unified GAN-based pipeline can reliably improve classifier performance in low-resource settings, though intrinsic metrics for medical images and privacy concerns remain important limitations. Future work will explore stronger generators such as StyleGAN and diffusion models, adopt domain-specific metrics like SSIM and LPIPS, expand datasets, and apply privacy-preserving strategies to support scalable and trustworthy generative augmentation.
