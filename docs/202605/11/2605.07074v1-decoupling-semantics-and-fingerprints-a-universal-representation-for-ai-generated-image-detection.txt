# Decoupling Semantics and Fingerprints: A Universal Representation for AI-Generated Image Detection 

Zhiyuan Wang, Yanxiang Chen, Yuanzhi Yao, Yunfeng Diao 

Abstract —Detecting AI-generated images across unseen archi-tectures remains challenging, as existing models often overfit to generator-specific fingerprints and semantic content rather than learning universal forgery traces. We attribute this failure to feature entanglement: detectors learn these factors as a single entangled representation, where universal forgery traces are inextricably confounded with both generator-specific fingerprints and semantic content. Crucially, our spectral analysis reveals that this entanglement is avoidable: distinct generator-specific fingerprints (e.g., GAN stripes vs. Diffusion Model spots) occupy disjoint frequency subspaces and coexist as independent super-positions. Leveraging this physical orthogonality, we propose the Orthogonal Decomposition and Purification Network (ODP-Net) to structurally disentangle these factors. Specifically, ODP-Net employs (1) Instance-aware Orthogonal Decomposition to project features into mutually exclusive subspaces: universal forgery traces, generator-specific fingerprints, and semantic content; (2) Perturbation-based Purification to enforce semantic invariance via cross-sample feature injection; and (3) Manifold Alignment to bridge domain gaps. By explicitly decoupling universal forgery traces from generator-specific fingerprints and semantic content, ODP-Net achieves state-of-the-art performance on unseen ar-chitectures (e.g., Stable Diffusion 3), validating that structural disentanglement is key to generalization. 

Index Terms —AI-generated image detection, feature disen-tanglement, spectral orthogonality, orthogonal decomposition, domain generalization, deepfake detection.

# GENERATIVE Artificial Intelligence has seen significant advancements, enabling the synthesis of hyper-realistic images that blur the line between reality and forgery. While early GAN-based models [1], [2] and modern Diffusion Models [3] have revolutionized content creation, they also pose severe risks to digital security. A primary challenge in forensic research is developing detectors that generalize to unseen generative architectures, as existing models often suffer from catastrophic performance degradation when encountering distribution shifts from novel generators [4]. We argue that this lack of generalization stems from a fundamental structural misalignment: current detectors process forgery signals as a single, inextricably tangled web, failing to recognize that these signals are actually composed of independent, separable layers. Intuitively, one might assume these various artifacts blend together into an inseparable noise. However, our differential analysis reveals a clean, highly structured phenomenon we term Spectral Orthogonality. Simply put, artifacts from different generators act like radio broadcasts transmitting on entirely different channels. They occupy distinct, non-overlapping frequency bands. Because they do not share the same frequencies, they do not destruc-tively interfere or “blend” into one another; instead, they simply stack on top of each other—a property of spectral additivity. This physical independence proves that a gener-ator’s specific fingerprint is not permanently fused with the image’s universal forgery traces. They exist as orthogonal (independent) variables. Standard detectors ignore this physical reality. Instead, they learn an entangled representation where the crucial universal traces are inextricably mixed with specific generator finger-prints and the underlying semantic content of the image. M ETHODOLOGY 

A. Framework Overview 

Existing forgery detectors often learn entangled feature representations, coupling generator-specific fingerprints and semantic content with intrinsic manipulation traces. This en-tanglement hinders generalization to unseen generative models or diverse semantic contexts. To explicitly address this, ODP-Net models the latent fea-ture vector z as a superposition of three mutually orthogonal components: 

z = zu + zs + zn, (1) where zu ⊥ zs ⊥ zn, with zu encoding intrinsic, universal forgery traces invariant to synthesis methods and semantics; 

zs capturing generator-specific fingerprints that uniquely char-acterize generative pipelines; and zn corresponding to residual nuisance factors primarily arising from semantic variance. The implementation of Eq. 1 is achieved through a three-stage pipeline. We first solve the structural separation problem using dynamic masking (Sec. III-B), followed by a semantic invariance optimization step (Sec. III-C), and conclude with a geometric regularization of the feature manifold (Sec. III-D).

A. Experimental Settings 

Datasets and Protocols. We conduct a comprehensive evaluation to assess cross-generator generalization and real-world robustness using the AIGIBench dataset [23]. The dataset is partitioned into disjoint source and target do-mains to simulate the realistic scenario where detectors must face unknown synthesis methods. The Source Domain (Training) comprises eight diverse architectures: ProGAN [2], R3GAN [24], BLIP [25], Infinite-ID [26], DALLE-3 [27], FLUX1-dev [28], BlendFace [29], and E4S [30]. The Target Domain (Unseen Evaluation) consists of eight novel genera-tors: StyleSwim [31], WFIR 1, InstantID [32], IP-Adapter [33], Midjourney 2, SD3 [34], FaceSwap 3, and InSwap 4. Addi-tionally, we evaluate on the In-the-Wild subset (Commu-nityAI [23] and SocialRF [23]) to assess real-world robustness. 

Evaluation Metrics. We employ Balanced Accuracy (bAcc) to measure classification performance and Negative Log Like-lihood (NLL) to evaluate probability calibration. bAcc is computed as bAcc = 12 ( T P T P +F N + T N T N +F P ), while NLL is defined as NLL = − 1

PNi=1 [yi log( pi)+(1 −yi) log(1 −pi)] ,where yi denotes ground truth and pi represents predicted probability. Specifically, a higher bAcc value indicates better classification performance, whereas a lower NLL value reflects more accurate probability calibration of the model. 

Baselines. We compare against seven state-of-the-art meth-ods: AIDE [7], FreqNet [12], RINE [35], NRR [36], VIB [19], FatFormer [6], and SAFE [37]. All baselines are retrained on the identical source domain for fair comparison. 

Implementation. ODP-Net adopts a pre-trained CLIP ViT-L/14 backbone and trains for 100 epochs using AdamW with cosine annealing ( 1 × 10 −6 minimum). We apply differential learning rates: 1 × 10 −5 for the backbone and 1 × 10 −4 for task-specific layers, with 1 × 10 −4 weight decay. All loss coefficients ( α, β, γ, λ ) are uniformly set to 1.0 to demonstrate robustness without extensive tuning. 

B. In-Domain Detection Performance 

We first assess whether disentanglement compromises in-domain accuracy. As shown in Table I, ODP-Net avoids the typical trade-off between robustness and precision, achieving competitive results across all source categories while signifi-cantly improving probability calibration (lowest NLL in 6/8 subsets). ODP-Net, however, maintains a consistent performance envelope. C ONCLUSION 

In this work, we attribute the lack of generalization in existing AI-generated image detectors to the entanglement of intrinsic manipulation traces with source-specific nuisances. Leveraging the observation that forgery artifacts exhibit spec-tral orthogonality, we proposed ODP-Net to structurally de-couple these components via instance-aware decomposition and perturbation-based purification. By effectively isolating invariant forensic signals from semantic and generator-specific factors, our method achieves state-of-the-art performance and probability calibration on unseen architectures, validating the effectiveness of physically grounded feature disentanglement.
