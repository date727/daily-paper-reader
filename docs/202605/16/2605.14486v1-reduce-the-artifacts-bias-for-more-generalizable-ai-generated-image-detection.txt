## Abstract 

As the misuse of AI-generated images grows, generalizable image detection tech-niques are urgently needed. Recent state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods adopt aligned training datasets to reduce content, size, and format biases, empowering models to capture robust forgery cues. A common strategy is to employ reconstruction tech-niques, e.g., VAE and DDIM, which show remarkable results in diffusion-based methods. However, such reconstruction-based approaches typically introduce lim-ited and homogeneous artifacts, which cannot fully capture diverse generative patterns, such as GAN-based methods. To complement reconstruction-based fake images with aligned yet diverse artifact patterns, we propose a GAN-based upsam-pling approach that mimics GAN-generated fake patterns while preserving content, size, and format alignment. This naturally results in two aligned but distinct types of fake images. However, due to the domain shift between reconstruction-based and upsampling-based fake images, direct mixed training causes suboptimal results, where one domain disrupts feature learning of the other. Accordingly, we propose a Separate Expert Fusion (SEF) framework to extract complementary artifact infor-mation and reduce inter-domain interference. We first train domain-specific experts via LoRA adaptation on a frozen foundational model, then conduct decoupled fusion with a gating network to adaptively combine expert features while retaining their specialized knowledge. Rather than merely benefiting GAN-generated image detection, this design introduces diverse and complementary artifact patterns that enable SEF to learn a more robust decision boundary and improve generalization across broader generative methods. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method yields strong results across 13 diverse benchmarks. Codes are released at: 

https://github.com/liyih/SEF_AIGC_detection .

## 1 Introduction 

Generative AI [ 21 , 17 , 58 ] advances rapidly, enabling easy creation of hyper-realistic images for creative design, media production, scientific visualization, and the entertainment industry. However, malicious use of such technology can spread disinformation, facilitate fraud, and manipulate public opinion, undermining information authenticity and social trust. Consequently, reliable AI-generated image detection [ 52 , 54 , 35 , 53 ] is urgently needed to mitigate these harms and enable responsible AIGC use. A critical challenge is achieving strong generalization [ 40 , 31 ], namely the ability to detect images generated not only by known models but also by unseen generators.

To achieve highly generalizable detection, recent SOTA methods [ 6 , 7, 36 ] typically adopt an aligned training paradigm, in which reconstructed images that closely resemble real images are used as synthetic negatives. These methods perform strongly across benchmarks, as they reduce training biases that impair generalization and drive the model to capture intrinsic, model-agnostic artifacts. In particular, prior approaches [ 45 , 20 ] effectively remove biases related to content, size, and format [7]. However, these methods overlook a critical limitation referred to as artifact bias . Specifically, these methods rely on only a single reconstruction strategy, such as VAE [ 46 , 7, 45 ] and DDIM [ 51 , 6]. They achieve excellent detection results on diffusion-based generated images but present distinct limitations for other [ 17 ] generators, such as GAN. The artifacts introduced by reconstruction methods possess inherent biases and cannot be easily transferred to GAN-based generation scenarios. Resolving this gap requires addressing two key technical questions as follows: 

How to construct an aligned dataset that mimics divergent artifacts? Since existing aligned datasets primarily capture diffusion-oriented artifacts, a complementary source is needed to introduce artifact patterns distinct from those of diffusion models to broaden the forgery spectrum while preserving alignment. We address this by leveraging SRGAN [ 29 ], a classic super-resolution (SR) method, to construct aligned synthetic negatives. As an SR-oriented GAN variant, SRGAN preserves the adversarial training dynamics and hierarchical feature mapping of standard GANs, enabling it to introduce GAN-style artifacts while keeping content, size, and format consistent with the real images. These artifacts mainly arise from three generator-level factors: checkerboard artifacts [ 39 ] induced by transposed-convolution upsampling, structural inconsistencies caused by limited receptive fields and architectural constraints [ 54 ], and spectral discrepancies characterized by unnatural frequency distributions due to inaccurate replication of high-frequency power spectra [ 14 , 16 ]. By capturing these mechanisms, SRGAN provides synthetic negatives with complementary GAN artifact patterns. 

How to train a model on an aligned dataset containing mixed artifacts? Empirical results show that simply mixing two datasets containing different types of artifacts at a fixed ratio leads to suboptimal performance. This degradation can be attributed to manifold incompatibility and gradient conflict. Specifically, these artifacts occupy different regions of the feature space, making it difficult for a single model to learn a unified representation through joint optimization. During mixed training, gradients from one artifact domain may conflict with those from the other, encouraging the model to rely on more salient domain-specific shortcuts while suppressing the subtle intrinsic cues needed for generalizable fakeness detection. To address this issue, we first train two specialized experts independently, allowing each expert to capture its own artifact manifold without cross-domain gradient interference. We then fuse the experts by unfreezing only a minimal subset of parameters while keeping the backbones frozen, thereby preserving their specialized knowledge. By decoupling artifact-specific feature learning from cross-domain knowledge integration, this strategy mitigates negative transfer and enables the model to learn a more robust and unified representation across diverse generative architectures. 2The overall framework is shown in Fig. 1. We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed framework across diverse generative scenarios. The results demonstrate that our model achieves superior and balanced detection performance on both GAN-based and diffusion-based images, effectively overcoming the artifact bias inherent in single-reconstruction methods. Furthermore, our approach consistently outperforms existing competitive methods across multiple large-scale benchmarks, confirming its robust generalization capability to unseen generators. In summary, the primary contributions of this work are threefold. • Identification of Artifact Bias: We identify the critical limitation of artifact bias in existing aligned training paradigms and analyze its impact on model generalization. • GAN-Artifact Aligned Dataset: We introduce a novel aligned dataset construction method using SRGAN to effectively replicate GAN-specific artifacts for complementarity. • Separate Expert Fusion Strategy: We propose a two-stage expert fusion strategy that decouples artifact feature extraction from knowledge integration to mitigate negative transfer and achieve a unified representation of diverse generative artifacts. 

## 2 Related Work 

AI-Generated Image Detection. Driven by the growth of generative models, AI-generated image detection has become crucial, with early works primarily relying on capturing low-level artifacts. CNNSpot [ 59 ] is a pioneer in synthetic image detection, employing a simple CNN to capture forgery traces. Building on this, some work [ 33 , 25 ] relies on spatial-based analysis. For instance, NPR [ 54 ] utilizes a localized differential operator to capture structural artifacts caused by up-sampling. Another significant line of research focuses on frequency-domain analysis [ 53 , 24 , 43 ]. For example, SAFE [ 30 ] utilizes the high-frequency components of the Discrete Wavelet Transform as input to expose subtle architectural inconsistencies that are often imperceptible in the spatial domain. Beyond spatial and frequency analysis, a third category of research identifies forgeries based on reconstruction error [ 10 , 8, 37 ]. DIRE [ 60 ] is the first to use reconstruction error calculated by the real and diffusion-generated images. Recent SOTA methods tend to finetune the visual foundation models, such as CLIP [ 44 ] and DINO [ 4]. These pre-trained models encapsulate highly generalizable knowledge derived from massive datasets, effectively complementing the fragile patterns learned from limited training sets. UnivFD [ 40 ] is the pioneer to use the CLIP for synthetic image detection, while Fatformer [ 35 ] adopts a forgery-aware adapter to finetune the pre-trained CLIP. C2P-CLIP [ 55 ] leverages contrastive learning to inject category-aware semantic concepts into the encoder. Effort [ 62 ] employs orthogonal subspace decomposition to effectively disentangle domain-specific artifacts from domain-invariant features. AIDE [ 61 ] combines high-level CLIP representations with the patch-aware low-level features for more robust detection. Simplicity Prevails [ 66 ] provides a comprehensive evaluation of diverse foundation models, exploring their efficacy for synthetic image detection. 

Alignment Training Paradigm. Beyond architectural design, the generalization of detection models is heavily influenced by training data bias, where minimizing such bias typically yields superior generalizable performance. De-fake [ 49 ] analyzes the dataset and observes that synthetic image dimensions are invariably integer multiples of 128. C2P-CLIP [ 55 ] identifies semantic bias within datasets through word frequency analysis. Grommelt et al. [18] reveals format in common datasets. In this view, some approaches are introduced to reduce these biases. SemGIR [ 63 ] generates corresponding synthetic images based on descriptions of real images to mitigate semantic bias, while DRCT [ 6] reduces the semantic bias via diffusion reconstruction. B-Free [ 20 ] uses self-conditioned inpainted reconstructions and content augmentation to reduce the dataset bias. AlignedForensics [ 45 ]adopts VAE to align both content and size more efficiently. DDA [ 7 ] further alleviates format-level bias, preventing the model from learning shortcut features. REM [ 36 ] injects controlled Gaussian noise into the VAE latent space, enabling the reconstruction of samples that maintain both visual realism and diversity. ## 3 Methods 

3.1 Artifacts Bias Reconstruction-Induced Bias. Recent aligned training paradigms [ 45 , 7] typically construct a synthetic dataset Tvae by mapping real images x ∈ D R through reconstruction models like VAEs [ 46 ], yielding aligned fake images xvae = Fvae (x). While this reduces semantic and layout biases, the resulting artifacts δvae = xvae − x are highly dependent on the decoder architecture. Due to latent compression and L2 reconstruction losses, VAE-induced artifacts tend to follow a constrained and homogeneous pattern. Consequently, models trained solely on Tvae are exposed to an incomplete artifact spectrum and may overfit to reconstruction-specific cues. When facing generators with divergent artifact formation mechanisms, such as GANs whose artifacts δgan exhibit a distributional shift from δvae , the learned boundary struggles to capture transferable forgery evidence. We define this performance degradation due to incomplete artifact coverage as Artifact Bias .

Simulating Divergent Artifacts via SRGAN. To mitigate this gap while preserving aligned training, we construct a parallel dataset Tsrgan with SRGAN, whose artifact distribution is farther from reconstruction-induced artifacts and thus complementary to Tvae . Specifically, for a real image x,we apply a deterministic degradation D(·) and restore it via xsrgan = Gsrgan (D(x)) . Since Gsrgan 

inherits the adversarial learning mechanisms and hierarchical upsampling of mainstream GANs, its residual δsrgan = xsrgan − x captures the structural and spectral inconsistencies of GAN forgeries. Together, Tvae and Tsrgan form content-aligned yet artifact-complementary datasets that expose the model to a broader artifact spectrum. As illustrated in Fig. 2, the two exhibit complementary characteristics . VAE causes milder distortion with statistics closer to real images but slight local edge distortions, while SRGAN exhibits more global degradation with higher error, lower sharpness and color fidelity, yet more realistic edge distributions and stronger block artifacts. To reduce bias in artifact regions, we apply mask-aware forgery augmentation to both Tvae and Tsrgan . Given a synthetic image If ake and its corresponding real image Ireal , we randomly generate a foreground or background mask M and obtain the augmented image ˆIf ake as:

3.3 Separate Expert Fusion 

Based on the theoretical analysis in Section 3.2, as shown in Fig. 4, we propose the Separate Expert Fusion (SEF) framework. The core philosophy of SEF is to physically isolate the forensic features of heterogeneous generators, thereby circumventing the antagonistic gradients identified in joint training. Instead of freezing entire experts, we find that unfreezing the last k LoRA parameters enables more flexible parameter tuning, which effectively preserves the pre-trained knowledge of experts while adapting to downstream tasks. As shown in Fig. 3, this decoupled training paradigm ( green bar )not only yields balanced high performance on both diffusion and GAN-based methods but even outperforms individual experts specialized in a single domain. This phenomenon arises because multi-source training data enables the model to learn a more robust decision boundary , further demonstrating that SEF achieves a synergistic “1+1 > 2” effect in multi-source mixed training. In the first stage, we focus on independent pre-training for domain-specific experts. For each forgery domain i ∈ { vae, srgan }, we incorporate LoRA [ 22 ] into a frozen foundation model Φ. By training only the LoRA parameters ∆θi and the final prediction head W(i)

4.1 Implementation Details 

Our framework employs DINO-v3-L [ 50 ] as the backbone, fine-tuned via LoRA [ 22 ] with a rank 

r = 8 and scaling factor α = 1 . To ensure consistency, we follow the format alignment established in the DDA [ 7] framework. Input images are resized to 336 × 336 pixels, utilizing random cropping during training and center cropping for evaluation. The training set is constructed using real images from the MSCOCO dataset [ 34 ]. To maintain precise spatial alignment between real and synthetic pairs, all image dimensions are padded or cropped to multiples of 8 before being processed by the VAE or SRGAN. For the SRGAN-based synthesis, we employ a 4× bicubic downsampling as the deterministic degradation function D(·). Optimization is performed using a learning rate of 1 × 10 −4

with a cosine annealing scheduler. All experiments are conducted on 8 NVIDIA V100 GPUs. The training is conducted in two stages with a constant gradient accumulation of four steps. In Stage 1, the model is trained for 10,000 iterations with a batch size of 16. In Stage 2, the batch size is increased to 32, and the training proceeds for 5,000 iterations. Additionally, γ is set to 0.1, and the number of last unfreezed layers k is set to 4. 

4.2 Benchmarks and Comparative Methods 

Firstly, we reorganize a cross-domain forgery benchmark that covers a diverse set of GAN-based and diffusion-based generative methods based on [ 32 , 68 , 59 ], including both classical and advanced approaches. We then evaluate performance across 12 off-the-shelf benchmarks, comprising (1) 11 whole-image forgery benchmarks: GenImage [ 68 ], DRCT-2M [ 6], Synthbuster [ 1], DDA-COCO [ 7], EvalGEN [ 7 ], AIGCDetectionBenchmark [ 65 ], ForenSynths [ 59 ], Chameleon [ 61 ], WildRF [ 5], SynthWildx [ 11 ], and BFree-Online [ 20 ]; and (2) one localized forgery (dedicated inpainting) benchmark: BR-GEN [ 3], which focuses on inpainting detection. For BR-GEN, we randomly sample 3,000 fake images from each subcategory along with their corresponding real images to construct the test set. All sampled fake images contain inpainting in the central region, ensuring that center-cropped inputs always include forged content. These benchmarks incorporate diverse authentic sources and varied generative architectures, providing a rigorous testbed to validate universal generalization across unseen domains. The compared detectors include (1) low-level based methods: NPR [ 54 ], SAFE [ 31 ], and AIDE [ 61 ], (2) foundational model based methods: UnivFD [ 40 ], FatFormer [ 35 ]and C2P-CLIP [ 55 ], (3) aligned methods: DRCT [ 6], Aligned [ 45 ] and DDA [ 7]. Balanced accuracy is adopted as the primary evaluation metric, with the decision threshold fixed at 0.5.

GAN-specialized models (e.g., FatFormer and C2P-CLIP) achieve strong performance on GANs but degrade significantly on diffusion models, while diffusion-oriented methods (e.g., AlignedForensics and DDA) show the opposite trend, highlighting the difficulty of learning unified forensic representations under heterogeneous generation mechanisms. 4.4 Ablation Study 

As shown in Fig. 5 (a), we first compare our default setting (SEF + gate) with several alternative strategy for fusing experts, including (1) single SRGAN or VAE expert, (2) taking the maximum or minimum score between these two experts, (3) averaging the scores from different scores, (4) simply concatenating the vectors of two experts. It could be observed that using the adaptive gate mechanism is significantly better than other ways. As shown in Fig. 5 (b) and (c), we allocate two important factors k and γ in the expert fusion stage. Experiments demonstrate that the best performance is achieved when k is set to 4, with results tending toward saturation when k exceeds this value. The optimal value for γ is 0.1. An excessively large γ would cause the second-stage fine-tuning to disrupt the domain-specific forensic traces pre-learned in the first stage, thereby compromising the original discriminative power of the experts. Refer to Appendix A.6 for the backbone ablation , consistent improvement achieve across DINO-v2-L, DINO-v3-L and DINO-v3-H+. 

4.5 Robust Analysis 

As shown in Tab. 4, our method consistently achieves the best performance under all perturbation settings, demonstrating the strongest robustness on the cross-domain forgery benchmark. Specifically, under blur, cropping, JPEG compression, noise, and the combination of all perturbations, our method reaches 87.9%, 91.4%, 88.6%, 88.3%, and 89.2%, respectively, outperforming all competing methods in every case. Compared with the strongest baseline, DDA †, our method achieves an average improvement of 3.0% points across all perturbation settings. Overall, these results indicate that our method remains highly effective not only under individual perturbations but also in the more challenging setting where multiple degradations are applied simultaneously. Details of the implementation of each perturbation are shown in Appendix A.5 following [16]. 

## 5 Conclusion 

In this work, we identify artifact bias as a key limitation of existing aligned training paradigms for AI-generated image detection. To address this issue, we introduce a GAN-based aligned data construction strategy using SRGAN, which better captures GAN-specific artifacts while preserving alignment in content, size, and format. We further propose a Separate Expert Fusion framework 9that decouples artifact-specific feature learning from knowledge integration, thereby reducing inter-domain interference between reconstruction-based and GAN-based synthetic negatives. Extensive experiments show that our method achieves strong and balanced performance on both diffusion and GAN-generated images, while consistently generalizing well to diverse unseen generators. We hope this work provides a practical step toward more robust and generalizable forgery detection, and inspires future research on bias-aware training paradigms for AIGC forensics. 

Limitation and Future Work. The proposed expert-based design may introduce some additional training complexity and stage, requiring more careful optimization and resource allocation during training. In the future, we will explore lightweight fusion strategies to retain the advantages of expert specialization while lowering training costs, and extend our framework to cover more artifact sources and generative traces from emerging image synthesis and editing paradigms.

A.1 Detailed Analysis of Gradient Conflicts in Mixed Training. 

To verify the existence of gradient conflicts in multi-source mixed training, we design a direct validation experiment. Specifically, we use a DINO-v3-L model with LoRA (rank = 8, alpha = 1), where the pretrained backbone parameters are frozen, and only the LoRA layers and the classification head remain trainable—fully consistent with the actual training setup. We select the first 50 iterations for analysis, and the batch size is setup to 8 to ensure statistical stability. Within each batch, we independently perform forward and backward passes using VAE-reconstructed data (SD 2.1) and GAN-based super-resolution data (SRGAN), respectively. We then collect the gradient vectors over all trainable LoRA parameters, denoted as ∇L vae and ∇L srgan , and compute their cosine similarity 

cos( ∇L vae , ∇L srgan ).As shown in Fig. 6, the batch-wise cosine similarity oscillates sharply within the range of 

[−0.04 , +0 .08] , frequently crossing zero. The alternating red (conflict) and blue (alignment) re-gions indicate that the gradient directions of the two data sources do not exhibit stable cooperation at each step. Overall, gradient conflicts occurred in over 20% (12 out of 50) of the iterations, signifi-cantly undermining the convergence. The histogram in Fig. 7 further shows that the mean cosine similarity is only 0.014, extremely close to zero, with a nearly symmetric distribution around zero. This indicates that the optimal gradient directions for VAE and GAN artifacts are nearly orthogonal in the high-dimensional space. As a result, joint optimization leads to suboptimal results for both, Gradient conflict occurs uniformly across layers. Fig. 8 presents the layer-wise cosine similarity across all 24 Transformer blocks of ViT-L. The mean similarity for every block is close to zero, while the standard deviation is large (error bars spanning [−0.2, +0 .2] ), especially in the shallow layers (Blocks 0–3), where the variance is most pronounced. This indicates that gradient conflict is not confined to specific depths of the model but is instead distributed throughout the entire network. The larger variance in shallow layers can be attributed to the fundamental differences in low-level visual features (e.g., edges and textures) captured by the two artifact types: VAE-induced frequency attenuation artifacts versus GAN-induced checkerboard artifacts. These findings provide direct empirical evidence supporting the use of an expert separation architec-ture. When the gradient directions from two data sources are nearly orthogonal, jointly training a single shared LoRA parameter space leads to mutual cancellation in update directions, preventing the model from learning effective representations for both artifact types simultaneously.

• PSNR : Evaluates image reconstruction quality by quantifying the ratio of the maximum pixel intensity to the reconstruction error. Higher values indicate smaller distortion and better visual authenticity. • HF Ratio : Computes the proportion of high-frequency energy in the frequency domain via FFT. Higher values indicate richer details, while lower values suggest smoothing or loss of fine structures. • Sharpness : Uses the variance of the Laplacian response to measure image clarity. Higher values indicate sharper images, while lower values imply blur. • Saturation : Calculates the mean saturation in the HSV color space, reflecting color vivid-ness. Lower values indicate desaturation. • TexEntropy : Measures texture complexity using entropy derived from the gray-level co-occurrence matrix (GLCM). Higher values indicate richer textures. • EdgeSkew : Computes the skewness of Sobel gradient magnitudes at edge pixels. Higher values suggest stronger or over-enhanced edges, while values closer to real indicate more natural edge distributions. • DCTBlockiness : The grayscale image is split into 8 × 8 blocks. We compute the ratio of gradient energy at block boundaries to the total gradient energy. A higher value denotes stronger grid-boundary discontinuities, while a lower value means smoother inter-block transitions.

Our work studies AI-generated image detection, which has a positive societal impact by helping miti-gate the spread of synthetic or manipulated visual content, including misinformation, impersonation, and fraudulent media. Reliable detection methods can support content moderation, digital forensics, copyright protection, and public trust in online visual information. At the same time, such systems may have potential negative impacts if they produce false positives, fail to generalize across domains or demographic groups, or are over-relied upon as definitive evidence. Moreover, detection methods may motivate adversaries to develop more evasive generation techniques. We therefore emphasize that AI-generated image detectors should be used as decision-support tools rather than sole arbiters, and should be evaluated transparently across diverse datasets and deployment scenarios. 21
