Security alert screening is the downstream task of filtering, prioritizing, correlating, and contextualizing alerts for analyst attention in Security Operations Centers. This survey reviews artificial-intelligence-driven alert screening and alert-fatigue mitigation from 2015–2026. We synthesize 119 records, including 87 core studies, into a four-stage workflow taxonomy covering filtering, triage, correlation, and generative augmentation. We find persistent gaps in deployment realism, adversarial robustness, cross-environment validation, and evaluation practice. The survey concludes with a research agenda toward trustworthy Cognitive Security Operations Centers. 1.1 Scope and Contributions 

This survey reviews AI techniques applied to SOC alert fatigue and downstream screening over a 2015–2026 systematic search window. Relative to prior surveys on human–AI teaming, prioritization, or LLM use cases, our focus is cross-category evidence synthesis: we map benchmark datasets to taxonomic categories, trace the Evidence-Quality Gap between Tier 1 field evidence and Tier 2/3 benchmark or conceptual evidence across all four workflow stages, and examine how downstream screening outcomes can inform upstream tuning. Section 2.2 positions this contribution against directly competing surveys. Our primary contributions are: 

• A workflow-aligned four-dimensional taxonomy of AI approaches to the security alert screening task, enabling practitioners to identify solutions by operational need. The taxonomy covers the complete screening pipeline: noise reduction before the analyst queue, prioritized triage of queued alerts, multi-alert correlation into incident hypotheses, and generative AI augmentation of analyst workflows. 

• Paper-level comparison tables for each taxonomic category, encoding method, dataset/setting, key metric, and a notable limitation for representative studies, together with a complete mapping of all 87 core studies to categories and source types in the supplementary material. 

• A systematic review of 22 benchmark and alert-level datasets, classified by primary research orientation (LLM, ML/DL, or upstream-only) and annotated with publicly reported class-imbalance ranges and representational gaps relative to real SOC environments. 

• An integrated analysis of Human–AI teaming (HAT) architectures, explainable AI (XAI) adoption barriers, reinforce-ment learning from human feedback (RLHF), and privacy-preserving federated learning, cross-cutting themes not jointly emphasized at the same paper-level depth in any single existing survey [23, 28, 65, 72, 75, 138, 142]. 

• A structured nine-direction research agenda derived from identified open problems, with operationally grounded prioritization criteria. Conflict-of-interest handling for self-authored Category I studies is disclosed in Section 13.

2 Security Alert Screening: Background, Prior Surveys, and Taxonomy 2.1 The Modern Security Operations Center 

A SOC integrates people, processes, and technology for continuous monitoring, detection, analysis, and response [ 106 ]. The canonical tier structure assigns Tier 1 analysts to initial triage, Tier 2 to incident investigation and escalation, and Tier 3 to threat hunting and forensics; empirical work documents inherent analyst–manager disagreement on priorities and tool effectiveness [ 78 ]. The technology stack centers on the SIEM (Splunk, IBM QRadar, Microsoft Sentinel) as aggregation and correlation layer [ 21 ], augmented by endpoint detection and response (EDR; CrowdStrike, SentinelOne), network detection and response or network traffic analysis (NDR/NTA; Darktrace, ExtraHop), user and entity behavior analytics (UEBA), threat intelligence platforms (TIPs), and security orchestration, automation, and response (SOAR). Figure 2 schematizes the upstream/downstream boundary that is the focus of this survey: the upstream layer (IDS, network security monitoring (NSM), EDR engines, SIEM correlation rules) generates alerts from raw telemetry; the downstream layer applies four workflow stages (filtering, prioritization, correlation, and analyst augmentation) to surface the subset requiring human investigation. Downstream quality is bounded by, but analytically distinct from, upstream quality: improving IDS recall without addressing screening merely raises alert volume.

2.3 Evidence-Quality Validation Rubric 

To ensure consistent assignment of the evidence tiers used in the per-category comparison tables (Tables 5, 6, 7, and 8), we apply a validation rubric. Tier 1 (Production/Field) denotes evaluation on non-synthetic operational data (e.g., SOC alert logs) with analyst-in-the-loop validation or field-deployment context. Tier 2 (Benchmark) denotes evaluation on heterogeneous, labeled public datasets (e.g., DARPA TC, CICIDS, CERT) or benchmark-grade environments with explicit threat scenarios and reported quantitative error rates. Tier 3 (Conceptual/Preliminary) denotes studies relying on limited or synthetic data, simplified simulation, or evaluation without sufficient quantitative error reporting. Reproducibility and deployment realism are treated as separate dimensions: a production paper may have weaker artifact transparency than a benchmark paper, but it still provides stronger evidence about operational validity; conversely, a fully reproducible conceptual paper may be methodologically valuable without establishing deployment readiness. This rubric therefore measures evidence context rather than intrinsic scientific merit. In the analytic synthesis reported later in the manuscript, only a small subset of core studies are Tier 1, whereas the representative Category III studies summarized in Table 7 are overwhelmingly Tier 2. The resulting imbalance is not a minor reporting artifact but a persistent deployment-evaluation gap: the field produces many technically sophisticated systems, yet comparatively little evidence that those systems remain effective under the temporal, organizational, and data-distribution pressures of real SOCs.

2.5 Taxonomy of the Security Alert Screening Pipeline 

We organize AI approaches along four primary workflow-aligned dimensions. Unlike technique-based taxonomies (which group all RL methods together regardless of SOC application), our taxonomy enables direct operational mapping: a SOC architect facing a triage bottleneck can navigate directly to Category II, while one addressing multi-stage incident investigation consults Category III. Cross-cutting concerns (Human–AI teaming, explainability, privacy) are treated separately in Section 7 as they apply across all four categories. 3.2 Supervised Classification 

The dominant paradigm trains supervised classifiers on labeled analyst triage decisions. Features typically encode static alert attributes (type, source, destination, severity), temporal features (inter-arrival time, hourly/daily frequencies), historical analyst decision patterns, asset context (business criticality, vulnerability state), and threat intelligence correlation scores. Random Forests and Gradient Boosted Trees (XGBoost, LightGBM) have been the most widely validated algorithm families due to their robustness to feature noise, efficient training on tabular alert data, and relatively interpretable output. Deep neural networks have been applied but offer limited advantage over ensemble methods on structured alert data absent sequence or graph structure. Representative filtering studies are summarized in Table 5. Among the log-analysis tools that underpin the log-based screening pipeline, SwissLog [ 81 ] deserves specific mention for its emphasis on robust anomaly detection across diverse system faults. In combination with Drain [ 58 ], LogBERT [ 48 ], and PLELog [ 159 ], it illustrates how log preprocessing and semi-supervised anomaly detection form enabling infrastructure for downstream SOC screening even when they are not themselves full alert-disposition systems. Ban et al., Ndichu et al., Aminanto et al., and colleagues form a sustained Category I line on real enterprise alert data. Ban et al. 3.3 DeepCASE and Cost-Sensitive Approaches 

DeepCASE [ 34 ] (van Ede et al., IEEE S&P 2022) is among the most technically developed alert-screening systems in the surveyed corpus. It addresses the contextual poverty formalized in Section 2.4 by combining a Context Builder (an LSTM with multi-head attention over a sliding event window) that summarizes preceding events into an attention vector, an Interpreter that clusters sequences with similar attention vectors into semantically coherent groups, and a semi-supervised interface where analysts label clusters rather than individual events. Once labeled, matching sequences are auto-classified. Although DeepCASE’s attention-based grouping is functionally an event-correlation step, we count it once under Category I (primary contribution: pre-queue noise reduction with semi-supervised contextual classification) and cross-reference Category III for its correlation properties. Evaluated on the LASTLINE commercial platform and Zeek/Suricata enterprise deployments, DeepCASE reports analyst workload reduction above 90% under the reported evaluation setting, with controlled false-negative rates through cluster-policy management; limitations include a cold-start period and theoretical vulnerability to adversarial event injection. Standard binary cross-entropy is inappropriate when false negatives and false positives differ in cost; cost-sensitive losses such as asymmetric focal loss [ 84 ], class-weighted cross-entropy, and calibrated probability thresholding remain standard, while Monte Carlo Dropout, deep ensembles, and conformal prediction add calibrated uncertainty for variable-threshold policies (suppress at high confidence, escalate at high uncertainty, otherwise pass to Tier-1).

3.4 Unsupervised, Semi-Supervised, and Adversarial Robustness 

Label scarcity is pervasive: analyst decisions are expensive at scale and historical labels may be stale. Unsupervised approaches (Isolation Forest, Local Outlier Factor [ 85 ], DBSCAN, Variational Autoencoders) flag structural deviants without labels but cannot distinguish novel benign events from novel threats; hybrid semi-supervised approaches that combine these with sparse labeled supervision on confirmed threats partially address this. Self- and semi-supervised representation learning (DeepCASE [ 34 ], self-supervised graph learning for provenance [ 68 ]) further reduce analyst effort, although direct evidence for alert-stream contrastive pre-training in production remains limited. Adversarial evasion is a critical and underexplored vulnerability: attackers who model the defense can craft semantically valid attacks that look benign to the classifier. Pierazzi et al. [ 113 ] and the IDS-pipeline survey of Alotaibi and Rassam [ 6]document analogous vulnerabilities (both retained as contextual references since their primary contribution is upstream detection robustness). Randomized smoothing, adversarial training with realistic models, and ensemble diversity are standard mitigations rarely applied to production alert filtering, leaving a significant security gap. 

4 Category II: Automated Triage and Prioritization (Screening Stage: Queue Ordering) 4.1 From Filtering to Prioritization 

Alert prioritization is the second stage of the screening pipeline: it receives the filtered queue 𝛼 𝑓 (𝑡 ) from Category I (or the raw queue when no filtering stage is deployed) and produces an ordering. Where Category I asks “should this alert be shown at all?”, Category II asks “of the alerts shown, which should be examined first?”. In congested SOCs where not all surviving alerts can be investigated within the operational window, prioritization determines which threats receive attention. The formal objective is to learn a ranking 𝑟 : A → R that maximises expected threat coverage within the analyst time budget when alerts are processed in descending score order; it is evaluated by ranking metrics (normalized discounted cumulative gain, nDCG; mean reciprocal rank, MRR) rather than classification metrics. Historically, the line includes earlier IDS-alert prioritization and response-support systems [ 94 , 104 ], analyst-trace retrieval and data-triage learning systems [ 164 , 165 ], and more recent context-aware prioritization and representation-learning methods [ 26 , 87 , 89 ], reinforcing that prioritization is also a queue-management and investigation-support problem. 

4.2 Multi-Feature Risk Scoring 

The dominant production approach combines multiple evidence signals into a composite risk score. Features used in published scoring models include: (a) alert-level features (tool severity, confidence score, alert type, source/destination reputation); (b) asset-context features (business criticality of affected host, known vulnerability state, network exposure level); (c) user/entity context from UEBA (behavioral anomaly scores, account privilege level, recent anomalous activity); (d) threat intelligence correlation (match against known indicators of compromise (IOCs), MITRE ATT&CK technique relevance, threat actor profile); and (e) temporal features (clustering with recent related alerts, proximity to known attack timing patterns). Survey and operational literature consistently argue that severity-only ranking is inadequate for modern SOC prioritization. Effective prioritization combines alert severity with entity context, asset criticality, historical behavior, and threat-intelligence enrichment; this design pattern is reflected both in survey synthesis [ 30 ] and in operational risk-based alerting documentation [ 136 , 137 ]. Finally, alert distributions are non-stationary, so trained models drift; explicit drift-detection methods (ADWIN, Page-Hinkley, DDM) monitor prediction-stream statistics and trigger retraining. Pendlebury et al. [ 112 ] show that ignoring temporal ordering in splits inflates reported performance; Lu et al. [ 92 ] embed ADWIN into feature-distribution monitoring and report 60% lower retraining cost via targeted, drifted-dimension retraining. 

4.4 Emerging Deployment Contexts 

Three deployment contexts are forward-looking but worth watching. Cloud-native environments (AWS Guard-Duty, Microsoft Defender for Cloud, Google Cloud Security Command Center) emit API-centric alerts that differ qualitatively from packet-centric NIDS alerts and include cloud-specific attack patterns (e.g., IAM privilege escalation), with operational documentation describing automated triage, enrichment, and multi-tenant Managed Security Service Provider (MSSP) workflows [ 8 , 95 ]. Operational Technology (OT) and Industrial Control Systems (ICS) have SCADA-specific protocols (Modbus, DNP3), deterministic process baselines, and safety-critical false-negative tolerances; decision-tree-based SCADA screening [ 45 ] illustrates the OT-specific signal model. Workload-aware routing and shift-aware alert distribution recognizes that analyst fatigue peaks at shift handoffs, building on ethnographic findings about analyst–management context-loss [ 140 ]. We treat all three as emerging contexts rather than mature categories in the current core synthesis. 

5 Category III: Alert Correlation and Incident Reconstruction (Screening Stage: Grouping and Narrative) 5.1 The Multi-Stage Attack Challenge 

Sophisticated attacks (Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs), ransomware campaigns, supply chain intrusions) generate distributed signal patterns across multiple tools, hosts, and time windows that may span hours, days, or weeks. A spear-phishing email (email/antivirus alert) enables credential theft, which facilitates lateral movement (authentication/NDR alert), enabling privilege escalation (EDR/UEBA alert), leading to data staging (data loss prevention (DLP) alert), and exfiltration (proxy/firewall alert). Each individual alert, viewed in isolation, may be unremarkable; their temporal and causal relationship constitutes the attack narrative. Alert correlation (the automated grouping of semantically and temporally related alerts into coherent incident hypotheses) is therefore both the primary mechanism for attack narrative reconstruction and a potent alert volume reducer (collapsing hundreds of related alerts into a single enriched incident).

5.2 Graph-Based Correlation 

Alert correlation predates graph learning. Early rule-based, probabilistic, and adaptive approaches established the clustering, inference, and cooperative-correlation foundations on which later graph methods build [ 29 , 31 , 70 , 105 ,114 , 147 ]. Contemporary systems instead model alerts as nodes in a property graph connected by semantic, causal, and temporal relations, then recover attack hypotheses via clustering, path analysis, or learned graph representations. Following the upstream/downstream boundary set in Section 2.4, we include provenance-based systems when their primary evaluated output supports downstream investigation, triage, or incident reconstruction rather than only upstream binary detection. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have become a prominent learning family for correlation. Heterogeneous graph models capture alert, host, user, and process entities within a unified message-passing framework, and provenance-graph representation learning has reported strong results on DARPA TC-style attack benchmarks [ 27 , 46 , 68 , 146 ]. Recent work also targets investigation quality and analyst effort through dependency-impact back-propagation [ 37 ], comparative provenance-IDS analysis [ 22 ], attribution optimization in ORTHRUS [ 69 ], and adversarially robust root-cause-aware detection in R-CAID [ 46 ]. The evidence-weighted conclusion is more cautious than the raw benchmark scores suggest: GNNs are benchmark-leading for high-fidelity correlation, but most evaluations remain tied to DARPA TC-style provenance graphs, and only a minority of papers report latency, memory overhead, or analyst-time savings under realistic enterprise loads. The literature therefore supports GNNs as benchmark-leading correlation models, not yet as universally deployment-ready SOC components. Representative studies are summarized in Table 7. Beyond GNN-based methods, hybrid rule-plus-learning approaches are also emerging for provenance-based intrusion detection. The CAPTAIN system [ 152 ] augments traditional rule-based provenance monitoring with gradient-based learning to adapt rules and reduce false positives without incurring high overhead, achieving improved detection accuracy and lower latency relative to static rule engines.

5.3 Provenance Analysis, Sequence Models, and Threat Intelligence Fusion 

Provenance analysis reconstructs attack causality from OS-level events such as process creation, file access, network connections, and registry changes. Its central challenge is dependency explosion. Systems including NoDoze [ 53 ], RapSheet [ 52 ], HOLMES [ 96 ], POIROT [ 97 ], PrioTracker [ 90 ], OmegaLog [ 54 ], SLEUTH [ 60 ], WATSON [ 154 ], UNI-CORN [ 51 ], SHADEWATCHER [ 161 ], threaTrace [ 153 ], KAIROS [ 27 ], DEPCOMM [ 157 ], MAGIC [ 68 ], R-CAID [ 46 ], and ATLAS [ 7 ] extend the line toward scalable streaming analysis, graph learning, robustness, and analyst-facing narratives. The provenance-IDS surveys of Inam et al. [ 63 ] and Zipperle et al. [ 167 ] synthesize this literature; NODLINK [ 82 ]targets online fine-grained APT detection. Deployment remains constrained by telemetry coverage, OS/application variability, and runtime overhead (e.g., WATSON’s 7–8% per-host CPU [ 154 ]). As a complementary route, LSTM, GRU, and transformer sequence models learn temporal attack-progression patterns directly from alert sequences [ 148 ],

6 Category IV: Generative AI and LLM Augmentation (Screening Stage: Analyst Cognitive Bandwidth) 6.1 LLMs in Security Operations 

Temporal and evidence-quality caveat. LLM capabilities advance on a months-rather-than-years cycle; specific quantitative claims, comparative leaderboards, and prompt-engineering recipes in this section are best read as a snapshot of the 2015–2026 search window, and 13 of the 20 core studies here are preprints or industry reports (compared with 0/11, 1/26, and 2/30 in Categories I–III), so conclusions reflect the trajectory of an evolving sub-field rather than settled findings. Section 12 reiterates this caveat under temporal validity. The fourth pipeline stage addresses a constraint that filtering, prioritization, and correlation cannot fully resolve: the cognitive bandwidth of human analysts. Generative AI and LLMs [ 16 ] augment that bandwidth across all earlier stages, explaining filter decisions, enriching prioritization context, narrating correlated incident hypotheses, and automating routine investigation steps. Unlike classical ML, LLMs offer multi-task capability through natural language and lower some adoption barriers for smaller SOCs that lack large labeled training pipelines, although rigorous cost-benefit evidence is still limited; a recent survey focused on LLMs for SOC environments documents the rapid expansion of this sub-area [49]. An empirical field study [ 131 ] analyzed 3,090 GPT-4 queries submitted by 45 SOC analysts over a 10-month produc-tion deployment. Three task categories dominated, namely clarifying log/command behavior (34%), assessing threat implications (41%), and drafting documentation (25%); iterative query patterns dominated, and a return-user rate of 78% was reported (single-organization, preprint, pre-replication; numbers should be treated as indicative). LLM use centered on sensemaking and context-building rather than autonomous high-stakes decisions.

6.3 Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Benchmarks, and Knowledge Graphs 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) couples the LLM with a real-time retrieval system over a continuously updated knowledge base, addressing the knowledge-staleness limitation of frozen-parameter models. RAG-based SOC assistants perform strongly on threat hunting and investigation tasks that require current knowledge [ 55 , 91 ]; production RAG requires knowledge-base freshness, retrieval precision, context-window management, and verifiable citation quality. Standardized SOC-specific evaluation remains a gap: SecBench [ 166 ] provides multiple-choice cybersecurity question-answering, CyberSecEval [ 20 ] (Meta AI) covers attack helpfulness, insecure code detection, and prompt-injection susceptibility, and the Agent Security Bench (ASB) [ 162 ] formalizes attack and defense evaluation for LLM-based agents, but none directly measures triage accuracy on real alert data, investigation speed, hallucination rates, or prompt-injection robustness in analyst tools (Research Direction R3). Knowledge graphs are a complementary architectural signal: AttacKG [ 83 ] constructs a technique knowledge graph from heterogeneous cyber threat-intelligence reports, and integrating such graphs with RAG combines structured relationship traversal with natural-language synthesis.

6.4 Security Foundation Models and Fine-Tuning 

General LLMs perform credibly on security tasks, but domain-specific pre-training and fine-tuning yield measurable gains: SecureFalcon [ 39 ] fine-tunes Falcon on security code for vulnerability detection, CyberPal.AI [ 3] instruction-tunes on security expert corpora to outperform general LLMs on threat analysis, and SecBench [ 166 ] provides a multi-dimensional evaluation benchmark. The optimal strategy (instruction tuning, RLHF with expert preferences, domain-adaptive pre-training, or combinations) is not yet systematically evaluated. Whether to prefer RAG or fine-tuned models for SOC summarization and investigation support is also open: RAG offers freshness, grounding, and citation trails [ 55 , 91 ] but inherits failure modes through retrieval quality and context truncation; fine-tuned models [ 3, 39 , 64 ]reduce jargon mismatch and latency for narrow tasks but become stale faster and provide weaker provenance. The defensible synthesis is conditional: RAG suits open-ended summarization and threat-intelligence-heavy reasoning, while smaller fine-tuned models suit bounded, latency-sensitive, schema-stable tasks. 

6.5 Agentic AI and LLM-Specific Risks 

LLM agents equipped with tool access (SIEM queries, TI APIs, sandbox detonation, network utilities) can execute multi-step investigations, retrieving alert context, querying host/user history, detonating artifacts, correlating with TI, and synthesizing reports. PentestGPT [ 32 ] demonstrates agent capability in complex sequential security tasks; CORTEX [ 155 ] extends this direction to collaborative LLM-based alert triage with specialized agents (preprint-stage). Recent agentic-SOC fieldwork by Banstola et al. [ 19 ] (embedded ReAct-agent design) and Hahn et al. [ 50 ] (six-month ethnographic deployment) shows initial real-world feasibility but limited generalizability [ 23 , 138 ]. The augmentation-to-autonomy transition introduces hallucination and prompt-injection risks absent from classical ML. Hallucination mitigations include RAG grounding with mandatory citations, confidence-elicitation prompting, multi-agent cross-checking, and mandatory human review of dispositions. Prompt injection (malicious instructions embedded in analyzed content) is acute in LLM-integrated SOC tools; Fang et al. [ 38 ] show that LLM agents can autonomously exploit web vulnerabilities, illustrating the dual-use risk, and Zhang et al.’s Agent Security Bench [ 162 ] benchmarks direct/indirect prompt injection, plan-of-thought backdoors, and memory poisoning along with candidate defenses. 

7 Cross-Cutting Themes 7.1 Human–AI Teaming (HAT) 

A convergent finding across the literature is that fully autonomous AI decision-making is neither ready nor appropriate for security-critical operations. Analysts contribute contextual judgment about organizational risk [ 78 ], creative adversarial reasoning, ethical accountability, and recognition of genuinely novel threats outside the AI’s training distribution; AI systems contribute high-volume structured-data processing, statistical anomaly detection, and synthesis of large threat-intelligence corpora. Human–AI teaming (HAT) frameworks combine these capabilities through deliberate interface design [ 11 ], and autonomy-tiered collaboration models map SOC task criticality to trust thresholds and oversight roles [ 99 ]. 7.2 Explainable AI (XAI) 

The black-box problem is a persistent adoption barrier: opaque suppression, escalation, or prioritization invites either over-trust (automation bias) or under-trust (automation skepticism). Post-hoc methods (SHAP/TreeSHAP [ 93 ], LIME, attention visualization, counterfactual explanations) provide feature-level attribution; counterfactuals are particularly suited to SOC contexts because they suggest actionable investigation pathways. Recent survey work argues that XAI value depends less on standalone widgets than on integration into existing analyst workflows [ 116 ]; the most persistent barriers are weak tool integration, explanation formats that do not map onto operational security concepts, and live-investigation performance overhead. LLMs offer a natural bridge because they can translate feature attributions into analyst-facing language. The PAIEL framework of Kaneko et al. [ 71 ] demonstrates this by injecting protocol metadata and contextual signals into LLM prompts to produce explanations for low-level network events that analysts judge more accurate and actionable than baseline model outputs. 

7.3 Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) 

RLHF aligns model behavior with human preferences and is increasingly applied to SOC prioritization. The pipeline collects analyst preferences over candidate prioritizations, trains a reward model that learns the organizational value function, optimizes a policy via RL (typically proximal policy optimization, PPO), and iterates. The L2DHF framework [ 66 ]is the most complete SOC instantiation, combining preference-based reward learning with an explicit learning-to-defer mechanism. Open questions include feedback-collection interfaces (explicit comparisons vs. implicit decision signals), preference inconsistency across analysts, and reward hacking against proxies that do not reflect detection quality. 

7.4 Privacy-Preserving and Federated Learning 

Alert data and analyst decisions are sensitive, regulated (e.g., GDPR Art. 6, CCPA §1798.140), and rarely shared, leaving individual organizations without enough data for robust models. Federated learning (FL) lets each SOC train locally and share only parameter updates; differential privacy (DP) provides formal guarantees against reconstruction of individual records, and Byzantine-robust aggregation (e.g., Krum, FLTrust) handles compromised participants. Adjacent IDS literature shows that federated and federated-transfer learning can approach centralized performance across heterogeneous environments [ 1]; open questions for SOC alert models include non-IID alert distributions, incentive mechanisms for participation, and system heterogeneity. 

7.5 Deployment Integration: SIEM, SOAR, and XDR 

How AI components integrate into operational architectures is underexamined in the literature. SIEM platforms are the primary integration surface (Splunk ML Toolkit, Sentinel notebooks); SOAR platforms (XSOAR [ 110 ], Splunk SOAR) couple AI-driven triage decisions to playbook automation, with confidence-tiered branches for auto-close, escalation, or auto-containment. Extended Detection and Response (XDR) architectures (CrowdStrike Falcon, Palo Alto Cortex XDR, Microsoft Defender XDR) ingest endpoint, network, identity, and cloud telemetry into a unified data lake with an integrated ML layer, but independent evaluation of their alert-fatigue impact remains limited. Empirical work also shows that playbook use is shaped by organizational, contextual, and usability factors rather than technical correctness

7.6 Closed-Loop AI SOCs 

The upstream/downstream boundary is analytically useful but not architectural isolation: analyst-confirmed false positives, stable benign patterns, and entity-level risk accumulation can be turned into upstream suppression recom-mendations, rule adjustments, or detector-threshold updates (Figure 2). The literature only partially operationalizes this loop. Ban et al. [ 17 ] and Ndichu et al. [ 103 ] record analyst decisions over time to improve later filtering, and the next-generation SIEM framing of Ban et al. [ 18 ] couples alert segmentation, cost-sensitive screening, and SOAR orchestration. Commercial integrations such as XSOAR [ 110 ] and risk-based alerting [ 136 , 137 ] also propagate down-stream signals into upstream behavior, although their detector-tuning logic remains proprietary. What is missing is a formalized, auditable feedback interface that translates downstream outcomes into upstream tuning without inducing suppression drift or attacker-controlled blind spots; recent work on authentication-event processing [ 56 ] illustrates one concrete instantiation. Closed-loop SOCs in which validated downstream outcomes reduce future false-positive load programmatically and safely are therefore the natural research frontier.

8.2 Evaluation Metrics and Protocol 

Standard classification metrics (precision, recall, F1, AUC-ROC) are necessary but insufficient for operational SOC evaluation. Security-ML pitfall catalogues [ 13 ] and pragmatic IDS deployment SoKs [ 12 ] highlight sampling bias, spurious features, weak baselines, and the benchmark-to-production gap; SOC-specific work also emphasizes analyst KPIs [ 2] and throughput/timing/queue-efficiency metrics [ 40 ]. We therefore recommend a complementary set: Alert Queue Reduction Rate (QRR) at a fixed false-negative-rate threshold (e.g., FNR ≤ 2% ), nDCG@ 𝑘 with 𝑘 tied to analyst daily capacity, MTTD/MTTR under workflow simulation, CDRI for month-over-month decay without retraining, ADR 

under specified evasion attacks, and Analyst Cognitive Load via validated scales such as NASA-TLX. Evaluation protocol matters as much as metric choice. Comparisons should include simple baselines (logistic regression or gradient-boosted trees for filtering, random or severity-based ranking for prioritization, signature- or co-occurrence grouping for correlation, and extraction-based summarization for LLM augmentation) so that gains

Splits should preserve chronology (e.g., 70/30 forward-chained or sliding-window) to avoid temporal leakage, and performance decay should be reported over time, preferably with held-out organizational environments for cross-site generalization. Human-subject studies should report sample size, task design, cognitive-load instruments (NASA-TLX), and IRB or equivalent approval; the released codebook includes a binary 

irb_approval field. LLM and agentic systems also require deployment-risk auditing of input sanitization, context isolation, tool-permission boundaries, and fallback escalation; the artifact bundle includes LLM_Risk_Checklist.md .

10 Open Research Challenges 

Our systematic analysis identifies six primary challenge clusters that constrain the real-world impact of current SOC AI approaches. We characterize each by its root cause, current mitigation state, and research priority. 

C1: Cross-environment generalization. ML models trained in one SOC consistently fail to generalize to others, a 

deployment gap driven by heterogeneous SIEM schemas, environment-specific feature distributions, and overfitting to organizational artifacts (e.g., a host that is always benign in training because it is a local scanner). Per-customer calibration, schema-agnostic features, transfer learning, and federated or federated-transfer learning partially address this [1, 118], but do not solve it. 

C2: Adversarial evasion. AI-based filters and rankers are prime targets for adversaries who can craft attack patterns that look benign to the classifier while preserving attack functionality [ 113 ]; analogous vulnerabilities extend to network IDS pipelines [ 6]. Semantic-validity constraints are a hurdle, not a prohibition. Certified defenses via randomized smoothing [ 111 ], realistic adversarial training, and ensemble diversity are standard mitigations but rarely applied to production filtering. A provenance-domain exception is R-CAID [ 46 ], whose immutable root-cause embeddings in a GNN maintain AUC 0.94 under white-box adversarial evaluation, showing that architecture-level choices can provide meaningful robustness. 

C3: Label scarcity, noise, and temporal drift. Labeled alert data are expensive to collect, potentially noisy under fatigue, and temporally non-stationary as the threat landscape evolves. Few-shot and transfer IDS work suggests paths for novelty under sparse labels [ 158 ]; semi-supervised, self-supervised, and active learning address scarcity but not quality, since fatigued labels may embed the very biases they seek to correct. Formal label-noise treatment (noise-robust losses, crowd-sourced correction, noisy-label learning) and longitudinal evaluation with explicit drift simulation remain underexplored [23, 92].

C4: Real-time performance at enterprise scale. GNN correlation on streaming graphs, TB-scale provenance analysis, and LLM inference must respect latency budgets that preserve operational utility. NoDoze’s graph reduction addresses provenance scale, UNICORN’s streaming sketches address memory, but full coverage requires system-level co-design that combines algorithmic approximation, GPU/NPU acceleration, hierarchical processing (fast heuristic pre-filter feeding deep analysis on survivors), and selective LLM invocation only on high-priority or uncertain cases. 

C5: Accountability, ethics, and automation governance. As AI systems suppress, rank, and auto-close alerts with decreasing oversight, accountability and audit questions become first-order. The NIST AI Risk Management Frame-work, ISO/IEC 42001, and the EU AI Act provide general guidance but are not tailored to SOC automation. Practical requirements include immutable audit logs with feature attribution, human-readable decision records accessible to investigators, configurable automation boundaries enforced at the system level, and regular adversarial red-team evaluation of automated decision systems. 

C6: Benchmark standardization and reproducibility. The absence of standardized evaluation protocols makes cross-paper comparison unreliable, since dataset choice, train/test split strategy, evaluation metric, and baseline vary widely. A community-maintained SOC AI evaluation framework, with datasets that reflect the production gap of Section 8, reproducible evaluation harnesses, shared reporting requirements (including the metric set above), and public leader-boards (in the spirit of MLPerf, TREC, or SemEval), would substantially accelerate transfer of research advances into practice. 

11 Research Agenda: Toward the Cognitive SOC 

We use the term Cognitive SOC to denote a security operations architecture in which AI systems augment human analysts across all stages of the screening pipeline, filtering, triage, correlation, and investigation, while maintaining human decision authority over high-stakes actions. The term was introduced by Binbeshr et al. [ 23 ] and is adopted here to frame the aspirational endpoint toward which the surveyed techniques collectively advance. We propose a nine-direction research agenda ordered by estimated impact-to-effort ratio. Figure 5 provides a horizon-shaded reading view; Supplementary Table S3 gives the full traceability between each R 𝑖 , the open-challenge clusters (C1–C6, Section 10) it targets, the evidence gap (Section 9) it would close, and an operational measurement plan. 

R1: Federated SOC Learning Networks. Establish privacy-preserving federated learning networks across consenting SOCs so alert patterns are shared without raw-data egress. Open problems are non-IID alert distributions, incentive mechanisms, and data-sharing governance; even 10–20-organization networks could yield training data an order of magnitude larger than any single SOC. 

R2: Security Foundation Models. Pre-train foundation models on curated security corpora (logs, alerts, threat reports, ATT&CK, CVE, STIX/TAXII, SOC playbooks) to provide stronger priors than web-text LLMs, with open releases that democratize access for smaller SOCs. 

R3: Production-Representative Benchmark Infrastructure. Build (a) privacy-preserving frameworks for anonymized analyst-labeled alert corpora, (b) high-fidelity SOC simulators with analyst-behavior and attack-scenario libraries, and (c) community evaluation harnesses with shared metrics, baselines, and reporting requirements.

12 Threats to Validity 

We follow the Wohlin et al. classification of validity threats, adapted for literature survey methodology, and disclose five threats spanning four Wohlin classes (internal, external, construct, conclusion).

Selection bias (internal). Despite a systematic PRISMA-aligned protocol, vocabulary mismatch may have left relevant studies unretrieved. Snowballing contributed 27 records in two complementary passes: 20 records via backward citation chaining of the eligible set (including the foundational SLEUTH and ATLAS works) and 7 additional in-scope studies via a bounded bibliography audit of the two directly competing surveys [ 65 , 142 ]; both are reported as one “snowball” line in the PRISMA pipeline (Table 1, Figure 1). Non-English studies are excluded; some Japanese, Chinese, and European contributions would likely be under-retrieved without domain-informed targeted search. Attribution errors and duplicates were reconciled, unverifiable records removed, and final counts aligned with the unified 2015–2026 corpus. 

Inclusion subjectivity (internal). The eligibility criterion “empirical evaluation with reported quantitative results” requires borderline judgment. Two raters independently coded a stratified random subset of full-text decisions, obtaining Cohen’s 𝜅 = 0.82 ; disagreements were resolved with a third author under a pre-specified rubric whose primary rule excluded papers primarily contributing to upstream IDS accuracy. Evidence-tier and A2C synthesis assignments are rubric-guided author observations and are not accompanied by a separate inter-rater statistic; counts derived from them should be read accordingly. Borderline cases include DeepCASE [ 34 ] (Cat I/T1a: semi-supervised workload reduction de-spite correlation-like clustering), AACT [ 144 ] (post-lock corroborating evidence, citation threshold unmet at screening), and L2DHF [ 66 ] (Cat II/T3: analyst feedback simulated on UNSW-NB15/CICIDS2017). Production generalizability and temporal validity (external). Most studies evaluate on benchmarks (CICIDS, DARPA TC, CERT) that do not capture full SOC complexity (Section 8); reported quantitative results should be treated as upper bounds. The few production-facing deployments we cite, such as Microsoft Security Copilot [ 35 ], report more modest gains. The 2015–2026 search window also makes specific LLM comparisons (Section 6) a snapshot of a fast-moving sub-field. 

Metric heterogeneity and scope boundary (construct). Per-paper tables list each paper’s primary metric without attempting normalized cross-paper comparison; the metric set proposed earlier targets this gap in future work. The upstream/downstream boundary (Section 2) is enforced consistently: five studies whose primary contribution is upstream detection (transfer learning, federated IDS, adversarial-ML-for-IDS) and two seminal works predating the 2015–2026 window are retained as non-core contextual references, which may undercount contributions in the overlap zone. 

Publication bias (temporal validity). The corpus contains very few well-documented negative or null results. Most published studies report headline gains over baselines, and reproductions or non-reproductions are seldom published. This is a known publication-bias hazard for systematic reviews; readers should treat reported metric gains as upper bounds. Open evaluation infrastructure (Research Direction R3) is the most direct mitigation.

14 Conclusion 

Security alert screening, the downstream task of filtering, prioritizing, correlating, and contextualizing alerts to surface those requiring analyst attention, remains the operational bottleneck in modern SOCs and is analytically distinct from upstream intrusion detection. The 2015–2026 systematic synthesis of 119 records (87 core) presented here organizes the field along a workflow-aligned four-stage taxonomy and shows that algorithmic progress consistently outpaces deployment evidence: encouraging gains in filtering, prioritization, provenance-based correlation, and LLM augmentation are dominated by benchmark results, production evaluations are scarce, reproducibility is limited, and heterogeneous datasets and metrics hinder comparison. Progress toward a Cognitive SOC depends on production-representative benchmark infrastructure, robustness and governance commensurate with security-critical decisions, privacy-preserving data-sharing across organizations, and interfaces that support analysts rather than bypass them.
