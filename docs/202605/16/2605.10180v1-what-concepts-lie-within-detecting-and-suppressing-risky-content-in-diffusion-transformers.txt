## Abstract 

The rise of text-to-image (T2I) models has increasingly raised con-cerns regarding the generation of risky content, such as sexual, violent, and copyright-protected images, highlighting the need for effective safeguards within the models themselves. Although exist-ing methods have been proposed to eliminate risky concepts from T2I models, they are primarily developed for earlier U-Net archi-tectures, leaving the state-of-the-art Diffusion-Transformer-based T2I models inadequately protected. This gap stems from a funda-mental architectural shift: Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) entangle semantic injection and visual synthesis via joint attention, which makes it difficult to isolate and erase risky content within the gen-eration. To bridge this gap, we investigate how semantic concepts are represented in DiTs and discover that attention heads exhibit concept-specific sensitivity. This property enables both the detec-tion and suppression of risky content. Building on this discovery, we propose AHV-D&S, a training-free inference-time safeguard for image generation in DiTs. Specifically, AHV-D&S quantifies each textual token’s sensitivity across all attention heads as an Attention Head Vector (AHV), which serves as a discriminative signature for detecting risky generation tendencies. In the inference stage, we pro-pose a momentum-based strategy to dynamically track token-wise AHVs across denoising steps, and a sensitivity-guided adaptive sup-pression strategy that suppresses the attention weights of identified risky tokens based on head-specific risk scores. Extensive experi-ments demonstrate that AHV-D&S effectively suppresses sexual, copyrighted-style, and various harmful content while preserving visual quality, and further exhibits strong robustness against adver-sarial prompts and transferability across different DiT-based T2I models. These results establish AHV-D&S as a practical safeguard for the safe deployment of modern T2I models.

Second, traditional denoising-based methods are primarily designed for U-Net-based T2I models and struggle to transfer to DiT-based T2I models . Specifically, as shown in Fig. 2(a) and (b), these methods can be further divided into two categories: erasing risky text rep-resentations in cross-attention [ 6 , 12 , 28 ], and erasing risky image representations in self-attention [ 25 , 27 ]. These strategies are effec-tive because U-Net-based T2I models adopt a relatively decoupled design, where cross-attention is used to inject text semantics, while self-attention mainly improves visual quality. However, as shown in Fig. 2(c), recent DiT-based T2I models abandon this decoupled architecture and instead adopt a joint attention mechanism that simultaneously handles text semantic injection and image genera-tion. Although joint attention improves image generation through complex interactions between the text and image, it also makes it more difficult to disentangle risky text and image representations, thereby reducing the effectiveness of traditional erasure methods. Therefore, these challenges highlight the critical need for a robust concept erasure strategy in DiT-based T2I models. 

To address this issue, we present a comprehensive study with three progressive stages. S1: We perform an analysis to understand how DiTs represent risky concepts (Sec. 3). S2: Based on above insights, we propose AHV-D&S, a training-free method that dynam-ically detects and suppresses risky representations in DiTs (Sec. 4). 

S3: We conduct extensive evaluations to demonstrate the effec-tiveness, robustness, and transferability of our method (Sec. 5 and Sec. 6). The details of each stage are presented as follows. 

S1: An Analysis of Concept Representation in DiTs. Un-derstanding how DiTs internally represent a concept is crucial for designing effective concept erasure methods. In particular, the model’s attention mechanisms provide a natural window into con-cept representation: the activation weight in attention heads asso-ciated with specific tokens reflects the semantic influence of those tokens on the generated image [ 17 , 18 , 29 , 30 ]. Therefore, we con-duct an investigation of the correlation between attention head maps and concept tokens. First, to study the relation between dif-ferent attention heads and specific concepts, we define a variable termed Attention Head Sensitivity , which is computed by aggre-gating the activation weights from image tokens to given concept tokens within an attention head map. Our experiments show that attention heads exhibit varying degrees of sensitivity to specific concept tokens (Fig. 3). Moreover, we observe that suppressing only a sparse subset of highly sensitive heads is already sufficient to alter the final visual outcome (Fig. 5). Second, we further extend this observation from a single concept to multiple concepts and define a vector called the Attention Head Vector (AHV) . Specifically, this vector is constructed by concatenating the sensitivities of all attention heads to given textual concepts into a one-dimensional representation. Intuitively, it can be understood as a trajectory that characterizes how the DiTs attend to concept tokens during the forward inference process. Our analysis reveals that AHV provides a discriminative and quantifiable signature of token-level genera-tive semantics (Sec. 3.3). It not only separates benign concepts with distinct visual semantics but also distinguishes risky concepts from safe ones, enabling us to estimate the model’s generative inclination toward risky content. 

S2: A Method for Risky Content Detection and Suppres-sion. Based on the above empirical observations, we propose AHV-D&S, a training-free method that enables safe image generation by dynamically detecting the AHV of input tokens for risk iden-tification, and suppressing the attention weights associated with risky tokens. Specifically, the proposed method consists of two modules: momentum-based AHV measurement and sensitivity-guided adaptive suppression. The first module aims to estimate the token-wise AHVs in real-time during the inference stage. Since T2I models require multiple denoising steps to generate a single image, the AHV of each token evolves dynamically throughout the de-noising process. To capture this behavior, we design a momentum update strategy that measures token-wise AHVs through global AHV initialization together with local momentum updates. The second module aims to detect the risk of token-wise AHVs and then suppress the attention weights associated with risky tokens. given that AHVs themselves exhibit strong distinctions between risk con-cepts and safe ones, we measure the risk score of an input token by comparing its similarity to risky AHVs and safe AHVs. ## 2 Background 2.1 Text-to-Image Models 

Existing T2I models mainly use a diffusion denoising network to iteratively denoise Gaussian noise into structured visual samples. Early T2I diffusion models are mainly built upon the U-Net ar-chitecture [ 9 , 33 ], where the denoising network processes latent image features through a multi-scale convolutional backbone. A key characteristic of U-Net-based models is the decoupled inter-action between text and image representations: textual conditions are injected into image features through cross-attention, while self-attention and convolutional blocks are mainly responsible for modeling spatial dependencies and refining visual details. This rela-tively modular design provides clear functional separation between semantic control and visual generation, which also makes U-Net amenable to targeted editing and safety intervention. Recent advances have shifted from U-Net-based architectures to Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) [ 2, 5, 10 ], which replace convolution-dominated backbones with Diffusion Transformer blocks for de-noising. Instead of maintaining a separate cross-attention module for text injection, many recent DiT-based T2I models adopt a more unified token interaction mechanism, where text and image tokens are processed jointly within the same attention layers. This design improves the flexibility and scalability of representation learning, and has shown strong performance in generation quality, seman-tic alignment, and model scaling. At the same time, however, it also entangles semantic injection and visual synthesis more tightly, reducing the effectiveness of U-Net-based methods. This transition from U-Net to DiT is not merely a change in back-bone design, but also a shift in how textual semantics are introduced and propagated during generation. As a result, many techniques de-veloped for U-Net-based T2I models, especially those relying on the explicit separation between cross-attention and visual denoising, cannot be effectively transferred to DiT-based models. 

## 2.2 Concept Erasure 

The rapid development of T2I models has facilitated access to high-quality visual generation, yet also introduced potential risks of synthesizing risky content. Concept erasure aims to mitigate these risks by selectively eliminating specific risky concepts (e.g., NSFW or copyright) from the model, while preserving the generation capability for unrelated concepts. Early works primarily target the U-Net architecture, exploiting its decoupled cross-attention or self-attention mechanism for con-cept erasure. This involves modifying the attention matrices to redirect risky semantics to safe ones via model editing [ 13 , 15 , 24 ]or fine-tuning strategies [ 6, 28 ]. In parallel, other approaches em-ploy erasure objectives for diffusion models, such as correcting noise predictions [ 11 , 23 ], steering denoising trajectory [ 22 , 34 ], aligning model preferences [27, 31], or suppressing specific visual semantics from collected samples [25]. However, transferring these methods to Diffusion Transform-ers (DiTs) faces significant obstacles. First, the architectural shift from U-Net to DiT changes the internal interaction from cross-attention to joint attention, rendering previous concept-redirect approaches ineffective. Second, the substantial increase in parame-ter scale makes fine-tuning computationally expensive and hard to design erasure objectives. Which causes erasure attempts on DiT are limited. While Safree [ 41 ] presents an early training-free at-tempt by filtering concepts in text embedding, which can be adapted to DiT models, it faces the robustness challenge against the adver-sarial prompts due to the inherent drawback in the text encoder. More recently, EA [ 14 ] pioneered concept erasure for DiT models. Motivated by the failure of U-Net methods on flow architectures, it formulates a bi-level optimization problem using LoRA and self-contrastive learning to enable robust erasure, but it still performs poorly in erasure capabilities and excessive training time. ## 2.3 Threat Model 

Our threat model involves two parties: an adversary and a model governor. The adversary aims to induce a T2I model to gener-ate risky content, including sexual content, harmful content, and copyrighted-protected artistic styles. We assume the adversary can submit textual prompts to the model and observe the generated outputs. The prompts may be naive risky prompts, manually crafted adversarial prompts, or prompts generated by red-teaming meth-ods. Following prior works [ 25 , 32 ], the adversary can query and interact with the deployed model, but cannot modify the model parameters, tamper with the defense mechanism, or compromise the runtime environment. The model governor is responsible for safely deploying the T2I model. The governor has access to the inference pipeline and can apply defense operations during generation. In our setting, the gov-ernor deploys AHV-D&S to estimate token-level risk directions and suppress attention weights associated with risky tokens at inference time. The governor has two objectives: preventing risky content generation under both naive and adversarial prompts, and preserv-ing benign generation utility in terms of text-image alignment, visual fidelity, and perceptual consistency.

## 4.1 Momentum-based AHV Measurement 

T2I models typically require multiple denoising steps to generate an image, which implies that token AHVs vary dynamically through-out the inference process. Specifically, given a text prompt 𝑐 with a token sequence length of 𝑙 𝑐 , we first define a global AHV memory of all tokens, denoted as V𝑔𝑙𝑜 ∈ R𝑙 𝑐 × ( 𝐿 ·𝐻 ) . ## 5 Experiment Setup 

In this section, we outline our implementation, datasets, baselines, and metrics used to evaluate AHV-D&S’s performance. 

Implementation . We use FLUX.1-dev [ 2] as our target model (57 transformer blocks, 24 heads each) with 28 denoising steps. The momentum coefficient 𝛾 is set to 0.2 and suppression intensity 𝛼 

to 50. For the AHV library (Eq. 10), we use 100 ImageNet-Mini objects as safe concepts. The risk library is task-specific: a sexual AHV prototype for sexual suppression, and four prototypes (blood, horror, weapon, drug) for harmful suppression. 

Baselines . We evaluate five baselines: ESD [ 11 ], UCE [ 12 ], Safree [ 41 ], EA [ 14 ], and CASG [ 37 ]. Safree and UCE are encoder-based; ESD, EA, and CASG operate during denoising. Following official EA setup, we implement EA and ESD by fine-tuning the KEY projection in joint-attention layers. CASG redirects image latent representa-tions toward safer distributional space at inference time. Except for EA, all baselines were originally designed for U-Net and are adapted by us for DiT. 

Datasets . We use four risky datasets to evaluate the method’s performance in suppressing risky content generation: Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY Trovato et al. 

• Sexual Prompt Dataset. We use 100 explicitly sexual prompts [ 42 ]to assess defense performance against sexual content generation. 

• Copyrighted Style Dataset. Following MACE [ 28 ], we use 100 prompts associated with copyrighted artistic styles, covering five artists: Pablo Picasso, Vincent van Gogh, Rembrandt, Andy Warhol, and Caravaggio. 

• Harmful Prompt Dataset. We follow T2I-RiskyPrompt [ 44 ] and select 1,000 harmful prompts spanning the categories of blood, horror, weapons, and drugs. 

• Adversarial Prompt Dataset. We adopt four sexual adversarial prompt sets: I2P [ 34 ] (931 manually crafted prompts), MMA [ 40 ](1,000 prompts), UnlearnDiff [ 46 ] (142 prompts), and RAB [ 35 ] (80 prompts), covering both manual and automated attack methods. Moreover, we use 1,000 prompts from the MSCOCO [ 26 ] to evaluate the preservation ability on benign prompts. 

Evaluation Metrics . For risky prompts, we use Risk Rate (RR) =

• FID [20 ] measures the distributional difference between gener-ated images before and after applying the defense method. Lower values indicate better distributional consistency. 

• LPIPS [ 45 ] measures the perceptual difference between paired generated images before and after applying the defense method. Lower values indicate higher perceptual similarity. 

• CLIP Score [19 ] measures the semantic alignment between gener-ated images and the input prompt.

This is consistent with the qualitative results in Fig. 12: AHV-D&S largely preserves the visual style, layout, and low-level appearance of benign genera-tions across diverse cases, including the multi-object composition in Fig. 12(d) and the complex event scene in Fig. 12(f). By comparison, some baselines may still maintain plausible semantics but introduce noticeable visual distribution shifts, such as changing the compo-sition and viewpoint of the mirror-selfie case in Fig. 12(a), and altering the visual style of the animal-interaction case in Fig. 12(c). Finally, CLIP mainly captures coarse-grained text-image align-ment. Since AHV-D&S suppresses the attention activation of spe-cific risky tokens, it may also slightly reduce the contribution of some tokens that are weakly correlated with risky concepts. As a re-sult, AHV-D&S obtains a slightly lower CLIP Score than the highest-scoring baselines. This effect is more likely to appear in prompts related to potentially risky semantics, such as the human-centric prompts in Fig. 12(a) and Fig. 12(b). Nevertheless, the generated images remain semantically close to the input prompts and pre-serve high visual quality. Together with our near-best FID and best LPIPS scores, these results demonstrate that AHV-D&S achieves stronger benign utility preservation and a more favorable safety-utility trade-off than existing methods.

This section analyzes how suppression strength 𝛼 and adaptive modulation factor 𝛽 𝑖,𝑘 (Eq. 10) affect the safety-utility trade-off. 𝛼 

controls the global suppression of risky tokens, while 𝛽 𝑖,𝑘 adap-tively adjusts this suppression based on attention head sensitivity. Intuitively, 𝛼 dictates the overall suppression intensity, whereas 

𝛽 𝑖,𝑘 selectively relaxes or strengthens it across different heads. Fig. 14 reports the performance under different 𝛼 values with two settings of 𝛽 𝑖,𝑘 : a non-adaptive setting with 𝛽 𝑖,𝑘 = 1, and our adaptive modulation setting with 𝛽 𝑖,𝑘 = 1 − ReLU (ˆ𝑠 𝑖,𝑘 ). As shown in Fig. 14(a), increasing 𝛼 consistently reduces the risk ratio, demon-strating that stronger suppression leads to better safety protection. However, under the non-adaptive setting, the improvement is rela-tively limited: the RR only decreases from 77.5% at 𝛼 = 30 to 56.8% at 𝛼 = 300 . In contrast, when the adaptive modulation setting is enabled, the RR decreases more significantly from 63.1% to 29.7%. This indicates that our adaptive modulation strategy effectively identifies risk-sensitive attention heads and applies more targeted suppression, substantially improving the suppression effectiveness. The utility metrics in Fig. 14(b)-(d) further reveal the trade-off controlled by 𝛼 . As 𝛼 increases, CLIP gradually decreases, while FID and LPIPS increase, indicating that stronger suppression may introduce larger degradation in benign generation. This trend is ex-pected, since overly strong intervention can suppress not only risky semantics but also weaken benign visual-textual responses that are weakly correlated with risky concepts. Nevertheless, the adaptive modulation strategy still achieves a more favorable safety-utility balance. For example, at 𝛼 = 50 , AHV-D&S with adaptive modula-tion strategy reduces the RR to 45.9%, which is substantially lower than EA’s 61.3%, while maintaining competitive benign generation quality with a CLIP Score of 30.84, FID of 22.16, and LPIPS of 0.07. In particular, the LPIPS score remains far lower than that of EA, suggesting that our method introduces much smaller perceptual changes to benign generations. Overall, these results show that 𝛼 acts as a controllable knob for adjusting the strength of safety intervention, while 𝛽 𝑖,𝑘 improves the efficiency and selectivity of the intervention. ## 7 Discussion 

Evaluation Metrics and Safety Definitions. Our evaluation relies on open-source classifiers and proxy metrics, which may introduce measurement bias. For example, we use NudeNet to quantify sexual content generation for fair comparison with baselines. However, sexual explicitness is inherently context-dependent and subjective, and some NudeNet categories, such as feet or buttocks, may not al-ways be considered sexually explicit across different social, cultural, or application contexts. Therefore, our reported risk ratios should be interpreted as detector-based proxies rather than absolute defi-nitions of unsafe content. To improve transparency, we report not only the overall risk ratio but also fine-grained category-specific results, allowing readers to inspect which types of content are sup-pressed. Moreover, we also complement quantitative metrics with qualitative visualizations. 

Safety-Utility Trade-off and Deployment Choices. Although AHV-D&S achieves strong suppression performance across sexual, harm-ful, and copyrighted-protected style generation, it may still in-troduce side effects on benign generation, as reflected by slight deviations in CLIP, FID, and LPIPS compared with the base model. Such degradation is an inherent trade-off in safety intervention: stronger suppression can reduce risky content generation but may also affect benign visual-textual responses that are weakly corre-lated with risky semantics. We mitigate this issue in two ways. First, our experiments (Tab. 1, Tab. 2, and Tab. 3) show that AHV-D&S achieves a more favorable safety-utility balance than existing baselines, substantially reducing risk while preserving competitive benign generation quality. Second, our hyper-parameter analysis shows that the suppression strength 𝛼 provides a controllable knob for deployment. In practice, model providers can adjust 𝛼 according to their risk tolerance and application scenario, choosing a stricter setting for high-risk platforms or a more conservative setting when benign utility is prioritized. 

Inference Overhead. We measure inference overhead on the same NVIDIA A800 GPU. As shown in Tab. 6, parameter-edit-based methods such as UCE, EA, and ESD incur almost no additional inference cost because their modifications are absorbed into model parameters before inference. In contrast, AHV-D&S provides dy-namic, inference-time protection against risky content, taking 12.9s per image and slightly higher than Safree but much faster than CASG (47.8s, 8.24 ×). The overhead mainly comes from our current custom attention implementation based on torch.matmul , which disables FlashAttention-style fused kernels to allow access and modification of attention weights. We expect that a fused imple-mentation could further reduce this cost. 

## 8 Conclusion 

In this paper, we presented AHV-D&S, a training-free defense for safe DiT-based T2I generation. Our key insight is that attention head vector offers a measurable way to characterize token-level generative semantics, making it possible to distinguish risky to-ken responses from benign ones during inference. Based on this insight, AHV-D&S dynamically measures token-wise AHVs and suppresses attention weights associated with risky tokens through sensitivity-guided adaptive modulation. This design avoids costly model retraining and supports flexible suppression of different risk concepts in the inference stage. Extensive experiments demonstrate that AHV-D&S achieves strong safety protection while preserving benign generation utility. Across sexual content, copyrighted-protected styles, and harmful content suppression, AHV-D&S consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines in safety-utility trade-off. It also shows robustness against diverse adversarial prompt benchmarks and transfers effec-tively to Qwen-Image, indicating that AHV-D&S is not limited to a single T2I backbone. We further show that the suppression strength provides a practical knob for deployment, allowing model providers to adjust the balance between safety protection and benign util-ity preservation. We hope our findings on AHV and the proposed AHV-D&S method can provide useful insights for building safer and more controllable generative models. What Concepts Lie Within?
