## Abstract 

Proprietary large language models (LLMs) face risks of intellectual property (IP) violation, as adversaries can replicate an LLM by collecting input-output pairs to train a surrogate model, causing financial setbacks. Watermarks offer a promising defense to verify ownership, but existing methods often struggle with semantic dis-tortion, factual inconsistency, and adversarial attacks. In addition, key-conditioned watermarks for provider-specific detection, especially in cross-provider and multi-user scenarios, remain largely underexplored. To address these challenges, we pro-pose S AFE SEAL , a novel key-conditioned watermarking framework that achieves strong detectability with minimal impact on model utility, effectively balancing detectability, utility, and robustness. S AFE SEAL preserves named entities while sub-stituting linguistic terms with context-aware synonyms through a key-conditioned Tournament sampling mechanism, maintaining semantic fidelity and factual con-sistency. For detection, we introduce a key-conditioned contrastive detector that jointly encodes the text and key, enabling provider-specific and robust watermark verification. We derive theoretical bounds on the utility-detectability trade-off and significantly reduce latency through lightweight models, batching, and parallelism. Extensive experiments show that S AFE SEAL outperforms baselines in utility, de-tectability, and robustness, achieving a BERTScore of 0.983 , entity similarity of 

0.963 , a 98 .2% detection rate, and the highest human ratings for text quality and content preservation, with latency comparable to the fastest baseline. To promote transparency and community-driven progress, we release the first public watermark leaderboard and an interactive demo. 

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs), such as ChatGPT [ 68 ], DeepSeek [ 57 ], and Gemini [ 26 ], have shown powerful capabilities in text generation, translation, and comprehension. Although LLMs are often deployed as services via APIs that limit access to model weights [ 2, 7], they remain vulnerable to model stealing attacks [ 106 , 104 , 99 , 46 , 44 ], where adversaries fine-tune surrogate models on proprietary outputs, threatening providers’ intellectual property (IP) [ 53 ]. LLM watermarks [ 45 , 102 , 55 , 98 , 41 , 76 , 13 , 78 , 111 , 12 , 23 ] address the risk by embedding imperceptible patterns into LLM outputs, enabling service providers to detect unauthorized use of their LLM-generated text. However, existing LLM watermarks [ 103 , 50 , 62 ] often cause substantial content deviation and utility loss to gain strong detectability, particularly under attacks [ 64 , 79 , 74 , 71 , 1]. An effective watermark should preserve semantics and factual consistency, offer high detectability, resist attacks, and maintain low latency. However, these goals are difficult to balance: weak signals reduce detectability, while strong signals degrade utility and may increase computational overhead. Watermark removal and

Contributions. This paper proposes S AFE SEAL , a novel key-conditioned LLM watermark that preserves utility while ensuring strong and robust detectability with provable guarantees. A key insight is that replacing words with context-aware synonyms can preserve meaning. S AFE SEAL 

identifies entities (e.g., times and names) and linguistic terms (e.g., nouns and verbs), preserving entities while replacing linguistic terms with context-aware synonyms. It builds a synonym lookup table using a lightweight LM and selects substitution via key-conditioned Tournament sampling, minimizing distortion and making watermark removal difficult without degrading text quality. For detection, we introduce a key-conditioned contrastive detector that verifies whether text is watermarked under a specific key. It fuses text and key embeddings and is trained on matched and mismatched text-key pairs using classification and contrastive losses. This design enables robust, provider-specific detection of subtle substitutions while resisting removal and model stealing attacks. Keys are tied to users and providers, ensuring only authorized verification and reducing risks of unauthorized detection or reverse-engineering. We further establish a theoretical utility-detectability trade-off, showing that lower synonym similarity improves detectability but reduces utility. Experiments on five open-source models [ 88 , 42 , 4 , 86 , 85 ] and three benchmarks [ 20 , 33 , 11 ]show that S AFE SEAL preserves 98 .3% content similarity and 96 .3% entity similarity, with detection rates up to 98 .2% . It outperforms state-of-the-art watermarks (KGW [ 45 ], EXP [ 48 ], SIR [ 56 ], SynthID [ 18 ], DTM [ 65 ], TW [ 101 ], and LW [ 31 ]), achieving a 58 .47% higher detection rate than the best baseline under removal attacks. Under model stealing attacks, S AFE SEAL maintains a 72 .8% 

detection rate while forcing noticeable utility degradation, limiting watermark removal without quality loss. Human evaluations further favor S AFE SEAL by 28 × without attacks and 6.5× under attacks. Latency is comparable to the best baseline. We also anonymously release the first Watermark Leaderboard 1 and a real-time demo 2 for transparency and standardized evaluation.

## 2 Background and Related Work 

LLMs and Watermarking. Given a token vocabulary V and prompt x ∈ V T , an LLM θ generates 

y ∈ V Q by modeling P (y|x) (T, Q > 0). A watermark embeds imperceptible signals in LLM outputs to verify origin via a generator W producing ywm = θ(x, W), and a detector D that identifies them. Watermarked text should preserve semantic meaning , i.e., contextual coherence and intent [ 19 ], and 

factual consistency with the original facts and entities [67], since distortions in either reduce utility. 

Linguistic Sensitivity. Certain tokens are more sensitive to perturbations. Named Entity Recognition (NER) identifies entities (e.g., names, locations, time expressions) that are critical for factual correct-ness, while Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging captures grammatical roles (e.g., nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs). Modifying such tokens can disproportionately harm meaning and downstream performance. 

Detection Settings. We consider cross-provider detection , where different providers use distinct keys, and multi-user detection , where a single provider assigns unique keys per user. In both cases, the detector must correctly attribute the source of watermarked text. 

LLM Watermarking Techniques. Existing LLM watermarks can fall into 1) in-processing methods, which embed signals during decoding by modifying logits, token distributions, or training [ 45 , 110 ,58 ], and 2) post-processing methods, which alter generated text through synonym substitution, rule-based replacement, or paraphrasing [ 18 , 24 , 35 ]. Although effective, both categories may degrade utility by altering semantic meaning or factual consistency, especially when entities are modified. Prior work often defines “distortion-free” watermarking as preserving the next-token distribution [ 18 ,48 , 36 , 15 ], but distributional preservation does not guarantee semantic or factual consistency. For example, EXP [ 48 ] and SynthID [ 18 ] can still introduce entity shifts or fabricated details due to cascading effects in autoregressive generation (Table 6, Appx.A). Similarly, utility-detectability trade-offs are often studied through proxy metrics such as sampling strength, entropy, or Pareto

## 3 Threat Models 

Setting. This work considers a service provider offering a proprietary LLM θ via an API. Users submit a prompt x and receive an output y (or ywm if watermarked). In this work, IP protection refers to preventing unauthorized use or stealing attacks via exploiting the outputs generated by θ.

Adversary’s Capabilities. We employ a practical black-box setting where the adversary can query the API but has no access to the proprietary LLM’s internals, preventing leakage via the outputs y.

Adversary’s Goals. We consider two common attacks: watermark removal , where an adversary uses paraphrasing, insertion, or synonym substitution [ 105 , 54 ] to produce an watermark-removed output 

¯y that bypasses detection; and model stealing , where the adversary trains a surrogate model θadv to imitate θ [8, 96, 43, 72, 90]. These attacks threaten IP protection, traceability, and service integrity. 

## 4 SAFE SEAL LLM Watermarking 

This work aims to develop an effective and practical watermark that minimizes utility impacts via minimal semantic distortion, achieves high detectability, resists removal and model stealing attacks, incurs low latency, and provides provable utility-detectability guarantees. To this end, we ask: Which tokens can be substituted to embed a robust watermark without changing the original meaning? 

Our solution includes: 1) Preserving entities while replacing linguistic terms with key-conditioned, context-aware synonyms via a lookup table and Tournament sampling ( Section 4.1 ). These steps preserve semantic meaning and factual consistency while enabling provider-specific detection; 2) Developing a key-conditioned, contrastive detector to improve robustness ( Section 4.2 ); 3) Bounding output deviation via token-level substitutions to control the utility-detectability trade-off ( Section 4.3 ); and 4) Reducing latency via parallelization, batching, caching, and employing lightweight models (Section B.3 ). 4.1 Key-Conditioned Watermark Generation Watermarkable Token Identification. Given an output y, we first use NER [ 34 ] to identify entities 

e ∈ E and exclude them from watermarking to preserve semantic and factual consistency. We then apply POS tagging [ 73 , 60 ] to identify linguistic terms critical to text meaning and structure, selecting 

nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs as the watermarkable token set T wm . These POS types provide more flexibility and broader synonym substitution than entities or other linguistic categories. Table 7 (Appx. B.1) summarizes the POS types used in S AFE SEAL .

Lookup Table Generation. Next, we build a lookup table L of context-aware synonyms. For each ti ∈ T wm , Lti stores substitution candidates derived from the overlapping context window 

C = {t1, . . . , t i, . . . , t N } containing ti3. In our experiments, C spans about 200 tokens. Candidates are generated and selected to preserve the meaning and grammatical form of ti in context, as follows. 

Substitution Candidate Generation. We use a lightweight LM A to generate context-aware synonyms for each ti. We mask ti in its context window C to obtain CM , then feed C ⊕ [SEP ] ⊕ CM with an instruction to produce a ranked candidate list Lti . We further filter candidates by removing duplicates, antonyms, incomplete terms, and non-WordNet entries [ 63 ], while enforcing POS consistency with NLTK [60] and contextual fit (e.g., replacing "an apple" with "a fruit" rather than "an fruit"). 

Substitution Candidate Selection. To preserve semantics, we keep candidates whose sentence-level similarity Sij between the original sentence Sti and substituted sentence Stj (where ti is substituted by tj ) exceeds threshold δ. Tokens with fewer than two valid candidates are skipped. Formally, for

4.2 Key-Conditioned Watermark Detection 

Unlike rule-based or statistical detectors [ 45 , 56 , 31 ] that are vulnerable to paraphrasing and edits, or model-based detectors [ 100 , 39 , 17 , 65 ] that improve robustness but lack provider-specific detection, we formulate detection as key-conditioned binary classification. Given text y and key k, the detector 

D predicts whether y is watermarked under k. Positive pairs contain text watermarked with k, while negative pairs are non-watermarked text or text watermarked with another key k′̸ = k.We extract detector features by fusing text, key, and pairwise interaction embeddings. Text y is encoded with a pretrained bidirectional transformer fenc (e.g., RoBERTa [ 59 ]) and mean pooling to obtain hy ∈ RH . For key k, we avoid trainable embeddings to reduce memorization and leakage [83 , 91 ] by using a PRF that maps k to v ∈ [0 , 1] H , which is rescaled to ˜v = 2( v − 0.5) ∈ [−1, 1] H

and passed through a frozen MLP fM LP to obtain hk = fM LP (˜ v). We obtain the element-wise Hadamard product hy ⊙ hk and the absolute difference |hy − hk|. We form the fused representation: 

ϕ = [ hy ; hk ; hy ⊙ hk ; |hy − hk |] ∈ R4H . (1)

We study the following questions on LLaMA-2. More results on Mistral and others are in Appx. D. 

Q1. What is the trade-off between utility and detectability of S AFE SEAL and watermarks? 

Fig. 3(a) compares watermark performance and shows that S AFE SEAL achieves the best utility-detectability balance. It attains the highest BERTScore ( 0.978 ), while others range from 0.858 (EXP) to 0.966 (DTM). Its detection rate ( 98 .2% ) is competitive, second only to TW ( 99 .8% ), though TW sacrifices utility ( 0.951 BERTScore). DTM also shows lower detectability ( 92 .3% ). Results on Mistral are consistent, and ROC curves further confirm near-perfect separability for S AFE SEAL . We attribute S AFE SEAL ’s strong utility and detectability to four factors: 1) excluding entities and using context-aware synonyms to reduce semantic drift, 2) key-conditioned sampling for provider/user-specific signals, 3) contrastive learning with text-key feature fusion, and 4) a non-intrusive design that independently watermarks tokens without altering generation, preventing cascade effects (Appx. D).

On the MMLU, S AFE SEAL preserves the original model’s accuracy (Table 8), thanks to its token-filtering watermarking step that avoids altering label tokens. Other methods reduce accuracy by 1.7% - 40 .2%

96 Conclusion and Future Work 

This work presents S AFE SEAL , a key-conditioned LLM watermark that balances detectability and utility with provable guarantees. By preserving entities and replacing linguistic terms with context-aware synonyms, S AFE SEAL maintains semantics and factual consistency. Key-conditioned Tournament sampling produces reproducible provider-specific signals, while a contrastive detector improves robustness across LLMs and users without retraining. S AFE SEAL also improves efficiency through lightweight components, caching, batching, and framework consolidation. We further release the first public watermark leaderboard and an interactive demo for standardized evaluation. 14 A Background and Related Work 

A.1 Preliminaries and Key Definitions Large Language Models (LLMs). Given a vocabulary V of words or word fragments, known as 

tokens , a sequence x = {x(i)}Ti=1 ∈ V T is a prompt of T tokens, and y = {y(i)}T +Qi=T +1 ∈ V Q is the output of Q tokens ( T, Q > 0). An LLM θ is trained to maximize a conditional probability P (y|x) = ΠTi=1 P (y(i)|x(1) , · · · , x (i−1) ), capturing long-range contextual dependencies for generation. 

Semantic Meaning and Factual Consistency. Semantic meaning captures contextual coherence and intended meaning [ 19 ], while factual consistency requires that watermarked text remain faithful to the facts and entities in the original content [ 67 ]. Distortions occur when substitutions alter either meaning or facts, making both essential for high-quality watermarking. 

Named Entity Recognition (NER) and Part-of-Speech (POS) Tagging. NER [ 66 ] identifies entities such as names, locations, and time expressions. Each entity e ∈ E is a word or a phrase treated as highly susceptible, as minor changes can alter semantic meaning, factual consistency, and compromise downstream tasks. POS tagging [ 87 ] assigns each word a grammatical category (e.g., nouns, verbs). Widely used NER and POS tagging tools are spaCy [ 34 ], Stanza [ 75 ], and NLTK [ 6 ]. 

Cross-Provider and Multi-User Detection. In practice, different providers or users may use distinct secret keys. We consider two settings: (1) cross-provider detection , where different LLM providers use different keys, and (2) multi-user detection , where one provider serves multiple users, each with a unique key. In both cases, the detector must identify the correct source of the watermarked text. 

Tournament Sampling [ 18 ]. This sampling uses cryptographically keyed pseudorandom scores over sliding contexts to select tokens, enabling scalable and low-latency generation. However, pseudorandom scoring can weaken semantic preservation and robustness, especially for short inputs. 

A.2 Watermarking Techniques in LLMs 

Watermarks embed imperceptible patterns in LLM outputs to verify origin and deter model stealing. They include 1) a generation function W producing a watermarked output ywm = θ(x, W), given the LLM θ and x, and 2) a detector D that identifies if a given text contains the watermark. 

Watermark Categories. Existing watermarks fall into (1) in-processing watermarks , which embed signals during generation by modifying logits, token distributions, or training [ 45 , 110 , 58 ]; and (2) post-processing watermarks , which modify generated text through synonym substitution, rule-based replacements, or paraphrasing [ 18 , 24 , 35 ]. Detection typically uses statistical tests or classifiers. However, both categories may distort semantic meaning or factual consistency by altering entities. Prior studies typically use secret keys to bias token sampling [ 45 , 48 , 37 , 15 , 101 , 94 ], or embed provider-specific triggers [ 97 , 107 , 32 ], but often assume single-key, attack-free settings with limited empirical detection analysis. In practice, cross-provider and multi-user scenarios require robust detection under unique private keys. 

Utility-Detectability Trade-off. Prior work studies this trade-off through watermark strength optimization [ 45 , 39 , 94 ], Pareto objectives [ 95 ], entropy-based hybrids and key randomization [38 , 93 ], and edit-robust guarantees [ 25 ]. However, these methods often rely on proxy metrics such as probability or entropy, which may miss semantic and factual distortions. Table 1 compares S AFE SEAL with related work. S AFE SEAL is a key-conditioned post-processing watermark that preserves entities and replaces linguistic terms with context-aware synonyms to maintain utility , defined as semantic meaning, factual consistency, and downstream utility. Its contrastive detector captures key-specific patterns for robust cross-provider and multi-user detection, while our evaluation covers utility, robustness, and the theoretical utility-detectability trade-off.

B.1 Linguistic Categories 

Table 7 presents a list of part-of-speech (POS) categories used in S AFE SEAL , focusing on nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs. We select these categories for their semantic flexibility, where synonym substitutions typically preserve overall meaning without compromising factual consistency. For instance, in the noun category, we include common singular and plural nouns, while excluding proper nouns, as replacing the proper nouns (e.g., “Google”) may change factual information. Similarly, we utilize different types of verbs, adjectives, and adverbs for watermarking, while excluding modal verbs, pronouns, determiners, numerals identified through POS tagging. By restricting substitutions to this selected set of POS categories, S AFE SEAL minimizes semantic distortion while preserving effectiveness.

## D Detailed Experimental Settings and Additional Results 

Our experiments were conducted on Python version 3.9.20, using NVIDIA A100 GPU with PyTorch (torch 2.5.1) and CUDA 12. 

D.1 Task Setup 

For text generation , we use 20 , 000 training and 1, 000 test samples, truncating each to 200 prompt tokens and generating up to 200 tokens [ 45 , 48 ]. We use spaCy [ 34 ] for NER and NLTK [ 6 ] for POS tagging. For lookup tables, RoBERTa-base (125M) [ 59 ] generates candidates and DistilBERT (67M) [81] computes similarity with δ = 0 .92 . Sampling uses α = 1 .0, c = 2 , h = 6 , and m = 10 .

For MMLU downstream task , we evaluate multiple-choice question-answering across 57 subjects. 

For text summarization , we test 1, 000 samples with outputs averaging 60 words. 

For detection , we train a RoBERTa-large model [ 59 ] (356M) with batch size 16 , 15 epochs, learning rate 5e−6, and contrastive weight λ = 0 .15 on 20 , 000 positive and 20 , 000 negative pairs. 

For watermark removal attack , we evaluate performance on 1, 000 test samples. 

For the model stealing attack , we adopt a task-specific threat model in which the adversary targets a text summarization service, a realistic scenario reflecting practical API abuse. The adversary queries 

20 , 000 prompts to collect S AFE SEAL -watermarked summaries and fine-tunes a surrogate model on the collected input-output pairs. We then evaluate the surrogate on 1, 000 held-out prompts against both watermarked and clean outputs to assess detectability and utility degradation. 

For real-world settings , we test 1) cross-provider detection with five LLMs using distinct keys, and 2) multi-user detection with one LLaMA-2 model serving five users with separate keys. 

For latency , we report the average latency in seconds (s) for 1, 000 generations of 200 -token outputs with LLaMA-2.

D.5 Cascade Effect in Watermarking 

The cascade effect refers to a phenomenon where modifying a single token in LLM’s generation phase can influence the prediction of subsequent tokens. Since LLMs generate text auto-regressively, each token is predicted based on the previous ones, thus even a small change can propagate through the sequence. When a watermark is injected in an intrusive manner, as in the case of in-processing watermarking, it modifies the generation of subsequent tokens, causing the output to diverge from the original text. This effect becomes particularly significant when the number of generated tokens increases. In Fig. 8, as text length increases, S AFE SEAL gains more favorable votes as the best watermark from AMT workers. For instance, at a text length of approximately 63 words, S AFE SEAL was selected 23 

times, which increased to 26 selections when the length reached 158 words. D.10 Justification for Q7. Cross-provider Performance 

Table 5(a) shows that our detector trained on LLaMA-2 can generalize well on other LLMs like Mistral and DeepSeek. We attribute this generalization to two complementary factors. First, the contrastive detector is trained on matched and mismatched text-key pairs, so outputs from other providers, which carry no S AFE SEAL key-aligned substitution signal, are naturally classified as non-watermarked. Second, different LLMs exhibit distinct output distributions and generation styles, which further separates their outputs from S AFE SEAL -watermarked text and strengthens detection, rather than hindering it. 

D.11 Additional Experimental Questions Q10. How does the choice of watermark detector affect S AFE SEAL ’s detectability? We evaluate the impact on detectability using three LMs of varying sizes, including DeBERTa-large-v3 [30 ] (304M), BERT-large-uncase [ 19 ] (336M), and RoBERTa-large [ 59 ] (356M). Across the LMs, detectability remains high, with a detection rate exceeding 96% , with DeBERTa achieving 99 .5% 

despite its smallest size (Fig. 10(a)). Overall, our key-conditioned contrastive detector is robust to the choice of LM backbone, demonstrating strong generality and effectiveness. 

Q11. How does the similarity threshold δ affect the utility-detectability trade-off? Larger similarity thresholds δ preserve higher utility but reduce detectability (Fig. 10(b)). For instance, with δ = 0 .95 , the model maintains high utility (BERTScore = 0 .986 ) but obtains a lower detection rate of 95 .3% . Reducing δ to 0.92 slightly decreases utility (BERTScore = 0 .978 ) while yielding a notable gain in detectability, with the detection rate improving to 98 .2% . This trade-off is consistent across different LLMs and aligns with our theoretical analysis in Theorem 4.1. To better balance the utility-detectability trade-off, we set δ = 0 .92 in our experiments.
