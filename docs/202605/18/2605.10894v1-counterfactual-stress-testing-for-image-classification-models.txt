# Counterfactual Stress Testing for Image Classification Models 

Moritz Stammel, Fabio De Sousa Ribeiro, Raghav Mehta, Mélanie Roschewitz, and Ben Glocker 

Department of Computing, Imperial College London, UK 

b.glocker@imperial.ac.uk 

Abstract. Deep learning models in medical imaging often fail when de-ployed in new clinical environments due to distribution shifts in demo-graphics, scanner hardware, or acquisition protocols. A central challenge is underspecification, where models with similar validation performance exhibit divergent real-world failure modes. Although stress testing has emerged as a tool to assess this, current methods typically rely on simple, uninformed perturbations (e.g., brightness or contrast changes), which fail to capture clinically realistic variation and can overestimate robust-ness. In this work, we introduce a counterfactual stress testing frame-work based on causal generative models that create realistic “what if” images by intervening on attributes such as scanner type and patient sex while preserving anatomical identity, enabling controlled and seman-tically meaningful evaluation under targeted distribution shifts. Across two imaging modalities (chest X-ray and mammography), three model architectures, and multiple shift scenarios, we show that counterfactual stress tests provide a substantially more accurate proxy for real out-of-distribution performance than classical perturbations, capturing the direction and relative magnitude of performance changes as well as model ranking. These results suggest that causal generative models can serve as practical simulators for robustness assessment, offering a more reliable basis for evaluating medical AI systems prior to deployment.

counterfactual reasoning · stress testing 

1 Introduction 

Medical image classification models that perform well on in-distribution vali-dation sets often fail on new scanners or different demographic groups, raising serious robustness and fairness concerns [2,8,15]. It has been found that strong performance on in-distribution data can obscure significant disparities in specific subgroups [24,3], yet reliable methods to anticipate these failure modes prior to deployment are lacking. Before clinical adoption can be considered, several criti-cal questions must be addressed. For instance, will a model trained on data from scanner A maintain its performance on scanner B? Furthermore, will it remain reliable under varying image quality and acquisition protocols?

Unlike classical stress testing, which is limited to simple perturbations (e.g., contrast adjustment, rotation), counterfactual stress testing uses a causal generative model to simulate targeted attribute-level changes (e.g., scanner-induced appearance shifts) while preserving anatomical identity of the original patient. A key challenge is underspecification , where a model can learn multiple solu-tions that perform identically on in-distribution data but exhibit vastly different failure modes on unseen data [4]. While stress testing has emerged as a tool to assess this [8], current approaches typically rely on simple, uninformed per-turbations (e.g., changes in brightness, contrast, flips, and rotations) [11,25,13], which fail to capture realistic clinical distribution shifts [23,18]. Since many per-turbations mirror training augmentations, this approach can overestimate ro-bustness. Recent work has used generative models to simulate clinical variation. Pérez-García et al. [22] introduced RadEdit, a diffusion-based method apply-ing text-guided, localized edits for stress testing, but it does not generate full counterfactual twins via causal interventions. Ktena et al. [17] used a diffusion model to synthesize matched images for data augmentation to improve model robustness, but not for evaluation under controlled shifts. To bridge this gap, we propose a counterfactual stress testing framework (see Fig. 1). Our goal is to create a high-fidelity synthetic environment that simulates specific, realistic clinical distribution shifts, allowing us to probe model robust-ness and fairness. We use Deep Structural Causal Models (DSCMs) [21,20,6,5], to generate realistic, anatomically consistent counterfactual images, by interven-ing on causal parent variables (e.g., scanner type, patient sex) while preserving anatomical identity, allowing us to systematically assess model robustness and performance drift under these targeted shifts. 2 Methods 

2.1 Datasets PadChest. We utilize the PadChest dataset [1], a large public collection of over 160,000 chest X-rays (CXR). We focus on adult posteroanterior (PA) images for the binary classification of pneumonia. The dataset is partitioned by patient ID into training (80%), validation (10%), and test (10%) subsets. 

EMBED. To demonstrate that our approach generalizes across imaging modal-ities, we also employ 2D mammography images from the EMBED dataset [14]. The dataset contains over 290,000 images and is split by patient ID into training (75%), validation (5%), and test (20%) subsets. 

2.2 Counterfactual Generation Model 

To generate images for our stress tests, we train a Deep Structural Causal Model (DSCM) [6], implemented as a hierarchical β-Variational AutoEncoder (HVAE). 4 M. Stammel et al. 

The model is trained to learn a disentangled latent representation by maximizing the Evidence Lower Bound, composed of a reconstruction loss and a weighted KL-divergence regularizer. To encourage robust disentanglement of the parent attributes (scanner and sex), we use a linear warmup schedule for the KL weight, 

β. Training stability for this large generative model is maintained through gra-dient clipping and accumulation, and we use an Exponential Moving Average of the model weights to produce the final, high-fidelity counterfactual images. In a benchmark study [19], HVAE-based counterfactual generation was found to out-perform other counterfactual generation methods based on axiomatic soundness tests [20]. For the PadChest dataset, the model is conditioned on both biological sex and 

scanner type , while for the EMBED dataset, it is conditioned solely on scanner type . This conditioning enables the generation of counterfactual “twin” images ˆx

of a given source image x, differing only in the specified parent attribute while preserving underlying anatomy. Sample generated counterfactuals for the CXR (PadChest) and mammography (EMBED) datasets are shown in Fig. 2. 

2.3 Classifiers 

To evaluate the robustness of standard medical image classifiers under covari-ate shift, we assess three widely used architectures: ResNet-50 [10], DenseNet-121 [12], and Vision Transformer (ViT-B/16) [7]. On PadChest, models are trained for pneumonia detection, while on EMBED, the task is to predict breast density across four BI-RADS categories. Training uses the Adam optimizer [16] with a learning rate of 1 × 10 −4, early stopping based on validation loss, and commonly used data augmentations including geometric transformations, color jitter, blurring, normalization, and random erasing. 

2.4 Experimental Design 

For each dataset, we construct controlled training and evaluation splits to enable targeted assessment under distribution shifts. On PadChest, models are trained on subsets corresponding to a single condition, such as one scanner manufacturer (e.g., Philips) or one patient sex (e.g., Male). Evaluation is then performed on held-out subsets representing the opposite condition (e.g., IDC or Female) to simulate a real out-of-distribution (OOD) shift. On EMBED, models are trained exclusively on images acquired with the Selenia Dimensions scanner and evalu-ated on images from the Senograph 2000D , Lorad Selenia , and Clearview CSm 

scanners. The out of distribution tests sets are age and prevalence matched to isolate the effect of counterfactual interventions. We then assess the relationship between model performance on synthetic counterfactual images and real OOD data by comparing their respective per-formance gaps. Composite Shift. We extend our framework to simulate composite distribu-tion shifts by jointly intervening on scanner and sex, training on one subgroup (e.g., Male, Philips ) and evaluating on its complement (e.g., Female, IDC ). 3.2 Mammography 

We evaluate our framework on the EMBED dataset to assess generalization across imaging modalities, measuring performance as ∆AUC (Area Under Curve - ROC) relative to IID. As shown in Fig. 5, classical stress tests consistently un-derestimate the impact of scanner shifts, suggesting minimal degradation, while real OOD evaluation reveals substantial performance drops that vary across scan-ners. Counterfactual stress testing more closely aligns with real OOD behavior, correctly capturing the direction of performance changes across all scanners, al-though it underestimates their magnitude, particularly for Selenia 2000D . This is supported by correlation analysis, where counterfactual stress testing achieves a Pearson correlation of 0.69 ( p < 0.001 ) and a Kendall’s τ of 0.53 ( p < 0.001 ), sub-stantially outperforming classical methods, whose correlations remain weak and 8 M. 4 Discussion & Conclusion 

Interpretation of results. Counterfactual stress testing reveals failure modes that are often missed by classical perturbations such as brightness or contrast adjustments. By intervening on causal factors (scanner type and patient sex) while holding anatomical identity fixed, the DSCM generates semantically tar-geted shifts that better reflect clinically plausible variation. We use real OOD evaluation as an external reference, but the objective is not to exactly pre-dict deployment performance; rather, it is to provide a controlled proxy for ro-bustness under meaningful, attribute-specific distribution shifts. The consistent agreement in direction and relative magnitude between counterfactual and real performance shifts suggests that causally structured generative models can serve as practical simulators for pre-deployment robustness assessment. 

Clinical and methodological relevance. Unlike conventional perturbation-based tests, counterfactual stress testing operates on the manifold of plausible clinical variation. Generated images maintain anatomical identity, enabling ex-pert verification of plausibility and interpretation of model responses. Given our results, the approach shows strong potential for integration into robustness assessment pipelines prior to clinical deployment. It could also support scenario-based auditing and ongoing post-deployment monitoring, similar to stress testing in other safety-critical domains. By combining causal interpretability with quan-titative performance evaluation, the method aligns with emerging requirements for transparency, fairness, and auditability in medical AI regulation. 

Limitations and future work. Our framework presumes knowledge of the causal graph and identifiability of counterfactuals from observed data. These assumptions may not hold in practice, affecting the causal validity of estimated counterfactuals. Recent advances in deep causal inference, such as DECI [9], suggest promising directions for relaxing these requirements through data-driven structure learning. In future work, we plan to explore structure-learning DSCMs that can automatically infer causal dependencies, develop quantitative metrics for counterfactual realism, and incorporate human-in-the-loop evaluation by ra-diologists. Extending the framework to three-dimensional imaging and multi-attribute interventions would further enhance its applicability. 

Conclusion. We presented a causally grounded framework for evaluating model robustness in medical imaging. Using counterfactual image synthesis, we showed that model performance under controlled interventions aligns with behaviour under real distribution shifts. This framework provides a principled way to assess robustness under clinically relevant variations, supporting safe deployment. Counterfactual Stress Testing for Image Classification Models 9
