# Abstract 

Generative models are powerful tools for sampling from a learned distribution 

P(Y | X), and inverse-design methods invert this map to find an input x that produces a desired point output y‚ąó. However, many design goals are naturally distributional rather than pointwise, incorporating the inherent uncertainty of Y

and targeting a specific form for it, a task not addressed by standard inverse design. To address this issue we introduce Conditional Distribution Matching (CDM) 1,a new inverse-design problem class in generative modeling: given a joint distri-bution P(X, Y ) and a target distribution G(Y ), find an input x‚ąó whose induced conditional distribution P(Y | X = x‚ąó) matches G. We formally define two variants: Conditional Distribution Matching Sampling (CDMS) and Conditional Distribution Matching Optimization (CDMO). To solve these problems, we propose 

MLGD-F (Matching-Loss Guided Diffusion with a Fast inner sampler ), a plug-and-play inference-time algorithm that combines a pretrained score-based diffusion model with a pretrained fast conditional sampler, requiring no additional training or fine-tuning. By leveraging single-step conditional sampling, MLGD-F enables tractable gradient computation, making the estimation of P(Y | X) both memory-efficient and computationally lightweight. We validate MLGD-F on synthetic benchmarks, structured image transformations, and generative editing optimization, demonstrating reliable recovery of inputs whose conditional distributions match diverse user-specified targets, including discrete mixtures and continuous low-rank supports. 

# 1 Introduction 

Generative models have emerged as a cornerstone of modern machine learning, achieving remarkable results in image synthesis, voice generation, and time-series modeling [ 19 , 39 , 43 ]. The paradigm has evolved from unconditional generation , learning to sample from an unknown data distribu-tion P(X) [ 17 , 33 , 34 ], toward increasingly controlled generation. Conditional generation allows sampling x0 ‚ąľ P (X | Y = y) for a user-specified label y [9 , 16 ], loss-guided methods [6, 35 ]generate samples that minimize a given objective L : X ‚Üí R while remaining faithful to the data distribution. A natural instantiation of loss-guided generation is inverse design : find an input x such that a forward learned model f (x) is close to a desired point output y‚ąó [ 31 ], successfully applied in protein engineering [ 8] and drug discovery [ 13 ]. However, this paradigm is inherently limited to point targets: it cannot express goals where the desired outcome is a distribution over outputs rather than a single value. In many modern settings the mapping from input to output is mediated by a complex generative process P(Y | X) that we cannot modify: a pretrained diffusion model with billions of parameters, a physical process such as protein folding, or a black-box simulator. Diffusion models. Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) [ 17 , 33 ] define a forward pro-cess that gradually adds Gaussian noise to data x0 over T steps, producing a sequence x0, x 1, . . . , x T ,and learn a reverse process that denoises xT ‚ąľ N (0 , I ) back to a sample x0 from the data distri-bution. A deterministic alternative to ancestral sampling is the probability flow ODE (used via the DDIM sampler [ 34 ]), which traces a deterministic trajectory through the same marginals and enables faster sampling with fewer steps. 2Few-step diffusion models. Standard diffusion models require 30 ‚Äď1000 sequential denoising steps at inference time [ 17 , 34 ], motivating research into faster inference strategies. One direction is consistency models [ 36 , 38 ], which enforce self-consistency along the probability-flow ODE trajectory without requiring a pretrained teacher. Another direction is diffusion distillation , which compresses a pretrained diffusion model into a student that generates samples in one or very few steps [ 30 , 32 ]. We exploit the single-step property common to both families to make inner-loop conditional sampling tractable at every denoising step of the outer diffusion process. 

Loss-guided sampling. Diffusion Posterior Sampling (DPS) [ 6 ] and Loss-Guided Diffusion (LGD) [ 35 ] modify the reverse diffusion process by incorporating gradient information from a differentiable loss function L(x0). At each denoising step, the score is augmented by ‚ąáxt L(ňÜ x0),where ňÜx0 is an estimate of the clean sample obtained via Tweedie‚Äôs formula. LGD further stabilises the gradient by averaging over multiple Monte Carlo Tweedie estimates. This framework enables flexible, zero-shot guidance for a wide range of objectives without retraining the generative prior. 

Distribution matching. A large family of generative methods trains a parametric generator so that its output distribution matches a target G, using losses based on Maximum Mean Discrepancy [ 21 , 22 ], Optimal Transport / Sinkhorn divergences [ 12 ], These approaches require differentiating through the generator and updating its weights, making them expensive and target-specific: a new G requires retraining. Our setting is orthogonal: the generative pipeline (both the diffusion prior and the conditional model fŌē) is frozen , and we instead optimise a single input x‚ąó such that P(Y | X = x‚ąó)

matches G. No generator weights are updated, no target-specific training is required, and the method operates entirely at inference time. 

Terminological note. The phrase "conditional distribution matching" has prior unrelated uses. In the domain-adaptation literature [ 41 ] it refers to aligning the source and target conditionals 

Psrc (X | Y ) and Ptgt (X | Y ) across two datasets so that a classifier learned on one transfers to the other. In concurrent work on discrete-diffusion distillation [ 11 ] it refers to a training objective in which a student model is trained to match a pretrained teacher‚Äôs reverse conditional transitions. Our use is unrelated to either: we study the inverse-design problem of finding an input x‚ąó whose induced conditional P(Y | X = x‚ąó) matches a user-specified target G(Y ), with a single fixed generative process. The three problems share only a phrase, not a setup or method. 

# 4 Methodology 

MLGD (Matching-Loss Guided Diffusion) specialises Loss-Guided Diffusion [ 35 ] to the conditional distribution matching setting (Problems 1‚Äď2) by combining the pretrained score network sőł for 

P(X) with a conditional sampler fŌē for P(Y | X). When fŌē is a few-step sampler, we refer to the instantiation as MLGD-F; MLGD is the broader framework that also covers slower choices of fŌē.The method has two components: an outer loop that performs loss-guided reverse diffusion with a black-box loss L(x) (the LGD skeleton), and an inner estimator that evaluates and differentiates the distribution-matching loss L(x) = ‚ą•P (Y | X = x) ‚ąí G (Y )‚ą• at each outer step. The inner estimator is the paper‚Äôs algorithmic contribution.

# 5 Experiments 

Our experiments address two core properties of MLGD-F: its capacity to optimize over high-dimensional input manifolds X, and its scalability to semantically complex output spaces Y . We evaluate across three settings of increasing scale: synthetic Mixture of Gaussians (Section 5.1), where low-dimensional X and Y allow verification against known ground-truth optima; an MNIST rotation task (Section 5.2), which scales X to image space ( R784 ) while keeping Y small; and a Stable Diffusion image optimization (Section 5.3), which scales both X and Y to high-dimensional spaces. 

5.1 Synthetic Simulations 

We compare MLGD-F against MLGD with a slow diffusion sampler on Mixture of Gaussians across multiple dimensionalities. We run 25 optimisation instances and select the top-10 by final loss (Problem 2). Reporting top-k reflects how multistart optimisation is used in practice: a user running 

R restarts returns the best result by the same objective, so top-k captures achievable quality under finite restart budgets. We also report all-25 statistics to characterise variance across seeds. Full models and training details are provided in Appendix A. As shown in Figure 1, MLGD-F recovers near-optimal solutions with an 11 √ó speedup over MLGD (4.03 s vs. 45 .58 s for the representative run shown; full per-dimensionality timing across all 25 runs is reported in Appendix A). Notably, ECDF analysis (Figure 4) reveals that MLGD-F‚Äôs relative performance improves with dimensionality. We attribute this to the trade-off between sampler fidelity and gradient variance : while MLGD with a slow sampler uses a more accurate conditional model, backpropagating through its unrolled K‚čÜ-step chain accumulates variance that scales poorly with dimension. MLGD-F‚Äôs single-step model provides a cleaner gradient signal, which becomes the decisive factor in high-dimensional settings (Appendix A.8). The ő≤-sweep in Figure 1 (bottom) empirically illustrates the sampling-to-optimisation interpolation of Problem 1: as ő≤ increases from 0

to large values, MLGD-F transitions from sampling the prior P to concentrating near arg min x L(x).

5.2 MNIST: Rotated Digits 

We apply MLGD-F to an image-space problem using the MNIST dataset [ 20 ], where X ‚ąą R784 is a digit image and Y ‚ąą R2 is its rotation angle encoded as (cos őł, sin őł); a standard diffusion model serves as P(X) and a consistency model as fŌē for P(Y | X). The goal is to find x‚ąó such that 

P(Y | X = x‚ąó) ‚Čą G for various user-specified target distributions G.Since the unconditional model can generate any digit at any orientation, the optimization searches over the full joint space of digit identity and rotation. We present the top-5 results by final loss across 15 runs (Figure 2, and Table 1); full qualitative results are provided in Appendix B.6. These runs are approximate samples from the ő≤-tempered posterior Qő≤ (x) ‚ąĚ P (x)e‚ąíő≤L(x) (Problem 1), and the digit-class distribution in Table 1 is the empirical realisation of this posterior. While the objective is distributional and agnostic to digit identity, MLGD-F recovers semantically coherent digits that satisfy the orientation constraint G: symmetric digits dominate under the uniform target (digit 0) and bimodal target (narrow 0, 1, 8, 6, 9), while oriented digits (2, 3, 7) emerge under the unimodal target.

To isolate the impact of the denoising step count, we observe that our setup utilizes an identical architectural backbone across both configurations: the UNet (‚Čą2.6B parameters), ControlNet [ 44 ], and CLIP text encoders [ 29 ] remain constant. Notably, SDXL-Turbo is obtained by distilling SDXL-Base with the architecture unchanged - the two models differ only in their weights, not their structure [ 32 ]. Consequently, the computational cost of a single forward pass is equivalent across both, and all observed differences in memory and speed are a direct consequence of the denoising step count alone. The observed differences in memory and speed are thus a direct consequence of the computation graph depth. Since SDXL Base requires K‚čÜ = 30 steps for high-quality reconstruction compared to Ks = 2 for SDXL Turbo, the resulting gradient graph is fundamentally 15 √ó deeper. To manage memory, one can employ gradient checkpointing ; however, while this technique avoids storing intermediate activations, the overhead of maintaining the computation graph nodes for each of the K

denoising steps still scales linearly with the number of iterations.

# 6 Conclusion 

We introduced Conditional Distribution Matching (CDM) , a new problem class that generalizes inverse design to the distributional setting. Rather than seeking an input x that produces a specific output y‚ąó, CDM seeks an input x‚ąó whose entire induced conditional distribution P(Y | X = x‚ąó)

matches a user-specified target G(Y ). To our knowledge, this problem class has not been explicitly studied before. We formalized two variants, CDMS and CDMO, and introduced MLGD-F to solve them at inference time without any training or fine-tuning. A central advantage of the plug-and-play design is that MLGD-F directly inherits quality gains from improvements in the underlying pretrained models: as stronger diffusion priors and few-step conditional samplers become available, optimization quality improves automatically. The few-step model is essential for tractability: as demonstrated in Section 5.4, replacing a single-step sampler with a standard multi-step diffusion model increases peak memory by nearly an order of magnitude and runtime from hours to days, rendering the method infeasible at scale. Empirically, we validated MLGD-F across settings of increasing scale and complexity, from synthetic Mixture of Gaussians, through an MNIST rotation task, to a large-scale Stable Diffusion image editing pipeline, demonstrating reliable recovery of inputs whose induced conditional distributions match diverse user-specified targets, including discrete mixtures and continuous low-rank submanifolds. 

Limitations. Optimization quality is bounded by the fidelity of the pretrained distilled model ‚ÄĒ if the conditional sampler fŌē poorly approximates P(Y | X), the guidance gradient will be biased regardless of the number of runs. The stochastic nature of the inner-loop loss estimation means multiple independent runs are often needed to find a good solution, which multiplies the compute cost. Runtime at large scale may be prohibitive for latency-sensitive applications. Finally, the method currently requires the conditional model to be differentiable end-to-end with respect to the input x;non-differentiable or black-box conditional models would require gradient-free extensions. 

Future Work. CDM opens a research direction of optimizing over induced conditional distributions, one we hope will inspire dedicated solvers, tighter theoretical guarantees, and new application domains. This work focuses on continuous input spaces; an important direction is extending CDM to discrete spaces. A complementary direction is replacing the LGD-style approximate sampler (Remark 2) by an asymptotically exact corrector for the outer loop, twisted SMC, MCMC, or Feynman‚ÄďKac reweighting, to obtain Qő≤ samples with quantifiable bias at the cost of extra compute. Finally, an intriguing question is whether few-step samplers offer advantages beyond speed: their single-step structure may yield cleaner gradient signals compared to unrolled multi-step chains, as suggested by Appendix A.8. 

Broader Impact. CDM opens both beneficial and potentially harmful applications. Distributional control can produce diverse and targeted outputs on complex models: for instance, the Stable Diffusion experiment (Section 5.3) demonstrates how MLGD-F can enforce demographic balance in generated portraits, a direct application to bias reduction. However, since G is defined with great freedom, the same mechanism could be misused to deliberately steer frozen pipelines toward harmful distributions. Since MLGD-F operates on frozen models, existing safety mechanisms remain fully active. 9References

B.2 Dataset Construction 

We construct an augmented MNIST dataset that captures rotational ambiguity across digit classes. 

B.2.1 Conditional Model 

For the conditional training the pipeline proceeds in two stages. 

Stage 1: Logical-angle entries. We start from the standard MNIST training split (60,000 images). For each base image, we always retain a copy at logical angle 0. We additionally include rotated copies at 90 , 180 , and 270 if a pretrained classifier assigns the rotated image to a digit class with confidence ‚Č• 0.9999 . This threshold ensures that only genuinely rotationally ambiguous digits (e.g., ‚Äú0‚ÄĚ, ‚Äú1‚ÄĚ, ‚Äú8‚ÄĚ, ‚Äú6‚ÄĚ/‚Äú9‚ÄĚ) contribute additional logical-angle entries. The classifier used is the dataset-construction CNN described below, trained on the standard MNIST training split. 

Stage 2: Uniform random rotation. Each logical-angle entry is then rotated by a uniform random angle őł ‚ąľ Uniform[0 , 360) . The stored label angle is (logical _angle + őł) mod 360 . This produces a dataset of 82,483 samples with continuous, uniformly distributed rotation angles within each rotationally ambiguous class. 

B.2.2 Unconditional Model 

The unconditional diffusion model is trained on a separate dataset consisting of all 70,000 MNIST images (train + test splits), each independently rotated by a uniform random angle at every epoch access, with no classifier filtering.

C.8 What Does MLGD-F Actually Change in the Scribble? Where MLGD-F edits the scribble. Figure 8 contrasts the source scribble with the MLGD-F-optimized scribble, along with their pixel-wise | ¬∑ | difference. The edits are spatially concentrated rather than diffuse: the largest modifications lie along the eye and eyebrow region and the lower hair outline. Most of the scribble is essentially unchanged; a minority of anatomically-meaningful contours account for the bulk of the edit magnitude. Yet even with the pixel-difference heatmap in hand, it remains unclear why these subtle edits suffice to shift the generated distribution toward a balanced male‚Äďfemale output. From a human perceptual standpoint, the changes are too small and too localized to constitute a legible gender signal. We hypothesize that MLGD-F has identified a structural sensitivity in the ControlNet‚ÄďSDXL pipeline: a region of the conditioning space where small perturbations disproportionately influence the generative process, not because they are semantically meaningful to a human observer, but because the diffusion model‚Äôs internal representations are unexpectedly responsive to them. This points to a potential vulnerability in the underlying generative model ‚ÄĒ one that MLGD-F successfully exploits to achieve distributional control, but that also raises questions about the robustness and interpretability of ControlNet-based conditioning more broadly.

Takeaway. The two analyses together resolve the apparent paradox of small pixel edits producing a large distributional shift. Among the strokes of the source scribble, MLGD-F does not drift diffusely: it injects an aligned gradient signal that preferentially modifies the subset of strokes the downstream CLIP score is structurally sensitive to. This validates the inner estimator of Algorithm 2 as a mechanism rather than a black box: despite the long differentiable chain from scribble through ControlNet, VAE, and CLIP, the pulled-back gradient at each denoising step retains enough semantic structure to localise onto the anatomical features actually predictive of the target attribute. The regions identified ‚ÄĒ the eye, eyebrow, and hairline ‚ÄĒ are anatomically intuitive as gender-discriminative features. Yet from a human perceptual standpoint, the magnitude of the edits is strikingly small: changes that are nearly imperceptible to a human observer are sufficient to produce a substantial shift in the generated gender distribution. This appendix analyses the plug-in loss estimator and the gradient estimator used inside the MLGD-F inner estimator (Algorithm 2; detailed form in Algorithm 3). E.8 Status of the fidelity assumptions Output gap őĶs. The output gap őĶs of Assumption 2 is an idealised fidelity assumption. Directly-trained samplers : when fŌē is trained directly on data (e.g. improved Consistency Training on MNIST [ 36 ]), score-based diffusion convergence results [ 1, 3, 5] give related output-distribution guarantees but do not directly imply the uniform conditional pathwise bound required here, because they bound marginal sampling error, not the same-ő∑ coupling between two specific samplers. Proposition 2‚Äôs bound is therefore qualitatively informative ‚ÄĒ it identifies the right two error sources and shows the gradient bias decomposes cleanly into them ‚ÄĒ but not quantitatively tight at current distillation quality. We view the propositions as a framework for measurement and 48 regime identification: they tell us which quantities to estimate and how they propagate, rather than supplying numerical guarantees on the algorithm. More broadly, this points to a gap in the theory of model distillation itself. Standard distillation analyses, and the training objectives that flow from them, control output fidelity : L2 or Wasserstein closeness between student and teacher. This is the right bound when the distilled model is used as a function evaluator . MLGD-F, by contrast, uses fŌē as an autograd oracle : its Jacobian is consumed, not just its values. Whenever a distilled or compressed model is deployed in a downstream 

optimisation rather than a downstream evaluation (MLGD-F, bi-level optimisation with learned inner solvers, differentiable simulators, gradient-guided inverse design in general), output-fidelity distillation bounds are insufficient, and an őĶg -type Jacobian-fidelity guarantee is required. Current consistency-model training provides the former but not the latter.
