## Abstract 

5G networks provide low-latency, high throughput, and massive connectivity, yet the control plane remains exposed to several se-curity threats. Among the most common and impactful threats are Denial-of-Service (DoS) attacks, with Radio Resource Control (RRC) signaling storms being particularly effective and difficult to mitigate. In this attack, a malicious User Equipment (UE) aims to exhaust Next Generation Node Base (gNB) resources, preventing legitimate UEs from establishing a connection. Existing defenses are typically limited to detection, only evaluated through numerical simulations, and cannot discern between high-load network condi-tions and attacks. Most of them also assume static setups and do not take mobility into account. In this paper, we first evaluate the feasi-bility of the signaling storm attack by using the OpenAirInterface (OAI) 5G protocol stack. Then, we propose StormShield, a signaling storm attack detection and mitigation technique implemented as an xApp on an O-RAN Near-Real-Time (near-RT) RAN Intelligent Controller (RIC). It fingerprints and blocks Malicious UEs (MUEs) before gNB resources are exhausted. We prototyped our solution on an Over-The-Air (OTA) testbed with OAI, NVIDIA Aerial, and two different gNB setups. The first one leverages an USRP X410 Software-defined Radio (SDR) with 8.1 functional split; the second a commercial Foxconn Radio Unit (RU) with 7.2 functional split. Our experimental evaluation demonstrates that StormShield effectively prevents gNB resource exhaustion, identifying and blocking MUEs with an average detection accuracy of 97.6% within 106.5 ms from the beginning of the attack.

## 1 Introduction 

As the 5th generation (5G) of cellular networks scales up to sup-port enhanced mobile broadband, ultra-reliable low-latency con-nections, and massive machine-type communications, ensuring high availability in the protocol stack control-plane emerges as a major security concern. Both user and control planes are exposed to Denial-of-Service (DoS) attacks that can degrade or interrupt network service [ 5 , 11 , 13 ], but control-plane attacks are more concerning, as they can be carried out by unauthenticated users. Among these attacks, the Radio Resource Control (RRC) signaling storm constitutes a relevant risk for the control-plane. In this at-tack, an adversarial User Equipment (UE), which we will refer to as Malicious UE (MUE) throughout the rest of the paper, repeat-edly generates RRC Setup Requests with different Radio Network Temporary Identifiers (RNTIs) without sending the RRC Setup Com-plete message [ 16 ]. In this way, the MUE avoids authenticating to the network but forces the Next Generation Node Base (gNB) to al-locate resources for its session (as shown in Fig. 1b). This malicious behavior depletes control-plane capacity and prevents legitimate connections [15]. The detection of these attacks is challenging because the initial observable behavior mimics genuine high-load events in which the gNB observes bursts of RRC Setup Requests within short time windows. This makes rate-based thresholds [ 21 ] prone to false alarms. Mitigation is even more challenging and far less explored in the literature. Since MUEs never complete the authentication

We leverage O-RAN as an architectural enabler for addressing the above challenges and design our solution. By exposing stan-dardized, open interfaces toward a Near-Real-Time (near-RT) RAN Intelligent Controller (RIC), O-RAN provides the observability and control loops needed to collect fine-grained Key Performance Met-rics (KPMs) and react rapidly in case of an attack [ 3 ]. Specifically, we introduce StormShield, a near-RT detection and mitigation al-gorithm for RRC signaling storm attacks that clusters UEs by using multiple radio-level indicators—Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI) and TA—and access pattern features (e.g., per-source burst of connection attempts and request-to-completion ratios) to build a robust MUEs fingerprint. The combination of multiple indicators is crucial, as existing defenses—mainly detection-only or volume-based—struggle to separate genuine high load from attacks, and TA alone is insufficient in dense networks where UEs share similar values for this parameter. Differently from prior work, StormShield (i) implements an effective attack detection and mitigation, reject-ing connection attempts from UEs flagged as malicious before gNB resources saturate; (ii) discerns high-load vs. attack scenarios; and (iii) remains robust in challenging environments, including multiple simultaneous attackers and mobility. We prototype StormShield on an Over-The-Air (OTA) testbed with programmable 5G protocol stacks (OAI, NVIDIA Aerial) [ 9, 10 ]and two different gNB setups. In the first, OpenAirInterface (OAI) drives an USRP X410 Software-defined Radio (SDR) with 8.1 func-tional split to perform signal transmission and reception. In the second setup, instead, the OAI protocol stack connects to the GPU-accelerated NVIDIA ARC solution, which implements the lower-layer functionalities of the gNB protocol stack, and connects to a commercial Radio Unit (RU) from Foxconn with 7.2 split. We proto-type the malicious UE by customizing the RRC state machine in the OAI software UE, and use commercial smartphones as legitimate users. Experimental results in the different setups demonstrate that StormShield is effective in detecting and mitigating signaling storm attacks before the gNB resources are depleted, achieving a 97.6% average accuracy. Our key contributions are the following: 

• Attack feasibility in realistic 5G setups - We reproduce the RRC signaling storm attack over different OTA testbed setups, including effects such as mobility and multiple con-current attackers. 

• Fingerprint-based detection of signaling storms - We design a detection algorithm that combines RRC-layer sta-tistics with spatial fingerprinting. This allows the system to distinguish signaling storm attacks from benign high-load network conditions even in challenging indoor scenarios where distance-based features alone are not sufficient. 

• Pre-authentication mitigation via fingerprint block-listing - We introduce a mitigation algorithm that operates before authentication and without relying on stable user identities. It maintains an adaptive block list of malicious fingerprints with aging timeouts that reinforce blocking for persistent attackers, while automatically expiring stale en-tries to avoid long-term exclusion of benign UEs. We demon-strate the effectiveness of this technique in the presence of multiple attackers, and both static and mobile devices. 

• O-RAN-based closed control loop implementation -

We deploy StormShield in an xApp over the near-RT RIC, enabling closed-loop detection and mitigation without mod-ifying the Radio Access Network (RAN) data plane. We ex-tend the O-RAN E2 data-exposure capabilities with a new security-oriented Service Model (SM), implemented accord-ing to O-RAN use case [ 16 ], that defines telemetry and con-trol semantics to obtain the KPMs required by StormShield. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews different types of DoS attacks in O-RAN and the detection and mitigation solutions proposed in the literature. Section 3 pro-vides a brief technical background on the Open RAN architecture and the integration of xApps within the near-RT RIC, highlighting the main interfaces leveraged in this work. Section 4 introduces our threat model and characterizes the signaling storm attack. Sec-tion 5 focuses on the detection and mitigation logic and details the implementation of the SM for KPMs collection. Section 6 describes the StormShield prototype and the experimental setup used in our evaluation. Section 7 discusses our experimental results under dif-ferent setups and conditions. Finally, Section 8 concludes our work and discusses possible future directions.

## 2.1 Denial of Service Attacks in Cellular Networks 

Numerous types of DoS attacks have been explored in recent liter-ature on 5G cellular networks, highlighting how adversaries can exploit protocol vulnerabilities and resource constraints to degrade or even disrupt service availability. One example is presented in [ 2 ], where the authors introduce TenaxDoS, a detection framework based on deep learning and federated continual learning, and de-ployed in an O-RAN 5G mobile network. This system focuses on identifying distributed DoS attacks targeting the traffic volume (e.g., bandwidth saturation), the protocol stack (e.g., handshake abuse), and application-layer aspects (e.g., resource draining). The authors of [ 13 ] design a tool capable of launching control-plane DoS attacks via the O-RAN fronthaul interface. The tool generates Real-time (RT) control messages over Ethernet using the eCPRI protocol and can spoof MAC addresses to appear as O-RUs or broadcast entities. Another work [ 5] demonstrates both control and user plane DoS attacks using a custom-built evaluation tool. The authors assess the impact on throughput, Block Error Rate (BLER), and the state evolution of O-Distributed Unit (DU)/RU. Further DoS vectors are described in [ 7, 12 ], both targeting the RIC E2 subscription mechanism via a malicious xApp that exploits weak access control and input validation to disrupt the near-RT RIC by sending numerous crafted or malformed subscription requests. The former work floods the near-RT RIC with legitimate-looking E2 requests to degrade service. The latter demonstrates that malformed messages can crash the E2 termination component of the RIC. The work in [ 8] reveals vulnerabilities that allow adversaries to cause DoS on the RU. Attackers with limited access can interrupt data transmission by changing Radio Frequency (RF) parameters, misconfigure VLANs to block access, or repeatedly reboot the RU to make it unavailable. In [11], the authors describe DoS attacks such as the Blind DoS and the Base Transceiver Station (BTS) resource depletion attacks. In the former, an adversary spoofs the SAE Temporary Mobile Subscriber Identity (S-TMSI) of the victim UE to initiate RRC con-nections on its behalf. In the latter, an adversary floods the gNB with RRC connection requests without completing authentication, exhausting the maximum number of allowed RRC connections and blocking legitimate UEs from connecting to the gNB. Our work focuses specifically on this type of attack, often referred to as a RRC Signaling Storm. Differently from the above literature works—and beyond reproducing and evaluating the attack on a real 5G OTA testbed with mobility and multiple MUEs—we propose a fingerprint-based detection and mitigation logic that blocks malicious connec-tion attempts, prevents them from exhausting the gNB resources, while providing service for legitimate UEs. 

## 2.2 Signaling Storm Detection and Mitigation 

These attacks are difficult to detect due to changing attacker identi-fiers and the similarity of the traffic pattern to that of a high-load network. Despite their impact, the literature lacks effective mitiga-tion strategies, because attackers never complete the authentication phase and continuously change their temporary identifiers. Table 1 summarizes the main literature approaches on this topic, and high-lights how our work differs in terms of setup, attack detection and mitigation capabilities. In [ 19 ], an Anomaly Traffic Detector xApp applies RT traffic classification using machine learning. It identifies anomalous users analyzing packets and using a random forest. If the UE anomaly ratio reaches 100%, the xApp triggers an RRC UE connection release, causing the UE to disconnect from the network. However, this method assumes the attacker has completed the authentication process, which is not the case in signaling storm scenarios where the attacker avoids authentication altogether. Another work [ 21 ] introduces a framework that detects several types of cellular protocol exploits in Long Term Evolution (LTE) networks, including attacks that exploit the evolved Node Base (eNB) (e.g., BTS resource depletion), the Mobility Management Entity (MME) of the core network, and even the UEs themselves. Considering the resource depletion attack, its detection method relies on the fact that fake RRC connections—dubbed “transient UEs”—are typically short-lived due to a missing Non-Access Stratum (NAS) authentication response. If the number of transient UEs exceeds a threshold, the attack is detected. However, this approach cannot reliably distinguish an attack from a legitimate high-load condition of the network. In [ 22 ], the authors propose 6G-XSec, a framework that com-bines unsupervised deep learning with Large Language Models (LLMs) to monitor and interpret network anomalies, including BTS resource depletion attacks. The system first extends O-RAN with “MOBIFLOW” to collect telemetry data including encoded RRC/NAS messages and parameters such as UE identifiers and state. The col-lected time-series traces are then fed to an unsupervised anomaly-detection xApp (namely, “MOBIWATCH”), trained on benign traffic WiSec ’26, June 30-July 03, 2026, Saarbrücken, Germany Noemi Giustini et al. 

only. In the case of BTS DoS attacks, the detector learns to flag sequences with abnormal patterns of RRC/NAS messages and de-vice identifiers, such as rapid bursts of uncompleted connection attempts with changing RNTI values. An LLM-based xApp is sub-sequently used to classify and explain the detected anomalies. A limitation of this approach is that benign but unusual conditions (e.g., multiple retransmissions or connection failures) can produce similar anomalous patterns, making it difficult to reliably separate them from malicious activity. In [ 15 ], a threshold-based detection technique is proposed. This uses RRC-layer metrics like the number of MSG3 messages (RRC Connection Request), and the MSG5-to-MSG3 and MSG5-to-MSG4 ratios, where MSG4 and MSG5 correspond to the RRC Connection Setup and RRC Connection Setup Complete messages, respectively. A surge in MSG3 indicates abnormal behavior, while a drop in the ratios can help distinguish an attack from high-load conditions, since in high-load conditions most UEs that receive MSG4 still respond with MSG5, whereas this does not happen during an attack. However, this approach is limited by its reliance only on fixed thresholds, which are not adaptive to different network conditions and may lead to false positives. Overall, the above methods mostly focus on detection rather than mitigation, as we instead do in this work. A partially implemented mitigation solution is proposed in [ 6], where a dedicated xApp uses TA to fingerprint devices early in the connection process. Since TA correlates with the physical distance between the UE and base station, this parameter helps to identify and block suspicious devices before they authenticate. However, this method becomes unreliable in indoor or dense scenarios, where UEs are close, as TA lacks the granularity to differentiate between them. Additionally, most of the above solutions have been tested in simulations or in limited testbeds, often involving only a single stationary attacker. By contrast, our logic handles single or multiple, stationary or moving attackers, and was validated on an OTA testbed with different setups and configurations. 

## 3 A Primer on the Open RAN Architecture 

The Open RAN architecture, and its embodiment in O-RAN, acts as a key enabler of closed-loop control for addressing 5G RAN performance and security issues. O-RAN builds on the 3rd Genera-tion Partnership Project (3GPP) gNB disaggregation into Central Unit (CU), DU, and RU elements. These elements can be virtualized on the O-Cloud infrastructure and interconnected via open and standardized interfaces [ 17 ]. O-RAN introduces two RICs acting at different time scales. The Non-Real-time (non-RT) RIC, integrated within the Service Management and Orchestration (SMO), operates closed-loop control of the RAN at time scales above 1 s; the near-RT RIC, deployed at the edge, operates at time scales between 10 ms and 

1 s. Both RICs host intelligent Artificial Intelligence (AI)/Machine Learning (ML) applications. The near-RT RIC hosts xApps that subscribe to telemetry (e.g., KPMs) exposed over the O-RAN E2 interface and enforce control actions on E2 nodes (i.e., CU, DU, and legacy eNB supporting this interface). xApps realize data-driven closed-control loops that can promptly react to network anomalies, such as the DoS attacks we focus on in this work. The non-RT RIC, instead, hosts rApps that receive telemetry at a larger granularity over the O-RAN O1 interface from the remaining Open RAN ele-ments (i.e., near-RT RIC, CU, DU, RU, and legacy eNB supporting this interface). rApps push high-level network policies to the near-RT RIC via the O-RAN A1 interface that connects the two RICs. Finally, the non-RT RIC connects to the O-Cloud infrastructure via the O-RAN O2 interface to carry monitoring operations, as well as programmatic provisioning, management, and update of network functions. In this work, we leverage the openness and control capabilities of the Open RAN architecture to implement StormShield ’s attack detection and mitigation logic, as discussed in Sec. 5. 

## 4 Attack Modeling 

The 5G connection establishment procedure, as defined in the 3GPP specifications [ 1], enables a UE to access the network for services such as data and voice communication. As illustrated in Fig. 1a, the procedure begins with cell search and synchronization. The UE then acquires the necessary uplink access parameters and initiates the Random Access Channel (RACH) procedure by transmitting a random preamble (MSG1) to the gNB. At this point, the UE also starts a timer (namely, timer T300), which sets the time limit for completing the connection setup. If the timer expires, the UE con-siders the attempt failed and restarts the process. Upon receiving the preamble, the gNB detects the transmission and responds with a Random Access Response (MSG2), which in-cludes a TA, an uplink grant, and a temporary C-RNTI. This allows the UE to proceed with MSG3, an RRC Setup Request message that carries the UE identity and the establishment cause. The gNB, then, replies with an RRC Setup (MSG4), which defines radio bearer configurations and resolves contention. Upon receiving this mes-sage, the UE stops the T300 timer and responds with an RRC Setup Complete message (MSG5), which includes a NAS Registration Re-quest. Finally, the gNB selects an appropriate Access and Mobility Management Function (AMF) and assigns a Next Generation Ap-plication Protocol (NGAP) identifier to the UE. This completes the UE connection process. In contrast, in the case of a RRC signaling storm attack, the MUE exploits this mechanism as illustrated in Fig. 1b. The attacker initiates the connection process by sending MSG1 through MSG3 but intentionally avoids completing the setup by not replying with MSG5 once it receives MSG4 from the gNB. Despite not completing the connection, the gNB still allocates radio resources and con-text to the UE based on MSG3, and starts a timer while waiting for MSG5 [ 15 ]. Once MSG3 is decoded, the gNB creates an RRC UE context, assigning an internal RRC UE identifier, binding the temporary RNTI, and allocating the associated control-plane state (e.g., security capabilities, bearer configuration and timers). Even though the MUE never completes the RRC procedure, this per-UE context remains stored in the UE context maintained by the gNB, needlessly occupying RRC context resources. Under a signaling storm attack, many of such unauthenticated contexts accumulate, rapidly exhausting the pool of RRC contexts available at the gNB and preventing new attachments from legitimate UEs. If 𝝁 is new, a fresh entry is created with an initial counter and timeout. For each new RRC connection attempt (e.g., Setup or Reestab-lishment), the gNB measures the current fingerprint f = (TA , RSSI )

and compares it with the active entries in B. Before matching, the algorithm removes any entry whose inactivity exceeds its timeout, i.e., all 𝑒 such that 𝑡 now − 𝑡 last ≥ 𝜏 . This aging mechanism is crucial, as, if an entry never expired, a benign UE establishing a subsequent connection with similar transmission conditions could match an old malicious fingerprint and incorrectly be blocked. For the remaining entries, the algorithm checks whether the cur-rent fingerprint f = (TA , RSSI ) is sufficiently close to any centroid 

𝝁 = (𝜇 TA , 𝜇 RSSI ) in the block list. 𝜖 TA and 𝜖 RSSI define the maximum allowed difference in TA and RSSI, respectively, for two points to be considered as belonging to the same UE. A match occurs if the following two conditions are satisfied for some centroid 𝝁 :

|TA − 𝜇 TA | ≤ 𝜖 TA (7) 

|RSSI − 𝜇 RSSI | ≤ 𝜖 RSSI , (8) in which case, the connection attempt is rejected and the corre-sponding RRC context dropped, thus preventing the attack from consuming additional resources. Otherwise, the connection pro-ceeds as normal. Overall, the combined detection and mitigation logic blocks new connection attempts that match the fingerprints of suspected MUEs, while still allowing legitimate UEs to access the network. This limits the number of active RRC contexts and prevents re-source exhaustion. Although attackers may continue to perform connection attempts, these are no longer admitted into the system, and any RRC resources previously allocated to malicious requests are automatically released by the gNB after the procedure times out. This allows the system to gradually return to a stable state.

## 6 Prototype and Experimental Evaluation Setup 

To evaluate the performance and robustness of StormShield in real-world scenarios, we implement its detection and mitigation logic as an O-RAN xApp running on a near-RT RIC based on FlexRIC [ 18 ], and test it into the two OTA setups shown in Fig. 3. All software components, including gNB, soft-UE, RIC, and xApp, are container-ized and orchestrated using Red Hat OpenShift, a Kubernetes-based container platform designed for automated deployment, scaling, and management of applications in hybrid cloud environments. This deployment choice allows us to reuse the same deployment artifacts across both testbed setups and to repeat experiments under controlled resource allocations, as done in [14]. The first setup, shown in Fig. 3a, leverages an USRP X410 SDR for signal transmission and reception. The gNB protocol stack is implemented using the OAI framework running within a Red Hat OpenShift pod, with a functional split 8.1. In this setup, both the gNB and the MUE rely on SDR hardware, and we use OctoClock clock distributors to distribute common Pulse-Per-Second (PPS) and reference signal to the SDRs to ensure frame-timing alignment. This improves the stability of the OAI softwarized UE when connecting to the SDR-based gNB. The second testbed setup, illustrated in Fig. 3b, adopts the 7.2 functional split and integrates a commercial Foxconn RU. This is connected to a gNB implementation distributed across two Open-Shift containers within a single NVIDIA ARC pod. The first con-tainer runs the OAI stack, implementing the CU and DU-high layers, while the second runs NVIDIA cuBB framework for DU-low func-tionalities [20]. The NVIDIA ARC pod runs on a Grace Hopper Supermicro ARS-111GL-NHR server with a Grace CPU (ARM-based, 72 cores), 512 GB of memory, an H100 GPU, and a Mellanox ConnectX-7 Network Interface Card (NIC), enabling high-throughput networking and hardware acceleration. In contrast, the OAI gNB and MUEs pods are hosted on a Microway EPYC server equipped with an AMD EPYC 7262 CPU, 256 GB of RAM, and a Mellanox ConnectX-6 NIC. ## 8 Conclusions 

In this work, we considered the problem of RRC Signaling Storm at-tacks in 5G networks, which can rapidly exhaust gNB resources by flooding connection requests in the early stages of the RRC connec-tion procedure. Through an experimental campaign on two OTA testbed setups, we first demonstrated that RRC signaling storm attacks can be reproduced and studied on realistic 5G systems with single/multiple, static and mobile attackers. Then, we proposed StormShield, a fingerprint-based detection and mitigation approach that combines RRC statistics with (TA , RSSI ) fingerprints to dis-tinguish attacks from high-load network conditions, and block malicious access attempts before gNB resources are exhausted. We implemented StormShield as an xApp running on an O-RAN near-RT RIC for closed-loop gNB control through a SM implemented as described in an O-RAN use case. By running ≈ 389 experiments on a real-world testbed, we demon-strated that our system achieved an average accuracy of 97.6% in identifying and blocking MUEs, including in scenarios with mobil-ity and multiple attackers. Detection and mitigation consistently occurred before gNB resource depletion, with an average response time of 106 .5 ms, which shows the effectiveness of StormShield. As future work, we plan to improve the accuracy of UE fin-gerprinting by integrating additional features, such as I/Q sample analysis to capture hardware-specific RF impairments and provide a more robust device signature. We also aim to expand the detection surface from a local-cell approach to a distributed system lever-aging the centralized abstraction of the RIC. In addition, we will validate the proposed fingerprint in outdoor deployments, where TA granularity better separates spatial regions. Finally, we will extend the threat model to include a larger population of benign victim UEs to capture high-load scenarios, as well as more advanced, adaptive, and stealthy adversaries capable of completing RRC and NAS authentication and dynamically modifying transmit power to evade detection.
