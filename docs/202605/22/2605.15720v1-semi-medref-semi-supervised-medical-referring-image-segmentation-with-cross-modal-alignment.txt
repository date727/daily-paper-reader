Abstract. Medical referring image segmentation (MRIS) requires pixel-level masks aligned with textual descriptions of anatomical locations, making annotation costly in low-label regimes. Semi-supervised learn-ing (SSL) can mitigate this burden by leveraging unlabeled data, but its success hinges on maintaining reliable image–text alignment under perturbations. Most existing SSL-based referred segmentation methods use either independent or simplistic multi-modal perturbations ( e.g., 

left–right flips), without fully addressing cross-modal alignment under strong augmentation, while CutMix, highly effective in single-modal SSL, remains underexplored in multi-modal settings due to its tendency to dis-rupt image–text coherence. We propose Semi-MedRef, a teacher–student SSL framework designed to explicitly maintain consistency between med-ical images and positional language through three alignment-preserving components: T-PatchMix, a cross-modal CutMix-style augmentation that synchronizes patch mixing with referring expressions via position-constrained and probability-driven rules; PosAug, a position-aware text augmentation that masks or fuzzes anatomical phrases; and ITCL, a position-guided image–text contrastive learning module, which lever-ages positional pseudo-labels to construct soft anatomical positives and strengthen medically grounded cross-modal alignment. Experiments on QaTa-COV19 and MosMedData+ demonstrate that Semi-MedRef consis-tently outperforms both fully supervised and semi-supervised baselines across all label regimes. 

Keywords: Medical Referring Image Segmentation · Semi-supervised Learning · Multi-Modal Learning. 

## 1 Introduction 

Medical image segmentation supports key clinical tasks such as lesion quantifi-cation, treatment planning, and disease monitoring [9, 16, 21]. While modern deep models have achieved strong performance across modalities and anatomical targets [9, 21], purely visual cues can be ambiguous. Recent systems therefore

incorporate textual information—such as diagnostic reports or concise referring descriptions—to specify what to segment. This gives rise to medical referring image segmentation (MRIS), where the segmentation target is guided by language (e.g., “bilateral pulmonary infection, lower left and lower right lung”). However, obtaining paired pixel-level masks and referring expressions is costly, and annotation scarcity is particularly severe for lesion-level tasks with ambiguous boundaries and high inter-observer variability. Models trained in low-label regimes consequently suffer from poor generalization. Semi-supervised learning (SSL) [1,2,28] mitigates annotation demand by leveraging unlabeled data through pseudo-labeling and consistency regularization, assuming that applied perturbations preserve the semantics of supervision. SSL has recently been explored for referring expression segmentation (RES) in natural images. Teacher–student [22] consistency approaches, such as RES-Match [27], introduce weak-to-strong consistency and limited multi-modal per-turbations, e.g., swapping left/right words under horizontal flips. SemiRES [23] improves pseudo-label quality using external priors such as SAM [10]. In the medical domain, Textmatch [11] applies an EMA teacher–student framework with multi-modal fusion and pseudo-label regularization. Yet, these methods largely treat image and text perturbations independently or provide only coarse mechanisms for maintaining cross-modal alignment. A key challenge in SSL-based MRIS is that modern consistency-based SSL relies on strong perturbations to enforce invariance, yet maintaining reliable image–text alignment under such perturbations is difficult. Spatially altering operations, such as CutMix-style [7, 26] patch replacement, random cropping, or aggressive transforms, can disrupt the correspondence between textual descrip-tions and referenced image regions, corrupting pseudo-supervision [27]. This issue is especially acute in medical settings, where referring expressions rely heavily on structured positional language (left/right, upper/middle/lower): even simple flips may invert laterality. While CutMix is highly successful in single-modal SSL, directly applying it to multi-modal MRIS often breaks image–text correspondence, making strong perturbations difficult to use safely in SSL. To address these challenges, we propose Semi-MedRef (Fig. 1), a semi-supervised framework that improves MRIS by explicitly stabilizing cross-modal alignment between images and positional language under strong perturbations. Semi-MedRef integrates three complementary mechanisms. T-PatchMix adapts patch-level mixing to the cross-modal setting by selectively mixing anatomically compatible regions and updating corresponding positional spans, allowing strong perturbation-based augmentation without breaking spatial grounding. PosAug regularizes the language stream by introducing controlled perturbations to posi-tional phrases, encouraging the model to rely on image evidence when textual location cues are partially absent or imprecise. Finally, position-guided image–text contrastive learning (ITCL) converts coarse positional pseudo-labels into explicit cross-modal supervision, shaping a region-aware contrastive objective that re-inforces alignment at representation level. These strategies help Semi-MedRef maintain reliable cross-modal consistency during teacher–student training. Semi-MedRef 3

2.2 Overview 

Figure 1 illustrates Semi-MedRef, our teacher–student framework for SSL-based MRIS. The student receives strongly augmented image–text pairs, combining T-PatchMix for alignment-preserving patch mixing and PosAug for position-aware text perturbation, while the teacher processes weak views to provide stable segmentation pseudo-labels. ## 3 Experiments and Results 

3.1 Datasets, Metrics and Implementation Datasets and Metrics. We evaluate Semi-MedRef on two public MRIS datasets, QaTa-COV19 [6] and MosMedData+ [19], following the official splits. QaTa-COV19 comprises 5,716 training, 1,429 validation, and 2,113 test samples. MosMedData+ comprises 2,183 training, 273 validation, and 273 test samples. The referring texts are structured infection-site descriptions from prior work [12]. For semi-supervised learning, we randomly sample labeled subsets from the training split with ratios r ∈ { 1% , 2% , 5% , 15% } and treat the remaining training samples as unlabeled. Subsequent refinement [25] of QaTa-COV19 provides posi-tion pseudo-labels; we extract the same labels for MosMedData+ and include them in the dataset. We employ Dice and mIoU to evaluate the performance. 

Implementation Details. We implement our method in PyTorch Lightning and MONAI [5], and train on one NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU. Input images are resized to 224 × 224 . Weak augmentation uses RandomZoom. Image strong augmentation applies ColorJitter and GaussianBlur. We optimize with AdamW [15] with an initial learning rate of 3 × 10 −4, cosine annealing to 10 −6, and batch size is 32. For ITCL, we set temperature τ as 0.07, supervised weight λsup itcl as 0.02 and unsupervised weight λunsup itcl as 0.1. For multi-modal augmentation, PosAug dropout probability ρ is 0.5 and block size of T-PatchMix is 16 . Unless noted, we use MMI-UNet [4] with a ConvNeXt [14] visual encoder and a CXR-BERT [3] text encoder. We also report results with alternative backbones to show generality.

3.3 Ablation Study 

Table 2 reports component-wise ablations on QaTa-COV19, indicating each component contributes positive gains, with the full model performing best. Table 3 summarizes augmentation and alignment ablations. Ours-V1 vs TextStrong (85.89% vs. 84.60%) shows that PosAug provides a more effective MRIS-specific text perturbation than generic text augmentations. Ours-V2 vs. CutMix (85.59% vs. 83.09%) demonstrates that T-PatchMix preserves image–text coherence, while naive CutMix disrupts alignment: its performance even drops below the baseline. Finally, Ours-V3 vs. Ours-All (85.35% vs. 86.39%) confirms that ITCL offers stronger cross-modal alignment than a CLIP-style loss in our setting. 

## 4 Conclusion 

This paper presents Semi-MedRef, a semi-supervised teacher–student framework for MRIS that stabilizes cross-modal alignment between images and positional language under strong perturbations. We integrate two alignment-preserving augmentations, PosAug for position-aware text and T-PatchMix for patch mixing Semi-MedRef 9

with synchronized location phrases, together with an ITCL module that provides explicit cross-modal supervision to reinforce alignment. Experiments on QaTa-COV19 and MosMedData+ show that Semi-MedRef consistently outperforms supervised and semi-supervised baselines across all label ratios.
