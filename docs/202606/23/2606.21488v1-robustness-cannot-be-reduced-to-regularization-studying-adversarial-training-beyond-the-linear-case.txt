## Abstract 

The vulnerability of ML models to adversarial examples has recently emerged as a major concern. While adversarial training is one of the most effective counter-measures to this issue, its high computational cost remains an obstacle to practical deployment. Recent progress in reducing this cost has relied, in the case of linear models, on a formal equivalence between the adversarial risk and a simpler form of regularized risk. This enabled significantly more efficient training procedures, which naturally raises the question of whether such an equivalence can be extended beyond linear models. In this work, we formally show that no such equivalence is possible for two-layer networks. Our proofs proceed via a reduction to key proper-ties that fundamentally separate the adversarial risk from any simple regularized risk which would only exhibit a weak form of data dependence. Beyond this setting, we provide empirical evidence on Wide-ResNets indicating that the same type of impossibility persists in deeper and more expressive architectures. 

## 1 Introduction 

Adversarial example attacks recently became a major concern in the machine learning community. An adversarial attack refers to a small, imperceptible change of an input that is maliciously designed to fool a machine learning algorithm [Biggio et al., 2013, Szegedy et al., 2014, Goodfellow et al., 2015]. The vulnerability of state-of-the-art classifiers to such attacks raises significant security concerns, particularly for deep neural networks deployed in safety-critical applications [Kumar et al., 2019, Paleyes et al., 2022, Carlini et al., 2024]. This limitation of existing models can largely be attributed to the standard training paradigm, which focuses on minimizing a point-wise loss L : Θ × X × Y → R

averaged along a ground-truth distribution D over X × Y , i.e., 

R(D; θ) := E(x,y )∼D [L(θ, x, y )] ∀θ ∈ Θ.

However, as noted early on by Goodfellow et al. [2015], this objective does not explicitly encourage the model to exhibit regularity properties beyond minimizing this expected loss (a.k.a. risk). Conse-quently, even when the point-wise loss of a model θ is small on a sample (x, y ) ∼ D , an adversary can construct a perturbed input x + τ (an adversarial example) such that ∥τ ∥ is small under a reference norm (1) ∥·∥ , but for which the loss increases substantially. To account for adversarial manipulations of the inputs, the standard objective must be revised to explicitly incorporate the adversary. The goal becomes minimizing the worst-case expected loss (a.k.a. adversarial risk) under δ-bounded perturbations. This adversarial risk Rδ can be written as

The main challenge in minimizing adversarial risk lies in the intractability of the associated min-max objective. Nevertheless, a substantial body of work has focused on solving this problem approximately through surrogate optimization schemes. These approaches, commonly referred to as adversarial training, currently constitute the state-of-the-art defense against adversarial attacks [Croce and Hein, 2020, Bartoldson et al., 2024]. In essence, adversarial training involves generating, at each optimiza-tion step, an adversarial example for every data point, typically via gradient ascent [Madry et al., 2018, Zhang et al., 2019]. Despite its strong empirical performance, this procedure is computationally demanding, as perturbations must be recomputed at every iteration. Moreover, it remains unclear how closely the resulting solutions approximate the true adversarial optimum. Consequently, under-standing the impact of adversarial training on the learning dynamics of a complex model remains an open problem. 

Actionable rewriting in the linear case. To address these limitations, recent works focused on refor-mulating the adversarial risk in more interpretable/tractable manners, yielding identical robustness at lower computational cost, by expressing the adversarial risk Rδ as a regularized objective of the form 

Rδ (D; θ) = R(D; θ) + λ · Ωδ (θ) ∀θ ∈ Θ, (1) where Ωδ (θ) is an easily computable and interpretable data-independent regularizer, and λ > 0 is a positive hyperparameter. Such reformulations can both clarify the mechanisms behind adversarial training and suggest more efficient surrogate methods for enforcing robustness. In this direction, Roth et al. [2020] showed that adversarial training is equivalent to a form of data-dependent operator norm regularization. While this perspective is valuable for interpretation, the data-dependency of the result does not directly yield a simpler or more efficient surrogate in terms of computational cost. More recently Ribeiro et al. [2023] showed that adversarial training for linear models is equivalent to a parameter-shrinking regularization that is inexpensive to compute. In particular, when L is a margin loss of the form L(x, y, θ ) = L(y⟨x, θ ⟩) where L : R → R is a lower-semicontinuous convex non-increasing function, the adversarial risk Rδ can be written as

This result is particularly promising, as its extension to more complex architectures could enable new interpretations and implementations of adversarial training, potentially unlocking robust learning at scale. It is therefore crucial to investigate whether similar reformulations can be derived for more expressive models.

## 3 Rejecting regularizers by mismatch of minima 

The first property we introduce for ruling out several candidate reformulations is the requirement that equivalent functions have matching minima . Informally, this property states that if two functions are equivalent, then, although their values may differ, their level sets (and thus their minima) must coincide. This property is formalized in Proposition 3.1 and proved in Appendix A.2. 

Proposition 3.1 (Equivalence implies matching restricted minima) . If Ω1 ∼P Ω2 then 

∀U ⊆ Θ, arg min U Ω1 = arg min U Ω2

This simple observation is of both theoretical and practical relevance. From a theoretical perspective, it means that it can be sufficient to analyze the “shape” of the set of minimizers inside an arbitrary region U to disprove equivalence, instead of having to deal with function values or global minima. We develop this idea in the case of two-layer neural networks in Section 3.1. On the other hand, from a practical standpoint, it provides a tool for probing such properties in large neural networks, via what we call slicing . We further devlop the concept of slicing and its use in Section 3.2.

## 4 Rejecting based on misalignment of gradients 

The restriction to subsets U ⊊ Θ in Proposition 3.1 enables the construction of simple counterex-amples and facilitates a visual search for non equivalence in more general architectures. By further localizing the analysis to neighborhoods of a single point θ ∈ Θ, we can rule out significantly larger classes of candidates through analytical arguments. ## 5 Weakening and Bypassing Equivalence 

We have shown that constructing a regularizers Ωδ (D; ·) equivalent to adversarial training, in the sense that Ωδ (D; ·) ∼P Aδ (D; ·), is inherently difficult. In particular, for two layer neural networks, this equivalence cannot be achieved using data-independent regularizations or loss modulations, in contrast to the linear setting of Ribeiro et al. [2023]. This leaves only data-dependent reformulations, such as Roth et al. [2020], which remain as expensive computationally as adversarial training itself. Thus the search for cheaper alternatives to adversarial training cannot rely on a direct rewriting of the adversarial gap. We review two weakenings of this ideal property which could be fruitful directions. 

Weakening equivalence: local control around (D0, θ 0). The obstruction to data-independent surrogates is that A(D; ·) depends non-trivially on the distribution D. In particular, the adversarial influence on weights ∂θ A(D; ·) changes direction when D is altered. Either the definition of robust-ness can be weakened to be more permissive [Pang et al., 2022], or a weaker equivalence can be used. For instance, computing a single simplified equivalent Ω0 ∼P A(D0; ·), for some D0 (e.g. standard gaussian) the approximation could remain usable around D0, such that Ω0 ≈ A(D; ·) when D ≈ D 0.The idea of local control has also been gaining attention in the study of convergence of neural networks by gradient descent. Studying an idealized “typical” case and characterizing a behavior allows to transfer this property to “sufficiently typical” instances. For instance, the random initialization θ0 of a neural network is well understood, especially for very wide networks, and the trajectory around “close enough” parameters ∥θ − θ0∥ ≤ R is shown to have good optimization properties, allowing convergence proofs [Chizat and Bach, 2018, Mei et al., 2018, Pham and Nguyen, 2021] and later quantitative convergence speeds [Scaman et al., 2022, Robin et al., 2022] leveraging this control. 

Bypassing equivalence: over-regularizing for robustness. Another option is to maintain a uniform claim over D by lowering its strength, such as a regularization that is not equivalent to the adversarial gap, but does enforce robustness without degrading performance too much. A natural option is the control of the global Lipschitz constant of the network, with estimates [Weng et al., 2018] or explicit bounds [Scaman and Virmaux, 2018, Piat et al., 2022], since a sufficiently regular network is robust by construction [Luxburg and Bousquet, 2004, Bartlett et al., 2017, Neyshabur et al., 2017]. The major obstacle is that there is no guarantee that learning such a function is possible. Indeed, many regularization attempts have shown large decreases in accuracy [Atsague et al., 2021, Sriramanan et al., 2021, Wang et al., 2024] and several optimization problems [Waseda et al., 2025]. Theory-guided surrogates built with guarantees such as L(θ+) ≤ (1 + ε) · L (θ⋆) [see Bartlett and Wegkamp, 2008] ensure the surrogate θ+ has manageable suboptimality gap (even ε ≈ 1). With no such guarantees, it is unclear whether a surrogate would be usable without extensive experiments. B.1 AT-PGD sampling procedure 

Samples are drawn from the CIFAR-10 dataset ( torchvision.datasets.CIFAR10 ) and aug-mented using random crops and horizonal flips (respectively v2.RandomCrop(32, padding=4) and  

> v2.RandomHorizontalFlip(p=0.5)

using torch.transforms.v2 ). After sampling (x, y ) ∈ Rd × [K], a 10-step gradient ascent is applied to generate an adversarial sample for L(θ, ·, y ) : Rd → R, as xk+1 = P (xk + η0 gk) where gk = sgn( ∂xL(θ, x k, y )) ∈ {± 1}d

and P is the projection to the convex set {u ∈ [0 , 1] d | ∥ u − x∥∞ ≤ δ}. We use initial uniform random noise, that is to say x0 = P (x + δ · ε) where ε ∈ Rd has independent identically distributed coordinates with εi ∼ U (−1, +1) . The numerical values used are δ = 8 /255 ≈ 0.3137 and stepsize 

η0 = 0 .2 × δ. This ascent procedure is performed in parallel on batches of 64 samples. This is strictly speaking closer to a multi-step FGSM (Fast Gradient Sign Method, see Goodfellow et al. [2015]) procedure, but has been referred to as AT-PGD since at least Madry et al. [2018, Section 2.1], so we keep the historical naming convention.

B.3 WideResNet-34-10 on CIFAR-10 classification 

For Wide ResNets, we use the implementation of https://github.com/RobustBench/robustbench, with depth 34 and widening factor of 10, with the default initialization of the implementation provided. We train networks with AT-PGD samples and SGD optimizer ( torch.optim.SGD ) with a cosine scheduler starting with step size η0 = 10 −1 and ending at η = 10 −3 after 100 epochs, with a momentum parameter of μ = 0 .9. When freezing the first neuron of the last layer ( model.fc.weight with shape 

10 × 640 ), we also reset momentum optimizer parameters to ensure that no momentum persists across the freeze, we multiply the learning rate of the first neuron by zero, and we check numerically that the parameters of this neuron remain constant between checkpoints. On a single A100 GPU, training with this procedure takes between four and five minutes per epoch, for a total (20 epochs for Fig. 1b and Fig. 1c and 100 for Fig. 2) of nearly nine hours. For Figure 1b and Figure 1c, each pixel requires a full pass on the training data, computing a single derivative with respect to the input, taking approximately thirty to forty-five seconds.
