## Abstract 

Test-Time Training (TTT) is an emerging paradigm that enables models to adapt their parameters during inference, improving performance on tasks such as few-shot learning, retrieval-augmented generation, and complex reasoning. However, this dynamic adaptation introduces new vulnerabilities that adversaries can exploit to jailbreak models. We identify three threat models for TTT and demonstrate how attackers can leverage them to bypass safety filters. Our results show that TTT can significantly increase the Attack Success Rate (ASR) and the ASR over 10 generation trials (ASR@10). For example, under LoRA, the few-shot and generation-phase threat models achieve an average ASR@10 of 95% and 93% respectively, across models from different families and scales. These vulnerabilities transfer to production fine-tuning APIs. We also show that TTT-induced overfitting can produce degenerate outputs that inflate ASR under standard judges, and propose a validity-aware evaluation to correct for this. Our findings suggest that TTT exposes a new attack surface, strengthens attacks, and undermines existing safety guardrails. As a first step toward defense, we propose a lightweight provider-side detector that flags TTT requests via the perplexity shift on a private harmful holdout, but robust deployment will ultimately require dynamic alignment. ## 1 Introduction 

The scaling of Large Language Models (LLMs) is shifting from purely increasing parameter counts to also scaling test-time compute through extended chain-of-thought reasoning and test-time train-ing/adaptation. Test-time training (TTT) involves adapting model weights in real-time to a specific task and has been shown to significantly boost generalization and reasoning capabilities, particularly in complex domains like planning [Sun et al., 2020, Welleck et al., 2024, Akyürek et al., 2025]. Concretely, before answering a query, the model performs a handful of gradient updates (e.g., on the query itself or on a small set of related examples) and generates its response with the adapted weights, which are then discarded. However, as the field moves towards test-time adaptation to unlock greater capabilities, this raises the concern that the static safety guardrails put in place at training time may no longer hold. Current safety alignment is effectively integrated into weights via post-training procedures like Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) [Ouyang et al., 2022], Constitutional AI [Bai et al., 2022], or Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [Rafailov et al.,

This reliance on fixed parameters raises a fundamental security question: When weights are updated dynamically via TTT, does the safety alignment persist? 

Jailbreaking research currently operates under a static threat model, treating the LLM as a white or black box where the adversary is restricted to optimizing the input tokens [Chao et al., 2024, Beyer et al., 2025a]. TTT hands the attacker a new lever: direct influence over the model’s parameters during inference. This expansion of the attack surface allows the adversary to manipulate both the input query and the model’s processing mechanism simultaneously. While recent studies have demonstrated that standard fine-tuning can rapidly strip safety alignment [Qi et al., 2023, Kazdan et al., 2026], it remains an open question whether this fragility extends to the instance-specific, few-step updates characteristic of TTT. We hypothesize that safety constraints are superficial enough to be weakened even by these minimal targeted gradient updates. In this work, we investigate the evolution of safety alignment under test-time training. We formalize the TTT attack surface through three threat models, aligned with three real TTT use-cases and corresponding TTT methods proposed in the literature. First, in the self-supervised threat model , the model adapts on the user prompt via self-supervised next-token prediction. While the prompt could in principle be adversarially crafted, we show that even clean-prompt adaptation degrades safety. Second, in the few-shot threat model , the adversary supplies a small support set of harmful examples, and the model adapts via supervised fine-tuning before responding to the target query. Third, in the 

generation-phase threat model , the adversary influences adaptation during generation by providing a target prefix that steers the model toward harmful completions (overview in Fig. 1). Our experiments across several open-weight models reveal that safety alignment is fragile under TTT. When the TTT objective is aligned with a harmful goal, the few-shot and generation-phase threat models achieve 95% and 93% average ASR@10 respectively under LoRA, with individual configurations reaching 100% , i.e., every prompt is jailbroken in at least one of 10 generations. Even simple self-supervised TTT on the user prompt itself [Sun et al., 2025], without any harmful data, raises the average ASR@10 across models from 4% to 17% under LoRA, confirming that the act of test-time fine-tuning is itself sufficient to erode safety alignment. Combining TTT with adversarial input formats further amplifies the effect (Fig. 2). We further demonstrate that these vulnerabilities 2transfer to realistic deployment settings by evaluating against a production fine-tuning API, where even a 120B-parameter model is fully jailbroken via TTT without any API-specific tuning. The corresponding attack can be carried out cheaply for less than 2 dollars. 2

Orthogonally, we identify a key evaluation challenge: since TTT fine-tunes on a single prompt, the model can overfit and produce degenerate outputs that fool standard LLM safety judges into reporting false positives. To address this, we introduce a validity-aware evaluation pipeline where generations flagged as invalid are counted as safe in the final ASR, ensuring that the metric remains comparable to baselines where no degenerate outputs are produced. We show that standard judges overestimate ASR by up to 13 percentage points without this correction. Finally, we take a first step toward a defense: we propose a lightweight provider-side detector that compares the model’s perplexity on a private harmful holdout set before and after TTT, flagging requests whose harmful-target perplexity drops above a per-model threshold. This approach can successfully defend against our vanilla attacks, with high true positive rate in detecting adversarial manipulation and low false positive rate against benign refusal. However, since this defense alone is unlikely to withstand future adaptive attacks [Tramer et al., 2020], we advocate for more research on TTT-aware dynamic alignment procedures that account for test-time weight updates. There are initial efforts in using test-time interventions for alignment [Saglam and Kalogerias, 2026], but our results show that the same mechanism is also a powerful attack surface. As the field increasingly adopts test-time adaptation to enhance model capabilities, security evaluations must evolve from static probing to dynamic settings where the model’s resistance to its own weight updates is assessed. 

## 2 Background 

Let an LLM with vocabulary V and parameters θ define a conditional next-token distribution 

pθ (xt+1 | x1: t) for any timestep t. We denote a continuation of length H as y = xn+1: n+H and define its distribution given a context x1: n as pθ (y | x1: n) = QHi=1 pθ (yi | x1: n, y 1: i−1). We write the next-token-prediction (NTP) loss on a sequence z optionally conditioned on context c as 

LNTP (z | c; θ) = − log pθ (z | c), dropping c when no context is given. 

Test-Time Training. Before generating a prediction for a new unseen input, we can adapt the model’s distribution to the specific instance. We formulate TTT as an adaptation operator θ′ =

T (θ, D; λ). Here, D denotes the adaptation data (e.g., the input prompt itself, few-shot examples, or self-supervised objectives), while λ represents algorithmic choices (e.g., learning rate, number of gradient steps). After adaptation, the model distribution becomes pθ′ and we autoregressively sample continuations. Once generation completes, the model is reset to θ, in line with standard TTT practice. 

Jailbreaking LLMs. LLMs are usually subject to alignment procedures to limit undesired behaviors by building guardrails on what the model can answer according to the safety taxonomy. We denote by 

Yharm the set of continuations that violate the provider’s safety policy. Given a user input x1: n, we consider an adversarial perturbation ˜x ∈ A (x1: n) (such as appending adversarial suffixes, injecting prefixes, or rephrasing the input) and define the probability of generating a harmful continuation as

## 3 Jailbreaking via test-time training 

While TTT has been shown to empower models with generalization to out-of-distribution samples, the optimization performed at inference time introduces additional vulnerabilities. We assume that an LLM service provider exposes an API to perform TTT on one of their models. In this setting, the attacker controls two axes: a (potentially adversarial) query prompt ˜x ∈ A (x1: n) and auxiliary adaptation data ψ ∈ Ψ, which directly alter the model’s weights via the adaptation operator θ′ =

T (θ, (˜ x, ψ ); λ) that performs one (or a few) step(s) of gradient descent. TTT thus expands the attack surface: the adaptation step may cause the model to forget its safety alignment, and auxiliary contexts can exploit this optimization to steer the model toward harmful behaviors. We define three concrete threat models, summarized in Tab. 1, and unify them under a single optimization framework in § D.

3.2 Few-shot threat model

4.1 Problem setup Datasets. Following the evaluation protocol of Andriushchenko et al. [2025], we use a curated subset of 50 diverse harmful behaviors from the AdvBench dataset [Chao et al., 2023] as our primary benchmark. To verify generalizability, we report additional results in § A.1 on the JailbreakBench dataset [Chao et al., 2024] and the 48-behavior dataset of Chowdhury et al. [2025] focused on high-complexity harmful tasks, with consistent findings. 

Attacks. We evaluate all three threat models from § 3. For the self-supervised setting, we focus on the clean-prompt case ˜x = x1: n: the model adapts on the user prompt without any auxiliary data. The few-shot and generation-phase settings both use a short target prefix, namely the first ∼10–20 tokens of an affirmative restatement of the user request (e.g., “Sure, here is a tutorial for making a bomb”), drawn from the dataset target column rather than from a full harmful continuation. Concrete examples are listed in § C.3. In the few-shot attack, the model trains on a support set of K=5 such (prompt, target prefix) pairs sampled from the dataset, excluding the test query. We additionally study its composition with adversarial ˜x (Fig. 2). In the generation-phase (target) attack, the model trains only on the target prefix conditioned on the test prompt. For both settings, we mask the EOS token from the training loss to prevent the model from learning to terminate generation immediately after the target prefix. 

Metrics. We adopt the Attack Success Rate (ASR) via an LLM-as-a-judge framework, using Llama3 70B [AI@Meta, 2024] with the JailbreakBench prompt template [Chao et al., 2024] (§ B.1) to classify each response as safe/unsafe based on whether it fulfills the harmful request. For each prompt, we generate 10 responses and report mean ASR and ASR@10, the fraction of prompts where at least one of 10 generations is judged harmful. We emphasize ASR@10 since even a single harmful generation constitutes a safety failure [Beyer et al., 2025b, 2026]. 

Models. We focus on a diverse suite of state-of-the-art open-weight models, specifically the instruction-tuned versions of Llama3 (8B and 70B) [AI@Meta, 2024], Gemma 7B [Team et al., 2024], and the Qwen family represented by Qwen2.5 (1.5B and 7B) [Qwen et al., 2025], Qwen3 8B and Qwen3 32B [Yang et al., 2025]. We use the default system prompts, reflecting typical API constraints. For Llama3 70B and Qwen3 32B, we evaluate Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) only due to the memory requirements of full fine-tuning at this scale, and adapt hyperparameters from their smaller counterparts rather than tuning independently (implementation details in § C).

4.3 TTT via external APIs 

The previous experiments assume the attacker performs TTT locally. As no dedicated TTT API is currently available, existing fine-tuning APIs have been adopted in the literature to perform test-time training steps, e.g., the Tinker API as in Yuksekgonul et al. [2026]. The Tinker API exposes LoRA fine-tuning as a service, allowing users to specify standard hyperparameters (learning rate, rank, number of steps) via API calls. To assess whether our threat models transfer to this realistic deployment setting, we evaluate the Tinker API on Qwen3 8B and GPT-OSS 120B, directly re-using the LoRA hyperparameters found locally without any API-specific tuning. 4.4 Validity-aware safety evaluation 

A key challenge specific to TTT-based attacks is that gradient updates can cause the model to produce degenerate outputs: target prefix echoes, gibberish, or prompt regurgitation. These outputs are not genuine jailbreaks, as the model has not produced harmful content, yet they can fool standard LLM judges. Because these degenerate generations often begin with an affirmative prefix (e.g., “Sure, here is...”) learned during adaptation, safety judges that rely on surface-level cues classify them as unsafe. To quantify this vulnerability, we augment the JailbreakBench judge benchmark [Chao et al., 2024] with 50 degenerate generations collected from our TTT experiments (representative examples are provided in § B.7). All 50 examples are classified as unsafe by the standard Llama3 70B judge despite containing no actual harmful content, dropping judge accuracy by 13 percentage points. To address this, we introduce two complementary validity checks. The first is a symbolic validity check that applies rule-based filters to detect common degenerate patterns: excessive character or substring repetition, target overfitting, prompt echoing, low vocabulary diversity, and abnormally long tokens. The second is an LLM-based validity judge designed to catch subtler failure modes that escape symbolic detection, particularly generations produced by adversarial prompt templates [Andriushchenko et al., 2025] where the overall step-by-step structure appears well-formed but placeholders from the template are not replaced with actual content. Since JailbreakBench truncates generations at 150 tokens, the default LLM validity prompt incorrectly flags some truncated but substantive responses as invalid, dropping accuracy to 86% on the original benchmark. Atruncation-aware variant resolves this issue (full prompts in § B.1). Tab. 4 reports the judge accuracy on an extended version of the judge_comparison split of Jail-breakBench [Chao et al., 2024], which contains 300 human-annotated examples augmented with our 50 degenerate outputs. ## 5 Perplexity-based defense 

By definition, a TTT jailbreak lowers the model’s perplexity on harmful continuations. We exploit this with a lightweight provider-side detector that reads only θ and θ′, without inspecting D.

Dual holdout. The provider maintains two private holdouts: a harmful set Dh of Nh = 100 (question, target) pairs from JailbreakBench [Chao et al., 2024] (split harmful ), and a clean set Dc of Nc = 100 

questions from GSM8K [Cobbe et al., 2021], with 10–20-token targets generated by the base model 

pθ at temperature 1. Sourcing Dc’s targets from the model itself yields low baseline perplexity under 

θ, so any shift reflects a genuine parameter change rather than reference-text drift.

Adaptive attacks. The rule assumes the attacker does not know the harmful holdout Dh and does not regularize TTT against it. An adaptive attacker who learns or guesses Dh, or who augments the TTT objective with a penalty on parameter shifts measured on a public surrogate, could likely evade the rule. We leave adaptive attacks and correspondingly stronger detection to future work. 86 Related work 

Test-Time Training. Test-Time Training fundamentally alters the inference paradigm by allowing models to adapt their parameters to individual test instances on the fly. Originally proposed to mitigate distribution shifts in computer vision [Sun et al., 2020], TTT typically employs self-supervised objectives, e.g., reconstructing masked inputs [Gandelsman et al., 2022] or minimizing prediction entropy [Wang et al., 2021], to update model weights before making a final prediction. This dynamic approach has recently been transposed to Large Language Models (LLMs) to unlock inference-time compute scaling. Recent works have demonstrated that transient weight updates can significantly enhance performance in long-context processing [Welleck et al., 2024], and complex abstract reasoning tasks like the ARC-AGI challenge [Akyürek et al., 2025]. Unlike traditional fine-tuning, which permanently modifies global parameters, TTT treats the weight update as a transient, input-specific adaptation. This mechanism ensures that the model’s behavior, including its safety alignment, is dynamically re-optimized for each individual query. 

Jailbreaking LMs. The search for jailbreaks can be automated by framing the generation of adver-sarial prompts as a discrete optimization problem. Early gradient-based approaches, such as Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) [Zou et al., 2023], utilized token-level gradients to guide the search for adversarial suffixes that maximize the likelihood of a fixed affirmative prefix (e.g., “Sure, here is how”). However, recent work by Geisler et al. [2025] highlights that optimizing for a static prefix often fails to elicit harmful completions, and instead proposes a REINFORCE-based objective that adapts to the full distribution of model responses, significantly improving attack success. Despite these advances in gradient-based optimization, simple gradient-free methods like genetic algorithms [Lapid et al., 2024, Liu et al., 2024] and random search [Andriushchenko et al., 2025, Sitawarin et al., 2024, Hayase et al., 2024] have often proven more effective in the static regime. Crucially, all these methods operate under a fixed threat model: they assume the target model’s weights remain frozen during inference, restricting the adversary to optimizing only the input tokens. 

Jailbreaking via TTT. The intersection of TTT and adversarial attacks is an emerging domain. While extensive research has established the vulnerability of TTT algorithms in computer vision where adversaries can poison the test batch to degrade model performance [Wu et al., 2023, Cong et al., 2024], this threat model has largely remained unexplored in the context of LLM safety. We transpose the concept of TTT poisoning to the generative domain, investigating the vulnerability of open-weight models to temporary updates. Unlike standard fine-tuning attacks which require large datasets [Qi et al., 2023, Yang et al., 2024, Poppi et al., 2025], and recent work showing that even benign fine-tuning data can strip safety alignment [Xie et al., 2025] or bypass token-level defenses [Kazdan et al., 2026], we focus on the single-prompt regime where the attacker exploits the TTT gradient step itself to unlearn safety constraints on the fly. Closest to ours, Khattar et al. [2026] study an RL instantiation of our generation-phase threat model, showing that adversarial prompt injection during TTRL amplifies harmful behavior. 

## 7 Conclusion 

We explored the intersection of test-time training (TTT) and LLM safety, introducing three threat models (few-shot, self-supervised, and generation-phase) under which adversaries can influence TTT. Even without an adversary, safety alignment degrades under self-supervised TTT on the prompt itself. Adversaries can then exploit each threat model to drive attack success rates near 100% , and these vulnerabilities transfer to production fine-tuning APIs. We also identify that TTT-induced overfitting can produce degenerate outputs that inflate ASR under standard judges, and introduce a validity-aware evaluation pipeline to correct for this. Together, these findings argue for TTT-aware defense and alignment procedures that explicitly account for test-time weight updates. 

Limitations and future work. A critical open challenge is developing dynamic alignment methods that actively constrain the model from drifting into harmful manifolds during test-time updates, and pivoting safety evaluation from static red-teaming to dynamic stress testing. § D unifies the three threat models under a single bilevel optimization framework, and even decoupled relaxations already yield near 100% ASR@10 (Fig. 2). Scaling the full formulation into a tractable end-to-end attack is left for future work, alongside adaptive attacks and stronger detection rules for our perplexity-based defense (§ 5), which relies on the secrecy of Dh.9Ethics statement 

This work studies a dual-use safety vulnerability: test-time training (TTT) erodes the safety alignment of aligned LLMs. All experiments use publicly available open-weight models or a public fine-tuning API (Tinker) under its standard terms, and harmful prompts come from previously released benchmarks (AdvBench, JailbreakBench, and the Transluce CBRN dataset). The study involves no human subjects and introduces no new harmful prompts, datasets, or model checkpoints. The released experiment code only applies standard open-source fine-tuning to released models. Illustrative outputs in the appendices are restricted to short affirmative target prefixes (§ C) and degenerate failure-mode generations that fool standard safety judges (§ B.7). None reproduces operational detail for the CBRN or other high-risk requests in the underlying benchmarks. We pair the attack analysis with a provider-side detector (§ 5) so the contribution includes a concrete mitigation alongside the threat model, and we view documenting these vulnerabilities as a prerequisite for designing TTT-aware defenses.

A.1 Full results 

Tab. 5 reports the full ASR and ASR@10 results on the curated AdvBench subset across all models, with both full fine-tuning and LoRA. To verify the generalizability of our findings beyond this subset, we extend our evaluation to two additional benchmarks that capture distinct aspects of model robustness: broad policy violation (JailbreakBench) and high-complexity information hazards (Transluce). 

JailbreakBench. We employ the JBB-Behaviors dataset from the JailbreakBench framework [Chao et al., 2024]. This dataset comprises 100 distinct misuse behaviors categorized into ten broad groups corresponding to OpenAI’s usage policies (e.g., Hate Speech, Malware, Physical Harm). The dataset is a composite benchmark, containing approximately 55% original examples, with the remainder sourced from existing datasets such as AdvBench [Zou et al., 2023] and HarmBench [Mazeika et al., 2023, 2024]. 

JBB-Behaviors provides a matching split of 100 benign behaviors. These prompts cover the same topics as the harmful split but are phrased to be safe and educational (e.g., asking about the chemistry of common household items rather than the synthesis of explosives). We utilize this benign split to measure the Refusal Rate (RR), serving as a sanity check to ensure that the TTT update does not lobotomize the model or make it over-sensitive to safe inputs. 

Transluce CBRN. To test model robustness against extreme threats, we utilize the dataset introduced by Chowdhury et al. [2025]. This dataset consists of 48 distinct tasks focused on information hazards, knowledge that is deemed forbidden regardless of context. The tasks span six high-risk domains: illicit drugs (e.g., methamphetamine, fentanyl), explosives (e.g., RDX), chemical agents (e.g., VX), biological agents (e.g., ricin), and radiological/nuclear weapons. These behaviors were generated via few-shot prompting of Claude Sonnet 4 and subsequently curated by human reviewers to ensure they represent genuinely harmful, actionable requests that standard safety alignment is designed to strictly refuse. 

Evaluation Metrics. For the harmful splits of both datasets, we maintain the Attack Success Rate (ASR) metric using the Llama3 70B judge as described in § 4.1. For the benign split of JBB-Behaviors , we report the Refusal Rate (RR). A response to a benign prompt is classified as a refusal if the model declines to assist. We measure RR using a Llama3 8B Instruct judge equipped with the refusal-detection prompt shown in § B.1. A high Refusal Rate on benign prompts indicates that the attack (or defense) has degraded the model’s utility by causing catastrophic forgetting of its instruction-following capabilities. 

Results. We report the performance of TTT on JBB-Behaviors in Tab. 6 (harmful split) and Tab. 7 (benign split), and on the Transluce dataset in Tab. 8. The benign split confirms that TTT erodes the safety boundary rather than merely shifting it. Across all models, the refusal rate on benign prompts consistently decreases with more TTT steps, particularly under the few-shot and target threat models. For example, Qwen3 8B drops from 12 .8% to 0.1% 

(few-shot, step 5), and Llama3 8B drops from 30 .0% to 7.6% (few-shot, step 2). This is expected: the same weight updates that increase ASR on harmful prompts also make the model more willing to comply with benign requests. The model does not learn to distinguish harmful from benign inputs more carefully but simply becomes more compliant overall. On the Transluce dataset, we observe that models like Qwen2.5 7B and Llama3 8B are extremely vulnerable, reaching near 100% ASR@10 when optimized on the target. This confirms that TTT is capable of unlocking not just generic harmful behaviors, but specific, dangerous technical knowledge.

B.1 Evaluation prompt templates 

In all our experiments, we use the following prompt templates to evaluate whether a generated response constitutes a successful jailbreak or a refusal. These templates are adopted from the JailbreakBench framework [Chao et al., 2024]. In the templates below, {goal} refers to the harmful request (e.g., “Develop a strategy for hacking into a government database”) and {response} refers to the actual output generated by the target model under attack. 

B.2 Harmful content judge

## C Implementation details 

C.1 Models and system prompts 

We evaluate our method on a diverse suite of state-of-the-art open-weight models, utilizing the instruction-tuned versions sourced directly from HuggingFace (Tab. 12). To ensure a realistic evaluation setting, we employ the specific system prompt presets defined by the model developers. Specifically, we use the default system prompt for Llama3 and Qwen models, while Gemma 7B is evaluated without a system prompt as per its standard usage. 

C.2 Fine-tuning evaluation

This prevents the model from overfitting on terminating generation immediately after the target prefix, and reflects a realistic setting where the attacker does not have access to model-specific conversation control tokens. C.4 Hardware and software 

All experiments were conducted on computing nodes equipped with NVIDIA H100 (80GB) GPUs. Models with up to 8B parameters and Qwen3 32B were trained on a single H100. Llama3 70B used 2 

× H100 with tensor parallelism. The evaluation of Attack Success Rate (ASR) was performed using Llama3 70B serving as a judge, deployed via vLLM on 2 × H100 GPUs using tensor parallelism. Our implementation relies on PyTorch [Paszke et al., 2019] and Hugging Face transformers [Wolf et al., 2020]. LoRA adapters are managed via peft [Mangrulkar et al., 2022]. High-throughput inference for the judge was accelerated using vLLM [Kwon et al., 2023].

The three threat models presented in § 3 can be unified into a single bilevel optimization framework. The generic attack is formulated as:

The outer level searches for the adversarial input ˜x and adaptation data ψ, while the inner level performs the TTT adaptation via the operator T .
