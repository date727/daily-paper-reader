Abstract 

Offline multi-objective optimization (Offline MOO) aims to discover novel Pareto-optimal designs based on static datasets without expensive environment interactions. While recent generative methods have achieved notable success, they pre-dominantly rely on external surrogate models. This depen-dency introduces significant computational overhead, suffers from deceptive evaluations, and deviates from the prevailing paradigm of jointly training mainstream generative models with conditions. To address these bottlenecks, we propose ParetoPilot, a novel zero-surrogate diffusion framework for offline MOO. ParetoPilot fully leverages the conditional pri-ors inherently embedded within pre-trained diffusion models. At its core, the framework introduces the Infer-Perturb-Guide (IPG) engine, which is seamlessly interleaved within the un-conditional denoising steps of the reverse generation process. First, it implicitly infers the instantaneous objective direction by matching conditional and unconditional noise predictions. Next, it mathematically orthogonalizes a parallel gravity field for strict convergence and an edgeness-aware repulsive force for mutual diversity, creating a dynamically annealed pertur-bation vector. Finally, this perturbed target seamlessly steers the generation process via standard Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG). Extensive experiments across 51 tasks demonstrate that ParetoPilot outperforms 14 state-of-the-art surrogate-based and inverse generative baselines. By eliminating auxil-iary proxy training, our approach preserves data privacy while achieving hypervolume improvement and robust Pareto front coverage. 

## Introduction 

Offline Multi-Objective Optimization (Offline MOO) is ubiquitous in real-world problems. Rather than simply screening existing samples, it aims to learn the underly-ing joint distribution from a static, pre-collected dataset and subsequently generate a set of novel Pareto-optimal designs through extrapolation or interpolation, and without further environment interactions. This paradigm is essential in real-world applications such as drug discovery (Antoniuk et al. 2026), material design (Fromer and Coley 2023), and hard-ware architecture search (Lu et al. 2023), where online func-tion evaluations are prohibitively expensive or strictly lim-ited. Unlike single-objective optimization, MOO requires simultaneously satisfying two inherently conflicting goals:

Convergence (pushing solutions toward the unobserved true Pareto Front) and Diversity (ensuring uniform and broad coverage across the objective trade-off space). To tackle these challenges, recent advances have pre-dominantly adopted an external surrogate paradigm. For in-stance, inverse generative approaches utilizing Flow Match-ing or Diffusion Models (e.g., ParetoFlow (Yuan et al. 2025), PGD-MOO (Annadani et al. 2026)) have achieved state-of-the-art results by relying on external proxy regressors or preference classifiers to guide the generative process. How-ever, this heavy reliance on auxiliary models introduces two practical bottlenecks. First, training separate surrogates or preference models for each objective incurs significant com-putational overhead and demands extensive hyperparameter tuning. Second, these external proxies are unreliable during extrapolation, frequently outputting deceptive scores for in-valid out-of-distribution designs and inducing severe man-ifold collapse. Third, and most importantly, training task-specific proxies necessitates continuous access to the origi-nal offline dataset, which fundamentally violates the prevail-ing foundation model paradigm of deploying models solely as weights (Bommasani et al. 2021) and proves strictly in-feasible under stringent data privacy regulations (Kaissis et al. 2020). In contemporary generative models, a ubiquitous prac-tice for handling labeled datasets is to train a conditional diffusion model jointly with the labels, and subsequently employ Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) (Ho and Salimans 2022) during sampling. This standard practice provides a broad and solid foundation for our approach. To overcome the aforementioned limitations, we propose ParetoPilot , a novel Zero-Surrogate framework for offline MOO. Our core motivation is to fully exploit the conditional priors inher-ently embedded within a standard, well-trained conditional diffusion model. By eliminating the need for any exter-nal surrogates or preference classifiers, ParetoPilot oper-ates seamlessly without breaking the widely-used CFG sam-pling mechanism, allowing it to be deployed directly on the learned distribution while eliminating external training over-head and strictly preserving data privacy. At the heart of ParetoPilot lies the IPG engine, which is directly formulated upon the reverse generation process of conditional diffusion models by interleaving its operations within the unconditional denoising steps:

## Background 

Offline Multi-Objective Optimization 

Without loss of generality, we consider a minimization prob-lem involving m distinct and potentially conflicting objec-tive functions. Let X ⊂ Rd denote the design space and 

Y ⊂ Rm denote the objective space. The goal of MOO is to optimize a vector-valued black-box function f (x) = [f1(x), . . . , f m(x)] ⊤. MOO relies on the concept of Pareto dominance to compare different designs. A design xA is said to strictly Pareto dominate another design xB (denoted as xA ≺ xB ) if fi(xA) ≤ fi(xB ) for all i ∈ { 1, . . . , m } and there exists at least one index j such that fj (xA) < f j (xB ). A design x∗ is considered Pareto-optimal if no other design in the feasible space can Pareto dominate it. The set of all Pareto-optimal designs is known as the Pareto Set, and their corresponding coordinates in the objective space constitute the Pareto Front. In the offline setting, the true objective function f (x) is entirely inaccessible for online queries. Instead, the opti-mization algorithm is strictly constrained to a pre-collected, static dataset D = {(x(i), y (i))}Ni=1 , where x(i) ∈ X is a previously evaluated design and y(i) = f (x(i)) ∈ Y

is its corresponding objective vector. Although the Pareto front of the offline dataset yD,pareto is accessible, the op-timal boundary implicitly extrapolated and interpolated by the generative model ϵθ typically lies beyond and extends further yD,pareto . Consequently, generative methods cannot simply condition the sampling process on yD,pareto or an ar-bitrary static extreme target. Instead, the optimization must be formulated as a step-by-step gradual approach to dynam-ically navigate the latent space and approximate the implic-itly learned ypareto .

Conditional Diffusion Models and Classifier-Free Guidance 

Diffusion models (Ho, Jain, and Abbeel 2020) are a class of generative models that learn to synthesize data through a parameterized Markov chain. The forward process gradu-ally corrupts a data sample x0 ∼ q(x) by injecting Gaussian noise over T timesteps, producing a sequence of increas-ingly noisy latent states x1, . . . , x T . The reverse denoising process aims to iteratively recover the original data from a standard Gaussian noise distribution xT ∼ N (0 , I ) using a neural network ϵθ trained to predict the injected noise. To enable conditional generation, the denoising neural network is typically conditioned on an external signal y,forming the conditional noise predictor ϵθ (xt, y, t ). A ubiq-uitous strategy to enhance the alignment between the gener-ated samples and the given condition is Classifier-Free Guid-ance (CFG) (Ho and Salimans 2022). During the training phase of a CFG-enabled diffusion model, the condition y

is randomly replaced with a null token ∅ with a predefined dropout probability, allowing the single network to simul-taneously learn both the conditional distribution ϵθ (xt, y, t )

and the unconditional data distribution ϵθ (xt, ∅, t ).During the sampling phase, CFG changes the noise pre-diction by pushing the conditional estimate away from the unconditional estimate. The modified noise prediction ˜ϵt is computed as follows: 

˜ϵt = ϵθ (xt, ∅, t ) + w · (ϵθ (xt, y, t ) − ϵθ (xt, ∅, t )) (1) where w is the guidance scale. In standard supervised generation tasks, the target condi-tion y is explicitly provided by the user. However, in offline MOO, the optimal objective coordinate ypareto correspond-ing to a promising but unobserved design is inherently un-known. 

## Related Work 

Online Multi-Objective Optimization. Adaptive experi-mental design has extensively explored MOO in an on-line sequential fashion. The most established framework in this domain is Multi-Objective Bayesian Optimization (MOBO), which iteratively updates probabilistic surrogate models using newly acquired environment data to guide the search for the Pareto front. Typical implementations in-clude the standard MOBO-Vallina based on expected hy-pervolume improvement (Daulton, Balandat, and Bakshy 2021), MOBO-ParEGO utilizing random scalarizations to decompose the objectives (Knowles 2006), and MOBO-JES employing information-theoretic joint entropy search (Hvarfner, Hutter, and Nardi 2022). While highly effective in data-scarce regimes, these sequential approaches require continuous queries to the black-box environment. Conse-quently, they are entirely inapplicable to offline settings. 

Offline Multi-Objective Optimization. To address the critical limitations of online queries, offline MOO restricts the optimization process entirely to a static, pre-collected dataset. The conventional forward paradigm involves train-ing deep neural networks as proxy evaluators on the of-fline data, followed by applying evolutionary search algo-rithms like NSGA-II (Deb et al. 2002) to find optimal de-signs. Recent benchmarking efforts (Xue et al. 2024) cat-egorize these forward surrogates into End-to-End architec-tures, Multi-Head models, and Multiple Independent Mod-els. To mitigate the inherent inaccuracies of proxy mod-els, various advanced regularization and loss balancing tech-niques have been widely integrated. These include gradi-ent manipulation methods such as GradNorm (Chen et al. 2018) and PcGrad (Yu et al. 2020) applied to End-to-End and Multi-Head architectures. Furthermore, robust adapta-tion and self-training strategies, including Conservative Ob-jective Models (COMs) (Trabucco et al. 2021), RoMA (Yu et al. 2021), IOM (Qi et al. 2022), ICT (Yuan et al. 2023), and Tri-Mentoring (Chen et al. 2023), are frequently em-ployed alongside Multiple Models to stabilize the predic-tions. Despite these extensive enhancements, forward meth-ods suffer significantly from out-of-distribution vulnerabil-ity. When the search algorithm extrapolates beyond the training distribution to discover better designs, these static surrogates inevitably produce overconfident and erroneous predictions, leading to invalid solutions and manifold col-lapse. 

Generative Inverse Approaches. To mitigate the out-of-distribution evaluation issue, inverse generative approaches attempt to model the conditional distribution directly. For example, ParetoFlow (Yuan et al. 2025) proposes a guided flow matching framework that decomposes the multi-objective problem using uniformly distributed weight vec-tors, heavily relying on separately trained scalarized proxy predictors. PGD-MOO (Annadani et al. 2026) introduces a classifier-guided diffusion approach, where an external pref-erence classifier is trained on pairs of noised offline data to predict Pareto dominance, explicitly incorporating a crowd-ing distance metric to encourage diversity. While these gen-erative methods achieve remarkable results, they fail to es-cape the paradigm of relying on trained surrogate mod-els, which severely limits their applicability to broad down-stream tasks. In practical deployment scenarios, generative models are typically shared solely as pre-trained weights without the original training datasets, rendering the down-stream training of task-specific proxies highly difficult or even impossible. Lalign (yt) = ∥ϵθ (xt, y t, t ) − ϵθ (xt, ∅, t )∥22 (2) Minimizing this loss via gradient descent yields the instan-taneous anchor direction. However, the noise predictions ϵθ

at large timesteps t are highly stochastic, causing significant high-frequency jitter across the diffusion steps if we opti-mize yt naively. To filter out this inherent stochasticity, we leverage the in-ternal momentum mechanics of an un-reset Adam optimizer .Rather than re-initializing the optimizer state at each prob-ing step t, we maintain a global Adam optimizer across the entire diffusion process. During the gradient updates to min-imize the alignment loss, the optimizer naturally computes and preserves the exponential moving average of the past gradients. By carrying these internal momentum states for-ward across the diffusion timesteps, the optimizer intrinsi-cally acts as a low-pass filter. This mechanism smooths out the high-frequency jitter of the diffusion noise, enabling a stable trajectory for y∗ 

> t

Results 

The experimental data reveals a structural limitation of methods relying on objective surrogate models, which in-clude all forward approaches and ParetoFlow. These meth-ods demonstrate satisfactory performance on continuous Synthetic and RE tasks. In these specific domains, nearly any generated coordinate in the design space naturally cor-responds to a mathematically valid objective value. Con-versely, these methods suffer performance degradation on complex discrete or structural tasks such as MO-NAS and SciDesign, often yielding severe negative HV improve-ments. This failure largely originates from the inherent noise and unreliability of the neural network surrogates. Even when presented with invalid designs, such as chem-ically nonsensical molecules or broken network architec-tures, these proxy evaluators still output arbitrary numeri-cal scores. Consequently, the optimization process is prone to out-of-distribution overestimation into exploring invalid out-of-distribution regions, leading to severe manifold col-lapse. To circumvent the inaccuracies of absolute value predic-tion, methods like PGD-MOO employ an external prefer-ence classifier. This relative comparison strategy effectively mitigates the evaluation noise on invalid designs, yield-ing strong results in the SciDesign and MO-NAS domains. However, this preference paradigm introduces its own se-vere drawbacks. The strict reliance on pairwise dominance makes the generative model overly conservative, severely re-stricting its ability to boldly explore the objective space. As a result, PGD-MOO exhibits inferior performance on Syn-thetic and RE tasks, where aggressive extrapolation is highly beneficial and safe. Furthermore, our empirical practice re-veals a critical computational bottleneck. The training phase for the auxiliary preference classifier often demands signif-icantly more time than training the foundational diffusion model itself, fundamentally negating the intended efficiency of offline generation. By contrast, ParetoPilot successfully navigates around both of these drawbacks. Operating entirely as a zero-surrogate framework, our method avoids the erroneous eval-uations of external proxy networks and strictly eliminates the excessive training overhead of preference classifiers. During the reverse generation process, the unconditional dif-fusion steps inherently restrict the generative trajectories to the valid data manifold, naturally preventing the synthesis of nonsensical molecules or invalid structures. Simultaneously, the kinematically orthogonalized IPG engine allows for bold and precise exploration toward the true Pareto front. Ulti-mately, ParetoPilot achieves robust, state-of-the-art extrapo-lation across all domains, securing an overall HV improve-ment of 10 .46% .

Ablation Studies 

To rigorously validate the efficacy of each kinematic and optimization component within the proposed ParetoPilot framework, we construct three specific ablation variants by systematically isolating key mechanisms. First, we design the w/o Smoothing variant by resetting the Adam optimizer states at the beginning of every probing step to disable the historical exponential moving average momentum. Second, we create the w/o Edgeness variant by removing the bound-ary amplification factor, compelling the system to rely solely on uniform distance-based repulsion. Third, we evaluate the 

w/o Orthogonalization variant by directly blending the raw repulsive force with the convergence direction without the Gram-Schmidt projection. The empirical results consistently confirm the importance of each proposed component. The w/o Smoothing variant suffers a performance drop, plunging to an overall aver-age rank of 3.08 and a drastically reduced hypervolume im-provement of 7.29% . Notably, it yields a severe negative improvement of −1.60% in the RE Suite, proving that un-filtered high-frequency noise causes trajectory oscillations that push samples off the valid data manifold. Similarly, the 

w/o Edgeness variant experiences a noticeable performance drop to an overall rank of 2.46 , confirming that particles fail to efficiently discover extreme trade-off designs without the exponentially amplified outward thrust at the margins. Fi-nally, the w/o Orthogonalization variant degrades the overall hypervolume improvement to 10 .06% with an average rank of 2.19 . This decline mathematically validates our geomet-ric hypothesis that raw repulsion inherently contains com-ponents opposing the gravity vector. Without strict orthogo-nal projection, these unorthogonalized forces mutually neu-tralize the convergence momentum, demonstrating that the Gram-Schmidt mechanism is strictly mandatory to decouple exploration from exploitation and ensure steady optimiza-tion speed. 

## Conclusion 

In this work, we proposed ParetoPilot as a zero-surrogate generative framework for offline multi-objective optimiza-tion. By avoiding the reliance on auxiliary proxy models for guidance, our approach seamlessly integrates with pre-trained conditional diffusion models. This design strictly preserves data privacy and aligns perfectly with practical downstream deployment scenarios where access to the orig-inal training dataset is restricted. Through the introduced IPG engine, we dynamically navigated the latent condition space to effectively balance convergence toward the optimal boundary and diversity across the objective space. Exten-sive empirical evaluations demonstrated that this plug-and-play methodology consistently achieves robust extrapolation and competitive performance across complex search spaces compared to existing surrogate-reliant baselines. Despite these advantages, our framework possesses cer-tain limitations that offer valuable opportunities for future research. The overall generation quality inherently depends on the capacity of the foundational diffusion model to ac-curately capture the initial offline data distribution. If the base model fails to model the underlying manifold appropri-ately, the latent kinematic navigation will be consequently impaired. Additionally, computing the alignment loss and executing the gradient probing steps during the reverse dif-fusion process introduces additional inference latency com-pared to simple forward surrogate evaluations. Furthermore, integrating explicit user-defined constraints directly into the perturbation step presents an exciting avenue for expand-ing the applicability of zero-surrogate optimization in highly regulated industrial environments.
