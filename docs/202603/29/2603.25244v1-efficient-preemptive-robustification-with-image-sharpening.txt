## Abstract 

Despite their great success, deep neural networks rely on high-dimensional, non-robust representa-tions, making them vulnerable to imperceptible perturbations, even in transfer scenarios. To ad-dress this, both training-time defenses (e.g., ad-versarial training and robust architecture design) and post-attack defenses (e.g., input purification and adversarial detection) have been extensively studied. Recently, a limited body of work has pre-liminarily explored a pre-attack defense paradigm, termed preemptive robustification, which intro-duces subtle modifications to benign samples prior to attack to proactively resist adversarial per-turbations. Unfortunately, their practical applica-bility remains questionable due to several limita-tions, including (1) reliance on well-trained classi-fiers as surrogates to provide robustness priors, (2) substantial computational overhead arising from iterative optimization or trained generators for ro-bustification, and (3) limited interpretability of the optimization- or generation-based robustification processes. Inspired by recent studies revealing a positive correlation between texture intensity and the robustness of benign samples, we show that image sharpening alone can efficiently robustify images. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first surrogate-free, optimization-free, generator-free, and human-interpretable robustification ap-proach. Extensive experiments demonstrate that sharpening yields remarkable robustness gains with low computational cost, especially in trans-fer scenarios.

Therefore, we seek to explore an effective preemptive ro-bustification method that is surrogate-, optimization-, and generator-free, while remaining human-interpretable and computationally efficient. Intriguingly, a recent measure of image attackability (Liang et al., 2026) suggests that images with stronger semantic textures exhibit significantly superior resistance to adversarial perturbations. Moreover, a prior optimization-based preemptive robustification method (Fro-sio & Kautz, 2023) reports a similar phenomenon: the robus-tified samples it generates tend to possess enhanced textures and contrast. ŌĆó This work reveals new properties of traditional image sharpening in the deep learning era, providing valuable insights to the machine learning community. 

## 2. Related Work 

2.1. Adversarial Attacks 

Adversarial attacks deceive DNNs by carefully crafted per-turbations. In the white-box setting, adversaries have full access to the model architecture and parameters, which allows gradient-based iterative attacks such as I-FGSM (Ku-rakin et al., 2016) to achieve strong performance. In the more practical black-box setting, adversaries have no ac-cess to the target model and thus rely on transfer-based or query-based attacks. Transfer-based attacks exploit the transferability of adversarial examples to launch attacks. To improve transferability, various strategies have been pro-posed, including gradient-based (Fang et al., 2024; Peng 2Efficient Preemptive Robustification with Image Sharpening 

et al., 2025), model-related (Ma et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025a), transformation-based (Liang & Pun, 2025; Guo et al., 2025), objective-optimization (Wang et al., 2024c; Liu et al., 2025), generative (Nakka & Alahi, 2025; Li et al., 2025), and ensemble-based (Chen et al., 2023b; Tang et al., 2024) approaches. Query-based attacks iteratively refine perturbations by leveraging the feedback from the target model. Depending on the type of accessible outputs, they can be categorized into decision-based (Wang et al., 2025b) and score-based attacks (Reza et al., 2025). 

2.2. Adversarial Defenses 

In response to the growing threat posed by adversarial at-tacks, a variety of defense strategies have been developed. Depending on the deployment stage, these strategies can be classified as training-time defenses, post-attack defenses, and the recently introduced pre-attack defenses. Training-time defenses aim to enhance model robustness during the training phase. Adversarial training (Wang et al., 2024d; Zhang et al., 2025) does so by incorporating ad-versarial examples into the training set. Robust architec-ture design (Peng et al., 2023; Niu et al., 2024) lever-ages module-level design strategies to mitigate perturba-tion effects. Robustness-oriented regularization (Dong et al., 2025; Z ┬©uhlke & Kudenko, 2025) improves model robustness through specially designed regularization terms. Post-attack defenses reduce adversarial threats by detect-ing or mitigating adversarial examples after attacks occur. Input purification defenses (Yang et al., 2024; Lei et al., 2025) adjust adversarial examples toward the benign data distribution via methods such as GANs and diffusion mod-els. Transformation-based preprocessing (Cohen & Giryes, 2024; Liang et al., 2025) improves robustness by transform-ing inputs to disrupt the structure of adversarial perturba-tions. Adversarial detection defenses (Liu et al., 2024; Kong et al., 2025) prevent malicious access by detecting adversar-ial threats in the input. Output correction defenses (Shan-mugam et al., 2025) aggregate multiple prediction copies to produce more reliable outputs. Recently, pre-attack defenses have been proposed to pro-tect benign samples before attacks take place. In particular, preemptive robustification defenses apply small robustness-inducing perturbations to benign samples, such that adversar-ial examples generated from them are rendered ineffective. Unfortunately, only a limited number of studies have ex-plored this intriguing direction. Bi-Level (Moon et al., 2022) preemptively robustifies natural images by solving a bi-level optimization problem to find nearby points that are robust to adversarial perturbations. A5 (Frosio & Kautz, 2023) leverages automatic perturbation analysis to craft certified defensive perturbations against bounded attacks. Despite the progress achieved by Bi-Level and A5, their practical applicability remains limited due to their reliance on surrogate models, optimization-based or generator-based designs, and the lack of human interpretability. To address these limitations, this paper explores a new efficient preemp-tive robustification method that overcomes these drawbacks. 

2.3. Image Sharpening 

Historically, image sharpening has been investigated as an image enhancement technique with the goal of improving human perceptual image quality. Such approaches range from classical spatial-domain and frequency-domain filter-ing (Gonzalez, 2009) to adaptive filtering (Schavemaker et al., 2000; Zhang & Allebach, 2008) methods, and more re-cently, DNN-based sharpening techniques (Xu et al., 2025). In recent years, pioneering works have explored new ap-plications of image sharpening in the deep learning era, such as using image sharpening as an augmentation strategy to enhance model generalization and robustness (He et al., 2023b;a; Heinert et al., 2024). Moreover, image sharpening has been employed as a post-attack defense by transforming adversarial examples, demonstrating promising robustness gains (Mustafa et al., 2019; He et al., 2023a). These obser-vations indicate that image sharpening retains unique value in deep learning security. In this paper, we rethink image sharpening from a pre-attack defense perspective and reveal its effects of preemptive robustification on benign images.

3.2. Motivation 

Since optimization- and generator-based methods inevitably incur high computational costs and rely on the surrogate for robustification to provide prior knowledge, they fail to satisfy practical requirements. Consequently, building upon prior investigations into the characteristics of robust benign samples, we attempt to establish an intuitive paradigm. 

Insights from Prior Preemptive Robustification Meth-ods. Visualizations reveal that samples robustified by the optimization-based method Bi-Level (Moon et al., 2022) and the generator-based certified method A5 (Frosio & Kautz, 2023) exhibit higher contrast and stronger textures compared to their original counterparts. This observation has been explicitly discussed in A5. Motivated by this, we hypothesize that enhancing the texture of benign samples constitutes an important pathway toward robustification. 

Evidence from the Image Attackability Measure. Re-cently, evidence from the image attackability measure OTI (Liang et al., 2026) provides a more direct indication of the role of texture enhancement in robustification. Specif-ically, OTI demonstrates a positive correlation between the texture strength of a benign image and its resistance to per-turbations. Images with stronger textures tend to produce adversarial examples that are more difficult to attack. This correlation is particularly pronounced when texture statistics are restricted to semantic regions. From this perspective, proactively enhancing the texture strength of benign images may reduce their susceptibility to adversarial attacks. Taken together, these insights suggest a common conjecture: 

effective robustification can be achieved solely by sharp-ening. To examine this conjecture, we formally describe and explain how image sharpening enhances robustness in Sections 3.3 and 3.4. Extensive experiments in Section 4 will empirically demonstrate this intriguing proposal.

3.4. Explanations of the Effectiveness 

In fact, this result can be theoretically explained by the texture dependence of neural networks in conjunction with decision boundary theory (Tanay & Griffin, 2016; He et al., 2018). A large body of prior work (Geirhos et al., 2018; Hermann et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022; Scabini et al., 2025) consistently shows that both convolutional neural networks and attention-based models rely heavily on texture features. Therefore, samples with stronger semantic textures are easier for the model to classify and thus lie farther from the decision boundary. In contrast, samples with weaker textures exhibit higher uncertainty, are more difficult for the model to recognize, and lie closer to the decision boundary. As illustrated in Figure 4, when a sample is close to the de-cision boundary, an adversary can easily push it across the boundary with a small perturbation ŽĄa, leading to misclassi-fication. However, when a sample is far from the decision boundary, achieving the same effect requires a substantially larger perturbation. Image sharpening essentially strength-ens the semantic textures of benign samples, pushing them farther away from the decision boundary, and thus serves as a preemptive robustification process. Moreover, since enhancing semantic textures drives samples away from the decision boundaries of different models, image sharpening also performs well in defending against black-box attacks.

## 5. Conclusions and Limitations 

This work first reveals that traditional image sharpening can serve as an efficient preemptive robustification defense, and demonstrates its strong effectiveness across various at-tacks, tasks, and datasets. The proposed method is resource-efficient, such that even one CPU is sufficient to support its deployment, enabling its practical applications. While this work focuses on the image modality, extending analogous approaches to other modalities such as audio and text re-mains an interesting direction for future research. Moreover, how to further optimize the sharpening operator to achieve stronger robustification is still an open problem. 8Efficient Preemptive Robustification with Image Sharpening
