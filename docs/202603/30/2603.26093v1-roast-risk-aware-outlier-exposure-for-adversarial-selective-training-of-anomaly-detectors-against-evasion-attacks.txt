Abstract —Safety-critical domains like healthcare rely on deep neural networks (DNNs) for prediction, yet DNNs remain vulner-able to evasion attacks. Anomaly detectors (ADs) are widely used to protect DNNs, but conventional ADs are trained indiscrimi-nately on benign data from all patients, overlooking physiological differences that introduce noise, degrade robustness, and reduce recall. In this paper, we propose ROAST, a novel risk-aware outlier exposure selective training framework that improves AD recall without sacrificing precision. ROAST identifies patients who are less vulnerable to attack and focuses training on these cleaner, more reliable data, thereby reducing false negatives and improving recall. To preserve precision, the framework applies outlier exposure by injecting adversarial samples into the training set of the less vulnerable patients, avoiding noisy data from others. Experiments show that ROAST increases recall by 16.2% while reducing the training time by 88.3% on average compared to indiscriminate training, with minimal impact on precision. 

Index Terms —risk, outlier exposure, selective training, anomaly detectors, evasion attacks. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Deep neural networks (DNNs) have gained traction in safety-critical fields like healthcare, ranging from medical imaging to personalized medicine [1], [2]. However, DNNs are susceptible to adversarial attacks such as evasion attacks, in the inference phase [3]. In evasion attacks, an adversary introduces minor perturbations to the input at inference time so that the DNN misclassifies or mispredicts the adversarial sam-ple, leading to poor accuracy and undesirable consequences for safety-critical applications [4]. In the healthcare domain, evasion attacks might lead to misdiagnosis of a patient’s future condition. For example, several COVID-19 diagnostic systems that rely on DNNs were shown to be susceptible to evasion attacks, resulting in incorrect COVID-19 diagnoses with high confidence [5]. We focus on protection against evasion attacks on healthcare DNN applications. 

Many defence techniques have been proposed to make DNNs more robust against evasion attacks, including adver-sarial training [6], training dataset strengthening [7], model algorithm enhancement [8], and anomaly detectors (ADs) [9]. While the first three techniques make the prediction model itself more robust to adversarial samples, they suffer from reduced prediction performance on benign data [10], and the inability to generalize to a diverse range of attacks [11]. On the other hand, ADs act as independent sanity checkers that verify the legitimacy of an input before it gets processed by the DNN, without altering its functionality [9]. This is crucial in healthcare, where both false negatives (FNs) (failing to detect an attack) and false positives (FPs) (misclassifying a benign input) can have adverse consequences. Therefore, our goal is to build more robust ADs that detect adversarial samples before they reach the DNN, to protect it against evasion attacks. General-purpose ADs are often trained indiscriminately on large datasets that cover a diverse range of scenarios [12]. Unfortunately, this approach presents unique problems in healthcare, as human physiology varies significantly among patients, leading to varying noise levels in patient data. Erratic fluctuations (noise) may arise from severe pathological states and external and lifestyle factors such as drug intake, diet, physical activity, and stress levels. Therefore, this approach presents three main problems. First , the inclusion of noisy pa-tient data obscures meaningful patterns needed for identifying malicious data, ultimately causing a decline in the AD’s re-call [13]. Second , it incurs increased computational cost due to training on the entire dataset instead of the most representative samples [14]. Third , ADs are traditionally trained using in-distribution data that represent benign behaviour, which may not generalize well to unseen outliers [15].

To address these problems, we propose ROAST 1, a novel Risk-aware Outlier-exposure framework for Adversarial Selec-tive Training for ADs. We hypothesize that training ADs using benign and adversarial data of patients who are less vulnerable to evasion attacks can enhance the AD’s recall while reducing the training set size to cut down the computational cost. We define “less vulnerable” patients as those whose data are less susceptible to successful evasion attacks. Vulnerability to evasion attacks does not mean medically unstable patients; instead, it means patients whose data distribution allows small adversarial perturbations to remain within physiological plau-sibility while changing the prediction of the DNN significantly. To power ROAST, we design a risk profiling stage that groups patients by their vulnerability to attacks and selects the less vulnerable ones for training. The intuition is that, within the entire patient population, the less vulnerable patients’ data is usually a better representation of a typical distribution of benign data [16]. This is because benign data usually lies further from the anomaly detection boundary, making it more robust and harder to manipulate, while data from more vulnerable patients is usually closer to the boundary, easier to perturb, and thus is less reliable for training. By training on the benign data of the less vulnerable patients, the AD excludes the noisy behaviour of the vulnerable patients, boosting recall. However, relying solely on their benign data may induce the model to learn a very narrow view of what is benign, potentially harming precision [15]. To address this, we introduce controlled inclusion of adversarial examples to complement the benign data of the less vulnerable patients and help the AD learn a richer representation of the sample space using unsupervised learning, improving its recall without significantly reducing the precision. We evaluate the efficacy of ROAST on three publicly avail-able healthcare datasets: OhioT1DM - blood glucose predic-tion [17], MIMIC - mortality prediction [18], and PhysioNet CinC - Sepsis prediction [19]. These datasets are gathered from different patients in real-world settings. Further, we use the Universal Robustness Evaluation Toolkit (URET) [20], a framework to manipulate data points at inference time, for adversarial sample generation. Finally, we use three ADs for our defence experiments: (1) k-nearest neighbours ( kNN), (2) One-Class Support Vector Machines (One-Class SVM), and (3) Multivariate Anomaly Detection for Time-series Data with Generative Adversarial Networks (MAD-GAN) [9]. In summary, we make four contributions in this paper. 1) Propose ROAST, a framework with a risk profiling stage to quantify the risk of evasion attacks and group patients into vulnerability clusters. 2) Develop a strategy to selectively train ADs on benign and adversarial data of the less vulnerable patients identified by ROAST. 3) Evaluate ROAST on three healthcare datasets and three AD techniques.

4) Provide insights into the characteristics of ADs and datasets that maximize ROAST’s effectiveness. Our experiments show that, compared to indiscriminate training, ROAST’s selective training substantially improves AD robustness against adversarial perturbations. The results show a recall improvement of 16.2% on average with aprecision reduction in only one out of nine experiments (i.e., a precision drop from ≈0.594 to ≈0.565, which is only 4.97%). From a practical perspective, ROAST helps detect a larger proportion of adversarially manipulated inputs that were previously undetected, while requiring 75.0%, 93.2%, and 72.0% fewer training samples on the OhioT1DM, MIMIC, and PhysioNet CinC datasets, respectively. This reduction trans-lates into much shorter training times (a minimum of 70.66% and up to 99.77% training time reduction in our experiments) and ultimately lower computational costs. Therefore, ROAST not only enables ADs to detect a larger number of adversarial inputs but also enhances efficiency by drastically reducing the computational overhead of training them. II. B ACKGROUND 

A. Evasion Attacks 

Evasion attacks are inference-time attacks where an adver-sary makes minimal perturbations to inputs to evade detection by a DNN, leading to incorrect decisions [4]. Adversaries use two main strategies to generate adversarial samples for evasion attacks, namely gradient-based and non-gradient-based techniques. Gradient-based attacks such as Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) [4], Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) [21], and Carlini-Wagner (C&W) [22] use the model’s gradient in-formation to perform minimal perturbations to the input while maximizing the chances of misclassification. Non-gradient-based attacks include reinforcement learning [23], and gener-ative adversarial networks (GANs) [24]. Evasion attacks may be black box [20], white box [4], [21], or gray box [25]. To simulate evasion attack in our experiments, we use URET, a non-gradient-based black-box general-purpose evasion attack framework that manipulates data points independent of input type or task domain [20]. Given a data point and a set of predefined transformations, URET generates a malicious version that is both semantically and functionally valid using a user-defined attack algorithm. 

B. Datasets 

In this paper, we consider three time-series medical datasets. 

First , the OhioT1DM dataset [17], which contains eight weeks of data from 12 Type 1 diabetes patients (six in 2018 and six in 2020, denoted as Subset A and Subset B , respectively). We label patients such that A_3, for example, is patient 3 of 

Subset A . Measurements include CGM, finger sticks, insulin doses, carbohydrate intake, sleep, and activity, recorded about every five minutes. Despite its small size, the dataset is dense, low-to-moderate dimensional, and temporally smooth. 

Second , the MIMIC dataset [18] which contains records from more than 65,000 ICU and 200,000 emergency patients with thousands of variables, including labs, medications, and vital signs. Measurements include heart rate, blood pressure, pacemaker, etc. The sampling frequency is irregular, i.e., vital signs are frequent, while labs are intermittent, making the MIMIC dataset sparse but more realistic. 

Third , the PhysioNet CinC Sepsis Prediction dataset [19] which includes more than 60,000 ICU patients with up to 40 clinical variables recorded every hour (e.g., vitals, labs, and length of stay), and, like MIMIC, is highly sparse. 

C. Defences 

We consider three ADs (used one at a time) to protect the DNN prediction model against evasion attacks: kNN, One-Class SVM, and MAD-GAN [9]. kNN flags a sample as adversarial if its k-nearest neighbors deviate significantly from the rest of the dataset, making it effective for local density estimation (as with the OhioT1DM dataset) and detecting anomalies that global methods might miss. One-Class SVM detects anomalies by optimizing a hyperplane around benign data, effectively capturing rare, non-linear patterns in high-dimensional spaces (as with the MIMIC and PhysioNet CinC datasets). MAD-GAN, an unsupervised GAN-based technique, models multivariate feature dependencies, making it well-suited for safety-critical domains like healthcare [9]. While prior work has extensively used kNN and One-Class SVM for anomaly detection in medical datasets [26], MAD-GAN has demonstrated great effectiveness in real-world CPS systems and is therefore worth using in healthcare. 

D. Connected Healthcare Architecture 

We consider the patient monitoring system architecture in Figure 1 as an example. The system comprises a data acquisition device (e.g., a continuous glucose monitor (CGM), heart rate monitor, etc.) that collects patients’ vital signs at regular intervals and transmits them to a smart app on a mobile device via Bluetooth. The app sends the measurements to the cloud, where an AD inspects them to detect malicious patterns. If a measurement is benign, it is passed to the DNN prediction model for processing and future predictions; otherwise, the patient may manually review the flagged measurement, and the data acquisition device may be prompted to perform a re-measurement in such infrequent cases. Next, the DNN model predicts future physiological values (e.g., future glucose, heart rate, etc.) and sends them to the smart app, which infers the recommended control action (e.g., required insulin dose or pacemaker rate modulation) and allows the patient to approve it before executing the control action. Healthcare DNNs can be categorized into two categories: (a) global models, which learn population-wide patterns across diverse patients and are often applied in cold-start scenarios when a patient is newly introduced to the system, and (b) patient-specific models, which are tailored to capture the unique physiological characteristics of an individual, thereby enabling more personalized predictions. E. Definition of Risk 

Existing work [29] defines risk as the potential for loss or harm when a threat exploits a system vulnerability, and is typically modelled as a function of likelihood and impact. In the context of this work, we define risk as the impact of a malicious input perturbation that successfully shifts a system from a safe to an unsafe state, increasing patient vulnerability or harm that may endanger the patient. For instance, if an at-tacker alters a patient’s glucose reading so their Blood Glucose Management System (BGMS) recommends an excessively high insulin dose, which transitions the patient from a safe 

state (normal glucose) to an unsafe state (hypoglycemia), this is considered high risk due to its impact on the patient’s health. 

F. Dataset Distillation and Noise Filtering 

Training models on large datasets has been an issue due to memory and computational constraints. The issue is ex-acerbated when the dataset is noisy due to the presence of a large number of outliers, which hinders the learning process. Researchers proposed dataset distillation as a solution that compresses large datasets into smaller, information-rich subsets that include the most representative training samples so that when models are trained on the distilled subset, they match the performance of models trained on the entire dataset [30]. Dataset distillation techniques include matching gradients of DNN weights that are trained on the full and the distilled data [31] and dataset factorization [32]. Other attempts to select the most representative training samples include dataset cleaning [33] and noise filtering [34]. However, noise removal may not be enough to build a robust AD since ADs are usually trained only on benign data, which could induce the AD to learn a narrow definition of benign. Therefore, researchers proposed alternate training techniques, such as outlier exposure that exposes ADs to benign and adversarial samples at training time to help ADs learn a richer representation of benign versus anomalous samples [15]. While ROAST attempts to select a more representative training set using clustering, its purpose and mechanism differ fundamentally from traditional dataset distillation or noise filtering techniques. Traditional techniques identify low-quality or uninformative samples to remove or downweight for efficiency or denoising. In contrast, ROAST takes a different approach by modelling the risk of launching an evasion attack on individual data points of individual patients and clustering them based on adversarial influence, not noise level or sample difficulty. The distinction based on estimated adversarial pres-sure is absent in prior dataset distillation and noise filtering strategies. As a result, ROAST is specifically designed for adversarial robustness rather than just data cleaning. III. M OTIVATIONAL CASE STUDY

M ETHODOLOGY 

ROAST comprises two stages, risk profiling and AD en-hancement, which span a five-step process (Figure 3). Risk profiling consists of steps 1-4, while step 5 belongs to AD enhancement. ROAST is an iterative process that can be repeated from steps 1 through 5 whenever the patient or attacker behavior changes, to keep patient risk profiles up to date. However, in our evaluation, we consider only a single iteration of ROAST before AD deployment. 

A. Risk Profiling 

To determine the most suitable samples for AD training, we design a risk profiling stage that identifies patients who are more resilient to the attack due to their natural physiology. We characterize resilient patients as those whose data are further away from the AD decision boundary such that, when subjected to manipulation, they are less likely to cause a shift from a safe state to an unsafe state. To do so, we cluster patients into groups of different risk levels depending on their vulnerability to the evasion attack. Once we identify the most resilient patients, we use them to selectively train ADs to recognize the robust features that allow them to combat the evasion attack. The first four steps of Figure 3 correspond to the risk profiling stage, a novel approach to evaluating and interpreting model vulnerabilities at the patient level. First , it simulates the evasion attack by generating manipulated inputs to deceive the main DNN model and evaluate its vulnerabilities. Second ,it quantifies the amount of risk imposed on each patient at every timestamp, providing a fine-grained measure of how adversarial manipulations affect individual data points over time. Third , it constructs a continuous, patient-specific risk profile to capture their temporal patterns. The risk profile is a time-series representation of all risk values calculated in Step 2. Fourth , it uses unsupervised machine learning to group time-series risk profiles into distinct risk categories, allowing differentiation of vulnerability levels. The novelty of the risk profiling stage lies in the personalized temporal risk assessment of individual patients instead of a collective model-level risk assessment, which offers insights into which patients are most vulnerable to adversarial effects and when. To elaborate on the operation of the proposed risk profiling stage, we take a closer look at its various steps. 

Attack Simulation. Our risk profiling stage is designed to be attack-agnostic, eliminating any dependency on the specifics of the attack algorithm. To simulate evasion attacks in this work, we employ URET [20], a general-purpose evasion attack framework that manipulates data points at inference time. We chose URET to mount evasion attacks on the three healthcare datasets due to its flexibility in generating malicious samples regardless of the input type or data domain. In addition, it allows us to define a customized sequence of input transformations and feature dependencies that ensure the physiological plausibility of the generated malicious samples. 

Risk Quantification. To quantify the instantaneous risk of an attack at every timestamp, our risk formula considers two factors: (a) magnitude, and (b) severity of deviation, from benign to adversarial data sample. The magnitude of deviation is essential for the risk formula since it determines the prediction’s state transition. For example, modifying the blood glucose prediction from 90 mg/dL to 210 mg/dL tran-sitions a patient from a state of normal glucose to a state of hyperglycemia. The severity of deviation is important since it weighs state transitions differently depending on the threats they pose to victim instances. We propose a generic weighted sum risk formula to cal-culate the instantaneous risks of manipulating patients’ vital measurements, as shown in Equation 4. In this formulation, 

R(t) denotes the instantaneous risk of manipulating patient data at time t, while Zi(t) represents the i-th risk factor at time t. The index i ∈ { 1, 2, . . . , n } enumerates the set of considered risk factors, and n is the total number of such factors included in the model. Each risk factor captures a specific type of manipulation; for example, Z1(t) may corre-spond to the difference between benign and malicious glucose values, Z2(t) may represent the manipulated blood pressure, and so on, up to n distinct factors. This allows ROAST to scale to stronger adversarial capabilities and not be tied to the minimal capabilities model that we assume in this paper. The parameter Si is the severity coefficient (weight) associated with the i-th risk factor, reflecting its relative contribution to the overall risk. Conceptually, the severity coefficient Si is intended to capture the impact of manipulating clinical variable

However, as we did not have access to such domain experts, we approximated the severity coefficients Si using the coefficients of a linear regression model (regression problems) or a logistic regression model (classification problems) fitted to patient data. This approach quantifies the empirical influence of each variable on model outputs, providing a principled, data-driven weighting scheme that captures the relative importance of different risk factors in influencing the likelihood of adverse outcomes.

Clustering. The next step that ROAST needs to do is to separate patients whose data is strongly distorted by the adversarial attack from those whose data remains stable, because mixing these two groups during training leads to unstable gradients and degraded robustness. This is because, as mentioned earlier, using data from all patients to indis-criminately train ADs may result in lower recalls and less robust defences. Therefore, once the framework generates patients’ risk profiles, it uses clustering to identify patients who are less vulnerable and more vulnerable to the attack. The clustering algorithm groups patients based on feature similarity, measured through a distance metric or similarity score. ROAST is clustering-agnostic because the vulnerability scoring and selective training components are modular and independent of the clustering algorithm. However, we selected hierarchical clustering for three reasons. First , unlike parti-tioning algorithms such as k-means that require specifying the number of clusters a priori and assume approximately spherical clusters, which do not reflect the structure of physio-logical data, hierarchical clustering does not force a predefined cluster count and groups patients into homogeneous groups [39]. Second , density-based algorithms such as DBSCAN are sensitive to density parameters and may leave some patients unclustered [40]. Third , hierarchical clustering provides atransparent dendrogram, allowing visualization of patients with similar physiological characteristics [41]. 

B. Anomaly Detector Enhancement 

Figure 4 shows the rationale behind ROAST. In conventional ADs (Figure 4a), the model is exposed indiscriminately to benign data from all patients (blue and orange), because the assumption is that benign data is clean and sufficiently representative of patients’ benign distribution. This might not always hold given the non-deterministic nature of human phys-iology and the large number of outliers of the more vulnerable patients (as shown in Table I). As a consequence, the model learns a broad representation of benign samples (Boundary A), which results in a low recall (i.e., high FNs shown by the red samples within Boundary A). We propose a two-stage solution to enhance AD recall without compromising precision (by much): (a) noise removal and (b) outlier exposure.

Outlier Exposure. With a conventional selective training technique (Figure 4b), the AD is trained on benign data from the less vulnerable patients only, which induces the model to learn a very narrow view of what is benign, shrinking the decision boundary from Boundary A to Boundary B. This results in an improved recall (i.e., a lower number of red samples within Boundary B) at the expense of lower precision (i.e., higher FPs shown by a higher number of blue and orange points outside the decision boundary). For this reason, we augment our selective training strategy by incorporating outlier exposure (4c), which includes adver-sarial samples on the less vulnerable patients during training in a controlled manner. For example, in a BGMS, a benign reading of 110 mg/dL could be adversarially perturbed to 125 mg/dL (borderline hyperglycemic, but still physiologically feasible). Such adversarial manipulation does not reflect a huge deterioration in the patient’s health but is designed to “blend in” with normal readings. Exposing ADs to such manipulations in an unsupervised manner helps them learn a richer representation of the feature space, including how adversarial examples “blend in”, helping the AD carve a more accurate decision boundary. This forces the AD to model subtle deviations within the less vulnerable cluster, which results in the decision boundary expanding to Boundary C, improving the recall compared to indiscriminate training (4a), while minimizing the impact on precision from conventional selective training (4b). The strategy of generating adversarial samples on the less vulnerable patients allows for structured, informative anomalies (and not just random outliers) that are close to the benign decision boundary, but are different enough to teach the AD where the true boundary should lie. It helps represent plausible-but-dangerous anomalies instead of over-whelming the training process with extreme perturbations [42]. VI. E VALUATION 

In this section, we answer the following research questions: 

RQ1: Does indiscriminate training of ADs result in a low recall rate (i.e., high FN rate) of ADs? If so, why? 

RQ2: How much does ROAST improve recall in ADs? 

RQ3: What is the corresponding impact on precision? 

RQ4: How much does ROAST reduce the training set size and training time? 

RQ5: How sensitive is ROAST’s clustering to variations in severity coefficients and dendrogram cut thresholds? We evaluated the efficacy of ROAST in boosting the recall of three ADs ( kNN, One-Class SVM and MAD-GAN) by selectively training them based on the vulnerability levels of patients from the datasets in Section II-B. 

A. Experimental Setup 

Victim models. Since DNN prediction models in healthcare are often confidential and/or proprietary, we approximated victim models using time-series prediction models developed by Rubin-Falcone et al. for blood glucose prediction [43], Gupta et al. for mortality prediction [44], and Nejedly et al. for sepsis prediction [45]. The three models use a bidirectional long short-term memory (LSTM) architecture suitable for time-series prediction. The glucose prediction model achieved average root mean squared errors of 18.2 and 31.7, and average mean absolute errors of 12.8 and 23.6 on the 30 and 60-minute horizons, respectively [43]. The mortality prediction model achieved 0.87 AUROC and 0.49 AUPRC [44] on the ICU data. The sepsis prediction model achieved a normalized utility score of 0.278 using the 2019 PhysioNet/Computing in Cardiology Challenge utility function [45]. 

Attack algorithm. As for the evasion attack, we use the universal robustness evaluation toolkit (URET), which is a general-purpose evasion attack framework for manipulating data points at inference time [20]. We chose the URET framework because it is suitable for our experiments since it is designed to handle the numeric data present in the three datasets we selected. In addition, the framework stands out by allowing us to define a customized sequence of input transfor-mations and feature dependencies. While URET was originally developed for manipulating individual data points and does not account for temporal feature dependencies, healthcare data is inherently time-series in nature. Therefore, we extended it to time-series data. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to extend URET for adversarial generation on time-series data to be better suited for our experiments on healthcare datasets .Our adaptation involves flattening data samples from the past n

timestamps before inputting them into URET for manipulation. In the case of the OhioT1DM dataset, we manipulate CGM measurements to cause CGM mispredictions in the future. The adversary raises current CGM measurements to bias the DNN model into predicting higher future values. To ensure that manipulated CGM values respect physiological levels, we restrict them to be between 125 and 499 mg/dL for fasting scenarios, since a hyperglycemic glucose level in a fasting state is greater than 125 mg/dL, and between 180 and 499 mg/dL for postprandial scenarios, since a hyperglycemic glucose level in a postprandial state is greater than 180 mg/dL (499 mg/dL is the highest reported glucose level in the OhioT1DM dataset). In the case of the MIMIC dataset, we manipulate pacemaker rates to cause mortality misclassification in the future. To ensure that manipulated pacemaker rates respect physiological limits, we restrict them to be between 150 and 200 bpm. In the case of the PhysioNet CinC dataset, we manipulate heart rate measurements to cause future misclassifications of sepsis. To ensure that the manipulated heart rate values are within physiologically feasible levels, we set them to 120-140 bpm. 

Clustering setup. Risk profiles are clustered using hierar-chical clustering from the DTAIDistance library, with pairwise distances computed using Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) and complete linkage. DTW accounts for temporal misalignments in physiological data by allowing non-linear alignment of events, whereas Euclidean distance was not considered since it assumes point-wise alignment and equal-length sequences, which can misrepresent similarity in temporally shifted clin-ical signals. Complete linkage merges clusters based on the maximum inter-cluster distance, to produce compact, well-separated clusters and prevent high-risk patients from being included in the “less vulnerable” group. 

Hyperparameters. kNN: We use the pyod library with neighbors=7 and contamination=0.5. One-Class SVM: We use the scikit-learn library with kernel=rbf, gamma=auto, tol=0.001, and nu=0.5. MAD-GAN: We use [9] with epochs=100, learning rate=0.1, batch size=500, sequence length=12, and sequence step=1.

Recall/Precision Trade-off. Given healthcare’s safety-critical nature, fully automated patient monitoring systems cannot yet manage increases in FPs independently; physicians or patients typically oversee system behavior. As a result, the rare precision drop observed (<5% in only one of nine experiments) is considered minimal in clinical settings [46], where workflows are designed to tolerate modest increases in false alarms under human supervision, providing a clinically acceptable trade-off for the achieved recall gains. For example, ICU monitoring systems and CGMs routinely produce FPs that doctors or patients can quickly act on or dismiss. In contrast, low recall (missed malicious samples) is far less tolerable, as it may result in severe patient harm. 

Threats to Internal Validity. There are three threats to internal validity. First , ROAST assumes training and testing data are drawn from the same distribution, overlooking concept drifts. Concept drift is a general challenge for ADs (including kNN, One-Class SVM, and MAD-GAN), and ROAST does not introduce additional drift sensitivity. To assess short-term drift, we use chronological splits ( ≈80% early samples for training, ≈20% later samples for testing) exposing the model to natural short-term physiological changes while avoiding temporal leakage. While this setup captures natural short-term physiological shifts, evaluating substantial long-term concept drifts such as recovery or treatment changes over months is im-portant; however, the available datasets do not span sufficiently long horizons to support a meaningful long-term drift analysis. 

Second , ROAST is highly dependent on risk quantification to build patients’ risk profiles. Hence, the quality of obtained clusters depends on the employed instantaneous risk formula. A poorly defined risk formula may result in poorly separated clusters, which may result in a less vulnerable cluster that is impure with many vulnerable patients that would eventually be used for AD training. For this reason, we recommend consulting a domain expert in the risk quantification step. 

Third , ROAST assumes that the more vulnerable patients exhibit higher uncertainty and noisier data. While it is safe to make such an assumption in healthcare, as demonstrated in Section III, in domains where vulnerability does not map to noise structure, ROAST’s selective training on the less vulnerable instances’ data may introduce artificial bias. 

Threats to External Validity. There are three threats to external validity. First , ROAST uses offline training to build a static risk profiler, which requires periodic updates to factor in future potential changes in a patient’s risk profile as they recover from medical conditions. However, since profiling operates on aggregated patient-level risk statistics rather than full AD retraining, this update process is lightweight and can be integrated into routine model maintenance cycles com-monly used in healthcare ML systems. Second , although our evaluation primarily used the black-box, non-gradient URET framework for adversarial data generation, we tested the white-box, gradient-based FGSM attack on the OhioT1DM dataset as a preliminary step toward generalizability. Selective ROAST training produced similar results to URET, yielding an average recall increase of 9.90% for kNN (t-test = 5.87, p-value = 

1.07 × 10 −4) and 1.88% for One-Class SVM (t-test = 0.928, p-value = 0.373 ) compared to indiscriminate training. Third ,we used a minimal attacker capabilities model to evaluate the effectiveness of ROAST. However, ROAST was designed to scale to stronger adversarial models for better generalizability. VIII. C ONCLUSION 

We present ROAST, a novel risk-aware outlier-exposed selective training strategy for anomaly detectors (ADs) against evasion attacks in healthcare. ROAST comprises two stages: (a) risk profiling, which identifies patients who are less vulner-able to an attack, and (b) AD enhancement, which trains ADs on the data of the less vulnerable patients with a controlled amount of adversarial samples for outlier exposure to improve AD recall without compromising precision. Our experiments show that ROAST achieves a recall improvement of 16.2% on average compared with indiscriminate training (i.e., reduces FN rate) with minimal impact on precision (i.e., minimal impact on FP rate) while reducing training time by 88.3% on average, enabling healthcare providers to respond more effectively to adversarial risks, enhancing patient safety. In the future, we plan to build an online risk profiler that considers varying attack environments and algorithms, and potential dataset shifts to design a more adaptive defence. In addition, we plan to test ROAST on personalized DNNs instead of generic models that tend to be less robust against attacks. Finally, we plan to evaluate ROAST’s generalizability to other safety-critical domains like autonomous vehicles. ACKNOWLEDGMENT
