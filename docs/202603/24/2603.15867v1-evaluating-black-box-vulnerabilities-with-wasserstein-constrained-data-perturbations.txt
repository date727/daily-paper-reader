Abstract —The massive use of Machine Learning (ML) tools in industry comes with critical challenges, such as the lack of ex-plainable models and the use of black-box algorithms. We address this issue by applying Optimal Transport theory in the analysis of responses of ML models to variations in the distribution of input variables. We find the closest distribution, in the Wasserstein sense, that satisfies a given constraintt and examine its impact on model behavior. Furthermore, we establish convergence results for this projected distribution and demonstrate our approach using examples and real-world datasets in both regression and classification settings. 

Index Terms —Wasserstein distance; explainability; distribu-tional robustness; sensitivity; stress testing 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Modern machine learning models often function as black boxes whose reliability under data distribution shift remains difficult to assess. In safety-critical applications, it is essential to stress such models by exposing them to plausible variations in data distribution and observing the impact on predictions. We propose a testing bench for black-box models on tabular data that systematically generates constrained distributional perturbations to evaluate model robustness. Our approach draws on concepts from optimal transport and distribution-ally robust optimization (DRO). In particular, we use the Wasserstein distance – a metric on probability distributions that measures the “cost” of transforming one distribution into another – to bound the magnitude of distribution shifts and ensure they remain realistic. By restricting attention to per-turbations within a Wasserstein ball around the original data-generating distribution, we generate plausible stress scenarios that respect the underlying data structure (e.g. preserving relationships between features) while probing the model’s weaknesses. This yields a principled test bench for model robustness, complementary to pointwise adversarial attacks. Our Wasserstein perturbation framework produces distri-butional shifts that are interpretable and controllable. For example, one can stress a credit risk model by increasing the marginal distribution of “debt-to-income ratio” within a certain range, or test a medical diagnosis model under an aging population distribution – all while constraining the shift’s

overall Wasserstein distance to maintain plausibility. These perturbations are obtained by solving optimization problems that find the worst-case or most influential distribution within an allowed Wasserstein radius (an approach inspired by dis-tributionally robust optimization as defined in [1], [2] for instance). Unlike classical global sensitivity analysis which varies inputs in a Monte Carlo fashion, our method directly perturbs the input data distribution itself. This provides a global view of model sensitivity: rather than asking how a single prediction changes when an input is tweaked, we ask how the model’s overall performance or output distribution changes under a small but targeted shift in the data-generating distribution. By casting this as an optimal transport problem, we leverage efficient algorithms and theoretical guarantees from the optimal transport literature as in [3]. The use of Wasserstein distance further ensures that stress test scenarios are constrained and realistic: because Wasserstein distance ac-counts for the geometry of the data space, a small perturbation (within a given radius) corresponds to a distribution shift that is limited in magnitude for each feature, avoiding implausible changes to the data (unlike unconstrained adversarial pertur-bations that might be unnatural in tabular data). Our contributions are twofold. First, we formalize a model-agnostic testing bench for black-box predictors on tabular data, defining distributional stress scenarios via constrained optimal transport. Second, we demonstrate how this framework can evaluate model robustness and global sensitivity: by analyzing performance under various distributional scenarios, we can identify which features or assumptions most strongly affect model predictions. We also provide a dual formulation of the Wasserstein projection problem that may be of independent interest in the context of DRO. The proposed approach unifies ideas from distributionally robust optimization, global sensi-tivity analysis, and explainable AI to provide a comprehensive assessment of model behavior under shift. In the following, we review related work and foundational concepts that underpin our methodology. 

A. Related work a) Distributionally robust optimization.: The idea of preparing for worst-case distributions in a neighborhood of the empirical data has been formalized in DRO. Classical DRO 

> arXiv:2603.15867v1 [cs.LG] 16 Mar 2026

traces back to Scarf’s work on ambiguous demand in inventory management in the 1950s, and has since evolved with modern robust optimization techniques. In the context of machine learning, DRO provides a principled way to train models that perform well under potential distribution shifts. Notably, researchers have proposed to minimize the worst-case risk over all distributions within a certain distance (ambiguity set) of the training distribution, see for instance [4]. A popular choice is an ambiguity set defined by a Wasserstein distance ball. In [5] they show that optimization over a Wasserstein ball yields tractable reformulations and strong out-of-sample performance guarantees. Similarly, [6] develop an optimal-transport based measure of distributional model risk, quantifying how much the output could worsen under an adversarial distribution shift constrained by a Wasserstein distance. Various works have applied Wasserstein DRO to improve model reliability. For example, [2] use an adversarial training lens to certify robustness against distributional shifts by considering worst-case perturbations to the data-generating distribution during training. Other divergences and uncertainty sets have also been considered (e.g. ϕ-divergences in [7], moment-based ambigu-ity sets in [8], etc.), but Wasserstein ambiguity sets have gained prominence due to their flexibility and statistical robustness. Our work is related to this DRO literature in spirit, but instead of focusing on robust model training, we focus on robust model evaluation. We do not modify model parameters; rather, we probe a fixed model with perturbed input distributions. This approach complements robust training: even a model not explicitly trained via DRO can be post-hoc evaluated for weaknesses using our Wasserstein stress tests. Recent studies have underscored the importance of evaluating models under domain shifts as for instance in [9] or [10]. Benchmarks like WILDS target specific covariate shifts, and fairness researchers have begun to treat distributional change as a first-class citizen in model assessment. For instance, [11] argue that a model’s fairness (or more generally, reliability) should be measured by its performance across a collection of stress test distributions, rather than by a single i.i.d. test set. This aligns with our motivation to systematically generate and evaluate on multiple distributions reflecting different plausible realities. 

b) Global Sensitivity Analysis and Stress Testing.: Our methodology is also inspired by the field of global sensitivity analysis (GSA) in simulation and modeling. GSA techniques (e.g. variance-based methods) assess how uncertainty in in-put distributions contributes to uncertainty in outputs. For example, Sobol’ indices can be indeed interpreted as the fraction of the output variance to which the input variables contribute. Methods have been developed to handle correlated inputs and higher-order effects. Classic references such as [12] and [13] provide frameworks for quantifying the influence of input variability on model output. They have been used in explainability and fairness in [14], [15] or [16]. However, tradi-tional GSA typically assumes a given input distribution (often applying random sampling) and does not consider adversarial or worst-case shifts – it’s more diagnostic than adversarial. In contrast, stress testing involves exploring targeted “extreme but plausible” scenarios that could induce failure. Stress testing originated in finance and risk management, where regulators ask “what if” the economy undergoes specific shocks. In [17] they introduced a systematic approach to scenario stress tests by selecting distributions that maximize portfolio loss subject to a plausibility constraint measured by relative entropy. This ensures scenarios are not only severe but also statistically reasonable (plausible under the baseline distribution). Their entropic plausibility constraint is conceptually similar to our Wasserstein constraint: both limit how far the stress scenario can deviate from the original data distribution. Our work bridges GSA and stress testing with machine learning: we gen-erate distribution shifts that are guided by domain knowledge or feature importance considerations (like GSA) but within a rigorous constraint that keeps them realistic (like in risk stress testing via entropy or Wasserstein bounds). 

c) Model Explainability via Distributional Perturba-tions.: Finally, our approach connects to emerging work in ex-plainable AI that moves beyond local, pointwise explanations (like SHAP [18] or LIME [19]) towards global explanation frameworks. One recent direction is to explain model behavior through its response to changes in the input data distribu-tion, rather than individual inputs. The work [20] introduced an entropic variable projection framework for explainability, assessing how variations in each input variable’s distribution affect a model’s predictions. They use an information-theoretic measure (based on entropic projections) to quantify each fea-ture’s influence on predictions and errors, providing a model-agnostic way to interpret global behavior. Our work shares this model-agnostic ethos: by observing performance over perturbed distributions, we gain insight into which features or distributional properties the model is most sensitive to. In par-ticular, we build on ideas of distributional feature importance :if perturbing the distribution of feature X (while keeping others roughly the same) causes a large drop in accuracy, then the model is highly reliant on X’s original distributional range or correlation structure. There have also been developments in robust interpretability and fairness that resonate with our goals. Quantile-constrained Wasserstein projections is proposed in [21] to study model stability, generating distributions where certain feature quantiles are shifted and evaluating the resulting change in predictions. Their framework, applied to engineering and ML models, preserves the empirical copula (dependencies between features) while adjusting one feature’s distribution, similar to how we constraint shifts to maintain plausibility. Such studies found that global feature importance rankings (e.g. based on Shapley values) can remain stable under mod-erate distributional shifts, but local predictions might change for even small shifts in certain regions. These findings underscore the need for systematic robust-ness assessment: a model might appear stable overall yet have pockets of vulnerability in the input space. Our test bench aims to reveal these vulnerabilities by comprehensive scenario generation. In the realm of algorithmic fairness, researchers have also used distributional changes to evaluate and improve fairness. For instance, [22] formulate fair classification as a DRO problem, seeking classifiers that perform well even if the sensitive attribute distribution shifts within a Wasserstein ball – effectively a robustness requirement against demographic shifts. Meanwhile, the stress-testing framework of [11] ex-plicitly constructs stakeholder-specific test distributions (which they call “unit tests” for fairness) to ensure a classifier’s performance is acceptable across a range of societal scenarios. These works reinforce the notion that evaluating models on a spectrum of carefully crafted distributions provides deeper insight than a single test set. Our contribution is a general methodology to craft such distributions for tabular data problems, with Wasserstein-distance constraints ensuring that each crafted distribution is a realistic variant of the original. In summary, the pro-posed approach synthesizes ideas from robust optimization, sensitivity analysis, and explainability to create a practical tool for probing black-box models. By citing and building upon the foundational works discussed above – from the mathematics of Wasserstein distances [23] to modern DRO theory [5], [6], from classical sensitivity analysis [12], [13] to entropic scenario design [17], [24], and from recent robust interpretability studies [20], [21] to fairness stress tests [11] – we ensure that our test bench stands on a strong theoretical and empirical foundation. In the sequel, we detail our methodology and then demonstrate its use in evaluating model robustness on several real-world tabular datasets.

C. Results and organization of the paper 

Our main contribution is to prove not only the existence, Theorem II.1, of this projected distribution but also its explicit formula and its asymptotic behavior II.2. This formalism is close to what is referred to as counterfactual explanations, see [26], as the method imagines what would have been predicted if the inputs were changed. It is fundamental to note the difference between our method-ology and methods of feature importance, such as Gini indices. Such approaches are either a purely measure of error or they do not explain the particular influence of a variable. Although our proposed framework produces new individuals, the projection ensures that the new distribution is not an outlier. Additionally, we provide the exact impact of a feature on the models output by saying that it has a positive or negative effect on the quantity of interest. The theoretical results are accompanied by experiments in real-world datasets on the regression and classification tasks. This application in ML follows two main steps, first we compute the closest distribution in Wasserstein sense of the empirical distribution and then we investigate the explainabil-ity measures to understand the positive or negative impact of a certain feature on the model’s output. In Section II we provide theoretical results of existence and consistency as well as examples for different constraintts applied to empirical measures. A dual formulation of the projection problem, that may be of independent interest, is described in Section III. Capital Gain and Loss , Occupation , marital status , gender and so on. The target variable is high income, more than 50000$ per year. We trained Gradient Boosting, Decision Tree, LDA and Naive Bayes. We used α = 0 .05 and 21 regularly sampled values of τ in [−1, 1] to generate the figures. As we can see by the figure V-A, the feature Age has a beneficial impact in positive classifications ( ≥ 50k income) up to an age close to 50. On the other hand, observing Figure V-A, the impact of

1) Disparate Impact: In the classification setting, an impor-tant measure to evaluate the fairness of a decision Y ∈ { 0, 1}

made by an algorithm g using the dataset X is the Disparate Impact (12): 

DI( g) = P (g(X) = 1 | S = 0) 

P (g(X) = 1 | S = 1) . (12) It quantifies how spread is the positive classification among the two subgroups given by the sensitive attribute S. It is desirable to have a DI as close as possible to 1, meaning that the classifications are more or less similar between subgroups. If S is gender, the probability of success between males and females should be the same. In other words, the algorithm should be blind to the sensitive attribute. The experiment made in this section is the following: first, we repeated the method described in the beginning of section 8 to compute new datasets with constrainted means. The second step is to compute the DI in each of these new datasets to investigate the impact of some features on this fairness criteria. The work [34] provides a way, used here, to compute the Disparate Impact with a confidence interval. In our case, the sensitive attribute is gender, where S = 0 means female and 

S = 1 means male. Figure V-A1 plots the impact of four features: Age, Education-Num, Capital Gain and Hours per week . We can see that the only feature that has a positive impact on DI is capital gain: increasing the average capital gain also increases the fairness criteria. The key product of this experiment is to observe that our mean calibration is done regardless the feature gender . It proves that is possible to produce a fake fair decision by only changing the average of a certain feature. This process is the so-called fairwashing .

We trained Gradient Boosting and Random Forest models in randomly chosen 80% of the observations and tested in 20% remaining ones.
