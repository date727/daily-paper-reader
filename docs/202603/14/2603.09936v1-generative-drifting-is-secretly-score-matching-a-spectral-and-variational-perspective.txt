## Abstract 

Generative Modeling via Drifting [Deng et al., 2026] has recently achieved state-of-the-art one-step image generation through a kernel-based drift operator, yet the success is largely empirical and its theoretical foundations remain poorly understood. In this paper, we make the following observation: under a Gaussian kernel, the drift operator is exactly a score difference on smoothed distributions . This insight allows us to answer all three key questions, which were left open in the original work: (1) whether a vanishing drift guarantees equality of distributions ( Vp,q = 0 ⇒ p = q), (2) how to choose between kernels, and (3) why the stop-gradient operator is indispensable for stable training. Our observations position drifting within the well-studied score-matching family and enable a rich theoretical perspective for subsequent analysis. By linearizing the McKean-Vlasov dynamics resulting from our formulation and probing these dynamics in Fourier space, we reveal frequency-dependent convergence timescales comparable to Landau damping in plasma kinetic theory: the Gaussian kernel suffers an exponential high-frequency bottleneck, potentially explaining the empirical preference for the Laplacian kernel. Our analysis also suggests a fix: an exponential bandwidth annealing schedule σ(t) = σ0e−rt that reduces convergence time from exp( O(K2max )) to O(log Kmax ). Finally, by formalizing drifting as a Wasserstein gradient flow of the smoothed KL divergence, we prove that the stop-gradient operator is not a heuristic but is derived directly from the frozen-field discretization mandated by the Jordan, Kinderlehrer and Otto (JKO) scheme, and removing it severs training from any gradient-flow guarantee. This variational perspective further provides a general template for constructing novel drift operators, which we demonstrate with a Sinkhorn divergence drift. 

## 1 Introduction 

The dominant paradigm in continuous generative modeling relies on learning the score function of data distributions and then using the learned score to guide the generation process. Energy-Based Models exploit the score to bypass the intractable partition function Grathwohl et al. [2019], Du and Mordatch [2019], Song and Kingma [2021]; Score-Based Generative Models and Diffusion Models [Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015, Ho et al., 2020, Song et al., 2021] are unified under this framework via Denoising Score Matching [Vincent, 2011] which established that training a denoiser is theoretically equivalent to score matching; furthermore, Flow Matching [Lipman et al., 2023] has recently been shown to fit within the same family [Gao et al., 2024]. Bridging these paradigms has allowed researchers to develop robust mathematical machinery and principled training practices across all of these models. A recent approach, Generative Modeling via Drifting [Deng et al., 2026], appears to depart from this formalism. Instead of learning a score or velocity field, drifting prescribes a kernel-based drift operator Vp,q that pulls generated samples toward data while pushing them away from one another. The generator is trained to match its own drifted outputs until the drift vanishes, achieving impressive one-step image generation without distillation, teacher models, or adversarial training. Despite its impressive empirical results, the mathematical structure of drifting remains largely underexplored. Specifically, Deng et al. [2026] left three foundational open questions: 1. Identifiability. Does Vp,q = 0 guarantee p = q?

4.2 Connection to Denoising Score Matching 

Theorem 4.1 establishes a direct connection between generative drifting and the score matching framework [Vincent, 2011, Song et al., 2021]. In standard Score-Based Generative Models and Diffusion Models, the intractable data score is circumvented via Denoising Score Matching (DSM), where a neural network ψθ (x)

is trained to approximate the score of the smoothed data distribution by regressing against the perturbation kernel score, −(x − y)/σ 2 (See Eq. (7) and Eq. (9-10) in Vincent [2011]). As highlighted in Eq. (6) generative drifting Deng et al. [2026] does not parameterize the score vector field directly. Instead, it parameterizes the generator fθ , which yields the empirical distribution q = ( fθ )#N (0 , I ),and explicitly constructs non-parametric Parzen density estimates for both the data p and the generated distribution q. The drift operator V (σ) 

> p,q

(x) thus computes the analytical residual between these two smoothed scores: σ2(∇x log pσ (x) − ∇ x log qσ (x)) . Consequently, drifting bypasses the need to learn an approximate score function; rather, it uses the exact analytical score residual to drive the generator’s optimization until the score difference vanishes globally. Put differently, in standard Diffusion/Score-Matching, the neural network parameters θ are used to build the vector field itself ( ψθ ). At inference time, one must evaluate this neural network repeatedly to solve an SDE/ODE. In contrast, in Drifting, the neural network fθ is a sampler (a map from noise to data). It does not output a score. The score is computed non-parametrically on-the-fly using the Parzen window over the particles, and we only use this analytical score difference during training to push your generator’s outputs toward the data. At inference, the score function vanishes from the pipeline entirely , and it is possible to perform sampling in one forward pass of fθ .

5.3 Stop-Gradient as a Structural Necessity 

A distinctive feature of drifting models is that the dynamical process occurs during training rather than inference. Indeed, while Diffusion and Flow models intergrate a learned probability flow at sampling time, drifting prescribes an analytical velocity field and updates a generator during optimization. The sequence of generators, fθi therefore induces a sequence of distributions qi = ( fθi )#ν, which defines a training-time probability flow. This raises a key question: what ensures that this flow minimizes a meaningful objective over distributions? A natural framework to address this is Wasserstein gradient flows [Santambrogio, 2015, Ambrosio and Savaré, 2007], where distributions evolve along the steepest descent of a function F [q]. If training dynamics correspond to the discretization of such a flow, the sequence {qi} may be shown to satisfy theoretical guarantees such as monotone descent toward the minimizer q = p. We now demonstrate that drifting corresponds to the Jordan-Kinderlehrer-Otto (JKO) scheme of a smoothed KL functional, and that the stop-gradient operator is not a stabilization trick but the frozen-field discretization required to realize this variational approach.

Remark 5.7 . This analysis clarifies a fundamental difference from Diffusion and Flow models. In those paradigms, a field is learned and generative guarantees arise at inference time via SDE or ODE integration (Eqs. (2) ). ## 7 Related Work 

One-step generation. Single-step generation originates with GANs [Goodfellow et al., 2014, Brock et al., 2019]; distillation methods [Zhou et al., 2024, Song et al., 2023] compress diffusion models but require expensive two-stage pipelines. Drifting [Deng et al., 2026] trains a one-step generator from scratch, avoiding both. Inductive Moment Matching [Zhou et al., 2025] is the closest concurrent method, using MMD with Gaussian kernels for few-step sampling; our spectral theory supplies mode-resolved diagnostics and a provable annealing schedule applicable to any kernel-based method. Li and Zhu [2026] independently reinterpreted drifting via long-horizon flow maps; our work provides mode-resolved rates and gradient-flow guarantees absent from their framework. 

Score-based methods. Score matching [Hyvärinen, 2005, Vincent, 2011, Song and Ermon, 2020, Song et al., 2021] and flow matching [Lipman et al., 2023] are the closest methodological neighbors. Our central contribution is showing that, within the drifting architecture , the kernel drift reduces to a score difference on smoothed distributions (Theorem 4.1), forging a direct bridge and resolving identifiability by reducing it to the injectivity of Gaussian convolution. The score-difference structure was independently established by Weber [2024] from a top-down KL-minimizing probability flow; that work contains neither the Fourier stability analysis, the Landau damping identification, nor the annealing schedule. We also note concurrent work [Lai et al., 2026] that exhibit the score identity with gaussian kernels but does not provide the same spectral and variational insights as our work. 

Optimal transport and Wasserstein gradient flows. The variational theory of §5.3 draws on the Jordan– Kinderlehrer–Otto framework [Jordan et al., 1998] and its functional-analytic treatment in Santambrogio [2015] and Ambrosio and Savaré [2007]. For the Sinkhorn-divergence drift, we draw on entropic optimal transport [Peyré and Cuturi, 2020] and the Sinkhorn divergence [Feydy et al., 2018]. 

Spectral analysis. The high-frequency bottleneck we identify is mechanistically distinct from spectral bias of neural networks [Rahaman et al., 2019, Tancik et al., 2020]; it is intrinsic to the kernelized particle dynamics. Carrillo et al. [2024] proved exponential KL convergence for continuous SVGD [Liu and Wang, 2019, Liu, 2017] via a Stein–log-Sobolev inequality, yielding global rates; our analysis provides mode-resolved timescales. The Landau damping analogy [Villani, 2002, Mouhot and Villani, 2011] has not, to our knowledge, appeared in prior work on generative models. 98 Conclusion 

We began with a simple question, what does the drifting kernel actually compute? We demonstrate that under a Gaussian kernel, this it is a score difference on smoothed distributions. This single identity serves as a foundation that helps resolve all three open theoretical questions simultaneously, each through a distinct analytical lens. Identifiability follows from Fourier injectivity. Landau damping offers an explanation for kernel selection: the Gaussian kernel exponentially suppresses high-frequency modes, justifying the empirical preference for the Laplacian and motivating the exponential annealing schedule σ(t) = σ0e−rt that reduces convergence time from exp( O(K2max )) to O(log Kmax ). The stop-gradient operator, far from being a heuristic, is the frozen-field discretization mandated by the JKO scheme; removing it leads to drift collapse, a spurious minimization that reduces the loss without transporting mass toward the data distribution. Beyond resolving these open problems, the gradient-flow formalism yields a modular template V =

−∇ (δF/δq ) that generalizes drift construction beyond the original kernel family, demonstrated here with a Sinkhorn divergence drift. 

Limitations. The spectral analysis rests on linearization and local homogeneity of pσ ; early-training, far-from-equilibrium dynamics are not captured. Experiments are on synthetic 1D/2D benchmarks; high-dimensional validation of the annealing schedule, most naturally by integrating it into the full ImageNet pipeline of Deng et al. [2026], is the most pressing open direction. 

Future directions. The dispersion relation λ(k) ∝ | k|2 ˆφ(k) suggests a kernel design program : opting for kernels with nice theoretical properties and with well-behaved spectrum to avoid exponential slowdowns. On the theoretical side, extending the Landau-damping analysis to the nonlinear regime, and investigating whether the frozen-field principle provides variational guarantees for target-network methods in RL and self-supervised learning, are natural next steps. We hope this work inspires explorations porting from the fields of kinetic theory, optimal transport and gradient flows and that they induce principled practical contributions to the promising class of drifting models.
