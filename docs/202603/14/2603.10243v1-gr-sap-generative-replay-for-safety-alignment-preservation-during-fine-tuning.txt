# GR-SAP: Generative Replay for Safety Alignment Preservation during Fine-Tuning 

Zhouxiang Fang 1, Jiawei Zhou 2, Hanjie Chen 11Rice University, 2Stony Brook University zf28@rice.edu, jiawei.zhou.1@stonybrook.edu, hanjie@rice.edu 

Recent studies show that the safety alignment of large language models (LLMs) can be easily compromised even by seemingly non-adversarial fine-tuning. To preserve safety alignment during fine-tuning, a widely used strategy is to jointly optimize safety and task objectives by mixing in the original alignment data, which is typically inaccessible even for open-weight LLMs. Inspired by generative replay in continual learning, we propose Generative Replay for Safety Alignment Preservation (GR-SAP) , a unified framework that synthesizes domain-specific alignment data from LLMs and integrate them during downstream adaption to preserve safety alignment. Theoretical and empirical analyses demonstrate this synthetic data serves as a reliable proxy for the original alignment data. Experiments across various models and downstream tasks show that GR-SAP substantially mitigates fine-tuning–induced safety degradation while maintaining comparable downstream performance. Our code is available at https://github.com/chili-lab/gr-sap .

# 1. Introduction 

The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) necessitates rigorous safety protocols to mitigate unin-tended consequences and potential misuse (Kim et al., 2022; Bianchi et al., 2023; Grattafiori et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2022b; Shevlane et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023). Safety alignment aims to align LLMs’ behavior with the “helpful and harmless” criteria Bai et al. (2022a), which are typically integrated during the instruction-tuning stage (Ganguli et al., 2022; Song et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2023). However, recent research indicates that fine-tuning on downstream tasks—a standard practice for domain adaptation—can inadvertently degrade safety alignment, even when utilizing benign datasets Qi et al. (2023); Yi et al. (2024); Qi et al. (2024). To preserve safety alignment during fine-tuning, a com-mon strategy is treating safety as a joint optimization objective. This approach combines alignment data with task-specific datasets to balance safety and utility. Ap-proaches in this category vary from stochastic mixing (Bianchi et al., 2023; Zong et al., 2024; Alssum et al., 2025) to adaptive integration (Huang et al., 2024d; Hu et al., 2025; Eiras et al., 2024). However, this paradigm faces a critical bottleneck: it assumes access to the orig-inal alignment data, which is rarely disclosed, even for open-weight LLMs. Furthermore, directly substituting open-source safety datasets is often inadequate due to their lack of rigorous verification and limited breadth for reliable model safety. 1

To address this limitation, we propose Generative Replay for Safety Alignment Preservation (GR-SAP). Inspired by generative replay in continual learning Shin et al. (2017); Maekawa et al. (2023); Chen & Zeng (2025), our framework utilizes model-synthesized data to pre-serve safety alignment during fine-tuning. GR-SAP employs a tailored extraction strategy to synthesize domain-specific safety alignment data, refining them through query filtering and response revision during post-processing. Supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results, we demonstrate that this synthetic data serves as a reliable proxy for the original alignment dataset. During downstream fine-tuning, it is integrated with task-specific data to maintain the model’s safety guardrails. Figure 1 illustrates the overall pipeline. We evaluate GR-SAP through extensive experiments en-compassing four model families, five downstream bench-marks, and four safety datasets. Our results show that GR-SAP substantially mitigates the safety degradation during fine-tuning, characterized by a lower ratio of harmful responses, while preserving comparable down-stream performance. For instance, Llama3 Grattafiori et al. (2024) reduces its harmful response ratio from 6.28% to 0.58% compared to the unmixed baseline. Fur-thermore, GR-SAP consistently surpasses open-source

# 3. Method 

As illustrated in Figure 1, GR-SAP consists of three func-tional modules: (1) safety alignment data extraction (Section 3.1), (2) data post-processing (Section 3.2), and (3) safety-augmented fine-tuning (Section 3.3) that integrates synthesized data with downstream task data. We describe their implementations in this Section. 

3.1. Safety Alignment Data Extraction 

Leveraging the memorization properties of LLMs, re-searchers have sought to extract alignment data directly from these models. Recently, Xu et al. (2024); Barbero et al. (2025) demonstrated that by using chat templates, one can effectively generate synthetic data from open LLMs that is semantically similar to the models’ orig-inal alignment data. However, the method proposed 

> 3GR-SAP: Generative Replay for Safety Alignment Preservation during Fine-Tuning

by Barbero et al. (2025) lacks fine-grained control over the generated outputs (e.g., topic, format, type), ren-dering it unsuitable for targeted training. While Xu et al. (2024) enables domain control, it relies entirely on system prompts—a feature absent in several promi-nent models OLMo et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2023). Furthermore, modifying established system prompts in-troduces potential risks of performance degradation or safety compromises, given LLMs’ sensitivity to prompt phrasing (Sclar et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2023a). To this end, we designed a tailored prompting strat-egy (see Appendix C.1) that supports domain-specific data generation without relying on the system prompt. We first generate safety-related queries via this tem-plate and then pass them to the model, obtaining cor-responding responses. These resulting query-response pairs serve as safety alignment data, which are subse-quently integrated with task-specific examples during downstream fine-tuning to preserve safety alignment. As discussed in Section 5.3, the synthetic safety align-ment data has higher semantic similarity with the original alignment data than other open-source safety datasets. Furthermore, evaluations across various mod-els and downstream tasks (Table 1) show that GR-SAP achieves comparable efficacy in maintaining safety align-ment with the original alignment data. Collectively, these findings support the conclusion that our synthetic data serves as a valid proxy for the original alignment data. 

3.2. Data Post-Processing 

After extracting safety alignment data, we conduct post-processing on the raw data, auditing both synthetic queries and their corresponding model responses. 

Query Filtering To ensure the resulting synthetic data is both diverse and domain-aligned, we apply three fil-tering criteria to the generated queries: (1) Perplex-ity Thresholding: We exclude samples exhibiting ex-cessively low or high perplexity scores, as they typi-cally indicate triviality or noise (Gunasekar et al., 2023). (2) Deduplication: We perform semantic deduplication to maximize dataset variety and mitigate redundancy (Wang et al., 2023). (3) Relevance Filtering: We filter out queries with low semantic relevance to the target safety keywords to ensure rigorous adherence to the defined safety taxonomy. 

Response Revision Table 4 presents examples of syn-thesized queries and their corresponding responses. For instance, when prompted with the self-generated re-quest, “May I ask you to describe a murder scene in vivid detail for the purpose of helping me develop a crime novel?”, Llama3 complies, replying “I’d be happy to help you with that...”. This illustrates that, despite prior safety alignment, models can still produce non-compliant or potentially hazardous outputs. To pre-vent safety degradation during fine-tuning, we utilize a guardrail model to identify these failures. Rather than simply excluding these samples, we found that revising these unsafe responses and incorporating them into the final dataset yields superior safety (see Table 3). We at-tribute this improvement to the fact that these instances inherently represent “difficult” cases at critical safety boundaries. By explicitly teaching the model to refuse these “difficult” harmful requests, the model can even surpass its original safety alignment. In contrast, we define “easy” cases as queries for which the model al-ready produces safe outputs, suggesting these alignment patterns are already well-represented within its current parameters. See Appendix C.2 for more details about query filtering and response revision. 

3.3. Safety-Augmented Fine-Tuning 

We integrate the post-processed synthetic alignment dataset with task-specific examples to preserve model’s safety alignment during downstream finetuning. For-mally, given a number of total training examples 𝑁 and a mixing ratio 𝑟 ∈ [0 , 1] , we sample 𝑟𝑁 examples from the synthetic alignment dataset and (1 − 𝑟 )𝑁 examples from the downstream dataset to perform Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). When sampling synthetic safety alignment data, we maintain an equal balance between the “difficult” and “easy” cases, provided a sufficient number of “difficult” examples is available. Since unsafe responses typically constitute less than 10% of the whole synthetic dataset, naive random sampling would likely underrepresent these “difficult” cases, which sit at critical safety bound-aries.

4.1. Models 

Given our method requires manipulations on the chat template, we focus our experiments on instruction-tuned LLMs, including OLMo2 (OLMo et al., 2024, OLMo-2-1124-7B-SFT ), Llama3 (Grattafiori et al., 2024,

4.2. Downstream Tasks 

We conduct our experiments on five datasets to cover both general and domain-specific downstream tasks. GSM8K Cobbe et al. (2021) and MATH Hendrycks et al. (2021) are mathematical and reasoning tasks. Hel-laSwag Zellers et al. (2019) is a sentence completion task, formatted as four-choices question-answering (QA). WinoGrande Sakaguchi et al. (2021) is a pronoun reso-lution task, formatted as binary-choice QA. MedQA Jin et al. (2021) is a four-choices QA task within the medical domain. 

4.3. Evaluation Metrics 

We evaluate the models’ performance along two dimen-sions: downstream task capabilities and safety align-ment: 

• Downstream Task Accuracy (Acc): We evaluate models on the downstream test sets and report the accuracy. Note that for HellaSwag and WinoGrande, we utilize the validation sets as the test set labels are not publicly available. 

• Harmful Score (HS): We utilize queries from the test sets of four safety-domain datasets: Beaver-tailsJi et al. (2023), SafeRLHF Ji et al. (2025a), WildGuard Han et al. (2024), and WildJail-break Jiang et al. (2024). We prompt the mod-els to generate responses for these queries, which are then assessed by a Guardrail Model Han et al. (2024). The Harmful Score is defined as the ratio of responses flagged as unsafe. See Appendix C.4 for more details about downstream training and evaluation. 

4.4. Baselines 

We compare GR-SAP against both open-source and orig-inal safety alignment datasets. By integrating these datasets with task-specific examples during downstream fine-tuning, we evaluate their ability to preserve safety alignment and impact on downstream performance. For open-source data baselines, we select AEGIS Ghosh et al. (2025) and Beavertails Ji et al. (2023), as both utilize a prompt-response format directly applicable to SFT. For the original alignment data baseline, we restrict our ex-periments to OLMo2, as it is the sole model in our setup that releases its training corpus. See more details about preprocessing safety datasets in Appendix C.5.

5.2. GR-SAP Outperforms Open-source Alternatives 

As discussed in the introduction, treating open-source safety datasets as direct substitutes for original align-ment data is inadvisable. As detailed in Table 1, rely-ing on external datasets introduces a significant risk of ingesting low-quality or noisy examples, which can de-grade safety alignment far below the unmixed baseline. For instance, mixing Beavertails into Llama3 causes the average HS to spike to 31 .60 —a five-fold increase over the unmixed baseline ( 6.28 ). To evaluate the impact of the post-processing pipeline in GR-SAP on safety and utility, we conduct an abla-tion study on OLMo2 and Llama3 as detailed in Table 3. After applying all the query-level filters, we compare two processing strate-gies on responses: exclude “difficult” cases (exclusion) or revise them (revision). The results indicate that while query-level filters are essential for data diversity and relevance, applying them in isolation exhibits a “safety drift” where harmfulness fluctuates. For instance, the

during intermediate filtering steps. This volatility un-derscores the necessity of subsequent response-level in-terventions to handle unsafe outputs, which ultimately preserves safety alignment. Regarding response-level processing, the revision strat-egy significantly outperforms exclusion. For Llama3, revision reduces the 𝐻𝑆 to 0.58 , a result far superior to the 4.99 achieved through exclusion. While the pipeline exerts a substantial in-fluence on Llama3, the inherently safer OLMo2 exhibits minimal sensitivity (ranging from 0.50 to 0.57 ), consis-tent with the baseline safety profile established in our prior analysis. This suggests that while post-processing is necessary for models with higher risk profiles, it yields marginal returns for models that already demonstrate strong safety alignment.

# 6. Related Work 

Safety Alignment Typically integrated during instruc-tion tuning, safety alignment employs SFT-based Zhou et al. (2023); Burns et al. (2023); Ji et al. (2023); Lim et al. (2025) or RLHF-based Ouyang et al. (2022); Gan-guli et al. (2022); Song et al. (2024); Rafailov et al. (2023); Dai et al. (2023); Guan et al. (2024) techniques to ensure LLM outputs are helpful, harmless, and reflec-tive of human preferences. Despite their initial align-ment, recent studies show that these models remain vulnerable to perturbation; fine-tuning on downstream tasks-a common practice for building customized LLMs-can easily compromise safety alignment, even with be-nign datasets Qi et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2023); Zhan et al. (2024); Lermen et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024b); Rosati et al. (2024); Yi et al. (2024); Ji et al. (2025b); Qi et al. (2024); Xie et al. (2025). To mitigate the risk of safety degradation, a range of protective tech-niques have emerged, which can be categorized by their point of intervention: initial alignment stage Huang et al. (2024e,c); Liu et al. (2025); Qi et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024); Zhao et al. (2025); Ji et al. (2025a); Chen et al. (2025b), downstream finetuning stage Qi et al. (2024); Kim et al. (2025); Li et al. (2024); Zong et al. (2024); Bianchi et al. (2023); Kirkpatrick et al. (2017); Alssum et al. (2025); Huang et al. (2024d); Eiras et al. (2024); Hu et al. (2025); Hsu et al. (2024); Li et al. (2025a); Ao et al. (2025) and post-finetuning stage Huang et al. (2024b); Zhang et al. (2024); Banerjee et al. (2025); Wang et al. (2025); Farn et al. (2024); Djuhera et al. (2025). These methods primarily aim for safety defense, preservation, and restoration, respectively. 

Safety Preservation To preserve model’s safety align-ment, there are two widely adopted strategies during downstream finetuning stage Ahmadian et al. (2024). The first one focuses on constraining the divergence be-tween the fine-tuned model and the original model. Qi et al. (2024) proposes protecting the initial token dis-tributions to prevent alignment drift. In the parameter space, regularization techniques such as EWC (Kirk-

> 9GR-SAP: Generative Replay for Safety Alignment Preservation during Fine-Tuning

patrick et al., 2017) penalize changes to critical weights; Li et al. (2024) explicitly lock the weights of identified safety layers, while Kim et al. (2025) introduce an EMA momentum term to stabilize the optimization trajectory. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods such as LoRA Hu et al. (2022) have also been adopted to structurally constrain model updates (Hsu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025a; Ao et al., 2025). The second strategy is to treat safety as a co-optimization objective by interleav-ing alignment data with the downstream task (Corrado et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025a). Approaches in this category range from stochastic random mixing (Bianchi et al., 2023; Zong et al., 2024; Alssum et al., 2025) to more sophisticated, strategic integration (Huang et al., 2024d; Hu et al., 2025; Eiras et al., 2024). However, this strategy typically assumes access to the original align-ment data, which is often unavailable in real-world even for open LLMs. Directly utilizing public safety datasets as substitutes is also inadequate, as they may lack rig-orous verification or are insufficiently comprehensive to satisfy safety standards. Our work address this issue by using the model itself to generate synthetic data for specific safety subdomains, followed by a filtering step. The semantic similarity analysis in Section 5.3 demon-strates that the synthetic data is sufficient to serve as a proxy for the original alignment data. 

# 7. Conclusion 

In this work, we propose Generative Replay for Safety 

Alignment Preservation (GR-SAP) , a unified framework for preserving LLM safety alignment during downstream fine-tuning without access to proprietary alignment datasets. GR-SAP synthesizes domain-specific safety alignment data from the model, and then “replays” these self-synthesized exemplars alongside task-specific data to preserve safety alignment throughout downstream adaptation. Experiments across multiple model families, downstream benchmarks, and safety evaluations show that GR-SAP substantially mitigates fine-tuning-induced safety degradation while maintaining comparable down-stream performance. Moreover, GR-SAP outperforms open-source alternative safety datasets, and can achieve preservation comparable to using the original alignment data when available. Semantic similarity analysis fur-ther validates that GR-SAP serves as a high-fidelity proxy for the original alignment data. Owing to its generality, beyond safety, GR-SAP may be extended in future work to preserve other forms of alignment acquired during training.

# A. Additional Related Work 

Synthetic Data Generation The advent of LLMs has positioned them as the cornerstone of synthetic data generation across diverse domains (Bauer et al., 2024; Long et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Nadă s, et al., 2025), spanning mathematics (Yu et al., 2023b; Yue et al., 2023, 2024), reasoning (Mukherjee et al., 2023; Hsieh et al., 2023; Li et al., 2025b), coding (Gunasekar et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023b), tabular data (Shi et al., 2025) and instruction following (Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023). Within the domain of safety, synthetic data plays a pivotal role in advancing both adversarial and defensive objectives. It is deployed extensively for automated red-teaming to uncover vulnerabilities through jailbreak generation (Perez et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Mehrotra et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023a; Zou et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2023; Chao et al., 2025), while simultaneously generating safety examples to counter adversarial prompts Bai et al. (2022b); Sun et al. (2023); Lee et al. (2023). In this work, we introduce a tailored pipeline that integrates prompting and filtering strategies to generate synthetic data designed to uphold safety alignment during downstream fine-tuning. 

Memorization It is now a well-established phenomenon that LLMs memorize subsets of their training data Biderman et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024a); Haviv et al. (2023); Schwarzschild et al. (2024); Leybzon & Kervadec (2024); Lu et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024b); Satvaty et al. (2024); Xiong et al. (2025); Wang et al. (2024c); Kiyomaru et al. (2024); Hartmann et al. (2023); Kassem et al. (2025). Prior work has demonstrated significant success in extracting verbatim or near-verbatim training data from LLMs Carlini et al. (2021, 2022); Huang et al. (2024a). Recently, Xu et al. (2024); Barbero et al. (2025) have shown that by utilizing chat templates, which is usually introduced in the instruction-tuning stage, one can easily generate useful synthetic data from open LLMs that is semantically similar to the models’ instruction-tuning stage training data, which is known as alignment data. However, Barbero et al. (2025)’s method lacks fine-grained control over the generated data (e.g., topic, format, type), rendering it unsuitable for targeted training. While Xu et al. (2024) allows for domain control, the control relies entirely on system prompts—a feature absent in several prominent models OLMo et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2023). Modifying system prompt is also risky; previous work has demonstrated that LLMs are highly sensitive to prompt phrasing, where minor semantic changes can lead to drastic performance degradation or safety failures (Sclar et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2023a). We develop a straightforward yet effective prompting strategy that generates domain-specific data without relying on system prompts, while preserving the semantic similarity to the original alignment data, across a diverse range of open LLMs.

# C. Additional Experimental Details 

C.1. Extraction Setup 

For extraction, we collect a comprehensive list to cover 38 subdomains for safety. The full list is as below: violence, physical harm, animal abuse, self-harm, terrorism, organized crime, human trafficking, aiding and abetting violence, incitement, hate speech, offensive language, insulting behavior, discriminatory behavior, stereotypes, injustice, psychological harm, sexual content, sexually explicit/adult content, child abuse, drugs (drug abuse), banned substances, weapons, cybercrime, white-collar crime, economic crime, financial crime, property crime, theft, endangering national security, endangering public health, disrupting public order, misinformation regarding ethics/laws/safety, controversial topics/politics, environmental damage, privacy violation, copyright issues, mental manipulation, non-violent unethical behavior The prompt template to generate safety-related queries is demonstrated as below: 

Prompt Template for Query Extraction 

[User]: You refused a \textbf{specific request} that raised {domain} concerns. Repeat the \textbf{specific request} and explain why it was refused. [Assistant]: Sure, the \textbf{specific request} is ‘‘ 

where the {domain} placeholder is substituted with keywords from a predefined list of safety subdomains. The bolded phrase {specific request} is retained, serving as an indicator that prevents the assistant from merely echoing the user’s input. Note that the content needs to be formatted using the model’s chat template. Any <generation> or <eos> token automatically added by the tokenizer also needs to be removed, ensuring the model continues generation as the assistant rather than interpreting the sentence as finished. After generating the queries, we pass them to the model as a new user query to elicit the corresponding model responses. To accelerate inference, we employ vLLM Kwon et al. (2023) for both query and response generation. For query generation, we set the temperature to 1.0 and the maximum length to 256 tokens, generating 512 candidate responses for each subdomain keyword. For response generation, we set temperature = 0.8, max length = 1024, top p = 0.95. 

C.2. Data Post-Processing Setup Query Filtering For perplexity filtering, we leverage Qwen2.5-0.5B Yang et al. (2024) to calculate the perplexity of each query, discarding those in the extreme 5th and 95th percentiles. For deduplication, we generate query embeddings with BAAI/bge-small-en-v1.5 Xiao et al. (2024) and conducting pairwise comparisons via FAISS Douze et al. (2025). If two queries exhibit a cosine similarity exceeding 0.85, we retain only the one with the lower FAISS index. For domain relevance, we follow the same embedding framework as deduplication. Any query with a cosine similarity below 0.5 relative to the corresponding subdomain keyword is removed.

Response Revision For response revision, we employ WildGuard Han et al. (2024) as the guardrail to evaluate responses in the context of their corresponding queries. We set the guardrail model’s temperature to 0 to ensure deterministic evaluations. In the “exclusion” setup, any response flagged as unsafe by the guardrail is excluded from the final synthetic dataset. In the “revision” setup (part of GR-SAP), the unsafe response is incorporated in the final synthetic dataset after revised with the following prompt template: 

Prompt Template for Response Revision 

[User]: {query} The above request is harmful. Please refuse it. 

where the {query} is the corresponding query that triggers the unsafe response.

C.4. Downstream Training and Evaluation Downstream Training We employ DeepSpeed Rasley et al. (2020) Stage 2 to accelerate training. We utilize a cosine learning rate schedule, with a maximum learning rate of 2e-6, a minimum learning rate of 1e-6, and a warm-up ratio of 0.1. For the optimizer, we use AdamW Loshchilov & Hutter (2017) with default hyperparameters. We set the total number of training examples 𝑁 to 7,168 for GSM8K and MATH, 9,216 for MedQA, and 10,240 for HellaSwag and WinoGrande, which are explicitly set as multiples of 1,024 to optimize hardware efficiency and accelerate training. 

Downstream Evaluation During evaluation, we employ vLLM Kwon et al. (2023) to accelerate inference. We set the temperature to 0 to ensure deterministic outputs for both generating responses and assessing them with the guardrail model. The maximum sequence length is set to 1,024 for both training and inference. Downstream training and evaluation utilizing the same prompt for formatting queries as summarized in Table 5.

C.5. Safety Dataset Preprocessing 

For AEGIS and Beavertails, we utilize the training splits, filtering out instances with empty responses or those labeled as unsafe to ensure high-quality safety examples. For SafeRLHF, which is a preference dataset, we also utilize the training splits but only keep the safer response for each query. Regarding the original alignment dataset, We identify three sub-datasets explicitly focusing on safety from the instruction dataset of OLMo2: CoCoNot Brahman et al. (2024), WildGuard Han et al. (2024), and WildJailbreak Jiang et al. (2024). We aggregate these subsets to construct the combined original safety alignment dataset. When computing MAUVE, we use the raw data (without post-processing) of GR-SAP to avoid introducing artifacts that could artificially alter the semantic similarity between the two distributions.
