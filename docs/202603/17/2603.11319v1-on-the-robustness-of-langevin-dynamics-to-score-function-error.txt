# On the Robustness of Langevin Dynamics to Score Function Error 

Daniel Yiming Cao 1 August Y. Chen 1 Karthik Sridharan 1 Yuchen Wu 1

Abstract 

We consider the robustness of score-based generative modeling to errors in the estimate of the score function. In particular, we show that Langevin dynamics is not robust to the L2 errors (more generally Lp

errors) in the estimate of the score function. It is well-established that with small L2 errors in the estimate of the score function, diffusion models can sample faithfully from the target distribution under fairly mild regularity assumptions in a polynomial time horizon. In contrast, our work shows that even for simple distributions in high dimensions, Langevin dynamics run for any polynomial time horizon will produce a distribution far from the target distribution in Total Variation (TV) distance, even when the L2 error (more generally Lp) of the estimate of the score function is arbitrarily small. Considering such an error in the estimate of the score function is unavoidable in practice when learning the score function from data, our results provide further justification for diffusion models over Langevin dynamics and serve to caution against the use of Langevin dynamics with estimated scores. 

# 1 Introduction 

Many sampling algorithms ubiquitous in statistics and Machine Learning (ML) – used from Bayesian inference to modern generative modeling – are score-based sampling algorithms. These sampling algorithms are widely used in a range of scientific and engineering applications, such as image generation (see e.g. Croitoru et al. (2023)), inverse problems in applied mathematics (e.g. Sanz-Alonso et al. (2023)), physical sciences (e.g. Zheng et al. (2025)), protein design and computational biology (e.g. Guo et al. (2024)), and medical image reconstruction (e.g. Chung et al. (2022)). Many such score-based or related sampling algorithms exist. An incomplete list includes Langevin dynamics (see e.g. Welling and Teh (2011); Durmus et al. (2018); Chewi (2025)), diffusion models (e.g. Ho et al. (2020); Song and Ermon (2019); Song et al. (2021a); Song and Ermon (2020); Song et al. (2021b) for some foundational early works), flow matching (e.g. Lipman et al. (2023)), and stochastic interpolants (e.g. Albergo et al. (2025)). Among these sampling algorithms, we investigate Langevin dynamics and diffusion models. Langevin dynamics is a classical technique for sampling from target distributions in statistics and ML, while diffusion models are a modern, popular and effective method for generative AI. At a high level, these algorithms approximately sample from a target distribution πtar in Rd by running a stochastic process driven by the 

score function ∇ log πtar in the case of Langevin dynamics, or a suitable sequence of score functions 

∇ log π0, ∇ log π1, . . . , ∇ log πk (the annealed score functions ) in the case of diffusion models, where 

π0 ≈ πtar and π0, . . . , π k forms a gradually noised version of the target distribution πtar . The continuous-time idealization of both these processes converges to πtar under mild assumptions on πtar , see Section 2.2.

Main Question: Is small L2 (or more generally Lp) score estimation error with respect to πtar sufficient for the success of score-based sampling algorithms? 

This question has been answered affirmatively in the context of diffusion models (see e.g. Chen et al. (2023a); Benton et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024); Liang et al. (with respect to π0, π 1, . . . , π k respectively) is small, rather than just small L2 estimation error of ∇ log πtar .In particular, let dπtar denote the output distribution. With an estimated score s and appropriate discretization, it is known that within a number of iterations polynomial in d, the sampling error scales linearly with the L2

score estimation error: 

TV( πtar , dπtar ) ≲ εscore ,

Significance of our results: Theorems 1, 7, and 11 demonstrate standard bounds on L2 error (more generally Lp) of learning the score is completely insufficient to control sampling error for Langevin dynamics, despite being the objective of choice in practice when estimating the score via score matching. Note an L∞

bound is not possible in score matching where one cannot hope to learn the score in regions not well-covered by πtar . As such our results provide strong evidence against the efficacy of Langevin dynamics with estimated scores (e.g. from data), even in very simple problems . This result stands in sharp contrast to diffusion models, where also convergence occurs in poly( d) time; as such our work provides further support for diffusion models from a novel angle. We emphasize the lower bounds of Theorems 1 and 7 are fundamentally caused by the lack of robustness of Langevin dynamics in high dimensions to L2 (more generally Lp) score estimation error; the examples are not degenerate. The target distribution is as simple as an isotropic Gaussian, the score estimates ˆs are Lipschitz, and the initializations considered are natural. We also emphasize the practical prescriptions of our work. Theorem 7 highlights that one should use 

fresh samples when performing data-based initialization, a prescription validated by simulation in Section 4. However, score functions are often trained on the entire dataset in practice. Our results serve to caution against doing so. Finally, our work also highlights the importance of annealing . By convolving πtar with a Gaussian and learning the scores along the convolution, an L2 or Lp estimate now becomes sufficient for sampling as per the theory of diffusion models. For Gaussian mixtures, learning the score function ∇ log πtar may be the hardest step of learning all the annealed score functions (Chen et al., 2025), as intuitively convolution with Gaussians can only ‘soften’ the annealed score functions (though we emphasize that this is not always the case, see Montanari and Vu (2025); Koehler and Vuong (2024)). 2.1 Langevin Dynamics 

Letting πtar ∝ e−V (x) be the target distribution on Rd, the associated score function is ∇ log πtar (x) = 

−∇ V (x). The overdamped Langevin dynamics is the stochastic differential equation (SDE) 

dXt = ∇ log πtar (Xt) d t + √2 d Bt, X0 ∼ μ0, (1) where μ0 is arbitrary. Under mild regularity conditions, πtar is the stationary distribution for the SDE (1), and Xt d

→ πtar as t → ∞ (Chiang and Chow, 1989). 

Non-Asymptotic Convergence: The SDE (1) converges efficiently to πtar in TV (in polynomial time in d) when πtar satisfies a Poincaré Inequality , with stronger guarantees on the convergence when πtar 

satisfies a Log-Sobolev Inequality (Villani et al., 2008; Bakry et al., 2014). The Poincaré Inequality holds for log-concave πtar (convex V (x)) (Bobkov, 1999). Meanwhile the Log-Sobolev Inequality holds for strongly-log-concave πtar (strongly convex V (x)) (Bakry and Émery, 2006). Specifically, if ∇2V (x) ⪰ αI d,then the Log-Sobolev constant of πtar is at most 1

Discretization: One discretizes (1) to sample from πtar with a discrete-time algorithm. There are several discretizations of (1) in the literature which enjoy polynomial convergence to πtar in TV when πtar is a Gaussian and more generally, when V is strongly convex (Chewi, 2025), and when ∇V is Lipschitz (which is the case for our lower bound constructions in Theorems 1, 7). Perhaps the most natural is the Unadjusted Langevin Algorithm (ULA):

Note all these discretizations aim to simulate the idealization (1), see Chewi (2025) for further discussion. 

2.2 Diffusion Models 

Diffusion models were introduced in several key early works (Song and Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021a,b), and were originally motivated by the idea of time-reversal . One considers an SDE or other stochastic process that converges from πtar to N (0 , I d), known as the forward process . One then applies the theory of time-reversal of SDEs (Haussmann and Pardoux, 1986) to obtain a stochastic process that converges from N (0 , I d) to πtar , known as the reverse process . One discretizes the reverse process, yielding an algorithm that samples from πtar .

Discretization: To sample from πtar with a discrete-time algorithm, one discretizes (4) in the case of the DDPMs, and more generally discretizes the suitable reverse process (e.g. the ODE from DDIMs). This leads to a gradually noised sequence of distributions π0 ≈ πtar , . . . , π k. Note π0 is only approximately equal to 

πtar due to early stopping often employed in practice, see e.g. Karras et al. (2022). To do so, one must learn estimates of the score functions ∇ log π0, . . . , ∇ log πk from i.i.d. data from πtar via score matching , see e.g. (Hyvärinen and Dayan, 2005; Vincent, 2011; Song and Ermon, 2019; Karras et al., 2022). It has been established that with an estimated score s and appropriate discretization, the sampling error in TV scales linearly with the L2 score estimation error, within poly( d) iterations. Specifically, the sampling error in TV scales linearly in a suitable weighted average of the L2 score estimation errors of 

∇ log π0, ∇ log π1, . . . , ∇ log πk. This result applies for the reverse process of both DDPMs and DDIMs; see e.g. Chen et al. (2023b); Benton et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024); Jiao et al. (2025), among many other works.

3.2 Data-based initialization 

A natural strategy for initializing Langevin dynamics when one has a score estimate ˆs learned from data drawn from πtar is data-based initialization , studied in Koehler and Vuong (2024); Koehler et al. (2025). Here, one initializes Langevin dynamics from i.i.d. samples x1, . . . , x n drawn from πtar : we let X0 ∼ 1

> n

Pni=1 δxi , the empirical distribution of these samples. This is a natural initialization when one has i.i.d. samples from πtar 

and aims to produce more of them. As discussed in Koehler and Vuong (2024), p. 4, data-based initialization is also closely related to contrastive divergence (Hinton, 2002, 2012; Xie et al., 2016; Gao et al., 2018). Koehler and Vuong (2024); Koehler et al. (2025) established several appealing properties of data-based initialization: 1) It is robust to L2-error (with respect to πtar ) in the score estimates ˆs, a natural assumption when ˆs is learned from data drawn from πtar .2) With n = poly( d) samples, it can alleviate the exponential in d mixing time of Langevin dynamics from many mixture distributions (Bovier et al., 2004, 2005; Menz and Schlichting, 2014) (though Langevin dynamics mixes rapidly for πtar in Theorem 7). Our result, Theorem 7, complements the work of Koehler and Vuong (2024); Koehler et al. (2025), by showing that the robustness of data-based initialization only applies when fresh samples different from those used to learn ˆs are used for the initialization. We believe this serves as an important warning to use fresh 

samples when applying data-based initialization or similar methods in practice. To state Theorem 7, we need the following definition.

# 4 Simulations 

We validate the practical implications of Theorem 7 via simulation. Specifically, we consider two tar-get distributions πtar : a single Gaussian N (1, 2Id) with d = 50 and a mixture of Gaussians (GMM)  

> 12

N (−1, 2Id) + 12 N (4 1, 2Id) with d = 25 . Full details and results are in Section 9. 

Learning the score: We learn an estimate ˆs of the score function ∇ log πtar with a fully connected neural network with 3 hidden layers trained with Adam (Kingma and Ba, 2015) for 150,000 epochs. To encourage an overfit or ‘memorized’ score estimate ˆs, we create a training set of 10,000 samples by drawing 1000 i.i.d. samples from πtar and duplicating each sample 10 times. We learn the score by score matching; we learn the denoiser at the 100 lowest noise levels out of a linear noise schedule of 1000 levels (e.g. Ho et al. (2020)). 

Sampling algorithms: We run fixed-step-size Langevin dynamics for 1000 iterations, evaluating the denoiser at the 10th lowest noise level out of the noise schedule to approximate ∇ log πtar at each iteration. We use 1000 iterations to simulate a poly( d) timescale, and as score-based generative models rarely use more than 1000 denoiser evaluations in practice (Karras et al., 2022). We initialize Langevin dynamics at n

points to produce n ∈ { 1500 , 7500 , 13500 , 16250 } samples the following three ways, yielding 3 different algorithms. 1) initialize n draws from N (0 , I d), 2) take 30 fresh i.i.d. draws from πtar each duplicated n/ 30 

times, and 3) take 30 random draws from the 1000 distinct training samples each duplicated n/ 30 times. Here the 30 samples represent a random subset of the training samples or fresh samples; we repeat n/ 30 

times to produce n total samples. 

Takeaway: We plot our results in Figure 1 for Gaussian πtar and Figure 2 for GMM πtar . Algorithms 1, 2, 3 are denoted ‘vanilla’, ’fresh’, ’train’ respectively. As predicted by Theorem 7, initializing from samples drawn from the training set (Algorithm 3) generally produces worse results than when the samples are fresh (Algorithm 2). For Gaussian πtar , the gap between Algorithms 2 and 3 is significant, and Algorithm 1 performs similarly to Algorithm 2. For GMM πtar , the gap between Algorithms 2 and 3 is smaller, although Algorithm 3 still generally does worse than Algorithm 2, and Algorithm 1 performs the worst of the three methods due to the poor spectral gap of the GMM. 

# 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we established that Langevin dynamics is not robust to Lp errors in the estimate of the score function in high dimensions, even when the Lp score estimation error is exponentially small. Our lower bounds apply to natural examples: simple isotropic Gaussian target distributions, Lipschitz score estimates, and natural initializations, namely from N (0 , I d) and from i.i.d. samples used to learn the score.
