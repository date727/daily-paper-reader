# Label Hijacking in Track Consensus-Based Distributed Multi-Target Tracking 

Helena Calatrava, Shuo Tang, Pau Closas 

Northeastern University, Boston, MA 02115, USA 

{calatrava.h, tang.shu, closas }@northeastern.edu 

Abstract —Distributed multi-target tracking (DMTT) in limited field-of-view (FoV) sensor networks commonly suffers from label inconsistency , whereby different nodes disagree on the identity of the same target. Recent track-consensus DMTT (TC-DMTT) strategies mitigate this issue by enforcing kinematic and label agreement through metric-based track matching. Nevertheless, their behavior under adversarial conditions remains largely unexplored. In this paper, we reveal identity-level vulnerabilities in TC-DMTT and introduce the concept of label hijacking :an attack in which an adversary injects spoofed tracks to corrupt target identities across the network. Drawing on an analogy to classical pull-off deception in radar, we formalize a notion of attack stealthiness and derive an optimization-based strategy for crafting such attacks. A three-sensor network case study demonstrates the impact of the proposed attack on label consistency and tracking accuracy, showing successful target impersonation. Overall, this work highlights the need to rethink robustness at the consensus layer in DMTT frameworks. 

Index Terms —Multi-Target Tracking, Sensor Networks, Ad-versarial Attack, Data Association. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Multiple target tracking (MTT) is a statistical inference problem concerned with estimating the number of physical targets present in a dynamic scene, their trajectories, and, in certain formulations, their identities (labels) from noisy sensor measurements [1]. Originating in the 1960s, MTT has since become a foundational technology for a wide range of perception and decision-making applications [2]. Modern MTT systems must contend with multiple sources of uncertainty beyond process and measurement noise, in-cluding time-varying target cardinality, false alarms (clutter), missed detections, target births and deaths, and data associa-tion (DA). Inspired by Mahler’s work on finite set statistics [3], random finite set (RFS) theory emerged as a mathematically elegant framework for handling these uncertainties implicitly within a unified Bayesian formulation [4], [5]. A fundamental question in MTT concerns how information should be processed in multi-sensor settings , where transmit-ting all raw measurements to a central processor is often impractical [6]. To address this, distributed MTT (DMTT) 

allows each node to perform local tracking and exchange summary information for fusion, either in the form of track-level state estimates (commonly referred to as track-to-track fusion ) or multi-object densities. Because the RFS framework

While widely adopted, GCI-based fusion methods suffer from two key limitations. First, under limited and non-overlapping field of view (FoV) conditions, their performance degrades [11], and labeled formulations are prone to label inconsistency , whereby different nodes may assign distinct identities to the same physical target [12]. Second, more generally, density-level fusion incurs significant computational and communication overhead compared to track-to-track ap-proaches [13]. Recent work on track-consensus DMTT (TC-DMTT) [14], [15] (reviewed in Sec. II) addresses these challenges through enhanced track-to-track fusion, enforcing both kinematic and label consensus using track-distance met-rics, typically drawn from the optimal sub-pattern assignment (OSPA) family [16]. While effective under benign operating conditions, TC-DMTT performance in the presence of adver-sarial threats has received limited attention. Although distributed architectures improve observability under limited FoV, they simultaneously expand the attack surface to cyberattacks [10] in distributed networks (see Fig. 1 for a classification). In this work, we focus on a false data injection (FDI) scenario in which an attacker injects spoofed tracks 1, i.e., tracks that do not correspond to any true (physical) target, into the fusion process. Nevertheless, a more critical failure mode arises when target identities are corrupted. As illustrated in Fig. 2, under label hijacking , the fusion process continues to follow a true target under incorrect labels, giving rise to what we term an identity theft attack (ITA) and breaking track continuity. Our main con-tributions are: (i) we introduce the track-consensus identity-theft attack (TC-ITA), which exploits this vulnerability through adversarial manipulation of track consensus; (ii) by framing TC-ITA as the data-layer analogue of classical pull-off radar deception, we define a notion of stealthiness and propose an model predictive control (MPC)-based strategy to generate such attacks; and (iii) we demonstrate successful identity hijacking under stealthy TC-ITA in a simulated distributed tracking scenario 2.The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. A. System Model 

Fusion is performed in a fully distributed manner, without global knowledge of the network topology or FoV of other nodes. Moreover, because the fusion stage operates only on the reported track estimates and does not modify the local multi-object densities, TC-DMTT is agnostic to the specific local MTT algorithms employed at each node. 

1) Network topology: A distributed sensor network is mod-eled as an undirected graph G = ( N , A), where N denotes the set of nodes and A ⊆ N ×N the set of bidirectional communi-cation links. Two nodes a, b ∈ N can communicate directly if they are within communication range [10]. The neighbor set of node a is defined as N (a) ≜ {b ∈ N | b̸ = a and (a, b ) ∈ A} .Each node is equipped with a limited FoV sensor subject to false alarms and detection uncertainty, as well as local processing capabilities. 

2) Message exchange and global labeling: At time k, each node a transmits its local labeled multi-object state estimate

B. Track Consensus 

Track consensus in TC-DMTT consists of two stages: (1) 

kinematic consensus, which aligns and fuses track states, and 

(2) label consensus, which propagates identity agreements across the network. 

1) Kinematic consensus: At time k, node a has access to its own active track set T(a) and those from its neigh-bors, i.e., {T(b)}b∈N (a). Here, active refers to tracks de-clared to exist and whose state estimates are available for fusion. Pairwise kinematic consensus is then performed using the two-node procedure in Fig. 3: for each neighbor b ∈N (a), node a applies this operation to the pair {T(a), T(b)},thereby producing a collection of intermediate consensus re-sults {X(a,b, con )}b∈N (a). These intermediate outputs are sub-sequently combined sequentially to form the full (network) kinematic consensus at node a, denoted as Xa, con (see [14], [15] for details). We now describe the two-node (pairwise) kinematic consensus procedure between the track sets T(a) and T(b) in detail, which consists of a track-matching stage followed by kine-matic fusion (Fig. 3). The role of track matching is to answer the question: Which track in T(a) corresponds to which track in T(b)? First, an optimal assignment between the two track sets is computed using a track-distance metric, yielding a one-to-one matching between tracks. We review the most common choice of track metric in Sec. II-C. The resulting pairs are then filtered using a cut-off distance c (i.e., only pairs with cost below c are considered valid matches). Each matched pair is subsequently fused to produce a single track estimate, typically via a weighted combination of the individual state estimates, where the weights reflect the relative confidence of each node. Unmatched tracks may be retained according to heuristic criteria (e.g., minimum track length) [14], [15]. 

2) Label consensus: Matched tracks identified during the pairwise kinematic-consensus stage (Fig. 3) are treated as local identity agreements, and are propagated transitively through the network via a label-consensus mechanism [14], [15]. We omit the specifics of this stage, as the proposed attack induces identity corruption through compromised track matching, which is then propagated by the label consensus mechanism (see Remark 1).

This section presents the adversarial model and feasibility assumptions for label hijacking (Sec. III-A), introduces the proposed TC-ITA strategy (Sec. III-B), and develops an MPC-based approach for generating stealthy spoofed trajectories in-spired by the takeover effect in RGPO attacks (Sec. III-C) [17]. The attack goal is to transfer the global label of a victim target to a controlled impostor target , such that the network continues to believe it is tracking the victim while in reality following the impostor. Remarkably, if the impostor is an attacker-controlled agent (e.g., a drone), its motion can be arbitrarily designed to induce any desired behavior under the stolen identity. 

Terminology: A compromised node is a sensor node whose communication channel is controlled by the attacker. A non-compromised node is a sensor node that is not under adversar-ial control; however, the attacker may intercept its transmitted messages or seek to influence its fusion output. A blind region 

is a region of the surveillance area that is not within the FoV of any non-compromised node. A non-compromised region is a region observed by at least one non-compromised node.

By aggregating this information, the attacker gains access to target trajectories even outside the sensing coverage of the compromised nodes, as long as the target is observed by at least one neighbor. From the intercepted data, the attacker constructs spoofed tracks and forwards them in place of the nominal local track estimates of the compromised nodes, thereby corrupting the local fusion processes and resulting consensus estimates of neighboring nodes. This mechanism follows the same inter-cept–modify–reinject paradigm as a replay attack .The proposed attack is developed under the following as-sumptions: A1) Sensor nodes have limited and partially non-overlapping FoVs. This allows the victim target to enter a blind region, which is required for label extraction. A2) The attacker must compromise direct neighbors of the nodes whose fusion processes it seeks to manipulate and of the nodes from which it aims to intercept track information. A3) Since nodes lack knowledge of the network geometry, non-compromised nodes cannot verify whether a reported track is physically observed by its sending node, and therefore accept spoofed tracks as valid during consensus. A4) The attacker can infer when the victim target enters and exits a blind region. The visibility inference in Assumption A4 enables the attacker to time the label extraction and injection stages described in Sec. III-B. It is obtained heuristically from intercepted communication patterns: the absence of reported updates over several time steps indicates that a target has left a node’s FoV, whereas continued reporting implies ongoing observability. Remarkably, the delay in detecting these transi-tions, which we assume to be negligible, depends on the local MTT track-management policy rather than on any knowledge of the network geometry.

Additionally, let t∗ denote the spoofed track transmitted by the attacker starting at time k0. We now describe the three stages of TC-ITA, as depicted in Fig. 5. 

1) Victim target mimicry: t∗ assumes the victim’s global label ℓv by mimicking tv closely enough to be selected by the track-matching rule in Sec. II-B1 (see Remark 2). This requires satisfying the matching condition ˜d(c)(tv, t ∗) < c for some k ∈ [k0, k 1), i.e., before the victim enters the blind region. At time k1, the attacker detects that the victim enters the blind region (Assumption A4), which triggers the start of the label extraction stage. 

2) Label extraction (pull-off stage): In the blind region, i.e., for k ∈ (k1, k 2), the network receives only t∗ as the candidate track associated with label ℓv. Since no competing track exists in this period, t∗ can evolve arbitrarily while retaining the hijacked label. Upon the victim’s re-entry to the non-compromised region at time k2, the separation condition 

˜d(c)(tv, t ∗) ≥ c must hold to ensure that tv is considered a new object and therefore assigned a different label than ℓv after confirmation; this condition must be maintained thereafter to prevent re-matching. Note that if the victim target does not re-enter the non-compromised region, t∗ is not at risk of being matched with tv, since ˜d(c)(tv, t ∗) = ˜d(c)(∅, t ∗) = c.Remarkably, this stage is directly analogous to a pull-off deception in radar tracking: whereas classical RGPO pulls the radar range gate away from the true target return, TC-ITA pulls off the target’s identity label. Note that label pull-off is achieved regardless of the stealthiness of t∗ during the blind interval (see the hard-switch variant in Sec. IV); the stealthy variant is further inspired by the RGPO takeover regime, providing kinematic plausibility by enforcing a smooth trajectory transition. 

3) Label injection: Assuming that the impostor target has entered the non-compromised region, the matching condition 

˜d(c)(ti, t ∗) < c must be enforced at some time k3 > k 2,causing ti and t∗ to be matched and thus share a global label. Since the track-matching mechanism retains the label of the longer-lived track, and assuming that the impostor target enters the scene later than the victim, the hijacked label ℓv is propagated to ti. If the injection is successful, then for k > k 3

the attacker can stop reporting t∗, after which ti becomes the sole candidate associated with ℓv and can thereafter evolve arbitrarily while retaining that identity. 

REMARK 1 (Targeted track consensus stage) . While the goal of TC-ITA is to hijack the victim target’s label ℓv, the attack does not manipulate the label-consensus mechanism itself. Instead, it operates entirely at the kinematic level by injecting a spoofed track t∗ that satisfies the track-matching constraints. This causes t∗ to be repeatedly matched with the victim track tv, drift during blind intervals, and eventually match an impostor track ti. Because label consensus is driven by persistent track matching, corrupting the kinematic consensus alone is sufficient to induce identity transfer. 

REMARK 2 (Single-step sufficiency) . Due to the time-averaged definition in (2) , a valid match between two tracks is established as soon as they are within distance c at any single time instant, irrespective of the window length. This property is exploited in the mimicry and label-injection stages of TC-ITA, where enforcing proximity for only one time step suffices to trigger track matching. During the label-extraction stage, the same property becomes critical: even a brief re-approach between the spoofed and victim tracks would immediately re-enable matching, necessitating sustained separation. C. Model Predictive Control for Stealthy Attack Design 

TC-ITA relies on satisfying the track-matching constraints at the transition instants shown in Fig. 5. This can be achieved through a hard-switch strategy , as illustrated in Fig. 6a: once the victim label has been extracted (i.e., the spoofed track has assumed ℓv), the attacker ceases transmission during the blind interval. When the impostor target becomes visible and is reported by non-compromised nodes, the attacker resumes transmission by forwarding a trajectory that matches the im-postor track ti. While sufficient to induce label hijacking, this strategy can produce kinematically implausible trajectories that may be detected by motion-consistency checks. To overcome this limitation, we propose a stealthy realization of TC-ITA that enforces kinematic consistency by enabling a smooth label pull-off, as shown in Fig. 6d. This strategy exhibits a takeover effect analogous to that observed in RGPO deception strategies [17]. Once the condition ˜d(c)(tv, t ∗) < c has been satisfied in the first attack stage (Sec. III-B1), the design of the spoofed track t∗ must address three main objectives: (i) smoothly converging toward the impostor track ti; (ii) maintaining sufficient separation from the reappearing victim track tv; and 

(iii) ensuring stealth by imposing physical constraints on the planned trajectory, such as velocity and acceleration limits (vmax and amax ). To systematically enforce these objectives and constraints, we formulate the design of t∗ as a constrained optimal control problem and solve it via MPC, which naturally accounts for system dynamics, prediction, and physical limits in trajectory generation. We emphasize that this optimization strategy is used exclusively by the attacker to generate spoofed trajectories and is not part of the tracking or fusion process. This paper reveals identity-level vulnerabilities in track-consensus distributed multi-target tracking. By exploiting the metric-based track-matching stage, the proposed track-consensus identity-theft attack enables label hijacking and tar-get impersonation. We introduce a hard-switch attack realiza-tion, showing that even abrupt and kinematically implausible trajectories suffice to trigger label hijacking under OSPA-based matching. We then propose a stealthy MPC-based realization that enforces smooth, physically consistent pull-off behavior while satisfying the same matching constraints. Simulations in a limited-FoV network confirm that both variants succeed.
