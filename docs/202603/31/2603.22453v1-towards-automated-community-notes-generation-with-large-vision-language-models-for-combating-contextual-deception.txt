# Towards Automated Community Notes Generation with Large Vision Language Models for Combating Contextual Deception 

## Jin Ma, Jingwen Yan, Mohammed Aldeen, Ethan Anderson, Taran Kavuru, Jinkyung Katie Park, Feng Luo, Long Cheng School of Computing, Clemson University 

## Abstract 

Community Notes have emerged as an effective crowd-sourced mechanism for combating online deception on social media platforms. However, its reliance on human contributors limits both the timeliness and scalability. In this work, we study the automated Community Notes generation method for image-based contextual deception , where an authentic im-age is paired with misleading context ( e.g. , time, entity, and event). Unlike prior work that primarily focuses on decep-tion detection ( i.e. , judging whether a post is true or false in a binary manner), Community Notes-style systems need to generate concise and grounded notes that help users recover the missing or corrected context. This problem remains un-derexplored due to three reasons: (i) datasets that support the research are scarce; (ii) methods must handle the dynamic nature of contextual deception; (iii) evaluation is difficult be-cause standard metrics do not capture whether notes actually improve user understanding. To address these gaps, we curate a real-world dataset, XC HECK , comprising X posts with asso-ciated Community Notes and external contexts. We further propose the Automated Context-Corrective Note generation method, named ACCN OTE , which is a retrieval-augmented, multi-agent collaboration framework built on large vision-language models. Finally, we introduce a new evaluation metric, Context Helpfulness Score ( CHS ), that aligns with user study outcomes rather than relying on lexical overlap. Experiments on our XC HECK dataset show that the proposed 

ACCN OTE improves both deception detection and note gener-ation performance over baselines, and exceeds a commercial tool GPT5-mini. Together, our dataset, method, and metric advance practical automated generation of context-corrective notes toward more responsible online social networks.

These two types of deception require different mitiga-tion strategies. Our work targets context manipulation ( i.e. , con-textual deception) because it naturally aligns with the goals of Community Notes. Specifically, X’s Community Notes guide [53] emphasizes that helpful notes should satisfy five criteria: (1) credibility: cite high-quality sources; (2) clar-ity: be easy to understand; (3) relevance: directly address the post’s claim; (4) veracity: provide important context; and (5) neutrality: use neutral or unbiased language. Despite extensive research on automated online deception detection, i.e. , determining whether the information is true or false [1, 17], little effort has been put into Community Notes generation. This gap stems from three practical chal-lenges. First, dataset limitations: most existing image-based deception datasets are either synthetic and fail to capture real-world complexity [26, 29, 55], are designed primarily for detection without explanatory notes [33, 35], or provide rationales that are not directly usable in a Community Notes-style format [13,40]. Second, methodological difficulty: con-textual deception is inherently dynamic, making it hard for pre-trained models to reliably infer the correct context. Third, 

evaluation mismatch: the goal of a note is to improve users’ understanding of the true context of a post, but standard text generation metrics that emphasize grammatical or lexical over-lap with reference text do not measure user helpfulness and can misrepresent real-world effectiveness. To overcome these three challenges, we take a “dataset →

method → evaluation” research approach accordingly. First, we curate a real-world dataset, XC HECK , consisting of X posts (image, text, timestamp, and metadata, etc. ), their correspond-ing Community Notes, and external contexts collected via re-verse web search. Second, we develop an Automated Context-

Corrective Note generation method ( ACCN OTE ), which is a retrieval-augmented multi-agent framework built on large vision-language models (LVLMs). It filters and organizes re-trieved evidence, generates multiple candidate notes through diverse reasoning paths, and selects the best note using Com-munity Notes criteria, improving grounding while mitigating noise from naive RAG [10, 20, 43]. Third, we introduce a new evaluation metric Context Helpfulness Score ( CHS ) that measures user-aligned helpfulness, addressing the mismatch between real-world goals and commonly used overlap-based text generation metrics. The contributions of this paper are as follows: • We curate XC HECK , a real-world multimodal dataset to facilitate research on automated Community Notes gen-eration. XC HECK contains X posts paired with ground-truth Community Notes, retrieved external context from reverse search, and annotations of topics and manipula-tion factors. • We propose ACCN OTE , a retrieval-augmented multi-agent framework that can be integrated with diverse foun-dation LVLMs to generate context-corrective notes, with explicit evidence filtering, stance-aware organization, and grounded citation. • We conduct a pilot user study and develop Context Helpfulness Score ( CHS ), a metric that evaluates gener-ated notes along five Community Notes criteria, includ-ing credibility, clarity, relevance, veracity, and neutrality.

## 2 Related Work 2.1 Contextual Deception Datasets 

In the field of image-based contextual deception, several datasets synthesize deceptive examples from non-deceptive data. For instance, NewsCLIPpings [29] begins with gen-uine image-text pairs from news outlets and constructs con-text manipulation cases by semantically mismatching images and captions using CLIP [39] and SBERT [41]. Similarly, MMOOC [55] recombines verified multimodal news articles to produce synthetic mismatches. MMFakeBench [26] in-stead leverages generative models and AI tools to construct a mixed-source multimodal misinformation benchmark. While such synthesization enables large-scale data creation, it may not capture the full complexity of real-world deceptive con-tent on social media platforms. By contrast, Fakeddit [33] and VERITE [35] collect real-world data from online websites, 

e.g. , Reddit and professional fact-checkers, but provide only veracity labels, without explanatory context. Recent datasets begin to include explanations to sup-port further research. AMMeBa [13] compiles a large-scale dataset of fact-checked claims with associated media through ClaimReview [16, 44]. Fin-Fact [40] aggregates image-text items and explanations from sources such as PolitiFact [48], 2Snopes [45], and FactCheck [14] with a focus on finance. However, these datasets are primarily drawn from already fact-checked items, limiting coverage of in-the-wild decep-tion. Therefore, XC HECK directly collects data from the X social media platform, covering data that has not been fact-checked yet (Section 3.2). Our XC HECK provides high-quality ground-truth explanations for developing and evaluating auto-mated note generation methods. 

## 2.2 Combating Contextual Deception 

In order to improve the explainability of online deception detection, multiple works have proposed adding explanations beyond detection results. In the text domain, Wang et al. [50] developed “competing wisdom” by organizing true and false evidence and prompting an LLM to produce dual justifica-tions, yielding rationales alongside decisions. Ma et al. [30] proposed LoCal, a multi-agent pipeline to produce structured and reviewable explanations, in which a decomposer breaks complex claims into sub-tasks, reasoning agents retrieve and answer with evidence, and evaluating agents enforce logi-cal equivalence and counterfactual robustness. De et al. [12] addressed rating latency in Community Notes by proposing Supernotes, which combines existing user-proposed notes and used a personalized “simulated jury” to select the candi-date most likely to be rated helpful, but still depends on the availability of human-written notes. However, these methods operate primarily on the text domain, thus do not directly extend to image-based settings. For combating image-based contextual deception, Abdelnabi et al. [2] retrieved textual and visual web evidence separately and assessed cross-modal consistency, while SNIFFER [38] checks both internal image-text consistency and alignment with external evidence us-ing an LLM. However, these systems were evaluated mainly on synthetic data such as NewsCLIPpings [29], restricting their applicability for real-world settings. In Section 6, we show that on the collected XC HECK , SNIFFER even underper-forms foundation LVLM baselines. Motivated by these works, we introduce ACCN OTE , a retrieval-augmented, multi-agent framework to generate context-corrective notes for combating image-based contextual deception.

## 3.1 Dataset Collection and Analysis 

XC HECK dataset was constructed in four stages as shown in Figure 2: deceptive data curation, non-deceptive control set addition, data cleaning and annotation, and context augmen-tation via reverse image search. 

3.1.1 Deceptive Data Curation 

We began by downloading the publicly available Notes 

dataset from the X Community Notes program [53], which provides note-level metadata for posts flagged as poten-tially misleading. As shown in Example 1, each commu-nity note includes a unique identifier ( noteId ), the cor-responding tweet ID ( tweetId ), a high-level classification label for indicating misleading or not ( classification ), binary subtype indicators showing the subcategories of deception ( e.g. , misleading: Missing Important Context ,

misleading: Outdated Information ), and an explanation written by the contributor ( summary ). Additional metadata includes the media relevance flags ( isMediaNote ), indicat-ing whether the post is image-based or not. We then iden-tified image-based contextual deception by retaining notes marked with isMediaNote=1 , and whose associated posts were tagged misleading: Missing Important Context=1 . To ex-clude content manipulations, we removed records labeled as misleading: Manipulated Media=1 , and we further dis-carded notes that did not explicitly reference images via a key-word filter ( e.g. , “photo”, “image”, “picture”, “photograph”). This procedure yielded an initial set of 3,574 notes. Because the Notes dataset does not include the tweet content, we used the X-API [54] to retrieve the corresponding tweet data for each remaining note, including the post text, image, times-tamp, and other metadata such as retweet count.

3.2.2 Factor Analysis 

Table 2 presents the deceptive factor taxonomy and the distri-bution of ten factors within the deceptive subset. The results show that deceptive posts frequently manipulate context fac-tors that are not directly observable from the image itself. Specifically, factors such as event, time, entity, location, and attribution each occur in more than 40% of deceptive posts, suggesting that these factors are the primary targets of con-textual deception. Overall, this distribution suggests that deceptive content preferentially exploits factors that re-quire external context beyond the image, enabling misleading narratives without altering the visual evidence.

## 4 Proposed Method 

Existing approaches cannot be directly applied to Commu-nity Notes generation because they struggle to meet the five criteria: credibility, veracity, clarity, relevance, and neutrality. To address this gap, we propose ACCN OTE , an automated context-corrective note generation framework that combines (i) retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) to improve cred-ibility and veracity by grounding the model in external evi-dence, and (ii) multi-agent collaboration to improve clarity, relevance, and neutrality by filtering noisy context, separating conflicting evidence, and refining the final note. ## 4.1 System Overview 

As shown in Figure 7, ACCN OTE comprises a Data Organizer agent, a pool of Reasoner agents, and a Judge agent. Given a social media post and its external context, the Data Organizer agent first filters the external contexts and clusters them into different groups according to their stance with respect to the post. Then, the Reasoner agents perform candidate notes gen-eration in parallel. Finally, the Judge agent selects the best one from all candidates based on the Community Notes criteria. The generation pipeline for each Reasoner agent is sum-marized in Algorithm 1. To improve note quality, we adopt a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting routine that encourages the agent to follow explicit intermediate reasoning steps be-fore drafting the final note. The process begins with Step 1, where the agent extracts the post’s main claim κ by jointly analyzing the image, the accompanying text, and publica-tion timestamp. In Step 2, the agent determines whether the claim is image-dependent. If the claim relies on the image, the agent verifies it against the image’s visual details and the factual context, and identifies potential contradictions. If the claim is not image-dependent, the agent evaluates it against the LVLM’s internal knowledge to detect inconsistencies. In Step 3, when external context is available, the agent further checks the claim against the retrieved evidence and again searches for contradictions. In Step 4, the agent assigns the post label based on whether any contradiction was identified in Step 2 or Step 3. If any contradiction is found, the post is labeled as deceptive , since it indicates context manipulation; otherwise it is labeled as non-deceptive . Finally, in both cases, the agent distills a concise rationale from its CoT trace and attaches the source URLs that were used during the resoning process to the output note. This structured CoT procedure makes the decision process explicit and promotes faithfulness by tying the final note directly to the supporting evidence.

## 5 Metric Development 

In order to evaluate the generated context-corrective notes, we need to measure how useful they actually are in helping users understand the deception, specifically against the five Community Notes criteria. Existing text generation metrics focus primarily on lexical overlap between generated text and reference text, and therefore do not directly measure the user-perceived helpfulness (Section 5.2). To evaluate whether a generated note actually helps users understand a post, we first conduct a pilot user study to characterize how users assess notes based on the five key dimensions, establishing a ground truth for what makes a note helpful. Guided by these user study results, we then propose Context Helpfulness Score (CHS), a new metric that runs five specific checks and aligns with user expectations. 

## 5.1 Pilot User Study

## 7 Limitations and Discussions 

The design of ACCN OTE relies on tight coupling among mul-tiple agents, which introduces a strong dependency on the full pipeline. Each component plays a structurally necessary role in producing the final note. For example, if the data organizer agent is removed, all retrieved external context collapses into a single cluster, which prevents multi-way stance-aware rea-soning. If the reasoner agents are absent, the system cannot generate multiple candidates conditioned on different evi-dence partitions, and the downstream selection stage becomes ill-posed. This dependence limits the modularization of in-dividual agents, and makes the overall system sensitive to failures in any single stage. A promising direction is to im-prove modularity by introducing more robust intermediate representations and explicit confidence signals such that the partial pipeline execution remains effective. We observe that most LVLMs, including GPT5-mini, ex-hibit higher recall than precision, indicating a tendency to incur more false positives (FPs). Adding external evidence can reduce FPs, but it may also increase false negatives (FNs), reflecting a non-trivial precision-recall trade-off. In our exper-iment, enabling web search for GPT5-mini reduces FP from 725 to 616 (-109), while FN increases from 79 to 122 (+43). For NVILA@15B, ACCN OTE reduces FP from 208 to 135 (-73), with a smaller FN increase from 115 to 138 (+23). This motivates future work on better calibration with the goal of reducing FPs without disproportionately increasing FNs. 

## 8 Conclusion 

In this work, we address the gap in automated Community Notes generation for image-based contextual deception. We introduce XC HECK , a real-world multimodal dataset of X posts paired with ground-truth Community Notes, together with re-trieved external context. We further propose ACCN OTE , a retrieval-augmented multi-agent framework that enhances LVLMs to generate context-corrective notes. To evaluate whether generated notes are helpful to users, we develop a new metric CHS that aligns with pilot user study outcomes. Extensive experiments show that ACCN OTE consistently out-performs foundation LVLM baselines and naive RAG on both deception detection and note generation tasks on XC HECK ,and also exceeds the commercial baseline GPT5-mini. Taken together, our dataset, method, and evaluation metric establish a solid and promising foundation for practical and scalable automated note generation. 13 Ethical Considerations 

This work studies automated Community Notes generation for contextual deception. Because the data originate from real-world social media content and the system may influence how users interpret posts, we consider ethics across data collection, annotation, user studies, and dataset release. 

Dataset collection and privacy. XC HECK is constructed from publicly accessible posts on X. We only use content that is available without accessing private accounts, private messages, or restricted groups. To reduce privacy risks, we minimize the amount of personally identifying information (PII) stored and presented. In particular, we avoid collecting or releasing user profile metadata beyond what is necessary for research purposes, and we do not attempt to infer sensitive attributes about individuals. When presenting examples in the paper, we avoid highlighting usernames or personal details unless they are essential for understanding the study. 

Annotation procedure and annotator welfare. Annota-tions are performed with written guidelines and exemplars to promote consistency and to reduce subjective or identity-based judgments. Because deceptive content can include dis-turbing or sensitive material, we adopt standard annotator welfare practices, including clear content warnings, the ability to opt out of specific items, and limiting exposure to poten-tially harmful content. 

User study protections. The user study is designed to evaluate note quality dimensions such as credibility, clarity, relevance, veracity, and neutrality. Participants are informed about the study purpose, the types of content they may en-counter, and that their participation is voluntary. They can skip questions or withdraw at any time without penalty. We collect only the minimum information necessary for analysis, store responses securely, and report results only in aggregate form. Since study materials may include misleading claims, we in-clude debriefing language that clarifies that some posts are deceptive and provides guidance to consult reliable sources when uncertain. The user study was approved by the Institu-tional Review Board (IRB). 

Dataset release and responsible use. To support repro-ducibility while respecting platform policies and copyright constraints, we release XC HECK in a policy-compliant for-mat that contains only tweet ID rather than redistributing raw media. Reconstructing full post content requires “hydration” via official X APIs or other compliant procedures, and any hydrated content remains subject to X policy terms and the original creators rights. 

Attribution for visual materials. All screenshots and pho-tos reproduced in this paper are sourced from X, and we credit X as the platform of origin. Copyright and ownership of the original media remain with their respective rights holders. These materials are included solely for research illustration.
