# Abstract 

With the rapid advancement of image-to-video (I2V) gen-eration models, their potential for misuse in creating ma-licious content has become a significant concern. For in-stance, a single image can be exploited to generate a fake video, which can be used to attract attention and gain bene-fits. This phenomenon is referred to as an I2V generation misuse . Existing image protection methods suffer from the absence of a unified benchmark, leading to an incomplete evaluation framework. Furthermore, these methods have not been systematically assessed in I2V generation scenarios and against preprocessing attacks, which complicates the evalu-ation of their effectiveness in real-world deployment scenar-ios. To address this challenge, we propose IP-Bench (Im-age Protection Bench) , the first systematic benchmark de-signed to evaluate protection methods in I2V generation sce-narios. This benchmark examines 6 representative protection methods and 5 state-of-the-art I2V models. Furthermore, our work systematically evaluates protection methods’ ro-bustness with two robustness attack strategies under practical scenarios and analyzes their cross-model & cross-modality transferability. Overall, IP-Bench establishes a systematic, reproducible, and extensible evaluation framework for image protection methods in I2V generation scenarios. 

# 1 Introduction 

Image-to-Video (I2V) generation has experienced rapid progress in recent years [1, 2], driven by the development of diffusion-based models such as seeddance2.0 [3], and hun-yuanVideo [4]. Specifically, I2V generation transforms static images into temporally coherent videos, pushing generative models beyond single-image synthesis. By drastically low-ering the barrier of video creation, this technology facilitates diverse applications in entertainment [5, 6], education [7], and content generation [8]. Ultimately, the ability to produce high-quality videos from simple inputs democratizes produc-tion, empowering individual creators and reshaping creative industries. Despite being powerful, I2V models may also introduce significant security risks, including the generation of deepfakes [9, 10] and the dissemination of misinforma-tion [11].

To mitigate these threats, prior research has proposed var-ious image protection methods designed to prevent unau-thorized content generation by I2V models [12]. Most ex-isting methods adopt perturbation-based strategies, such as I2VGuard [13] and VGMShield [14]. These methods build upon earlier studies that aim to protect images from unau-thorized editing by Image-to-Image (I2I) diffusion models, including PhotoGuard [15], Mist [16], and EditShield [17]. Despite the emergence of these image protection meth-ods, existing assessments are often limited to specific mod-els or single metrics, making it difficult to fairly compare different protection strategies or assess their generalization capabilities [18].While some studies, such as EditShield [17] and Mist [16], incorporate preprocessing attacks to evaluate the robustness of protection methods, others do not, lead-ing to a lack of unified standards for assessing defensive re-silience. To address this gap, we introduce IP-Bench (Im-age Protection Bench), the first systematic benchmark for image protection methods in I2V generation scenarios. We assess 6 protection methods (including both I2V-specific and I2I-based approaches) across 5 widely used I2V models fea-turing distinct structures, such as DiT and U-Net [19, 20]. Addition-ally, we examine cross-model and cross-modality general-ization to demonstrate the transferability of protection meth-ods across different I2V model architectures and across dif-ferent generation scenarios. I2VGuard [13] demonstrates notable robustness against pre-processing attacks, maintaining a degradation rate above 0.02 across most models. VGMShield [14] demonstrates strong performance on specific models such as WAN [22], achiev-ing a degradation rate of 0.036. PhotoGuard [15] achieves a balanced performance, with a moderate PSNR of 31.77 and a degradation rate of 0.041 on Skyreel [23]. # 2 Related Work 

Image-to-Video Generation Models. Recent advances in diffusion models have significantly accelerated the devel-opment of Image-to-Video (I2V) generation. Existing ap-proaches mainly differ in their backbone architectures. Early methods, such as Stable Video Diffusion (SVD) [21], adopt a U-Net-based framework with a 3D VAE to model spa-tiotemporal representations. More recent works increas-ingly employ Diffusion Transformers (DiT) [19], which of-fer improved scalability and modeling capacity. Represen-tative models include Wan2.2 [22], CogVideoX [25], and Skyreel [23], which enhance multimodal understanding and generation quality through transformer-based designs. In addition to backbone evolution, temporal modeling has become a key research focus [26]. Techniques such as Tem-poral VAE explicitly compress dynamic information across frames to improve temporal consistency [24, 27]. The di-versity of architectural designs, ranging from U-Net to DiT and specialized temporal modules [28], leads to substantial differences in how visual and motion information are pro-cessed [29]. 

Perturbation-based Image Protection. Perturbation-based defense is the dominant paradigm for protecting images against diffusion models [18, 30, 31]. These methods intro-duce adversarial noise to disrupt the generation process, typ-ically by interfering with latent representations or attention mechanisms. Early works mainly targeted Image-to-Image (I2I) editing [32–34]. For example, PhotoGuard perturbs the encoding stage of diffusion models, while EditShield and MIST improve robustness and transferability through opti-mized adversarial objectives [15–17]. Glaze [35] focuses on protecting artistic style by obscuring stylistic features. Benchmark and Evaluation Metrics. Evaluating image protection methods [36] involves two key aspects: (1) pre-serving the visual quality of the protected image [37] and (2) degrading the quality or consistency of the generated video [38]. For image fidelity, common metrics include PSNR, SSIM, and LPIPS, along with semantic similarity measures such as CLIP Similarity Score. For generated videos, evaluation requires assessing both visual realism and temporal coherence. Metrics such as Fréchet Video Distance (FVD) [39] are widely used to mea-sure distributional similarity between video sets. Bench-marks like VBench [40, 41] further evaluate the generated videos in multiple well-defined dimensions including Sub-ject Consistency, Motion Smoothness, etc. 

# 3 Design of IP-Bench 3.1 Threat Model 

We formalize the image protection task with a threat model specifying the objectives and capabilities of two parties: the adversary (unauthorized I2V user) and the defender (image protector). 

Adversary’s Objective and Capability. The adversary aims to generate high-quality videos from protected images using I2V models, with success measured by visual fidelity, gener-ation quality, and semantic alignment. Regarding the capa-bility, we assume the adversary can only access the protected image instead of the protection method, and can apply image transformation methods like JPEG Compression, Gaussian Noise, or geometric transformations to bypass protection. 

Defender’s Objective and Capability. The defender applies imperceptible perturbations before image release to degrade video generation quality while preserving visual quality for users. The defender employs the protection methods and op-timizes perturbations to interfere with the generation process of I2V models. This threat model establishes a clear adversarial dynamic: the defender proactively poisons images to sabotage genera-tion attempts, while the adversary attempts to overcome this sabotage.

I2V Models. We evaluate protection methods across 5 state-of-the-art I2V models with diverse architectures. Table 2 summarizes the models used in IP-Bench. The selected models cover both U-Net (SVD) and DiT (LTX, Skyreel, CogVideoX, Wan2.2) architectures, enabling evaluation of cross-architecture transferability. The DiT-based models support text prompt guidance, allowing us to assess protec-tion effectiveness under semantic conditioning, while SVD serves as a strong image-conditioned baseline. 

Dataset. We construct the Flickr30k-MSCOCO-300 

dataset by selecting 300 high-quality images from Flickr30k [42] and MSCOCO [43]. Video generation prompts are generated using Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct [44] to ensure semantic alignment with source images and encourage meaningful motion dynamics. The full dataset will be released with IP-Bench. 

Preprocessing Attacks. To evaluate the robustness of pro-tection methods, we consider several attacks where adver-saries attempt to remove or neutralize perturbations through preprocessing. We focus on two representative strategies based on elimination and addition perspectives. • Elimination-based Attack (JPEG Compression). 

Adversaries may apply lossy compression to eliminate high-frequency adversarial signals. We evaluate protec-tion robustness against JPEG Compression [45]. We employ a standard quality factor (QF) of 75, a common setting that balances visual fidelity and file size, to test if adversarial perturbations can survive lossy quantiza-tion. • Addition-based Attack (Gaussian Noise). Adver-saries may add random noise to mask or overwhelm the protective perturbation. We apply additive Gaussian Noise with standard deviations of 0.01 to test whether the protection signal can withstand signal-to-noise ratio degradation. These preprocessing attacks represent practical attempts to bypass protection without requiring knowledge of the de-fense mechanism, providing a rigorous test of real-world ro-bustness. 

Metrics. To comprehensively assess IP-Bench, we employ a multi-dimensional evaluation protocol focusing on protec-tion effectiveness and visual fidelity. • Protection Effectiveness. We utilize the VBench 

suite [40] to evaluate the quality of generated videos across four critical dimensions: Subject Consistency ,

Motion Smoothness , Aesthetic Quality , and Imaging Quality . A significant drop in these scores indicates successful disruption of the I2V generation process. Furthermore, we employ the Fréchet Video Distance (FVD) [39] to quantify the distributional discrepancy between videos generated from original versus pro-tected (attacked) images. A higher FVD score reflects a stronger protective effect, as it indicates a greater devi-ation from the intended high-quality video distribution.

Implementation Details. All experiments are conducted on an NVIDIA L20 GPU server. Due to the high memory re-quirements of I2V model inference and varying image reso-lutions across the dataset that prevent efficient stacking, we set the batch size to 1 for all protection and generation exper-iments. All protection methods are implemented in PyTorch, and images are processed at their original resolutions with-out resizing to preserve semantic consistency. For I2VGuard, as no official implementation is publicly available, we re-implement the method based on the description in the orig-inal paper. For VGMShield, we adopt its undirected variant in our experiments. For PhotoGuard, we adopt its encoder attack variant in our experiments. For all remaining meth-ods, we employ their official implementations with default parameters. To ensure the reliability of our benchmark in assessing long-term temporal consistency, we configure each model to generate extended video sequences rather than short clips. Specifically, we set the generation length to 25 frames for SVD, 33 frames for CogVideoX, and 41 frames for Wan2.2. For LTX, Skyreel, the generation is extended to 49 frames.

# 4.2.1 Evaluation of Protection Persistence 

Figure 4 illustrates the resilience of protection methods against two common preprocessing attacks: JPEG Compres-sion and Gaussian Noise. Overall, we observe a significant “protection erosion” effect, where the effectiveness of all methods diminishes when the protected images are prepro-cessed by the adversary. 

Resilience Against JPEG Compression. As shown in Fig-ure 4, JPEG Compression (quality factor=75) acts as an over-whelmingly powerful “purification” step, revealing a general lack of robustness across almost all evaluated methods. Our results indicate that Mist is the only method capable of maintaining a non-negligible disruptive effect under such an attack, though its average VBench degradation rate still drops from 6.6% to approximately 4.0%. Despite minimal visual recovery (as seen in Table 4a), I2VGuard’s residual effectiveness drops below 1%. This suggests that while its perturbations persist in the pixel domain, compression erodes their moderate initial strength until they can no longer over-come the model’s inherent denoising. Other methods, such as PhotoGuard and EditShield, lose their utility too, showing an overall negligible degradation rate of VBench scores. Interestingly, JPEG Compression exerts a minimal impact on FVD scores; for most methods, the FVD between original and attacked videos remains largely consistent with those be-tween original and protected videos prior to the attack, sug-gesting that while the disruptive power is eroded, the tempo-ral distribution shifts remain relatively stable. The extreme vulnerability to JPEG Compression can be at-tributed to its fundamental signal-processing characteristics: • High-Frequency Erasure: Most protection methods rely on fine-grained, high-frequency adversarial noise. JPEG’s Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) and subse-quent quantization steps are specifically designed to discard these high-frequency components, effectively “smoothing” the image and neutralizing the adversarial gradients before they reach the model’s latent space. • Non-Differentiable Manifold Shift: JPEG intro-duces blocky artifacts and non-differentiable distor-tions. These modifications shift the image away from the specific adversarial manifold for which the pertur-bations were optimized, rendering the protection inef-fective even if some noise remains visible. 

Resilience Against Gaussian Noise. Under Gaussian Noise attack ( σ = 0.01), the protection effectiveness exhibits pat-terns largely consistent with JPEG Compression, but with notable exceptions. As shown in Figure 4, and confirmed by the image quality metrics in Table 4b, the additive noise further degrades the visual quality of protected images. Sim-ilar to JPEG Compression, Mist and PhotoGuard show sub-stantial recovery in their protection effectiveness, with degra-dation rates dropping significantly across most models. This suggests that Gaussian Noise, like compression, can partially neutralize structured adversarial perturbations. However, an intriguing phenomenon emerges for I2VGuard: unlike other methods, its protection effectiveness increases under Gaussian Noise on several models. On CogVideoX, the VBench degradation rate rises from 1.6% to 3.4%, and on LTX from 0.9% to 2.0%. This divergent response to Gaussian Noise suggests that different protection mechanisms possess varying levels of resilience to random interference. While most methods experience a masking effect where noise diminishes the adversarial impact, the disruption caused by I2VGuard appears more persistent, and in some cases, worsened by the noise. This indicates that I2VGuard’s specific perturbation patterns may be more structurally aligned with the vulnerabilities of temporal at-tention in certain models, allowing them to remain effective or even become amplified under stochastic noise. Similar stability in FVD is observed under Gaussian Noise across most protection methods and models. # 4.2.2 Visual Quality under Attacks 

Table 4 presents the visual quality metrics of protected im-ages after undergoing attacks (JPEG Compression and Gaus-sian Noise). 

Impact of JPEG Compression. When subjected to JPEG Compression (quality factor = 75), nearly all protection methods exhibit a significant recovery in visual quality met-rics: PSNR, SSIM, and CLIP scores increase, while LPIPS and FID decrease. This trend indicates that JPEG Compres-sion effectively dissipates adversarial perturbations in the pixel domain, causing attacked images to revert closer to the original distribution. Notably, I2VGuard and Mist demon-strate higher robustness against the image quality recovery compared to the RandomNoise baseline. After JPEG Com-pression, their image quality metrics show minimal recovery (e.g., I2VGuard PSNR increases only 0.01, Mist increases 0.07), whereas RandomNoise exhibits substantial quality re-bound (PSNR increases 5.26). This indicates that the pertur-bations generated by I2VGuard and Mist are more resilient to compression-based removal. PhotoGuard also shows moder-ate robustness, with quality recovery generally lower than the baseline except in LPIPS. These results confirm that JPEG Compression acts as a potent defense against adversarial per-turbations, largely neutralizing protection effects. 

Impact of Gaussian Noise. In contrast, adding Gaussian Noise ( σ = 0.01, approximating L∞ ≈ 8/255) leads to fur-ther degradation in visual quality preservation compared to the protected images. Unlike JPEG Compression, which smooths out high-frequency perturbations, additive noise compounds the existing adversarial perturbations, resulting in additional structural disruption in the pixel domain. This is evidenced by lower PSNR and SSIM scores across all meth-ods (e.g., EditShield drops from 38.31 to 35.97 in PSNR), indicating that the superposition of protection noise and at-tack noise exacerbates visual distortion rather than mitigating it. Through this subsection of experiments, we have the fol-lowing insight. 

Takeaway: Existing protection methods demonstrate a critical lack of robustness against preprocessing attacks, particularly JPEG compression. While current methods often target latent representations, the resulting perturba-tions are ultimately superimposed on the pixel domain without explicit spectral constraints. These noise patterns frequently reside in high-frequency bands—areas where human visual sensitivity is low (ensuring imperceptibil-ity) but which are the primary targets for removal dur-ing JPEG’s quantization process. The lack of frequency-aware optimization means that the “protection” is easily purified by standard signal-processing transformations. A key direction for future research lies in strategically em-bedding perturbations into more resilient frequency com-ponents (e.g., mid-frequencies) to balance robust defensive utility with visual imperceptibility. 

# 4.3 Cross-Model & Cross-Modality General-ization Analysis 

In this subsection, we analyze the generalization ability of the protection methods from the perspective of cross-model transferability and cross-modality transferability. 

Cross-Model Transferability. Among the evaluated pro-tection methods, I2VGuard targets SVD’s temporal atten-tion blocks and VAE encoder, while VGMShield disrupts two specific embedders of SVD: the first embedder layer for conditional frame embeddings and the fourth embedder layer for temporal-level prediction. However, as illustrated in Fig-ure 2, we do not observe any remarkable algorithm-specific transferability. Instead, the VBench degradation rates and FVD metrics exhibit uniform trends across different mod-els regardless of the protection algorithm used. For instance, nearly all methods yield the lowest VBench degradation rates on LTX and Skyreel, and the minimum FVD on CogVideoX. These consistent patterns suggest that the observed perfor-mance variance stems from the inherent sensitivity of the tar-get models to perturbations, rather than the specific design of the protection methods. 

Cross-Modality Transferability. As evidenced in Figure 2, image-level protection methods demonstrate superior effi-cacy when transferred to the video domain. Specifically, Mist achieves the highest VBench degradation rates across all models, while PhotoGuard maintains a remarkable bal-ance between high protection strength and visual fidelity. We attribute this success to the fact that these methods target static components (e.g., VAE and U-Net) without the burden 7of optimizing for temporal consistency. This simpler opti-mization objective allows for the generation of more potent adversarial noise in shorter time. Furthermore, the shared ar-chitectural foundations between I2I and I2V models, such as the inherited VAE and latent manifold, act as a bridge that ensures these image-centric perturbations remain highly dis-ruptive during video generation scenarios. 

Takeaway: The evaluated methods exhibit limited cross-model transferability, as their protective efficacy is primar-ily dictated by the target models’ inherent sensitivity rather than specific algorithmic designs. Conversely, image-level methods demonstrate robust cross-modality transferabil-ity; by bypassing the complexities of temporal consis-tency, these methods leverage simpler optimization objec-tives and shared architectural foundations (e.g., VAE and latent manifolds) to maintain high disruptive potency when transitioned from the image to the video domain. 

# 5 Limitations 

In this section, we reflect on several limitations of our current benchmark that may influence the interpretation of the results and point toward future research directions. 

Absence of Closed-source Models. Our evaluation is re-stricted to open-source architectures. While covering diverse models like SVD and Wan2.2, the omission of proprietary systems (e.g., Sora, Kling) limits our understanding of pro-tection efficacy in true black-box scenarios. Commercial APIs may employ undisclosed preprocessing or safety fil-ters that interact unpredictably with perturbations. Assess-ing transferability to these closed-source models remains a critical objective for future benchmark iterations. 

Potential Bias in Prompt Distribution. Our prompts are currently generated exclusively by Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct. While ensuring descriptive accuracy, this reliance may introduce semantic "priors" or a specific linguistic style that I2V models respond to more predictably. To en-hance diversity, we plan to incorporate human-expert-crafted prompts and multi-LLM outputs in future updates to ensure a more robust and generalized evaluation. 

Lack of Standardized Constraints and Complexity Anal-ysis. Our evaluation currently lacks a unified noise bud-get (e.g., fixed L∞ bounds) and a formal complexity analy-sis. This absence of standardized constraints may introduce bias in performance comparisons, while the lack of quan-titative data on computational overheadlimits insights into real-world deployability. Future work will benchmark these methods under equivalent perturbation and provide a rigor-ous analysis of their practical efficiency. 

# 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we present IP-Bench, the first systematic bench-mark that evaluates 6 protection methods in I2V generation scenarios across 4 key dimensions: protection effectiveness, visual fidelity, robustness against potential attacks, and cross-model as well as cross-modality transferability. A granular analysis of our experimental results indicates the inherent trade-off between protection effectiveness and visual fidelity, while simultaneously highlighting the challenges regarding robustness against attacks.
