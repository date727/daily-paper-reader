Towards Physically Realizable Adversarial Attenuation Patch against SAR Object Detection 

Yiming Zhang, Weibo Qin, Feng Wang* 

Key Laboratory for Information Science of Electromagnetic Waves (MoE) School of Information Science and Technology, Fudan University Shanghai 200433, China zhangym21@m.fudan.edu.cn, wbqin23@m.fudan.edu.cn, fengwang@fudan.edu.cn 

Abstract—Deep neural networks have demonstrated excellent performance in SAR target detection tasks but remain susceptible to adversarial attacks. Existing SAR-specific attack methods can effectively deceive detectors; however, they often introduce noticeable perturbations and are largely confined to digital domain, neglecting physical implementation constrains for attacking SAR systems. In this paper, a novel Adversarial Attenuation Patch (AAP) method is proposed that employs energy-constrained optimization strategy coupled with an attenuation-based deployment framework to achieve a seamless balance between attack effectiveness and stealthiness. More importantly, AAP exhibits strong potential for physical realization by aligning with signal-level electronic jamming mechanisms. Experimental results show that AAP effectively degrades detection performance while preserving high imperceptibility, and shows favorable transferability across different models. This study provides a physical grounded perspective for adversarial attacks on SAR target detection systems and facilitates the design of more covert and practically deployable attack strategies. The source code is made available at  https://github.com/boremycin/SAAP .

Keywords—Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR); Target Detection; Adversarial Patch; Adversarial Examples; Electronic Jamming. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Synthetic aperture radar (SAR) is an activate sensing system that leverages platform motion to synthesize large virtual aperture and employs coherent signal processing to produce high-resolution images [1, 2]. It possesses the unique capability to conduct observations under all-time and all-weather conditions [3]. With the rapid advancement of deep learning, deep neural networks (DNNs) have been extensively adopted for SAR image interpretation, especially in object detection tasks [4, 5]. 

Despite their remarkable performance, DNNs has the intrinsic vulnerability to adversarial examples, as demonstrated by Szegedy et al. [6], where carefully crafted perturbations can induce erroneous predictions. Motivated by this finding, numerous attack approaches have been proposed across diverse DNNs-based tasks, demonstrating potent effectiveness and notable adversarial transferability [7-10]. While conventional adversarial attacks distribute quasi-imperceptible perturbations across the entire image, patch-based attacks restrict perturbations to localized regions  while relaxing the imperceptibility constraint, offering improved practicality for real-world deployment [11-13] . In the SAR domain, recent works have attempted to incorporate domain-specific characteristics adversarial examples generation [14-17]. For instance, Duan et al. [18] introduced a region-adaptive local patch attack by considering the size of location of perturbation; while Yuan et al. [19] utilized the attributed scattering center (ASC) model [20] into adversarial patch generation for SAR detectors. These approaches highlight the importance of integrating SAR-specific priors into adversarial attack design. 

However, unlike Electro-Optical  images, SAR images are typically grayscale and primarily characterized by sparse structures with limited semantic redundancy . As a result, directly applying image-domain patch-based attacks often introduces conspicuous artifacts that deviate from the underlying scattering mechanism, making the perturbations easily detectable and hindering the real-world deployment. This limitation stems from the fundamental mismatch between image-domain perturbations and the physical SAR imaging process.  To tackle the issue, we incorporate the SAR imaging mechanism into more physical viable adversarial patch design. Inspired by barrage jamming strategies for SAR systems  [21, 22], we propose a novel Adversarial Attenuation Patch (AAP) attack that achieves notably effective attacks as well as preserves the structural consistency and perceptual plausibility of the target by selectively suppressing the backscattered energy of target regions and constraining the 

magnitude of the adversarial patch, thereby enhancing physical realizability for real-world deployment.  In summary, the main contributions of this paper are as follows: 

(1) A physically realizable patch-based attack method termed Adversarial Attenuation Patch (AAP) is proposed, where the adversarial patch is constrained in both location and magnitude to ensure compatibility with the feasible electronic jamming implementations. 

(2) Extensive experiments on the SAR-Ship-Dataset [23] demonstrate that our method outperforms benchmark patch-based methods. Furthermore, the AAP maintains superior perceptual stealthiness, making adversarial examples significantly harder to be identified as abnormal instances.

III. E XPERIMENTS 

A. Experimental Setup 

The experiments are conducted on the SAR-Ship-Dataset [23], which is composed of 39729 ship instances with an image resolution of 256 × 256 pixels. We adopt YOLOv5 [25] and YOLOv11 [26] as the target detection models. Specifically, YOLOv5-M is used as the white-box model. To further evaluate the transferability of the generated adversarial examples, we consider multiple unseen models for black-box evaluation, including YOLOv5-L, X and YOLOv11-N, M, X. To comprehensively assess the effectiveness of the proposed AAP, we compare it with three representative patch-based adversarial attack methods, namely AdvPatch [12], DPatch [13] and RobustDPatch [24]. AdvPatch is initially designed for classification tasks and reconfigured for detection task in this study, while DPatch and RobustDPatch are inherently tailored for Electro-Optical object detection. 

For a fair comparison, all baseline methods follow their original setup and several common parameters are unified across all attacks. To be specific, we randomly selected 100 test images, set the number of optimization iterations to 3000, and utilize a learning rate of α = 5.0 . For AdvPatch, the patch size is confined to a ratio of 0.15 relative to the input image size, and for DPatch and RobustDPatch, the size of the patch is set to 120 × 120 . All aforementioned patches are located at random position. In contrast, the placement of AAP, including patch size and location, is determined on the ground-truth labels. For the EOT setting, the crop size, brightness adjustment range, and rotation probability weights are set to [50, 50], [0.7, 1.4], and [0.4, 0.2, 0.2, 0.2], respectively.

C. Adversarial Effectiveness and Transferibility 

Table 1 shows the effectiveness of our AAP attack compared with other patch-based methods. The well-trained YOLOv5-M model serves as the white-box target and achieves mAP50 of 96.7% and mAP50-95 of 69.1%, indicating strong baseline performance and posing achallenging benchmark for attack evaluation. 

Our AAP exhibits predominant superiority over other methods. Specially, it reduces mAP50 and mAP50-95 by 

26.8% and 32.6% more than the second-best AdvPatch respectively. In addition, AAP yields the highest FNR and FPPI of 0.413 and 1.021, surpassing AdvPatch by margins of 0.109 and 0.352 , respectively. 

The results illustrated in Fig. 2 validates that adversarial examples generated from the surrogate model YOLOv5-M maintain strong attacking effectiveness when transferred to black-box target models with different scale (e.g., YOLOv5-L and YOLOv5-X) and distinct architectures (e.g., YOLOv11-N, M, and L). It can be seen that the proposed AAP exhibits strong cross-model adversarial transferability. 

This suggests that the proposed optimization and deployment strategies enable the learned perturbation to capturing model-agnostic vulnerabilities, highlighting the practically potential in physical deployment scenarios.

IV. CONCLUSION 

In this work, we propose AAP, a novel and potentially physical-realizable adversarial patch tailored for SAR object detection. By integrating an energy-constrained optimization strategy with an attenuation-based deployment mechanism, our approach achieves significant drops in mAP while maintaining predominantly imperceptible compared with replacing patches. Experimental results reveal that AAP effectively induces diverse failure modes, including missed detection, false alarm and confidence drop. 

Specifically, AAP demonstrates robust cross-scale and cross-architecture adversarial transferability, facilitating its operational utility for real-world application.
