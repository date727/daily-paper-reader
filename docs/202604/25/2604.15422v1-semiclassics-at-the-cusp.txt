# Semiclassics at the cusp 

Jahmall Bersini ✩, Domenico Orlando ★✩ Susanne Reffert ✩, and Jesse Woods ✩

✩ Albert Einstein Center for Fundamental Physics, Institute for Theoretical Physics, University of Bern, Sidlerstrasse 5, CH-3012 Bern, Switzerland 

★ INFN sezione di Torino. via Pietro Giuria 1, 10125 Torino, Italy 

We study cusped Wilson line operators in the Abelian Higgs model in 

d = 4 − ϵ at large external charges. Using a double-scaling limit Q → ∞ ,

ϵ → 0 with Qϵ fixed, we develop a semiclassical framework that provides analytic control beyond fixed-order perturbation theory. We compute the cusp anomalous dimension for arbitrary charges up to next-to-next-to-leading order in the gauge coupling, while resumming scalar self-interactions to all orders. Our results interpolate between perturbative and large-charge regimes, accessing domains that are invisible in fixed-order perturbation theory. As an application, we provide new predictions for various defect 

cft observables, including the Mandelstam–Schwinger-dressed two-point function characterizing the superconducting phase transition.

21. Introduction 

Semiclassical calculations in a regime of large charge, or in a double-scaling limit involving large charge, have proven to be an extremely successful tool for accessing strongly coupled systems over the last decade [1, 2]. This framework has been applied to the three-dimensional O(N) model at the Wilson–Fisher point [3–5] and has subsequently been extended to fermionic models [6–8]. An obvious question is how to apply this formalism to systems with gauge symmetry. This immediately raises a variety of issues related to the requirement that observables be gauge invariant. The Gauss constraint implies that gauge-invariant charged operators must be non-local. It follows that, by Elitzur’s theorem [9], the naive n-point correlation function of charged fields vanishes identically. The traditional way to generalize these correlators to gauge theories has been to dress products of operators à la Dirac [10] by including the associated Coulomb fields 1,or à la Mandelstam and Schwinger [12–15] by adding a Wilson line joining the insertions. In this article, we take a general approach and consider a class of observables constructed from line intersections. The simplest case is a cusp: two semi-infinite lines carrying different charges Q1 and Q2 meeting at a point (the cusp). The problem of line operators in conformal field theories ( cft s) has been addressed repeatedly in the recent literature [16–25]. The so-called cusp anomalous dimension ΓQ1Q2 is the primary quantity of interest, along with the spectrum. It measures the strength of the divergence produced by a cusp in a Wilson loop. A relatively modern perspective on Wilson lines and other extended operators is in terms of defect operators [39, 40]: their insertion in the path integral can be absorbed as a new term in the action localized on a sub-manifold. Defects have been widely studied due to the relevance of impurities or boundaries [41–45], as well as brane dynamics [46]. For Q2 = 0, the dimension of the defect-creation operator ∆Q0 controls the critical behavior of the Mandelstam– Schwinger ( ms ) dressed two-point function, which is of interest in characterizing the superconducting phase transition [56–58]. A natural setting for studying the behavior of a cusp in a gauge cft is the Abelian Higgs model. At weak coupling, this is a system of N scalar fields, all with unit charge under a gauged U(1) symmetry. The theory is known to flow in the ir to an interacting 

cft . In 4 − ϵ dimensions, the fixed point is under perturbative control and is real for 

N sufficiently large [58–60]. At criticality, it describes phase transitions in a variety of physical systems ranging from superconductors [58, 61, 62] to liquid crystals [59] and cosmic strings [63]. The cusp is identified with two semi-infinite straight Wilson lines meeting at the origin, where a scalar field is inserted to satisfy the Gauss constraint (the presence of matter in the theory makes it possible to end Wilson lines). In this paper, we use a semiclassical approach to calculate the quantities of interest, such as the cusp anomalous dimension and the spectrum, concentrating on the limit where both charges are large and of the the same order Q ≫ 1. In this regime, we use a double-scaling limit Q → ∞ , ϵ → 0 with Qϵ fixed in which the path integral localizes 4around the saddles of a reduced action that takes into account the presence of the cusp. This action is written in terms of appropriate ’t Hooft-like couplings, κ for the scalar self-interactions and G for the gauge coupling. Its quantum fluctuations are controlled by ℏ = 1/Q. We obtain a controlled expansion in inverse powers of the charge, even when the microscopic couplings are not parametrically small. Working at large charge turns the Wilson line into a semiclassical object whose backreaction on the gauge and scalar fields can be treated systematically. In spite of the resulting major simplifications, the full solution to the equations of motion remains elusive because we are dealing with a set of non-linear partial differential equations ( pde s). We therefore limit ourselves here to a regime of small G with κ fixed, in which the semiclassical analysis partially overlaps with the standard perturbative approach. This allows us both to validate our technique and to extend previous results found in the literature [56, 64]. The main advantage of our approach is that we can access regimes that are invisible in fixed-order perturbation theory, where interactions are effectively strong due to large sources, despite weak microscopic couplings. Other regimes will be explored in a companion paper. Our main result is the expression for the cusp anomalous dimension at next-to-next-to-leading order ( nnlo ) in G:

The plan of this paper is as follows. In Section two, we describe our setup, starting with the actions and defining the Wilson line with a cusp. The main objective of this work is to compute the cusp anomalous dimension. We set out to compute it semiclassically in Section three, discussing first the equations of motion ( eom ) and the boundary contributions. 4. Symmetries and spectrum 

The first step towards understanding the spectrum of fluctuations around our semiclas-sical solution consists in describing the corresponding symmetry-breaking pattern. The (Euclidean) Abelian Higgs model with N complex scalar fields at the ir fixed point has symmetry SO (d, 1 ) × SU (N)/ ZN. The insertion of the cusp, with its vertex sitting in the completely symmetric representation of SU (N) with Q1 − Q2 boxes, breaks the conformal group to the SO (d − 2) rotations orthogonal to the plane of the cups times the dilatations 𝒟 , and the global SU (N)/ ZN to SU (N − 1)/ ZN−1. All in all, 

SO (d, 1 ) × SU (N)/ ZN → SO (d − 2) × 𝒟 × SU (N − 1)/ ZN−1 . (4.1) In the cylinder frame, the symmetries are realized geometrically: SO (d − 2) corresponds to the isometries of the d + 1 sphere with two non-antipodal punctures, and 𝒟 generates the time translations. In order to count the Goldstone fields resulting from the breaking of the global 21 symmetry, we observe that in the case of only the global symmetry, the breaking pattern would be the same as for the O(2N) model: SO (d, 1 ) × O(2N) → SO (d) × 𝒟 ′ × U(N − 1),where 𝒟 ′ is the helical symmetry obtained as the sum of time translation and a global 

U(1). In this case, the low-energy theory contains 1 type-I Goldstone with linear dispersion relation, N − 1 type-II Goldstones with quadratic dispersion, and N − 1

massive modes with mass proportional to μ0. In the presence of the gauge symmetry, the Higgs mechanism comes into play: the type-I Goldstone combines with the photon to give a mass to the vector field. The remaining massless fields are the type-II Goldstones with parabolic dispersion relations [2, 3, 69]. The fundamental difference with respect to the large-charge analysis of systems with only a global symmetry is that the universal conformal type-I Goldstone is not present here due to the Higgs mechanism [11]. For generic values of q1, q2 and α∗, we thus have a massive spectrum and do not expect to see fluctuations with unit energy (in the scale of R) to be identified with cft descendants of the lowest operator of fixed charge. 6. Perturbative ϵ-expansion 

As mentioned earlier, while our results are valid for any fixed value of κ, the limit of small κ overlaps with the regime of validity of standard (Feynman diagram) perturbation theory in 4 − ϵ dimensions. 7. Conclusions 

When applying the semi-classical methods of the large-charge expansion to gauge theories, the need to work with gauge-invariant quantities leads directly to the notion of nonlocal operators, and particularly those constructed from Wilson lines. A natural set-up is to consider two Wilson lines meeting at a cusp, with the two arms carrying different charges Q1, Q2. This case encompasses both the semi-infinite line, and the straight Wilson line without cusp but with a local operator insertion, as limiting cases. From the viewpoint of defect quantum field theory, a Wilson line defines a one-dimensional defect, and the large-charge limit probes sectors where it carries large quantum numbers, in close analogy with the standard large-charge expansions in 

cft . This framework provides an alternative organizing principle for gauge theories, complementary to weak coupling, and enables a systematic study of non-linear and backreaction effects in Wilson line observables. In this paper, we have focused on the Abelian Higgs model in d = 4 − ϵ dimensions. We have successfully studied two Wilson lines carrying charges Q1, Q2 with a defect-changing operator at the cusp. To be able to work semiclassically, we have focused on the double-scaling limit in which Q1, Q2 are large and Qϵ is constant. Despite taking this limit, we still needed to work perturbatively in the gauge-coupling G (but for finite 30 values of the self-coupling κ). The semiclassical computation of the cusp anomalous dimension to nnlo in G constitutes our main result. Schematically, we found

Our results reproduce those of the standard perturbative calculation in the literature while greatly extending the accessible regimes, since they are valid for Q1 ≠ Q2 and for large values of the charges. There are a number of directions one could follow from here. 

• The small-coupling expansion that we have used breaks for G ≈ 2π. This points to a new phase transition (analogous to the one discussed in [20] for a straight Wilson line) that could be discussed with our semiclassical methods. 

• The physics of non-Abelian gauge symmetries is quite different and much richer. Also in that case, a semiclassical analysis might lead to new nontrivial regimes of Wilson line observables and defect dynamics. 

• It would be interesting to see whether our semiclassical approach would be useful in more general geometric settings involving multiple cusps such as e.g. Wilson lines with two or more cusps, or Wilson loops with n cusps forming a polygon, and whether it could describe interactions between cusps. 

• There is a large literature on more general geometries with junctions in which more than two Wilson lines meet, or networks with junctions [70–73]. It could be worth exploring whether working semiclassically would be useful or could capture interactions between junctions. 

• We have shown that focusing on sectors of the theory where the Wilson lines carry large quantum numbers leads to important simplifications. This should naturally extend to higher-dimensional defects seen as semiclassical objects to be studied systematically in a large quantum number expansion.
