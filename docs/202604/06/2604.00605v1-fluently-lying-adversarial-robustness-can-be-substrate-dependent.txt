Abstract. The primary tools used to monitor and defend object detec-tors under adversarial attack assume that when accuracy degrades, detec-tion count drops in tandem. This coupling was assumed, not measured. We report a counterexample observed on a single model: under standard PGD, EMS-YOLO [31], a spiking neural network (SNN) object detec-tor, retains more than 70% of its detections while mAP collapses from 

0.528 to 0.042 . We term this count-preserving accuracy collapse Quality Corruption (QC), to distinguish it from the suppression that dominates untargeted evaluation. Across four SNN architectures and two threat models ( ℓ∞ and ℓ2), QC appears only in one of the four detectors tested (EMS-YOLO). On this model, all five standard defense components fail to detect or mitigate QC, suggesting the defense ecosystem may rely on a shared assumption calibrated on a single substrate. These results pro-vide, to our knowledge, the first evidence that adversarial failure modes can be substrate-dependent. 

Keywords: adversarial robustness · safety monitoring · spiking neural networks · object detection · failure modes 

# 1 Introduction 

The primary tools used to monitor and defend object detectors under adversarial attack share one implicit premise: when an attack degrades accuracy, detection count drops in tandem. Detection-rate metrics quantify the drop [11, 32]; count monitors flag it; defenses are trained to reverse it [23,37]. This coupling between count and accuracy is a widely shared foundation across the defense ecosys-tem (Secs. 2 and 5) — yet no metric has ever tested whether the two actually move together (Sec. 3.3). The coupling was assumed, not measured. The assumption went untested because no prior result had violated it. Un-targeted adversarial attacks on object detectors produce one dominant outcome: suppression [2, 3, 29]. Detections vanish, confidence collapses, and the failure is visible. Suppression guarantees coupling by definition, so the premise held not because it was verified, but because no failure mode had ever violated it. This pattern persisted across every backbone, threat model, and detector architec-ture evaluated to date (Sec. 2). Producing a less detectable failure consistently required a more complex, targeted attack [6, 33, 34]. The ordering — simpler

attack, more detectable failure — was treated as a reliable empirical pattern. It held on every architecture, but every architecture ran on the same computational substrate. We test this pattern on a different substrate. Four published spiking neural network (SNN) object detectors — spanning three encoding schemes, two neuron types, and temporal depths from 1 to 5 (Tab. 1) — are evaluated under standard PGD with identical loss and identical ε (Sec. 3). Three produce suppression, consistent with prior ANN results; the fourth does not (Figure 1). Under identical PGD, one of the four — EMS-YOLO [31] (ICCV 2023), the only directly-trained fully-spiking detector in the evaluation — retains more than 70% of its detections while mAP collapses from 0.528 to 0.042 . Bounding boxes, labels, and confidence scores persist — nearly all wrong. We refer to this pattern — count-preserving accuracy collapse under untargeted attack — as 

Quality Corruption (QC). The system does not go silent; it produces confident, wrong outputs indistinguishable from correct operation. The attack is standard PGD, unchanged from a decade of ANN evaluation; the primary difference is the computational substrate. This single observation carries three implications for evaluating adversarial robustness on emerging substrates. First, the ordering between attack complex-ity and failure detectability — stable across a decade of ANN evaluation — reverses on the spiking substrate: the simplest standard attack produces the least detectable failure, one absent from the existing taxonomy. Second, five in-dependently developed defense components all assume that detection count and accuracy degrade together (Sec. 5); on EMS-YOLO, Quality Corruption breaks that coupling, suggesting that what appears to be a diverse defense ecosystem may rest on a shared assumption — calibrated on one substrate and tested on no other. Third, the architectural properties that distinguish EMS-YOLO from the other three detectors are precisely those required for neuromorphic chip deploy-ment (Secs. 2 and 3.2); whether this co-occurrence reflects a structural tension between deployability and safety is discussed in Sec. 6. The implications extend beyond spiking networks. Every model deployed on edge hardware undergoes a substrate transition — quantization, pruning, or compilation — yet failure-mode preservation across these transitions has rarely been evaluated (Sec. 6). All four detectors evaluated target autonomous driving [22, 25, 26, 31]; in a deployment scenario, a downstream planner would receive corrupted output while existing count-based monitors report normal operation. No signal currently tracked by these monitors would distinguish corrupted output from correct op-eration. We make three contributions. (i) QCI, a metric designed to measure count– accuracy coupling, and the failure mode it reveals: Quality Corruption (Sec. 4).

# 2 Related Work 

Neuromorphic hardware constraints. The energy advantage of neuromorphic chips rests on a structural asymmetry in digital arithmetic: accumulation re-quires fewer gate transitions than multiplication, a gap that persists across pro-cess nodes. At 45 nm, one accumulate-only (AC) operation costs 0.9 pJ versus 4.6 pJ for multiply-accumulate [13, 22]. The three constraints defined in Sec. 1 (binary spikes, AC-only arithmetic, no dense matrix computation) represent the operating regime where this advantage is maximized. First-generation chips (TrueNorth [24]) enforce all three; newer architectures (Loihi 2 [8]) relax binary spikes to integer payloads, while others (Speck [38]) retain binary spikes; both restrict computation to integer arithmetic. Operations that violate these con-straints — including integer-valued spikes, floating-point FFT, and attention — appear in every non-deployable detector evaluated here (Tab. 1). 

SNN object detection and robustness. Spiking-YOLO [16] first demonstrated ANN-to-SNN conversion for detection, but conversion-based pipelines retain real-valued weights incompatible with AC-only arithmetic. EMS-YOLO [31] (ICCV 2023), the first directly-trained SNN detector, uses binary LIF neurons trained with surrogate gradients [27] in a fully-spiking backbone with no at-tention, satisfying the three constraints in its processing pipeline (Sec. 3.2). Four subsequent detectors advanced accuracy by relaxing at least one con-straint (Tab. 1): SpikeYOLO [22], SpikingYOLOX [26], Adv-SpikingYOLOX [25], and MSD [17] (includes non-spiking MAC operations, violating constraint (ii); codebase was not publicly available at submission time). Each earned top-venue recognition [22, 25, 26], but each moved beyond the energy-optimal operating regime defined above. EMS-YOLO remains the only hardware-deployable detec-tor. SNN robustness research has studied only classifiers. Defensive work reports inherent advantages on CIFAR-10/100 [9, 21, 30, 35]; offensive work develops SNN-specific attacks [4, 18, 36], again all on classifiers. Whether classifier-level robustness transfers to multi-box detection was unknown. To our knowledge, no prior work attacks an SNN object detector. 

Adversarial attacks on ANN detectors. Standard untargeted attacks (PGD [23], FGSM [11]) on ANN detectors produce suppression [3,5] across CNN and Trans-former backbones [2, 29]. Specialized attacks can produce non-suppression out-comes through explicit objectives: TOG [6] and DAG [34] use explicit misla-beling or fabrication objectives; Daedalus [33] targets NMS to generate dense false positives visually distinct from normal operation. The outcomes of Quality Corruption — wrong detections at preserved count — superficially resemble fab-rication or mislabeling from these targeted attacks; the distinction is that QC emerges under standard untargeted PGD with no explicit preservation objective. On ANN substrates, less detectable outcomes have consistently required more complex, targeted objectives. To our knowledge, no standard untargeted attack 4 D. Kang and H. Baek 

on an ANN detector has been shown to preserve detection volume while corrupt-ing quality — but, to our knowledge, no existing metric tests for it (Sec. 3.3). Standard ANN defense strategies (detection-rate metrics, count monitoring, input purification, adversarial training [23], and empirical certification) were developed and validated exclusively under suppression, where detection count and accuracy degrade together; whether this coupling holds on other substrates is tested in Sec. 5. 

# 3 Experimental Setup 

3.1 Preliminaries 

We use computational substrate to refer to the class of arithmetic a model exe-cutes at inference time: what operations are performed, not how layers are ar-ranged. A model satisfying all three hardware constraints (binary spikes, accumulate-only arithmetic, no dense matrix computation; Secs. 1 and 2) operates on the 

hardware-deployable substrate : it satisfies the necessary architectural conditions for neuromorphic chip deployment. This classification is ours; it is grounded in published chip specifications (Sec. 2) but is not an industry standard. Whether this boundary coincides with the boundary of Quality Corruption is an empir-ical observation, not a causal claim (Sec. 6). A model violating any constraint operates on the ANN-like substrate , regardless of whether it uses spiking neurons. Under the Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) model [27], Ut = β U t−1+Xt, firing when Ut > V th and resetting. At T =1 the LIF reduces to a single threshold with no temporal dynamics.

3.3 Metrics

On ANN detectors, DRR tracks accuracy: when detections vanish, mAP drops in tandem. 3.4 Attack Protocol 

The primary evaluation is white-box. The primary threat model is ℓ∞-bounded with ε ∈ { 2, 4, 8}/255 ; ℓ2-bounded PGD ( ε ∈ { 10 , 20 , 30 }) is used to verify that QC is not specific to a single perturbation geometry (Sec. 4.1). PGD [23] uses 10 steps (step size ε/ 4 for ℓ∞; ℓ2 protocol in supplementary); robustness to step count is verified up to 100 steps and under APGD in Sec. 4.1. FMP (Sec. 4.4) and black-box transfer attacks (Sec. 4.3) are detailed in their respective sections. The attack loss Ldet is the sum of detection confidences across all output cells; the attack minimizes this sum via projected gradient descent (sign updates with 

ε-clipping), so that reducing Ldet degrades detection performance. Every attack configuration (loss, optimizer, ε, steps) is constant across mod-els; only the computational substrate varies. Evaluation uses COCO 2017 val (first 1,000 images by ID); comparison against the full 5,000-image set confirms stable estimates (supplementary). Clean mAP varies by up to 0.03 across runs due to non-deterministic GPU operations; all relative metrics are computed against each run’s own baseline. Adversarial training uses 2,000-image subsets (full protocol in supplementary).

# 5 The ANN Defense Toolkit on the Spiking Substrate 

Five standard components protect object detectors against adversarial attack: detection-rate metrics, count monitoring, input purification, adversarial train-ing, and empirical ε-certification. Developed independently over a decade, they appear diverse but rest on a single implicit premise: detection count and accu-racy degrade together (Sec. 3.3). Quality Corruption breaks that premise. On the single hardware-deployable model tested, every component behaves unpre-dictably: some shift the failure mode without resolving it, others produce no meaningful effect (Tab. 3). On non-deployable models, all five work as designed. These results suggest the ecosystem may be less diverse than it appears, resting on a shared assumption calibrated on one substrate. DRR and count monitoring fail by definition (Sec. 3.3): both measure detection volume, which QC preserves. The remaining three components are tested below. Input purification. We test ten purification methods [37] spanning four signal domains (frequency, spatial-linear, spatial-nonlinear, and value; full results in supplementary). All ten methods increase DRR substantially at ε=8 , shifting the dominant failure mode toward suppression: detection counts that survived the unpurified attack (DRR = 31 .5% in this evaluation run; per Sec. 3.4) collapse under filtering (DRR = 58 –75% ), but the best-case adversarial mAP ( 0.249 ) re-mains below half of clean performance. On ANN detectors, purification restores suppressed detections; on the deployable pipeline, it eliminates the detections that make Quality Corruption invisible — converting a silent failure into a visi-ble one, but restoring nothing. 

Adversarial training. We train EMS-YOLO under PGD-AT across five learning rates ( 10 −4 to 10 −6). At lr =10 −4, clean mAP collapses to near zero within three epochs: the model forgets detection entirely. At lr =10 −5, we extend training to 20 epochs to track the full trajectory. Clean mAP degrades monotonically (0.59 → 0.33 → 0.21 → 0.16 at epochs 0, 5, 10 , 20 ) while PGD mAP barely changes ( 0.05 → 0.13 → 0.11 → 0.08 ). QCI transitions from +67 (strong QC) to −33 (suppression) — but not because Quality Corruption is corrected: detec-tion count collapses (DRR rises from 24% to 82% ), leaving too few detections to corrupt. The mechanism mirrors purification: AT eliminates the detections that carry corruption rather than restoring their accuracy. Results across all five learning rates are reported in supplementary. SpikingYOLOX under identical AT (same learning rates, same epochs) produces the expected direction of an accuracy–robustness trade-off at lr =10 −5 (clean mAP decreases, PGD mAP im-proves; supplementary), confirming that the protocol suffices where suppression holds — and that its failure on EMS-YOLO reflects the substrate, not under-training.

All five components rest on count–accuracy coupling; on the single model where that coupling breaks, none operates as designed. Defense-in-depth assumes in-dependence among defensive layers, but one failure mode — Quality Corruption — defeats the entire toolkit simultaneously. Under suppression, the passive mon-itors flag the anomaly and the system can fail safe. Under Quality Corruption, no count-based monitor would flag the anomaly, and no fail-safe mechanism that relies on count would activate. In a shared-weight deployment, the vulnerability would be correlated: identi-cal weights imply identical failure modes across instances, and count-based mon-itors would remain silent on all of them. The monitoring blind spot observed on this model is systematic rather than stochastic. The search space for QC-aware defenses is not empty. QC outputs do dif-fer from clean outputs in distribution (lower confidence, smaller boxes, collapsed class entropy), each observable without ground truth (details in supplementary). But per-image QCI ranges from −254 to beyond +1 ,300 under identical condi-tions (Figure 3); no fixed threshold can handle this range, and the methodology for defending against a heterogeneous failure does not yet exist. 

# 6 Discussion 

Three research directions. Quality Corruption challenges assumptions held inde-pendently by three communities, each facing open questions. Failure pattern —as opposed to failure accuracy — has rarely been a standard evaluation target on any substrate; QCI offers a tool to make it one. For adversarial robustness: can defenses be built on premises other than count–accuracy coupling? Both active defenses tested here eliminate QC only by destroying the detections that carry it (Sec. 5). A defense that corrects corrupted detections requires handling per-image QCI that spans −254 to beyond +1 ,300 

under identical conditions (Figure 3); no uniform threshold or fixed decision boundary can cover this range, suggesting that detection-rate–based defenses alone may be insufficient. The optimization trajectory (Sec. 4.1) compounds the problem: current certification defines worst-case by the strongest optimizer, but on this model QC contradicts that definition — which optimizer constitutes worst-case may be substrate-dependent, so certification protocols may need to parameterize over substrate as well as attack strength. Whether failure-mode characterization should become a standard safety deliverable alongside accuracy measurement is an immediate methodological question. For neuromorphic deployment: among published SNN detectors, each succes-sor to EMS-YOLO advanced accuracy by relaxing at least one hardware con-straint (Tab. 1), moving the field away from the chip-deployable regime. Quality Corruption was observed only on the pipeline that satisfies all three chip con-straints; relaxing any one constraint to avoid QC reintroduces operations the chip prohibits (Tab. 1). Whether this co-occurrence is coincidental or structural Adversarial Robustness Can Be Substrate-Dependent 13 

cannot be determined from a single model. The question is open. SNN detec-tors targeting autonomous driving have appeared at ICCV 2023, ECCV 2024, and CVPR 2025 [17, 22, 31], and EMS-YOLO remains the only one that sat-isfies all three chip constraints (Tab. 1). If such a pipeline were deployed with shared weights, the vulnerability observed here would be correlated across in-stances (Sec. 5). QC evaluation may warrant consideration for SNN detectors approaching chip deployment. For object detection at large: this paper shows that Quality Corruption has not been observed on ANN substrates in a decade of evaluation. But it has not been ruled out either, because no evaluation protocol has included a met-ric sensitive to count–accuracy decoupling. The ANN baselines in this study provide, to our knowledge, the first such measurements: QCI confirms that cou-pling holds on YOLOv3-tiny, YOLOv8s, and YOLOX-S under standard PGD at full precision (Tab. 2; supplementary). Under quantization and pruning [12, 19], suppression likewise persists: INT8 PTQ, INT4 QAT, unstructured pruning (30– 70%), and structured channel pruning (5–10%) all yield QCI ≤ 0 on YOLOv3-tiny (supplementary). These measurements cannot be extrapolated beyond the conditions tested; substrate transitions that ANN detectors routinely undergo — compilation for edge accelerators or extreme low-bit quantization (INT2) — have not been measured, and whether count–accuracy coupling survives them is unknown. Without QCI, the question could not even be posed. Count–accuracy decoupling was first observed on the spiking substrate, but may not be confined to it. Even where QC does not occur, the verification that it does not is itself a measurement that has rarely been performed — and one that QCI now makes possible. 

What the evidence constrains. The controls in Sec. 4 exclude four candidate explanations — temporal dynamics, architecture and encoding, surrogate gradi-ent type, and gradient artifacts — leaving the conjunction of all three hardware constraints as the remaining candidate among those tested. FMP confirms that membrane-level computation is an exploitable structural component exclusively on the deployable substrate (Sec. 4.4). We characterize where QC occurs (the hardware-deployable pipeline), what it produces (count-preserving accuracy col-lapse), and how to detect it (QCI) and diagnose it (FMP). The mechanism remains an open question, but the safety consequence — silent failure under standard monitoring — does not depend on identifying it. The claim that fail-ure modes are substrate-dependent is observational: under identical attack con-ditions, the outcome differs by substrate, and this observation holds regardless of the underlying mechanism. The universal claim that untargeted attacks always produce suppression re-quires only one counterexample to fall; the controls in this paper support that this counterexample is genuine (Secs. 4.2 to 4.4). One practical implication is that failure-mode characterization may need to accompany substrate transitions before deployment; QCI provides a tool to perform it. 14 D. Kang and H. Baek 

Limitations. We evaluate one dataset (COCO 2017), two threat models ( ℓ∞ and 

ℓ2), and one hardware-deployable detector (EMS-YOLO). All experiments use frame-based input (COCO 2017) to isolate the computational substrate as the sole variable; event-based and physical-world evaluations are left to future work. The single-model restriction reflects the current state of the field, not a selection bias. Black-box evaluation is limited to transfer attacks; query-based methods remain future work. The primary signature of obfuscated gradients [1] is transfer attacks outperforming white-box; our results show the opposite (transfer is inert while white-box produces extreme QC; Sec. 4.3), which is inconsistent with the obfuscation hypothesis. FMP selectivity and the monotonic DRR trajectory from PGD-10 to APGD-100 (Secs. 4.1 and 4.4) independently confirm that gradients are informative; the evidence is consistent with the inversion being a property of the pipeline, not an artifact of gradient masking. APGD at 100 steps reaches the QC/suppression boundary (QCI = + 0 .4) but does not cross into suppres-sion (Sec. 4.1); full adaptive evaluation including the complete AutoAttack suite remains future work. 

# 7 Conclusion 

Quality Corruption is a silent failure: the detector maintains output volume while nearly every detection is wrong, and on the single model tested, no existing monitoring tool detected it (Sec. 6). Without QCI, any future occurrence of Quality Corruption would register as a robust detector with low detection-rate reduction. QCI applies to any detector, on any substrate. It is useful not only where Quality Corruption occurs, but wherever count–accuracy coupling has not been explicitly verified. 

Implications for substrate transitions. Every deployment involves a substrate transition; few have been tested for failure-mode preservation. The single transi-tion evaluated here produced a qualitatively different failure mode that existing monitoring tools did not detect. If safety evaluation remains substrate-agnostic, certificates issued on one substrate may not transfer to the substrate where in-ference actually runs. Two implications follow: failure-mode preservation should be verified across every substrate transition before deployment; and QCI — or a metric sensitive to count–accuracy decoupling — may need to enter standard safety evaluation. Preliminary analysis of distributional signals that distinguish QC from clean operation (supplementary) suggests that QC-aware runtime mon-itoring is feasible, though the methodology does not yet exist. If adversarial robustness is substrate-dependent, safety evaluation should account for the sub-strate on which inference runs.
