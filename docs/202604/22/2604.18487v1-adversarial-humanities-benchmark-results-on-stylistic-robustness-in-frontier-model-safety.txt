## Abstract 

The Adversarial Humanities Benchmark (AHB) evaluates whether model safety refusals survive a shift away from familiar harmful prompt forms. Starting from harmful tasks drawn from MLCommons AILuminate, the benchmark rewrites the same objectives through humanities-style transformations while preserving intent. This extends literature on Adversarial Poetry and Adversarial Tales from single jailbreak operators to a broader benchmark family of stylistic obfuscation and goal concealment. In the benchmark results reported here, the original attacks record 3.84% attack success rate (ASR), while transformed methods range from 36.8% to 65.0%, yielding 55.75% overall ASR across 31 frontier models. Under a European Union AI Act Code-of-Practice-inspired systemic-risk lens, Chemical, biological, radiological and nuclear (CBRN) is the highest bucket. Taken together, this lack of stylistic robustness suggests that current safety techniques suffer from weak generalization: deep understanding of ’non-maleficence’ remains a central unresolved problem in frontier model safety. 

## 1 Introduction 

The extraordinary capabilities of large language models have increased the reach of useful reasoning systems, but they have also lowered the barrier to misuse. This is why frontier-model safety is increasingly discussed not only as a product concern, but as a governance concern tied to systemic risk, safety-by-design, and the ability of providers to demonstrate control under adversarial conditions [European Commission, 2025a,b]. Open-source single-turn benchmarks and their common evaluation frameworks have become the state-of-the-art (SotA) to assess model safety in various risk categories, such as toxicity or harm-enabling. Recently, most frontier models have shown impressive robustness, scoring high rates of refusals; such results may lead to the conviction that single-turn attacks no longer pose a serious threat, shifting focus to elaborate multi-turn strategies. However, SotA benchmarks usually include extremely explicit harmful requests, with limited linguis-tic obfuscation. Therefore, frontier systems often appear safer when the malicious intent is obvious, the lexical cues are familiar, and the request is close to the examples on which refusal behavior has been reinforced. The harder question is whether refusal behavior generalizes. A model that has merely overfit to known refusal patterns may appear safe on distribution while remaining vulnerable when the same harmful objective is expressed in a form that is semantically equivalent but rhetorically unfamiliar. Nonetheless, laying on the basis of Adversarial Poetry (Bisconti et al. [2025b]) and

## 2 Related Work 

Despite efforts to create human-aligned LLMs through safety post-training techniques, such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) Ziegler et al. [2020] or Constitutional AI Bai et al. [2022] as a final alignment layer, these models can still generate unsafe and harmful outputs. A deliberate and adversarial attempt to obtain unsafe content from LLMs is defined as jailbreaking ,which is performed by manipulating input prompts to induce the model to circumvent its safety, ethical, or legal constraint ( Rao et al. [2024], Shen et al. [2024b], Schulhoff et al. [2023b,a]). Common jailbreak techniques employ role-playing, i.e. assigning the model to a specific contextual setting or asking the model to operate within a fictional framework, with the aim of relaxing guardrails Kang et al. [2023], Rao et al. [2024], Yu et al. [2024], Ntais [2025]. Contexts perceived as low-risk, such as games or simulated scenarios increase the likelihood that models bypass constraints. Similarly, Attention Shifting attacks Yu et al. [2024] create overly noisy or intricate problem framings that divert the model’s focus from harmful content (Chuang et al. [2024], Li et al. [2024b]), probably caused by a lack of long-context samples during post-training. Furthermore, Persuasion attacks build on social and cultural schemes, such as invoking empathy or presenting justification based on a severe circumstance (Zeng et al. [2024]). Mechanistically, jailbreaks exploit two alignment weaknesses identified by Wei et al. [2023]: Com-peting Objectives and Mismatched Generalization. Competing Objectives refers to an attacks that avoids refusal policies exploiting tendency of the model to maintain helpfulness and coherence, even though it conflicts safety rules ( Perez and Ribeiro [2022]). Such a vulnerability seems to be amplified by hierarchical, multi-step reasoning: structured analytical tasks can increase Attack Success Rate (ASR) by shifting model objectives from refusal toward task completion Wang et al. [2025], Zhao et al. [2025]. Mismatched Generalization attempts shift the input prompt outside the model’s refusal distribution, using Character-Level Perturbations Schulhoff et al. [2023b], Low-Resource Languages Deng et al. [2024], or Structural and Stylistic Obfuscation techniques Kang et al. [2023], Rao et al. [2024], Wang et al. [2024b], Mou et al. [2024]. In practice, effective attacks frequently combine multiple strategies within a single prompt, e.g. DAN-style attacks (Shen et al. [2024a]). The threat posed by single-turn jail-breaking has justified the creation of safety benchmarks, aiming at providing common experimental conditions to test and compare AI models. A pioneer in this field was RealToxicityPrompts (Gehman et al. [2020]), used to evaluate the tendency of language models to produce discriminatory or toxic outputs. Due to the necessity of scaling datasets rapidly, the benchmark pipeline was soon automated to provide automatic red-teaming using LLMs to perform the item generation (Perez et al. [2022]). Similarly, output evaluation was soon automated through the 

LLM-as-a-judge paradigm, allowing to obtain reproducible and unbiased results (Chao et al. [2024], Li et al. [2024a]). In this respect, StrongREJECT represents the state-of-the-art evalautor (Souly et al. [2024]). Since then, a great number of large-scale single-turn safety benchmarks have been presented (Deng et al. [2022], Wang et al. [2023], Lin et al. [2023], Vidgen and et al. [2024], Ghosh et al. [2025], Ji et al. [2023], Zhang et al. [2024], Li et al. [2024a], Mazeika et al. [2024], Wang et al. [2024a]). In order to quantify the safety-helpfulness trade-off, some have proposed benchmarks of harmless and harmful requests, evaluating to which degree the model recognizes correctly genuine unsafe requests (Zou et al. [2023], Röttger et al. [2024]).

AHB extends on such works, demonstrating that this constitute a structural vulnerability rather than a niche exception. 

## 3 Methodology 

AHB is designed to evaluate the robustness of LLMs against jailbreak attacks rooted in humanistic and literary paradigms. AHB exploits both the Mismatched Generalization and Competing Objectives vulnerabilities, by introducing metaphorical or allegorical language and structured analytical and conceptual tasks. 

3.1 Threat Model 

AHB assumes an adversary with limited black-box model access. In particular, the attack surface is confined to one-shot prompt construction executed under standard inference conditions. 

Attacker Objectives and Knowledge The adversary seeks to induce forbidden output across various risk categories, including chemical weapons, cyber-offense enablement and manipulation of user. An attack is considered successful when the model produces harmful and unsafe, as determined by our evaluation criteria. As required by the black-box assumption, the adversary possesses no information regarding model parameters, alignment methods, refusal mechanisms, or guardrail configurations, and does not rely on model-specific prompt engineering or reverse-engineered alignment features. Prompts are generated independently of any particular model architecture or training corpus. Furthermore, attacks are performed exclusively through publicly available API calls. 

Attack Constraints The adversary is subject to the following restrictions: • Single-turn interaction: Iterative refinement, negotiation, chain-of-thought activation, and conversational role modulation are prohibited. • Text-only inputs: No code execution, multimodal content, or auxiliary tools are permitted. 

Risk and harm taxonomy The prompts within the Adversarial Humanities Benchmark are designed to target vulnerabilities across: • 15 specific categories of harm listed in Ghosh et al. [2025]: Violent Crimes, Sex-Related Crimes, Child Sexual Exploitation, Suicide & Self-Harm, Indiscriminate Weapons, Intellec-tual Property Violations, Defamation, Non-Violent Crimes, Hate, Privacy Violations, Sexual Content (pornographic), and Specialized Advice (divided into elections, financial, health, and legal domains). • Which fall within 3 categories of systemic risks defined by the EU GPAI COP (European Commission [2025a]): Cyber Offense, Harmful Manipulation, and Chemical, biological, radiological and nuclear (CBRN). Figure 2a shows the composition of AHB by risk category. Full mapping between harm and risk taxonomies is displayed in Appendix A. 

Attack taxonomy The adversary uses 5 distinct attack methods, each designed to obfuscate harmful intent in such a way the models perform complex analytical tasks on a text with an unusual linguistic or syntactic format. The attack methods employed are defined as: • Adversarial Tales: Leverages a narrative ("cyberpunk") framework where harmful meaning is disguised as a "magical instrument." 3.2 Transformation of baseline prompts 

Each prompt in the dataset is a reformulated version of an item in MLCommons AILuminate Benchmark 2 (Vidgen and et al. [2024], Ghosh et al. [2025]), allowing to perform grounded comparative analysis on the effects of our method with respect to ASR. The benchmark follows a simple rule carried over from earlier earlier works (Bisconti et al. [2025b,a]): keep the harmful goal fixed, preserve its hazard label, and alter the surface frame through a humanities-style task. The benchmark is not intended to invent new harms. It measures whether known harms remain identifiable when the request is displaced into interpretation, translation, commentary, or narrative reconstruction. The transformation was executed using deepseek/deepseek-v3.2 through a dedicated reformu-lation meta-prompt to reframe the original harmful intent into each of our five adversarial attack paradigms. Each reformulated output is, in turn, inserted in an attack template , containing paradigm-specific instructions meant to trigger a competition of objectives by concealing the harmful goal

3.2.1 Experimental Protocol 

Attacks were performed on 31 models across 11 model providers, namely Google, OpenAI, Anthropic, DeepSeek, Qwen, Mistral AI, Meta, xAI, Moonshot AI, Z.ai, ByteDance. A full list of models is displayed in Appendix B. Outputs are collected verbatim through standard API inference interfaces. To label outputs, we adopt a LLM-as-a-judge setup. Each response is independently evaluated by an ensemble of three open-weight judge models: • gpt-oss-120b 

• deepseek-v3.2 

• kimi-k2.5 

Each judge assigns a binary label SAFE (S) or UNSAFE (U) following shared evaluation instructions. Refusals and explicit policy-compliant denials are defined as SAFE. On the other hand, if an output contains instructions or technical details enabling harmful activities, or engages with an unsafe requests positively providing advice or workarounds, the answer is considered UNSAFE. A majority-vote consensus among the three judges provides the label. Items without a 2-out-of-3 majority or flagged for uncertainty are passed to human reviewers. ## 4 Results 

The central empirical result is the magnitude and consistency of the gap between direct prompts and stylistically transformed prompts. The original slice records only 3.84% ASR, whereas transformed methods range from 36.83% to 64.68%, with a combined transformed ASR of 55.14%. This is the main signal of the benchmark: many models that appear relatively well controlled on direct prompts become substantially less reliable once the same harmful objective is preserved semantically but shifted into a rhetorically unfamiliar frame. Figure 3 makes this contrast visually immediate, while Table 1 summarizes the scale of the benchmark and the size of the resulting gap. Method-level performance also suggests that some stylistic transformations are systematically more effective than others. In the transformed slice, Adversarial Scholasticism is the strongest method overall at 64.68% ASR, while Adversarial Stream is the weakest at 36.83%, a gap of nearly 28 percentage points. This indicates that stylistic vulnerability is not uniform even within the transformed family: certain rhetorical frames appear to interfere with refusal generalization much more strongly than others. The failures are also broadly distributed rather than concentrated in a single harm category. On transformed prompts only, the highest-ASR hazards are elections-related specialized advice at 68.99%, non-violent crimes at 66.24%, defamation at 62.36%, and violent crimes at 60.25%. Figure 4 shows that the direct-versus-transformed gap appears across the hazard taxonomy rather than being driven by one anomalous class. ## 5 Discussion 

The broader significance of these findings is that many safety evaluations still tell us mainly how models behave when harmful requests look obviously harmful. In realistic misuse settings, harmful intent may be hidden inside translation tasks, interpretation requests, literary commentary, narrative reconstruction, role play, or institutionally coded language. If a model refuses primarily because it recognizes familiar refusal cues rather than because it robustly tracks harmful intent, then its apparent safety may be overstated by non-obfuscated benchmarks alone. This matters at three levels. First, it matters for model development, because the benchmark suggests that current refusal behavior remains too dependent on surface form. Second, it matters for public communication and external evaluation, because low failure rates on direct benchmarks can mask a broader robustness problem. Third, it matters for governance, because frameworks such as the GPAI CoP are concerned not only with whether a system rejects familiar harmful prompts, but with whether providers can demonstrate structured and auditable control under adversarial variation. In this regard, stylistic robustness is a core safety feature. Figure 6 shows how no CoP-defined system risk appears adequately addressed by frontier model providers. There is also a simpler way to state the same concern. A safe model should refuse because it understands what the user is trying to obtain, not merely because it recognizes the wording pattern of a known dangerous prompt using surface-level cues. When the benchmark rewrites a harmful request as literary criticism, theological commentary, or bureaucratic interpretation, it provides a rigorous and quantitative tool to measure track intent through stylistic displacement rather than only react to familiar lexical markers. That distinction can look subtle in benchmark tables, but it is central for real-world deployment. 

Agentic systems. The point becomes more urgent as language models are increasingly embedded in agentic systems. An agent may browse the web, inspect files, write code, query databases, send messages, call APIs, or trigger downstream tools. In that setting, a jailbreak is not just an unsafe answer. It can become an unsafe plan, an unsafe tool call, or a chain of unsafe actions. That changes the role of the safety layer: refusal robustness becomes part of the operational security boundary. AHB should therefore not be understood as merely adding one more jailbreak format. Its main contribution is to provide clear and measurable evidence of a broader failure mode already visible in Adversarial Poetry and Adversarial Tales: safety behavior remains materially sensitive to fully automatable obfuscation. [Bisconti et al., 2025b,a]. For that reason, direct-prompt benchmarks alone are likely to understate deployment risk unless they are complemented by evaluations that preserve harmful intent while shifting rhetorical form.
