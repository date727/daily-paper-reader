# Ranking Abuse via Strategic Pairwise Data Perturbations

Abstract 

Pairwise ranking systems based on Maximum Likelihood Estima-tion (MLE), such as the Bradley-Terry model, are widely used to aggre-gate preferences from pairwise comparisons. However, their robustness under strategic data manipulation remains insufficiently understood. In this paper, we study the vulnerability of MLE-based ranking sys-tems to adversarial perturbations. We formulate the manipulation task as a constrained combinatorial optimization problem and propose an Adaptive Subset Selection Attack (ASSA) to efficiently identify high-impact perturbations. Experimental results on both synthetic data and real-world elec-tion datasets show that MLE-based rankings exhibit a sharp phase-transition behavior: beyond a small perturbation budget, a limited number of strategic voters can significantly alter the global ranking. In particular, our method consistently outperforms random and greedy baselines under constrained budgets. These findings reveal a fundamental sensitivity of MLE-based rank-ing mechanisms to structured perturbations and highlight the need for more robust aggregation methods in collective decision-making sys-tems. 

Keywords: Pairwise ranking, Bradley–Terry model, adversarial manipula-tion, social choice, ranking robustness 

# 1 Introduction 

Social choice theory centers on the structural aggregation of individual preferences to facilitate collective consensus. While traditionally character-1

A prominent statistical paradigm for inferring collective preferences is the utilization of Random Utility Models (RUMs). By formalizing the utility of discrete options as random variables, RUMs effectively accommodate the stochastic noise and heterogeneous nature of agent behavior. Within this do-main, Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE), specifically when integrated with the Bradley-Terry model, has emerged as a cornerstone methodology for synthesizing global rankings from pairwise comparison datasets. Its ca-pacity to extrapolate a comprehensive preference hierarchy from sparse, head-to-head observations renders it an indispensable tool for large-scale decision-making infrastructures. However, the inherent sensitivity of MLE-based estimators introduces profound adversarial vulnerabilities. The integrity of both democratic in-stitutions and organizational decision-making frameworks rests on the pre-supposition that input data authentically reflects agent preferences. Yet, the non-linear dynamics of the MLE objective function imply that strategic agents—acting either individually or in collusion—can perturb their inputs to disproportionately distort the estimated utility parameters. Such strate-gic exploitation fundamentally undermines the core tenets of representative-ness and distributive justice in social choice. In this paper, we conduct a rigorous vulnerability analysis of MLE-based ranking frameworks, contextualizing our work within the established liter-ature of axiomatic social choice and pairwise statistical inference [1]. We formalize strategic preference interference as a combinatorial subset selec-tion problem and introduce the Adaptive Subset Selection Attack (ASSA). ASSA is an innovative heuristic algorithm designed to identify high-impact data perturbations. By leveraging an adaptive mechanism to isolate high-influence manipulation subsets, ASSA achieves superior alignment between the synthesized global ranking and adversarial targets, surpassing the effi-cacy of traditional greedy or stochastic baseline strategies. Our empirical evaluation, substantiated by extensive synthetic simula-tions and validated through real-world electoral data from Berkeley and the UK Labor Party, reveals a critical structural fragility in these systems. Specifically, we identify a distinct phase-transition phenomenon: beyond a modest perturbation budget, the global ranking undergoes a precipitous shift toward the adversarial target. This research elucidates the disproportion-2ate influence of a small, informed cohort in sparse electorate environments, providing pivotal theoretical and empirical foundations for the development of the next generation of robust, manipulation-resistant voting protocols. Beyond algorithmic considerations, our findings have broader implications for the integrity of collective decision-making systems. The results sug-gest that widely used statistical aggregation methods may exhibit systemic vulnerabilities under coordinated strategic behavior, raising concerns about fairness, robustness, and trust in computational social systems.

# 3 Strategic Manipulation Framework 

We formalize preference manipulation as a constrained combinatorial subset selection problem . This framing aligns with contemporary re-search in adversarial preference learning, which highlights the structural vulnerability of pairwise comparison models to strategic data injection [4]. 

3.1 Baseline Heuristics 

To establish a performance floor, we define two fundamental heuristics: 

• Random Flip (RF): A stochastic control where a random subset of comparisons is inverted, establishing a baseline for unstructured interference. 

• Greedy Flip (GF): A local search strategy that iteratively selects flips providing the maximum immediate reduction in Kd. While in-tuitive, GF often converges to sub-optimal local extrema due to the non-convex nature of the ranking landscape. 

3.2 Randomized Subset Attack (RSA) 

The Randomized Subset Attack (RSA) serves as a stochastic search pre-cursor to our main contribution. RSA explores the perturbation space by partitioning the adversary’s available comparisons into b random subsets. Unlike a pure brute-force approach, RSA iteratively evaluates the collective 5impact of flipping these subsets, retaining only those perturbations that yield a monotonic reduction in the Kendall Tau distance relative to the adversarial target.

3.5 Computational Complexity and Scalability 

A critical advantage of the Bradley-Terry MLE framework in large-scale systems is its data-invariant parameter estimation. For a set of m candidates and N pairwise comparisons, the log-likelihood L(p | D ) depends only on the aggregate counts nij , forming an m × m adjacency matrix. Consequently, the complexity of each iteration in ASSA is O(b·Cost MLE ), where Cost MLE is the cost of solving the m-dimensional optimization prob-lem (typically O(m3) or O(I · m2) using iterative methods). Notably, the computation primarily depends on aggregated pairwise counts rather than the raw number of comparisons, allowing ASSA to scale seamlessly to datasets involving millions of pairwise comparisons, provided the number of candi-dates m remains computationally tractable.

4.4.1 UK Labor Party Dataset 

The UK Labor Party leadership vote dataset provides a unique opportunity to evaluate the algorithm’s performance in an environment characterized by incomplete preference rankings. We observed that incomplete ballots inherently increase the system’s susceptibility to manipulation, as the MLE estimator operates under fewer constraints to maintain the global ranking’s stability. By applying the ASSA framework with our proposed complementary ranking strategy, we successfully identified critical preference gaps. Our re-sults indicate that strategic voters can exploit these gaps to disproportion-ately influence the final parameter estimation of the Bradley-Terry model. This finding underscores the necessity for more robust data-imputation pro-tocols in digital voting systems to mitigate the risk of adversarial interference derived from sparse data [5]. 

4.4.2 Berkeley Election Dataset 

In the District 7 Berkeley City Council election dataset ( |V | = 4 , 189 voters), we simulated the impact of collusive voter behavior. The initial MLE predic-tion aligns with the historical outcome: Kriss W orthington > George Beier > Ces Rosales > Jesse Arreguin .Our experimental analysis quantified the **collusion threshold** re-quired to subvert the election outcome. Specifically, for a voter group targeting a preference shift with an initial Kendall distance of Kd = 2 (e.g., Target: [3 , 2, 1, 4]), we determined that a coalition of 353 strategic voters—approximately 8 .4% of the total electorate—is sufficient to flip the global ranking in favor of the adversarial target. When the manipulation objective is more aggressive (initial Kd = 3), the required coalition size increases to 465 voters (11 .1%). 5.1 Pairwise Comparison and Probabilistic Ranking 

The interpretation of pairwise comparison data is a foundational challenge in computational social choice. Noothigattu et al. [1] investigated the ax-iomatic properties of machine learning models in this domain, demonstrat-ing that while Maximum Likelihood Estimators (MLE) like the Thurstone-Mosteller model achieve Pareto efficiency, they may diverge from majority consensus under specific noise distributions. Further axiomatic analysis by Gonz´ alez-D´ ıaz et al. validated the Bradley-Terry model as a robust frame-work for general tournaments, emphasizing its computational viability in practical voting systems. Meanwhile, recent advances in Bradley-Terry and pairwise comparison models have focused on efficient estimation and gener-alization [6,7], but their robustness under adversarial manipulation remains underexplored. 

5.2 MLE and Statistical Social Choice 

The conceptualization of voting rules as maximum likelihood estimators, popularized by Conitzer and Sandholm , provides a probabilistic lens to as-sess the ”correctness” of electoral outcomes. By deriving optimal estimators from hypothesized noise models, this line of inquiry illuminates how statisti-cal assumptions can either mirror or distort the collective will. However, the reliance on MLE for parameter estimation introduces significant vulnerabil-ities. Recent research by Liu and Mueller suggests that MLE frameworks are particularly fragile in sparse preference graphs, where limited informa-tion allows for disproportionate parameter shifts. Our work builds upon this statistical foundation, focusing on the deliberate manipulation of these parameter estimation processes. 

5.3 Robustness and Manipulation in Voting Systems 

The vulnerability of voting mechanisms to strategic manipulation has led to the development of robust voting rules through the lens of algorithmic statistics [8]. Expanding on this, Zhao and Xia [5] explored the learning of mixtures of Random Utility Models (RUMs) from incomplete preferences, offering methodologies to secure preference models against strategic misrep-resentation. Despite these defensive strides, the computational challenges of implementing social choice rules under complex models like Mallows [2] persist. Contemporary studies in 2024 and 2025 have begun to investigate 16 the role of AI-driven strategic agents in identifying these computational bottlenecks to facilitate large-scale manipulation. Recent work has also demonstrated the vulnerability of ranking systems under adversarial per-turbations, particularly in neural ranking models and preference learning settings [ ?, 9, 10]. 

5.4 Adversarial Machine Learning in Ranking 

While significant attention has been paid to defensive robustness, the al-gorithmic construction of adversarial attacks against MLE-based ranking systems has received relatively little scrutiny. Wang et al. [4] pioneered ad-versarial preference learning, focusing on the robustness of pairwise compar-isons against data injection. In the context of networked systems, strategic perturbations have been shown to impact collective decision-making sig-nificantly. Our research complements this body of work by introducing the Adaptive Subset Selection Attack (ASSA), which addresses the gap between theoretical vulnerability and practical, scalable adversarial strategies in both synthetic and real-world electoral landscapes. 

# 6 Conclusion 

This research provides a comprehensive vulnerability analysis of pairwise comparison models under the Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) frame-work. By introducing the Adaptive Subset Selection Attack (ASSA), we have demonstrated that strategic interventions can significantly distort col-lective preference hierarchies, even within constrained adversarial budgets. Our empirical findings reveal a critical non-linear response in ranking stabil-ity: manipulation effectiveness does not scale linearly with perturbation size, but rather exhibits distinct threshold effects and diminishing returns. This phase-transition behavior underscores a fundamental structural fragility in the Bradley-Terry model when faced with coordinated data perturbations. Ultimately, this work highlights the imperative need for integrating robust statistical safeguards into digital voting infrastructures to ensure that col-lective decisions authentically reflect the electorate’s will. 

# 7 Limitations 

Despite the efficacy of the ASSA framework, several constraints bound the scope of this study. First, the heuristic nature of our subset selection mech-17 anism, while computationally efficient, does not guarantee a theoretically optimal manipulation path in all preference landscapes. The stochastic el-ements involved in subset partitioning suggest that the algorithm’s perfor-mance may vary under extreme data sparsity. Furthermore, while validated against real-world datasets from Berkeley and the UK Labor Party, a significant portion of our granular analysis relies on synthetic preference models. These models assume a degree of voter tran-sitivity and rationality that may not fully capture the idiosyncratic or noisy behaviors prevalent in large-scale, real-world social choice scenarios. The assumption of a white-box threat model, while essential for assessing upper-bound vulnerabilities, also overlooks the practical challenges an adversary would face in acquiring complete real-time preference data. 

# 8 Future Work 

The findings of this study open several promising avenues for future in-quiry. To address current limitations in data representation, our subsequent research will focus on the acquisition of high-fidelity, longitudinal electoral datasets. We aim to model more complex voter dynamics, including dynamic preference shifts and the impact of misinformation on the initial preference formation process. Moreover, we intend to expand our adversarial analysis beyond the Bradley-Terry framework to include alternative social choice mechanisms such as Approval Voting, Preferential Voting, and Cumulative Voting. In-vestigating the cross-model resilience of these systems will facilitate the de-velopment of a unified theory of algorithmic influence. The ultimate objective of this trajectory is the design of certified robust ranking estimators. By leveraging adversarial training and robust optimiza-tion, we aim to engineer next-generation voting protocols that maintain fairness and representativeness even in the presence of sophisticated strate-gic actors, thereby upholding the integrity of democratic processes in the digital age.
