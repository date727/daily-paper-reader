## Abstract 

Invisible watermarking for autoregressive (AR) image generation has re-cently gained attention as a means of protecting image ownership and tracing AI-generated content. However, existing approaches suffer from three key limitations: (1) they embed only zero-bit watermarks for binary verification, lacking the ability to convey multi-bit messages; (2) they rely on static codebook partitioning strategies that are vulnerable to security attacks once the partition is exposed; and (3) they are designed for specific AR architectures, failing to generalize across diverse AR paradigms. We propose UniMark, a training-free, unified watermarking framework for au-toregressive image generators that addresses all three limitations. UniMark introduces three core components: Adaptive Semantic Grouping (ASG) ,which dynamically partitions codebook entries based on semantic similar-ity and a secret key, ensuring both image quality preservation and security; 

Block-wise Multi-bit Encoding (BME) , which divides the token sequence into blocks and encodes different bits across blocks with error-correcting codes for reliable message transmission; and a Unified Token-Replacement Interface (UTRI) that abstracts the watermark embedding process to sup-port both next-token prediction (e.g., LlamaGen) and next-scale prediction (e.g., VAR) paradigms. We provide theoretical analysis on detection error rates and embedding capacity. Extensive experiments on three AR models demonstrate that UniMark achieves state-of-the-art performance in image quality (FID), watermark detection accuracy, and multi-bit message extrac-tion, while maintaining robustness against cropping, JPEG compression, Gaussian noise, blur, color jitter, and random erasing attacks. 

## 1 Introduction 

The rapid progress of autoregressive (AR) image generation models has led to a proliferation of high-quality AI-generated images (Tian et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024). These models tokenize images into discrete visual tokens via learned codebooks and autoregressively predict them, achieving quality competitive with diffusion models (Xiong et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2026; Song et al., 2026). Recent advancements have also extended this paradigm to more complex scenarios, such as vision representation compression for efficient video generation (Zhou et al., 2026) and omni-modal understanding and generation frameworks (Xin et al., 2025). However, the democratization of such powerful generators raises serious concerns regarding copyright infringement, content provenance, and malicious misuse (Ren et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024). Invisible image watermarking offers a promising solution by embedding imperceptible signals into generated images that can later be extracted for ownership verification or content tracing (Cao, 2025; Duan et al., 2025). While watermarking techniques for diffusion models have been extensively studied (Wen et al., 2023; Fernandez et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), watermarking for AR image generators remains a nascent and underexplored area. 1

Recent works have begun to address this gap. IndexMark (Tong et al., 2025) proposes a training-free framework that partitions codebook entries into red and green groups and biases generation toward green tokens. Safe-VAR (Wang et al., 2025) embeds water-marks through a trained encoder-decoder pair. Other concurrent works explore lexical biasing (Hui et al., 2025), bitwise watermarking (Kerner et al., 2025), and robustness im-provements (Lukovnikov et al., 2025; Jovanovi ´c et al., 2025; Meintz et al., 2025). Despite this progress, we identify three critical limitations shared by existing methods: Zero-bit only. Most existing methods (Tong et al., 2025; Jovanovi ´c et al., 2025; Lukovnikov et al., 2025) embed zero-bit watermarks that only support binary verification (watermarked or not), without the ability to encode multi-bit messages such as user IDs or timestamps. This significantly limits practical deployment scenarios. Static partitioning. The red-green parti-tioning strategy in IndexMark and related methods is fixed once determined. If an adversary discovers the partition, the watermark can be easily removed or forged, compromising security. Architecture-specific. Existing methods are tailored to specific AR paradigms, either next-token prediction (Sun et al., 2024) or next-scale prediction (Tian et al., 2024), lacking a unified framework that generalizes across different AR architectures. To address these limitations, we propose UniMark (Uni fied Adaptive Multi-bit Waterma rk ing), a training-free watermarking framework for AR image generators. Our contributions are: 1. We propose Adaptive Semantic Grouping (ASG) , a key-dependent dynamic code-book partitioning strategy that leverages semantic similarity between codebook entries and a cryptographic hash to generate position-dependent green/red groups. ASG ensures that semantically similar tokens are available for replacement, preserv-ing image quality, while the key-dependent partition prevents forgery attacks. 2. We design Block-wise Multi-bit Encoding (BME) , a scheme that divides the gener-ated token sequence into blocks and encodes different message bits across blocks. Combined with BCH error-correcting codes, BME enables reliable multi-bit message embedding and extraction even under various image distortions. 3. We introduce a Unified Token-Replacement Interface (UTRI) that abstracts the token generation and replacement process, enabling UniMark to seamlessly operate on both next-token prediction models (e.g., LlamaGen) and next-scale prediction models (e.g., VAR) without model-specific modifications. 4. We provide theoretical analysis on the false positive rate and embedding capacity of UniMark, and conduct extensive experiments on LlamaGen, VAR, and Open-MAGVIT2, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance across image quality, water-mark detection, multi-bit extraction accuracy, and robustness against six types of attacks. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Autoregressive Image Generation 

Autoregressive (AR) image generation converts images into discrete token sequences us-ing visual tokenizers (e.g., VQ-VAE, VQGAN) and generates them token-by-token via transformer architectures. MAGVIT-v2 (Yu et al., 2023) demonstrates that a strong tok-enizer is key to competitive generation quality. LlamaGen (Sun et al., 2024) applies the Llama architecture to image generation in a next-token prediction manner. VAR (Tian et al., 2024) introduces next-scale prediction, generating images from coarse to fine reso-lutions. Open-MAGVIT2 (Luo et al., 2024) provides an open-source implementation with improved codebook utilization. Recent advances in this field focus on refining condition errors with diffusion loss (Zhou et al., 2026) and employing entropy-guided optimization for stable synthesis (Song et al., 2026). Furthermore, developments in vision representation compression (Zhou et al., 2026), omni-modal Large Language Models (LLMs) like Lumina-mDMOO (Xin et al., 2025), and more efficient tokenizers (Qu et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a;b; Yu et al., 2024) continue to push the boundary of AR image generation. 22.2 Watermarking for Generative Models Diffusion model watermarking. Tree-Ring (Wen et al., 2023) embeds watermarks in the initial noise of diffusion models. Stable Signature (Fernandez et al., 2023) fine-tunes the decoder to embed watermarks. Gaussian Shading (Yang et al., 2024) provides prov-able performance-lossless watermarking. Other methods explore adversarial optimiza-tion (Huang et al., 2024), low-dimensional subspace embedding (Li et al., 2024), super-resolution-based approaches (Hu et al., 2024), guidance-based methods (Gesny et al., 2025), multi-bit encoding (Xing et al., 2025), robustness against deepfakes (Sun et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025), and IP protection of model weights (Peng et al., 2023). Beyond generation-time watermarking, post-hoc approaches embed watermarks into existing images for tamper localization (Zhang et al., 2023) or detect fine-tuning-based art theft (Luo et al., 2023). Com-prehensive surveys (Duan et al., 2025; Cao, 2025) and benchmarks (Xu et al., 2024) provide systematic overviews. 

LLM watermarking and Alignment. Kirchenbauer et al. (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023) pro-pose the red-green list framework for LLM watermarking, partitioning vocabulary tokens into red and green sets and biasing generation toward green tokens. Subsequent works improve reliability (Kirchenbauer et al., 2024), ensemble strategies (Niess & Kern, 2024), and certifiable robustness (Feng et al., 2024). The development of LLMs also involves aligning long contexts (Si et al., 2025a), efficient planning for agent tasks (Si et al., 2025b), and cross-domain multi-task learning through multi-modal alignment prompts (Xin et al., 2024a;b; Li et al., 2025). These techniques, along with benchmarks like SpokenWOZ (Si et al., 2023), highlight the complexity of modern generative agents where token-level watermarking remains a crucial tool for content attribution. 

AR image generation watermarking. IndexMark (Tong et al., 2025) is the first training-free watermarking framework for AR image generators, using a match-then-replace strategy with static red-green codebook partitioning. Jovanovi ´c et al. (Jovanovi ´c et al., 2025) extend the LLM watermark framework to image tokens. Safe-VAR (Wang et al., 2025) trains a water-mark encoder-decoder for VAR. BitMark (Kerner et al., 2025) addresses bitwise AR models. Lukovnikov et al. (Lukovnikov et al., 2025) improve robustness of red-green watermarking. Other works explore lexical biasing (Hui et al., 2025), radioactive watermarks (Meintz et al., 2025), and concept-based watermarks (Sadasivan et al., 2025). Compared to these methods, UniMark is the first to simultaneously achieve multi-bit encoding, adaptive key-dependent grouping, and cross-architecture generalization in a training-free manner. 

## 3 Method 

3.1 Preliminaries and Problem Formulation Autoregressive image generation. An AR image generator first encodes an image x into a sequence of discrete tokens t = ( t1, t2, . . . , tN ) using a visual tokenizer with codebook 

C = {c1, c2, . . . , cK }, where K = |C| is the codebook size and N is the sequence length. The generator then autoregressively predicts each token: p(ti | t<i, y), where y is an optional conditioning signal (e.g., class label or text). Two main paradigms exist: • Next-token prediction (e.g., LlamaGen): tokens are generated left-to-right in raster order, ti ∈ C for all i.• Next-scale prediction (e.g., VAR): tokens are generated scale-by-scale from coarse to fine, with tokens at scale s denoted t(s), each drawn from a shared codebook C.

Watermarking objective. Given a secret key κ and a multi-bit message m =(m1, m2, . . . , mB) ∈ { 0, 1 }B, our goal is to design: • An embedding function Embed (t, m, κ) → ˜t that modifies the token sequence to encode m;3• A detection function Detect ( ˜x, κ) → { 0, 1 } for zero-bit verification; • An extraction function Extract ( ˜x, κ) → ˆm for multi-bit message recovery; such that: (1) the watermarked image ˜x = Decode (˜t) is visually indistinguishable from the original x = Decode (t); (2) m can be reliably extracted even after image distortions; (3) the method is training-free and model-agnostic. 

Notation. We denote the semantic similarity between codebook entries ci and cj as sij =

sim (ei, ej), where ei is the embedding vector of ci. The similarity matrix is S ∈ RK×K. A cryptographic hash function is denoted H(·).

3.4 Unified Token-Replacement Interface (UTRI) 

To enable UniMark to work across different AR paradigms, we define an abstract interface that decouples the watermark embedding logic from the specific generation process. 

Abstract interface. UTRI defines three operations: 1. G ET TOKEN SEQUENCE (model , y) → t: generate the raw token sequence from the AR model. 2. R EPLACE TOKENS (t, G, S) → ˜t: apply watermark embedding by replacing tokens according to Eq. 4 and 6. 3. D ECODE IMAGE (˜t) → ˜x: decode the watermarked token sequence back to an image. 

Next-token adapter. For next-token models (e.g., LlamaGen), GET TOKEN SEQUENCE re-turns the 1D raster-order sequence directly. Token indices correspond to the shared codebook

Table 4 presents the robustness evaluation under six types of attacks. We report both zero-bit TPR@FPR=1% and 32-bit extraction accuracy. Codebook scalability. Figure 7 examines how codebook size affects watermarking per-formance. Larger codebooks provide more replacement candidates, leading to both better image quality (lower ∆FID) and higher bit accuracy. The improvement is most significant when scaling from 1K to 8K entries, after which returns diminish. This trend explains why Open-MAGVIT2 ( K = 262144) achieves the best quality preservation despite having the same token sequence length as LlamaGen ( N = 256). The result also suggests that UniMark will naturally benefit from the trend toward larger codebooks in future AR models (Xiong et al., 2025). 

Component contribution. Figure 8 visualizes the contribution of each component via grouped bar charts. The full UniMark achieves the best balance across all three metrics. The most impactful component is the BCH error correction, whose removal causes bit accuracy to drop from 99.2% to 93.6%, a 5.6 percentage point decrease. This underscores the importance of error-correcting codes in multi-bit watermarking, as the token re-encoding process inevitably introduces some errors. The ASG component primarily affects quality (∆FID drops from 0.07 to 0.16 when replaced with static partition), confirming that semantic-aware replacement is essential for preserving image fidelity. 12 5 Conclusion 

We presented UniMark, a training-free, unified watermarking framework for autoregres-sive image generators. UniMark introduces three core components: Adaptive Semantic Grouping for secure and quality-preserving codebook partitioning, Block-wise Multi-bit Encoding for reliable multi-bit message embedding, and a Unified Token-Replacement Interface for cross-architecture generalization. Theoretical analysis establishes guarantees on false positive rates and embedding capacity. Extensive experiments on LlamaGen, VAR, and Open-MAGVIT2 demonstrate that UniMark achieves state-of-the-art performance in image quality, watermark detection, multi-bit extraction, and robustness against common attacks. Our work bridges the gap between zero-bit verification and practical multi-bit content tracing for the rapidly growing family of autoregressive image generators. 

Limitations and future work. The embedding capacity is fundamentally limited by the token sequence length and codebook size. Future work could explore adaptive capacity al-location based on image content, extend to video AR generators, and investigate robustness against adversarial regeneration attacks.
