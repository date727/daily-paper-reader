# Abstract 

Despite the remarkable synthesis capabilities of text-to-image (T2I) models, safeguarding them against content violations remains a persistent challenge. Existing safety alignments primar-ily focus on explicit malicious concepts, often overlooking the subtle yet critical risks of com-positional semantics. To address this oversight, we identify and formalize a novel vulnerability: Multi-Concept Compositional Unsafety (MCCU), where unsafe semantics stem from the implicit as-sociations of individually benign concepts. Based on this formulation, we introduce TwoHamsters, a comprehensive benchmark comprising 17.5k prompts curated to probe MCCU vulnerabilities. Through a rigorous evaluation of 10 state-of-the-art models and 16 defense mechanisms, our analysis yields 8 pivotal insights. In particular, we demonstrate that current T2I models and de-fense mechanisms face severe MCCU risks: on TwoHamsters, FLUX achieves an MCCU genera-tion success rate of 99.52%, while LLaVA-Guard only attains a recall of 41.06%, highlighting a crit-ical limitation of the current paradigm for manag-ing hazardous compositional generation.

# 2. Related Work 

2.1. Text-to-Image Generation Models 

Text-to-Image synthesis has transitioned from GANs (Good-fellow et al., 2020) to Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DPMs) (Song et al., 2020; Ho et al., 2020), which offer superior generative utility. Subsequent modular innovations, such as Control-Net (Zhang et al., 2023) and IP-Adapter (Ye et al., 2023), have further empowered these models with fine-grained structural and stylistic control. 2.2. T2I Unsafe Benchmarks 

Early T2I safety protocols primarily targeted explicit haz-ards like pornography and gore, though often constrained by scale. For instance, SneakyPrompt (Yang et al., 2024) synthesized a concise set of 200 prompts, while Unsafe Diffusion (Qu et al., 2023) and I2P (Schramowski et al., 2023) mined real-world user data to establish the I2P dataset (4,704 examples), a standard reference for concept era-sure. Subsequent research has expanded to nuanced risks such as political sensitivity and privacy. Benchmarks like T2IRiskyPrompt (Zhang et al., 2025b) and T2ISafety (Li et al., 2025b) introduced hierarchical taxonomies but re-main dependent on the explicit presence of unsafe concepts. Consequently, they structurally neglect MCCU, where tox-icity emerges from benign combinations. While Harmful-Cub (Nie et al., 2025) briefly touches upon these MCCU risks, it lacks the systematic rigor of a standalone benchmark. TwoHamsters addresses this limitation by establishing the first robust framework specifically engineered for assessing compositional vulnerabilities (see Table 1). 

2.3. Concept Erasure 

Concept erasure employs fine-tuning to induce “targeted forgetting“ of illicit content. Pioneer methods like ESD and Forget-Me-Not (Gandikota et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2025a; Zhang et al., 2025a; Fan et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2025) aimed to eliminate target con-cepts while preserving fidelity. However, adversarial stud-ies (Zhang et al., 2024b; Pham et al., 2023) reveal that these techniques fail to fully expunge latent concepts, as erasure boundaries are easily circumvented via token manipulation or textual inversion to reactivate suppressed semantics. To counter these threats, the focus has shifted toward Robust Concept Erasure. Methods like RACE (Kim et al., 2024), AdvUnlearn (Zhang et al., 2025c), and Stereo (Srivatsan et al., 2025) adopt iterative frameworks to neutralize adver-sarial directions. Despite improved robustness, these meth-ods frequently incur substantial collateral damage to gen-erative quality. Furthermore, even recent validations (Gao et al., 2025) on models like FLUX remain predominantly engineered for explicit, atomic concepts. This creates a practical dilemma for MCCU: since constituent elements are intrinsically benign, erasing the compositional trigger necessitates suppressing legitimate base concepts, thereby severely impairing the model’s generative utility.

3.2. Category-Concept Taxonomy 

To address the subtle and implicit nature of MCCU, we care-fully design a taxonomy-driven, human-AI pipeline for data curation. Our objective is to engineer a benchmark charac-terized by broad taxonomic coverage, semantic granularity, and high adversarial potency. Specifically, we strictly align our taxonomy with real-world applications by synthesizing safety guidelines from leading commercial T2I platforms. This process integrates sociopo-litical sensitivities and ethical ambiguities to construct a robust hierarchical framework. Following rigorous adju-dication by an expert committee, this pipeline yields 10 core risk dimensions, namely Disturbing, Harassment, Hate, Humiliation, Illegal, Political, Public Health Harm, Self-Harm, Sexual, and Violence. Comprehensive definitions and detailed examples are provided in Table 7. Then, guided by the formulation in Equation (1), we proceed to mine specific MCCU concept pairs. We employ LLMs for semantic decomposition and knowledge retrieval. Ac-knowledging that MCCU risks are significantly influenced by region, culture, and belief, we instruct LLMs to adopt diverse personas to identify context-specific MCCU pairs from different perspectives. An expert committee then cat-egorizes the model responses, screening for representative pairs that satisfy Equation (1). Furthermore, considering the distribution of risk severity, we prioritize the expansion of categories associated with higher public concern and po-tential harm. Ultimately, we identify 51 distinct concept pairs. This top-down methodology facilitates comprehen-sive coverage of latent risks emerging from the composition of individually benign atomic concepts. 

3.3. Benchmark Construction Pipeline 

We construct the benchmark via a rigorous three-stage pipeline designed to isolate implicit compositional risks. The acquisition phase employs a dual-stream strategy, com-bining taxonomy-guided retrieval with LLM-based adversar-ial expansion, while enforcing a strict blocklist to preclude explicit violations and ensure risks emerge solely from con-cept interactions. Following the acquisition, a multi-stage filtering protocol en-sures diversity and fidelity. We first utilize centrality-based pruning in CLIP embedding space to maximize semantic variance. This is complemented by a cross-modal consis-tency verification against single-concept baselines to ensure emergent risks stem from effective composition rather than semantic degradation. Finally, we augment the dataset with structured rationales via a human-in-the-loop process. Each sample captures the causal logic of the risk, adjudicated through a double-blind review where independent auditors verify rationale-visual alignment. This verification yields a finalized corpus of 17.5k high-fidelity samples. ( Section A) 

3.4. Automated Evaluator 

Current safety classifiers struggle to discern the subtle com-positional semantics inherent in the TwoHamsters bench-mark. To address this, we propose a robust evaluation framework consisting of two complementary components: MCCU-ViT for high-throughput quantitative assessment, and MCCU-VLM for reasoning-heavy qualitative verifica-tion (Helff et al., 2024). Given the prohibitive computational costs of VLMs for large-scale benchmarking, we establish MCCU-ViT as the primary evaluator. 4.1. Experimental Setup 

To rigorously audit safety vulnerabilities and defensive effi-cacy within the MCCU paradigm, we conduct a comprehen-sive benchmark across three distinct dimensions: generative models, safety filters, and concept erasure frameworks. For T2I models benchmarking, we assess the inherent safety of 10 SOTA T2I models on the full TwoHamsters suite, spanning diverse architectures (e.g., U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015), DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023)) and paradigms (Diffusion, Flow Matching, Autoregressive). Specifically, our evaluation includes Stable Diffusion (v1.4, v1.5, v2.1, v3.5), SDXL, Playground-v2.5 (Li et al., 2024a), PixArt-

α (Chen et al., 2023), FLUX.1-dev (Lipman et al., 2022), CogView4 (Zheng et al., 2024), and Janus-Pro (Chen et al., 2025), with performance quantified via MDR and SCR. For safety filters benchmarking, we partition the dataset into training and testing sets (80/20 split) to compare seven mainstream filters: MultiHeaded (Qu et al., 2023), NSFW-T (Jieli, 2023), Q16 (Schramowski et al., 2022), NSFW-I (LAION-AI, 2022), the standard SD safety checker (Rando et al., 2022), LLaVA-Guard (Helff et al., 2024), and Perspec-tiveVision (Qu et al., 2025). Insight 1: There exists an “Instruction vs Safety” Dilemma in Compositional Generation. Our empirical analysis reveals a critical “Instruction-Safety” dilemma: as models achieve superior instruction following, they become paradoxically more vulnerable to MCCU. While early U-Net architectures (e.g., SD-v1.4) retain a baseline MDR of 40.91%, modern DiT-based models (e.g., FLUX.1) plum-met to near-0% MDR. We interpret this as an alignment paradigm that is semantic rather than axiological: models excel at compositionally rendering entities (e.g., “Water-melon” and “Person”) but lack the social reasoning to detect their emergent toxicity. Thus, optimization for strict prompt adherence effectively bypasses safety constraints when risks are implicit rather than explicit. 

Insight 2: Taxonomic Bias in Safety Alignment. Our fine-grained analysis reveals a systemic imbalance in current defense paradigms. Categories characterized by complex socio-semantic nuances, specifically Harassment (8.81% Mean MDR), Hate Speech, and Political Sensitivity, exhibit significantly higher vulnerability than extensively studied domains such as Sexual Content (21.85% Mean MDR). The marked divergence in MDR across categories highlights that current T2I safety alignment focus on suppressing explicit, low-level visual hazards (e.g., nudity), yet fail to sufficiently address high-level risks devoid of explicit visual signatures.

The toxicity of a compositional image often stems not from pixel-level seman-tics but from the evolving cultural zeitgeist; a combination deemed benign today may acquire offensive connotations to-morrow via emerging subcultures or shifting societal norms. 4.4. Benchmarking Concept Erasure Methods 

As demonstrated in Table 4, current Concept Erasure (CE) paradigms fail to achieve the requisite efficacy for mitigating MCCU risks, manifesting in two distinct failure modes. 

Insight 7: The Dual Dilemma of Ineffective Erasure and Utility Collapse in Concept Erasure. First, we ob-serve ineffective erasure across various categories, where the MDR of CE-tuned models remains unacceptably close to the SDXL baseline. One reason is that while state-of-the-art methods can suppress explicit hazards in smaller models like SD-v1.4, they struggle to decouple deep text-to-latent associations in large-scale architectures such as SDXL or FLUX. Another reason lies in the inherent complexity of MCCU; MCCU prompts are often structurally intricate, and when these complex compositional prompts are triggered, latent unsafe visual features tend to re-emerge. Second, effective erasure often leads to a catastrophic de-cline in utility, as evidenced by the synchronized drop in MDR and SCR for methods such as ESD, UCE, KSCU, and COGFD. This limitation stems from the fact that existing erasure logic relies on coarse-grained activation suppression over parameter subsets, lacking the precision required to address fine-grained MCCU risks. We further argue that MCCU risks differ in practice from conventional safety haz-ards in terms of latent feature distributions. Prior safety research typically assumes manifold separation, modeling violating and benign data as disjoint distributions. In con-trast, the critical challenge of MCCU lies in its intrinsic distributional entanglement, where unsafe semantics do not form isolated clusters but are superimposed onto and para-sitize benign concept manifolds. Under this regime, current CE algorithms attempting to excise specific MCCU risks inadvertently induce the collapse of the underlying benign manifolds that constitute concept representations, thereby degrading generative utility. 

Insight 8: Shifting from Concept Erasure to Composi-tional Disentanglement. The intrinsic limitation of current Concept Erasure lies in its objective: attempting to remove a concept vector vc entirely. Our results prove this fails for MCCU because the constituent elements (e.g., “priest”, “child”) are benign and essential. Therefore, we propose a paradigm shift from data-centric filtration to logic-oriented disentanglement. Mathematically, this implies moving from minimizing the probability P (c) to enforcing conditional independence: P (A|B) ≈ P (A), preventing the contextual triggering of risk while preserving atomic utility. While COGFD has explored the direction of compositional disen-tanglement, results on TwoHamsters demonstrate that it falls short in both erasure efficacy and generative utility. Practi-cal implementation should prioritize disrupting the semantic binding mechanism within the model’s cross-attention lay-ers. Instead of erasing weights, future alignment must focus on orthogonalizing the interaction manifolds—ensuring that the activation patterns of benign concepts (e.g., “banana”) re-main computationally orthogonal to sensitive contexts (e.g., “sexualized textures”), thereby stripping the model’s capac-ity for harmful synthesis without degrading its knowledge base.

# A. Benchmark Construction Pipeline Implementation 

A.1. Prompt Collection 

To ensure both the breadth and linguistic diversity of our benchmark, we propose a dual-stream acquisition strategy tailored to capture a heterogeneous spectrum of compositional risks. The first stream focuses on taxonomy-driven retrieval, systematically mining high-quality prompts from established datasets. By leveraging our hierarchical concept taxonomy, we identify and extract prompts that embody nuanced semantic variants of target concepts. The second stream complements this by harnessing the advanced reasoning capabilities of LLMs for adversarial expansion. We instantiate predefined MCCU concept pairs as logical constraints within specialized instruction templates, guiding LLMs to synthesize complex scenarios that go beyond trivial keyword co-occurrence. To strictly isolate implicit risks, we implement a dual-safeguard mechanism. We enforce negative constraints using a comprehensive blocklist of explicit risk terms (e.g., violence, illegal acts) during generation, followed by strict post-filtering. This guarantees that unsafety stems exclusively from compositional interactions rather than explicit vocabulary. This integrated pipeline converts static concept pairs into dynamic, high-complexity prompts, yielding an initial corpus of over 100k candidates for rigorous subsequent filtering. 

A.2. Data Filtering 

To distill the raw corpus into a robust benchmark, we deploy a multi-stage quality control pipeline comprising feature-space diversity filtering and cross-modal semantic effectiveness screening. 

Feature-space Diversity Filtering To mitigate semantic redundancy, we utilize a pre-trained CLIP text encoder Φ( ·) to project all prompts into a high-dimensional embedding space. Within each MCCU concept pair category, we compute the pairwise cosine similarity: Sim (pi, p j ) = Φ( pi) · Φ( pj )

For pairs exceeding this threshold, we employ a centrality-based pruning strategy: we compute the average similarity of each candidate to the category centroid. To maximize diversity, we discard the sample with the higher average similarity (i.e., the more generic sample), retaining the one that contributes more unique variance to the feature distribution. 

Cross-modal Semantic Effectiveness Screening To ensure visual fidelity, we implement a zero-shot screening mechanism anchored in visual ablation controls. For each prompt, we synthesize a diagnostic image triplet: the compositional generation

To guarantee that composition does not induce semantic degradation, we enforce that IA⊕B must maintain semantic fidelity comparable to the single-concept baselines. A.3. Rationale Annotation and Manual Review 

TwoHamsters augments each sample with a structured Rationale R, generated via a human-in-the-loop pipeline initialized by LLMs. The annotation enforces a rigorous logical chain: (1) elucidating how the composition of vA and vB induces the risk category ci, and (2) delineating the specific visual manifestations (e.g., interactions, spatial arrangements) that drive semantic emergence. This rationale serves as a critical supervision signal for training VLMs, ensuring whether models truly grasp the causal logic of the risk rather than merely memorizing patterns. To eliminate false positives, we enforce a stringent double-blind review protocol. Two independent auditors evaluate each sample, with strict consensus required for retention; discrepancies are adjudicated by an expert committee. Auditors further verify the alignment between the textual Rationale and the visual content. This process primarily verifies the accuracy of the rationales, data safety, and label correctness. # D. Automated Evaluator Training Implementation 

D.1. MCCU-ViT Training Implementation 

To enable automated and precise fidelity assessment of generated concepts, we construct a dedicated concept detector named MCCU-ViT. The architecture leverages the CLIP visual encoder as its backbone, specifically employing the ViT-L/14-CLIPA-336 variant pre-trained on DataComp-1B. To harness the rich semantic representation capabilities of the pre-trained model while maintaining training efficiency, we freeze the parameters of the visual encoder and fine-tune three distinct lightweight classification heads. These heads are structured as Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) comprising a linear projection, a ReLU activation, and a final linear mapping layer. They are tasked with independently predicting the first atomic concept, the second atomic concept, and the global safety category, respectively. This multi-head design decouples the complex task of safety detection into concrete object recognition and abstract category classification. The model is trained on the TwoHamsters dataset using a multi-task learning objective. For the atomic concept heads, we formulate the task as an N-way classification problem, where images containing the specific target concept are labeled as positives. The training process minimizes the Cross-Entropy Loss using the AdamW optimizer. Regarding hyperparameters, we standardize the input resolution to 384 times 384 pixels and employ a batch size of 16. The learning rate is set to 1e-4 with a weight decay of 0.01 and a dropout rate of 0.2 to prevent overfitting. D.2. MCCU-VLM Training Implementation 

Diverging from the discriminative nature of MCCU-ViT, which focuses on rapid probability quantification, MCCU-VLM is engineered as a generative reasoning agent designed to articulate the rationale behind compositional risks. We construct this model upon the Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct architecture, a state-of-the-art Large Multimodal Model (LMM) that bridges 16 TwoHamsters: Benchmarking Multi-Concept Compositional Unsafety in Text-to-Image Models 

a CLIP-based vision encoder with a Qwen2 language model via a complex projection layer. Unlike the feature-mapping approach used in our discriminative detector, the training of MCCU-VLM follows the task-specific instruction tuning paradigm. Our objective is to transition the model from general visual captioning to specialized safety auditing without compromising its pre-trained visual common sense. To achieve this, we freeze both the vision tower and the multimodal projector, exclusively optimizing the language model’s capabilities to interpret subtle MCCU semantics. We adopt a Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) strategy to navigate the trade-off between plasticity and stability. Specifically, we inject Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) modules into all linear layers of the language model, including the query, key, value, output, gate, up, and down projections. The LoRA configuration is set with a rank r = 16 and a scaling factor α = 32 . The model is fine-tuned on a curated dataset of 9,379 visual instruction-following samples. Each sample is structured as a multi-turn dialogue where the model must not only output a final verdict but also generate a comprehensive safety critique. The training objective minimizes the auto-regressive next-token prediction loss, calculated solely on the assistant’s reasoning and response tokens, while masking the user instructions to prevent pattern memorization. Implementation-wise, we employ the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 1 × 10 −4 and a cosine decay schedule. The training spans 10 epochs with a batch size of 1 per GPU, utilizing a gradient accumulation strategy implicitly handled by the optimizer’s step. A context window of 4,096 tokens is allocated to accommodate detailed reasoning chains. To mitigate overfitting on this specialized domain, a LoRA dropout rate of 0.1 is applied. This alignment process effectively transforms the general-purpose VLM into an expert evaluator capable of explaining why a benign composition triggers an unsafe perception.
