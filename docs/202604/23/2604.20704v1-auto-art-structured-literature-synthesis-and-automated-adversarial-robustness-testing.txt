# Abstract 

Adversarial robustness evaluation underpins every claim of trustworthy ML deployment, yet the field suffers from frag-mented protocols, undetected gradient masking, single-norm tunnel vision, and premature generalization from stylized benchmarks to production code. We make two contributions. 

(1) Structured synthesis. We analyze nine peer-reviewed corpus sources (ICML, NeurIPS, ICLR, UAI; 2020–2026) through seven complementary protocols—citation-chain trac-ing, gap scanning, methodology auditing, cross-paper syn-thesis, assumption stress-testing, knowledge mapping, and lay-accessible distillation—producing the first end-to-end structured analysis of what the adversarial evaluation com-munity agrees on, disagrees about, and cannot yet resolve .

(2) A UTO -ART framework. We introduce A UTO -ART [ 1], an open-source framework that directly operationalizes ev-ery identified gap: 50+ attacks across seven categories, 28 defence modules, the Robustness Diagnostic Index (RDI; 

∼30 × faster screening), First-Order Stationarity Condition (FOSC) gradient-masking detection, multi-norm evaluation (ℓ1/ℓ 2/ℓ ∞/semantic/spatial) with worst-case reporting, adap-tive memory-guided attack selection, and compliance map-ping to NIST AI RMF, OWASP LLM Top 10, and the EU AI Act. Empirical validation on the CIFAR-10 RobustBench leaderboard demonstrates that A UTO -ART’s pre-screening gate correctly identifies gradient masking in 92% of flagged configurations, that RDI rankings agree with full AutoAt-tack within Kendall τ ≥ 0.81 , and that multi-norm evaluation exposes a 23.5 pp gap between average and worst-case ro-bustness on state-of-the-art models. Three novel technical directions—worst-case multi-norm adversarial training curric-ula, architecture-conditional RDI calibration for Vision Trans-formers and LLMs, and ecologically valid agent red-teaming benchmarks—are proposed with concrete feasibility assess-ments and ablation designs. To the best of our knowledge, no prior work combines structured meta-scientific analysis with an executable evaluation framework bridging literature gaps directly into engineering. 

Keywords: adversarial robustness, evaluation protocols, gradi-ent masking, multi-attack benchmarks, diffusion purification, LLM red teaming, automated adversarial testing, NIST AI RMF, OWASP. 

# 1 Introduction 

Machine-learning models deployed in safety-critical per-ception [ 2, 3], language understanding [ 4], and decision-making [ 5] are expected to resist adversarially chosen input perturbations. A decade of work on adversarial training [ 3 , 6 ]and certified defences [ 7] has produced steady progress on standard benchmarks. Yet a recurring pattern undermines confidence in these numbers: defences are proposed with opti-mistic robustness claims that collapse under stronger attacks, richer threat models, or correct gradient accounting [ 8 –11 ]. The cycle repeats because the failures are not merely technical but structural—rooted in evaluation practice itself: (i) Reliance on single ℓp norms when real adversaries com-bine spatial, semantic, and compression perturbations [ 5]; (ii) Gradient masking inflating attack-based estimates across both deterministic [11] and stochastic [10] pipelines; (iii) Under-sampling of randomized-defence draws [10]; (iv) Premature transfer from stylized CTF tasks to production code [4]. We address these failures through two complementary contri-butions. 

Contribution 1: Structured literature synthesis. We analyse a curated corpus of nine verified sources (Table 1) using seven protocols drawn from systematic review methodology. Unlike narrative surveys that summarise papers sequentially, our syn-thesis traces intellectual lineages (who originated, challenged, and refined each concept), identifies testable assumptions the literature relies on but never validates, and maps the knowl-edge topology of the field. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first application of structured meta-scientific analysis— combining citation-chain tracing, gap scanning, methodology auditing, assumption stress-testing, and knowledge mapping in a single manuscript—to adversarial ML evaluation. 

Contribution 2: A UTO -ART framework. We introduce AUTO -ART ( Auto mated Adversarial Robustness Testing) [ 1], an open-source Python framework that directly operationalises every gap identified in the synthesis (Section 11). Key capa-bilities: • 50+ attacks across evasion (37+), poisoning (10+), extrac-tion (3), inference (8+), audio (2), NLP/text, LLM, and agentic categories; • 28 defence modules including TRADES [ 6], AWP, OAAT,

# 2 Related Work 

Adversarial robustness foundations. Goodfellow et al. [ 2]introduced FGSM, demonstrating that linear perturbations suffice to fool DNNs. Carlini and Wagner [ 12 ] showed that defensive distillation [ 13 ] fails against optimisation-based at-tacks, establishing the arms race paradigm. Madry et al. [3]formalised PGD adversarial training as a min-max optimi-sation, defining the standard ℓ∞ robustness benchmark on CIFAR-10 at ε = 8 /255 . Athalye et al. [9] catalogued obfus-cated gradients as a systematic failure mode, breaking seven ICLR 2018 defences. 

Evaluation standardisation. Croce and Hein [ 8 ] assem-bled AutoAttack—a parameter-free ensemble of APGD-CE, APGD-DLR, FAB, and Square Attack—re-evaluating >50 defended models and documenting >10 pp overestimation in most cases. RobustBench [ 14 ] operationalised this into a living leaderboard. Zhang et al. [ 6] proposed TRADES, de-composing robust loss into clean and adversarial terms; Li 

et al. [11 ] later showed that TRADES itself suffers gradient masking under certain hyperparameters. 

Multi-attack and continual robustness. Dai et al. [ 5] in-troduced MultiRobustBench (16 models × 9 attacks × 20 strengths), revealing worst-case multi-attack robustness below random guessing. Dai et al. [15 ] addressed temporal attack evolution through continual robust training with logit-space regularisation. 

Stochastic defence evaluation. Cohen et al. [7 ] established randomised smoothing for certified ℓ2 robustness. Kassis et al. [10 ] demonstrated that diffusion-based purification (DBP) collapses under correct gradient computation and proper ran-domness sampling, breaking even majority-vote variants. Xiao 

et al. [16 ] independently confirmed these findings through DiffHammer, a systematic framework for evaluating DBP robustness across multiple purification strategies. Beyond stochastic defences, Guo et al. [ 17 ] identified imbalanced gradients —a gradient masking phenomenon distinct from ob-fuscated gradients, where one loss term’s gradient dominates, pushing attacks suboptimally—and proposed the Margin De-composition (MD) attack that exceeds AutoAttack on 16/20 defence models. He et al. [18 ] introduced SoundnessBench, revealing bugs in three established neural network verifiers and underscoring that certification tools themselves require rigorous validation. 

Automated adversarial testing. The IBM Adversarial Ro-bustness Toolbox (ART) [ 19 ] provides attack/defence prim-itives. Microsoft Counterfit [ 20 ] targets ML model security assessments. Garak [ 21 ] scans LLM vulnerabilities across 

∼100 attack vectors. PyRIT [ 22 ] automates multi-turn red teaming. HarmBench [ 23 ] standardises LLM red teaming across 18 attack methods and 33 target models. NIST AI 100-2e2025 [ 24 ] provides the authoritative taxonomy of adversarial attacks and mitigations. Carlini et al. [4 ] benchmark LLM agents as autonomous adversaries, finding a 4–6 × success gap between CTF-like and real tasks. 

LLM jailbreaking and agent security. Zou et al. [ 25 ] intro-duced GCG, a gradient-based token-level suffix optimiser that generates universal adversarial prompts transferable across aligned LLMs. Jia et al. [26 ] improved GCG with diverse target templates and multi-coordinate updating, achieving nearly 100% ASR. Huang et al. [ 27 ] proposed IRIS, which targets the refusal direction in LLM activations, reaching 90% ASR on GPT-3.5-Turbo and 76% on GPT-4o. Geisler et al. [ 28 ] replaced the standard affirmative-prefix objective with a REINFORCE-based distributional objective, doubling attack success on Llama 3 and lifting defended-model ASR from 2% to 50%. Chao et al. [29 ] proposed PAIR, an iterative black-box refinement method using an attacker LLM, achieving 60–80% success rates with ∼20 queries. Mehrotra et al. [30 ] extended this to tree-structured search with pruning (TAP), reaching 70–85% success. Jin et al. [ 31 ] introduced AutoRedTeamer, a dual-agent system with lifelong attack memory that achieves 20% higher attack success rates at 46% reduced cost. Chao et al. [32 ] standardised evaluation through JailbreakBench with 100 behaviours and an evolving artefact repository. Wu et al. [ 33 ] dissected adversarial robustness of multimodal LM agents, identifying systematic vulnerabilities in visual ground-ing and tool use under adversarial perturbation. On the agent security front, Debenedetti et al. [34 ] constructed AgentDojo with 97 tasks and 629 injection test cases, finding that GPT-4o drops from 69% benign utility to 45% under prompt injec-tion. Zhan et al. # 5 Research Gap Analysis 

Ranking criterion: product of safety relevance (S), deploy-ment breadth (D), and corpus evidence strength (E), each scored 1–5. Gaps are ranked most-to-least significant; if fewer than five genuine gaps exist we would state so explicitly. 

G1: Worst-case multi-attack robustness (S5 ×D5 ×E5). 

Gap: Average leaderboard gains mask catastrophic worst-case failures; under worst-case attack selection, evaluated models perform below random guessing [C2]. 

Why it exists: Methodological barrier —exhaustive adver-sary unions are computationally prohibitive ( O(|A|×| ε|) per model); no architecture resists heterogeneous ℓp, spatial, and semantic failures simultaneously. 

Closest paper: Dai et al. [C2] came closest with 16 models ×

9 attacks × 20 strengths, but did not propose a training recipe that closes the avg/worst-case gap. 

Path to resolution: Multi-norm adversarial training curricula with worst-case-aware loss functions; scalable attack union approximations; deployment-time attack selection oracles. 

G2: Agent transfer failure (CTF →real) (S5 ×D4 ×E4). 

Gap: Best LLM agent achieves 75% on CTF tasks but only 13% on real implementations; a stronger LLM reaches 54%/21% respectively [C3]. 

Why it exists: Assumed but untested —the community im-plicitly treats stylised benchmarks as predictive of real code, but production defence code introduces library complexity, versioning, and environment coupling absent from CTF tasks. 4Closest paper: Carlini et al. [C3] quantified the gap but did not propose methods to close it. 

Path to resolution: Ecologically valid benchmark suites sam-pling production codebases; agent architectures with retrieval over API documentation; staged evaluation from unit-level to integration-level adversarial tasks. 

G3: Stochastic defence collapse (S4 ×D4 ×E5). 

Gap: Diffusion-based purification (DBP) robustness collapses under correct gradient computation and proper randomness sampling, defeating even majority-vote variants [C6]. 

Why it exists: Methodological barrier —prior evaluation pro-tocols under-sampled randomness draws and relied on approx-imate gradients through the diffusion stack, yielding inflated robustness. 

Closest paper: Kassis et al. [C6] fully characterised the failure but confined analysis to image-domain DBP; audio and text purification remain unevaluated. 

Path to resolution: Standardised multi-draw evaluation proto-cols ( n ≥ 50 draws); differentiable-through-diffusion toolkits; modality-agnostic purification benchmarks. 

G4: Proxy metric generalisation (S3 ×D4 ×E3). 

Gap: RDI [C5] achieves ∼30 × screening speedup but is val-idated only on CNN feature geometries; generalisation to Vision Transformers (ViTs), LLMs, and multi-modal encoders is untested. 

Why it exists: Lack of data —the metric study used only CNN-based classifiers; ViT and LLM feature manifolds have fundamentally different geometries (local receptive-field fea-tures vs. global self-attention representations). 

Closest paper: Song et al. [C5] demonstrated strong CNN correlation but acknowledged the CNN-only scope as a limita-tion. 

Path to resolution: Cross-architecture validation on ViT, DeiT, Swin, and decoder-only LLMs; architecture-conditional cali-bration of the RDI threshold; feature-geometry normalisation layers. 

G5: Continual robustness under open-world drift 

(S4 ×D3 ×E3). 

Gap: CRT [C4] improves backward-attack retention but of-fers no guarantees against adversary types outside training coverage—the “open-world” setting real deployments face. 

Why it exists: Ethical/logistical constraint —longitudinal field studies of adversarial drift require sustained deployment monitoring that no academic lab currently conducts at scale. 

Closest paper: Dai et al. [C4] demonstrated >100 attack combinations in continual training but used a closed attack vocabulary. 

Path to resolution: Industry–academia partnerships for longi-tudinal deployment telemetry; open-vocabulary attack gener-ation via LLM-guided perturbation synthesis; online robust training with novelty detection. 

# 6 Methodology Audit 

Step 2: Synthesis. Dominant methodology: Computational ML experiments and benchmark construction account for 7 of 9 entries. Rationale: executable attacks operationalise claims where analytic certification is prohibitive [C5], and repro-ducibility demands public code and checkpoints [C1,C2,C6]. 

Absent despite relevance: Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) of deployment pipelines, ethnography of security prac-titioner workflows, prospective field studies of adversarial incidents in production, and user studies of how practitioners interpret robustness reports. These absences limit the field’s ability to validate that lab findings transfer to operational set-tings. 

Step 3: Weakest methodology. Among peer-reviewed entries, Song et al. [C5] is most vulnerable to methodological criticism: • Sample size adequacy: Validated on a per-paper selection of CNN models; no systematic sampling across architecture families or scales. • Control for confounds: Feature-space geometry varies fun-damentally between CNNs (local receptive fields), ViTs (global attention), and decoder-only LLMs (sequential to-ken embeddings). The metric’s inter/intra-class distance ratio may conflate architecture-specific geometry with gen-uine robustness signal. • Replicability: Code availability is partial; key implementa-tion details (layer selection heuristic, centroid computation for imbalanced classes) are underspecified. • Transparency of reporting: Confidence intervals on the correlation between RDI and attack-based rankings are not reported; the ∼30 × speedup claim lacks wall-clock variance across hardware configurations. 

Criterion most clearly failed: Control for confounds—the CNN-only validation leaves the metric’s validity for the fastest-growing model families (ViTs, LLMs) entirely unsubstanti-ated.

# 8 Assumption Stress Test 

We identify eight assumptions the corpus relies on but never explicitly validates (Figure 2). Assumptions are ranked from most to least consequential. Each is foundational to the conclusions drawn and plausibly false or context-dependent. 

A1. A fixed attack suite represents safety. Shared by: C1, C2, C5. Risk: High. Consequence: If false, all leader-board rankings are temporally bounded artifacts—conclusions need wholesale revision, and the entire comparative robustness paradigm collapses. New attacks routinely bypass previously “sufficient” suites; no temporal validity guarantee exists. 

A2. Average robustness approximates worst-case safety. 

Shared by: C1, C2, C4 (implicitly, through reporting con-ventions). Risk: High. Consequence: If false, “progress” metrics systematically overstate deployment safety. Dai et al. [C2] already demonstrate contradiction: worst-case robust-ness falls below random guessing even when averages improve. Key findings across five years of benchmarking would require reinterpretation. 

A3. RDI generalises beyond CNN feature geometry. Shared by: C5, C9. Risk: Medium–High. Consequence: If false, fast screening mis-ranks ViTs and LLMs, producing false negatives (unsafe models pass) or false positives (safe models waste compute on full evaluation). The 30 × efficiency claim becomes architecture-conditional. 

A4. CTF performance predicts production adversarial capa-bility. Shared by: C3, agent-benchmarking community. Risk: Medium. Consequence: If false, investment in agent-based red teaming yields low real-world ROI; engineering effort is misallocated toward tasks that do not transfer to deployment settings. 

A5. Single randomness draws suffice for stochastic defence evaluation. Shared by: Prior DBP evaluations cited in C6. 

Risk: Medium. Consequence: If false, an entire defence class (diffusion purification, randomised smoothing variants) requires re-evaluation; published robustness numbers collapse under proper sampling [C6]. 

A6. Continual robust training closes the deployment lifecy-cle gap. Shared by: C4, practitioners adopting CRT. Risk: Medium. Consequence: If false, CRT provides false lifecycle confidence; models degrade against out-of-vocabulary attacks post-deployment. 

A7. TRADES default hyperparameters yield stable robust-ness. Shared by: Practitioners using default configurations; C7 deployments. Risk: Medium. Consequence: If false, widespread overestimation in mis-tuned regimes; users who adopt TRADES without hyperparameter validation inherit masked gradients. 

A8. Leaderboard checkpoints faithfully match published code. 

Shared by: C8 users, downstream evaluations. Risk: Low– Medium. Consequence: If false, replication failures prop-agate silently through downstream comparisons; checkpoint provenance becomes a supply-chain risk.

# 10 The “So What” Test 

Written for a smart non-expert—no citations, no jargon, no hedging. 

1. What has been established. When researchers construct stronger attacks and apply stricter testing protocols, AI sys-tems are consistently less robust than originally reported— sometimes by large margins. Testing against many attack types simultaneously reveals that a system can score well on average yet fail its hardest test worse than random guessing. 

2. What remains open. No single evaluation pipeline has been accepted as sufficient for the largest deployed systems, across all realistic types of input manipulation, or for auto-mated tools that match human expert findings on production code. Independent laboratories can reach contradictory safety conclusions using testing approaches that each appear method-ologically sound. 

3. Why this matters. Safety decisions about products, reg-ulatory compliance, and legal liability depend on robustness numbers that the literature examined here shows are system-atically inflated. The EU AI Act now requires demonstrated robustness for high-risk systems, and the gap between reported and actual robustness translates directly into regulatory and engineering risk.

## 13.2 Direction 2: Architecture-Conditional RDI Calibration for Vision Transformers and LLMs 

Core idea. RDI (Eq. 1) is validated only on CNN feature geometries [C5]. Vision Transformers produce patch-based representations with global attention patterns, and decoder-only LLMs produce sequential token embeddings—both have fundamentally different inter/intra-class distance structures than CNNs. We propose architecture-conditional calibration :learn a lightweight mapping gα : RDI raw 7 → RDI cal that ac-counts for architecture-specific feature geometry. Concretely, for each architecture family F (CNN, ViT, LLM), fit an iso-tonic regression gF on a calibration set of (RDI, AutoAttack rank) pairs. 

Edge-case checklist: 

• Data leakage: Calibration set disjoint from evaluation set; calibration uses held-out RobustBench entries not in the main evaluation. • Compute: RDI computation on ViT-B/16 requires ∼2 GB VRAM for 500 samples; LLM RDI on 7B models requires 

∼14 GB (single A100). Calibration fitting is trivial ( <1 s). • Reproducibility: Layer selection heuristic (which layer’s features to use), centroid computation for imbalanced classes, isotonic regression implementation. • Baselines: Raw RDI [ 43 ], Clever score [ 54 ], Lin region counting. • Ablation variables: (i) layer choice (penultimate vs. multi-layer aggregation), (ii) calibration method (isotonic vs. Platt scaling vs. temperature), (iii) number of calibration models (5, 10, 20). 

## 13.3 Direction 3: Ecologically Valid Agent Red-Teaming Benchmarks 

Core idea. Carlini et al. [C3] demonstrated a 4–6 × gap between LLM agent success on CTF-like tasks and produc-tion defence code. We propose constructing an ecologically valid benchmark by sampling real defence implementations from GitHub repositories (filtered by stars > 100 , active maintenance, diverse frameworks), creating standardised at-tack interfaces, and measuring agent performance on tasks that require understanding library APIs, version-specific be-haviour, and environment coupling. Each task is annotated with a difficulty taxonomy: (i) single-function attacks (analo-gous to CTF), (ii) multi-file attacks requiring import tracing, (iii) environment-coupled attacks requiring build system un-derstanding. 

Edge-case checklist: 

• Data leakage: Ensure benchmark code is not in founda-tion model training data by using repositories created after model training cutoff dates; provide contamination detec-tion via canary strings. • Compute: LLM agent inference costs dominate; bud-get ∼$50–200 per full benchmark run (GPT-4 pricing). Feasible for academic budgets with open-weight models (Llama 3 70B, Mixtral). • Reproducibility: Fix agent system prompt, temperature (0.0 for reproducibility), maximum turns (20), tool definitions. Report per-task success rates with 95% bootstrap CIs. • Baselines: AutoAdvExBench [ 4], AgentBench, SWE-bench (for code agent capability reference). • Ablation variables: (i) agent model (GPT-4, Claude, Llama 3), (ii) tool access (code execution vs. read-only), (iii) context window utilisation (4K, 32K, 128K). 

# 14 Discussion 

The evaluation crisis appears structural. Our synthesis suggests that the challenges facing adversarial evaluation are not isolated technical shortcomings but reflect a systemic pat-tern: five incompatible paradigms coexist (Section 4), and the two strongest assumptions—that fixed suites represent safety and that averages approximate worst cases—are con-tradicted by evidence within the corpus itself (Section 8). A plausible interpretation is that the field’s implicit evaluation contract—where proposers choose their own attack suite and report average-case numbers—creates incentives that system-atically inflate robustness estimates. 

Positioning relative to prior work. To the best of our knowledge, no prior work in adversarial ML simultaneously: (i) applies structured meta-scientific analysis (seven proto-cols spanning citation tracing through assumption stress-testing) to evaluation methodology; (ii) maps identified gaps directly to framework modules with explicit traceability (Ta-ble 6); (iii) combines literature synthesis, formal metric def-initions, and an executable evaluation framework in a single manuscript. Existing surveys [ 40 , 41 ] offer breadth without structured methodology; existing frameworks [ 19 , 20 ] and LLM benchmarks [ 23 ] provide tooling without gap-driven architecture. We note that the novelty claim rests on this spe-cific combination—individual components (attack ensembles, proxy metrics, compliance mapping) have precedents. 12 Regulatory implications. The EU AI Act [ 48 ] (Article 15) mandates “accuracy, robustness and cybersecurity” for high-risk AI systems—explicitly mentioning adversarial examples, data poisoning, and model poisoning—yet does not specify evaluation protocols. NIST AI 100-2e2025 [ 24 ] supplies an authoritative taxonomy, and the GenAI Profile [ 49 ] maps over 200 actions across 12 risk categories, but neither prescribes a concrete evaluation pipeline. MITRE ATLAS v5.3.0 [ 37 ]now catalogues 84 techniques including agentic AI attack vectors. ISO/IEC 42001 [ 55 ] provides the first certifiable AI management system standard, with Clause 6.1 requiring identi-fication of adversarial threats. OWASP’s LLM Top 10 [ 50 ] and Agentic Applications Top 10 [ 36 ] standardise agent-specific threat taxonomies. Our analysis suggests this regulatory under-specification carries risk: the same model can appear robust or vulnerable depending on the evaluation pipeline. A UTO -ART’s compliance mapping provides a concrete bridge: NIST AI RMF function MEASURE maps to multi-norm evaluation; OWASP LLM01 (Prompt Injection) maps to agentic attack modules; EU AI Act Article 15 maps to the pre-screening gate and worst-case reporting. 

Threats to validity. Internal validity. Pre-screening gate thresholds ( τmask = 0 .1, WB/BB gap 0.15 ) were tuned on the RobustBench CIFAR-10 leaderboard; these may require recalibration for different data distributions, model families, or perturbation budgets. The ablation study (Table 9) mitigates this by showing robustness across five random seeds, but cross-dataset validation remains future work. 

External validity. Our empirical evaluation uses CIFAR-10 models exclusively. While CIFAR-10 is the standard Robust-Bench benchmark, generalisability to ImageNet-scale models, multimodal architectures, and production LLMs is not yet demonstrated. The structured synthesis is bounded by nine corpus sources; future systematic reviews with larger corpora may reveal additional patterns or contradict our gap rankings. 

Construct validity. Kendall τ between RDI and AutoAttack rankings measures ordinal agreement but not calibration— a high τ does not guarantee that RDI absolute values are meaningful thresholds for deployment decisions. 

Limitations. (i) Corpus is nine sources; broader surveys may reveal additional patterns. (ii) A UTO -ART does not yet implement certified multi-norm training (G1) or longitudinal benchmark versioning (G2). (iii) Pre-screening gate accu-racy depends on model architecture; FOSC is less informative for non-differentiable components. (iv) Evaluation uses Ro-bustBench models on CIFAR-10; ImageNet and multimodal validation is future work. (v) Runtime benchmarks (Table 10) are single-GPU; distributed scaling behaviour is untested. 

Broader impact and responsible use. Rigorous evaluation protects downstream users from false safety assurances; con-versely, the same tools could be repurposed to find and exploit model vulnerabilities. AUTO -ART mitigates this dual-use risk through several mechanisms: attack results are reported in defensive SARIF format (designed for security workflows, not weaponisation), compliance integration maps findings to remediation actions rather than exploit chains, and the frame-work’s open-source licence encourages transparent auditing. We note that withholding evaluation tools does not prevent attacks—the offensive techniques already exist in published literature—but it does prevent defenders from assessing their exposure. 

# 15 Conclusion 

This work presents a structured literature synthesis and the AUTO -ART framework. The analysis identifies three intel-lectual threads (reliable evaluation, gradient masking, multi-attack threats), five ranked gaps with explicit resolution paths, eight untested assumptions ranked by consequence severity, and a unifying open question: what evaluation stack should be mandatory before certifying a model as safe against adaptive, multi-channel adversaries? 

On the empirical side, A UTO -ART’s pre-screening gate de-tects gradient masking in 92% of flagged configurations, RDI rankings agree with full AutoAttack at Kendall τ = 0 .82 ±0.03 ,and multi-norm evaluation exposes a 23.5 pp worst-case gap hidden by single-norm reporting. Ablation confirms that the three-signal ensemble (FOSC + RDI + WB/BB discrepancy) outperforms any single signal on both ranking fidelity and masking detection rate. Runtime analysis indicates 30 × pre-screening speedup and 5–6× end-to-end improvement over full AutoAttack. Three technical directions emerge from the synthesis (Sec-tion 13): worst-case multi-norm adversarial training with attack-union curricula, architecture-conditional RDI calibra-tion for ViTs and LLMs, and ecologically valid agent red-teaming benchmarks that bridge the CTF-to-production gap. Each direction is accompanied by concrete feasibility assess-ments and ablation designs. The gap between robustness research and deployment prac-tice will likely close not through better defences alone, but through evaluation infrastructure that subjects the measure-ment of robustness to the same rigour the field demands of robustness itself.
