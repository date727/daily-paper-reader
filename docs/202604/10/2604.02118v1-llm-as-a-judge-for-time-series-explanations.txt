# Abstract 

Evaluating the factual correctness of natural language explanations gener-ated by LLMs and grounded in time-series data remains an open challenge. Although modern models can generate textual interpretations of numerical signals, existing automatic evaluation methods are limited: reference-based similarity metrics and consistency-checking models require ground-truth explanations, and traditional time-series analysis techniques operate purely on numerical values and cannot assess free-form textual reasoning. As a result, there is currently no general-purpose method for directly verify-ing whether an explanation is faithful to the underlying time-series data without predefined references or task-specific rules. In this work, we study large language models as both generators and evaluators of time-series explanations in a reference-free setting. Given a time series, a question, and a candidate explanation, the evaluator assigns a ternary correctness label based on pattern identification, numeric accuracy, and answer faithfulness, enabling principled scoring and comparison of explanations. To study this setting, we construct a synthetic benchmark of 350 time-series cases spanning seven query types, each paired with correct, partially correct, and incorrect candidate explanations. We evaluate models across four tasks: explanation generation, relative ranking, independent scoring, and multi-anomaly detection. Our results show a clear asymmetry between generation and evaluation: while explanation generation is highly pattern-dependent and exhibits systematic failures on certain query types, with accuracies ranging from 0.00 to 0.12 for Seasonal Drop and Volatility Shift, to 0.94 to 0.96 for Structural Break, evaluation performance is substantially more stable, with models often able to correctly rank and score explanations even when their own generated responses are incorrect. These findings demon-strate the feasibility of data-grounded LLM-based evaluation for time-series explanations and highlight the potential of such models as reliable evalua-tors of data-grounded reasoning in time series domain. Code and data are available at: https://github.com/Prxxthxm/LLM-Timeseries-Evaluation 

# 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used to generate natural language expla-nations for structured and numerical data, including time series signals in domains such as finance, healthcare, climate science, and industrial monitoring (Zhang et al., 2024). As these systems become more capable of producing fluent textual interpretations of numerical patterns, ensuring the factual correctness of such explanations becomes critical. In high stakes settings, even subtle numerical inaccuracies or unsupported statistical claims can 1

In this work, we study the task of evaluating time series explanations. The input consists of a time series, a natural language question about the series, and a candidate explanation. The objective is to determine whether the explanation is faithful to the underlying numerical data, numerically accurate, and correctly answers the question. Unlike standard text generation evaluation, correctness in this setting depends on alignment with structured numerical evidence rather than similarity to a reference answer. This makes the task inherently challenging since explanations may vary in wording and structure, numeric errors may be subtle, and multiple reasoning aspects must be jointly assessed (Bowman et al. (2015); Box et al. (2015)). Existing evaluation approaches are limited for this setting. Reference based metrics such as BLEU, ROUGE, and embedding based similarity measures assess lexical or semantic overlap between generated text and ground truth references (Papineni et al. (2002); Lin (2004); Zhang et al. (2020)). While effective for tasks such as machine translation and summarization, these methods assume the availability of reference explanations and operate purely on textual similarity without access to the underlying numerical data. Natural language inference models similarly evaluate entailment between texts but require reference statements and do not directly verify numerical grounding (Bowman et al. (2015)). Conversely, classical time series analysis methods operate directly on numerical signals and support tasks such as trend detection and anomaly identification, but cannot assess free form textual reasoning (Hamilton (1994); Box et al. (2015)). Recent work on LLM based evaluators shows that models can approximate human judgments under structured prompting (Fu et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023); Zheng et al. (2023); Chiang et al. (2024)), but these approaches primarily operate in text to text settings and do not address evaluation grounded in structured numerical data. As a result, there is currently no general purpose, reference free framework for verifying the factual correctness of time series explanations. To address this gap, we adopt a rubric guided, reference free evaluation framework and extend it to a multi experiment setting that enables systematic analysis of model behavior. We construct a synthetic benchmark of 350 time series instances spanning seven query types, each paired with correct, partially correct, and incorrect candidate explanations. We evaluate models across four tasks: explanation generation, relative ranking of candidate explanations, independent correctness scoring, and multi anomaly detection. This design allows us to study how models perform both as generators of explanations and as evaluators of correctness under structured prompting. Our experiments show that model performance is highly dependent on the type of query being asked. In explanation generation, models achieve strong results on several query types but exhibit systematic weaknesses on others, with accuracy ranging from 0.94–0.96 for Structural Break to 0.00–0.12 for Seasonal Drop and Volatility Shift, indicating that their ability to reason over time-series data is uneven. However, even in cases where generation breaks down, the same models are often able to assess correctness, rank competing expla-nations, and identify errors when guided by structured prompts and explicit evaluation criteria, achieving up to 0.96 accuracy in these tasks. These findings suggest that while open ended generation remains brittle for specific query types, structured prompting can substantially improve evaluative performance, highlighting the potential of LLMs as reliable evaluators in data grounded reasoning settings.

# 2 Related Work 

2.1 Reference-Based Text Evaluation 

Automatic evaluation of generated text has traditionally relied on reference-based metrics such as BLEU, ROUGE, and embedding-based similarity measures (Papineni et al., 2002; Lin, 2004; Zhang et al., 2020). These metrics quantify lexical or semantic overlap between a gen-erated output and a gold reference, and have been widely adopted in machine translation, summarization, and question answering. Natural language inference (NLI) models have also been used to assess entailment between candidate outputs and reference statements (Bowman et al., 2015). While effective in many NLP tasks, these approaches assume the availability of ground-truth explanations and operate purely in a text-to-text setting. They do not incorporate structured numerical evidence and therefore cannot directly verify whether a free-form explanation is faithful to underlying time-series data. In settings where reference explanations cannot encode all numeric constraints or edge cases, these methods may fail to detect subtle inconsistencies or hallucinated statistics. 

2.2 Time-Series Analysis and Language Integration 

Classical time-series analysis provides principled methods for modeling temporal dynamics, including trend detection, anomaly identification, and forecasting (Hamilton, 1994; Box et al., 2015). More recently, large language models have been integrated into time-series pipelines, either by reprogramming pretrained LLMs for forecasting or by using them to generate natural-language interpretations of numerical signals (Jin et al., 2023; Zhou & Yu, 2024). These works demonstrate that LLMs can process temporal information and produce fluent explanations grounded in time-series inputs. However, evaluation in such systems typically focuses on forecasting accuracy (e.g., MAE, MSE) or relies on surface-level textual similarity for explanation quality. There remains limited work on systematically evaluating whether generated explanations are numerically faithful to the underlying data. 

2.3 Time-Series Question Answering and Explanation Benchmarks 

Recent datasets such as TSAQA introduce question-answering benchmarks for time-series analysis, requiring models to produce free-form explanations grounded in temporal signals (Jing et al., 2026). These benchmarks highlight the growing importance of explanation-level reasoning in time-series systems. However, evaluation protocols often depend on human annotation or reference-based text metrics, leaving open the question of how to perform automated, reference-free verification of explanation faithfulness. 

2.4 LLMs as Evaluators 

There is increasing interest in using large language models themselves as evaluators of generated outputs. Survey and empirical studies have investigated LLM-as-a-Judge frame-works in dialogue, summarization, and general language generation, demonstrating that LLMs can approximate human judgments under carefully designed prompts and scoring 3Preprint. Under review. rubrics (Fu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023). Benchmarks such as MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena further study the reliability and calibration of LLM judges in com-parative settings (Chiang et al., 2024). Additionally, reference-free evaluation approaches such as RAGAS highlight the feasibility of model-based evaluation without reliance on ground-truth references (Akin et al., 2023). Despite these advances, most existing LLM-judge work operates in text-to-text evalua-tion settings. The applicability of LLM-based judges to numerically grounded domains, where correctness depends on reasoning over structured time-series data rather than textual similarity, remains underexplored. In particular, it is unclear whether LLM judges cap-ture substantive data-grounded reasoning or simply replicate heuristic similarity patterns observed in non-LLM baselines. 

Positioning of Our Work. Our work bridges these lines of research by extending the LLM-as-a-Judge paradigm to a data-grounded, reference-free evaluation setting. Unlike reference-based metrics, our approach directly conditions on raw time-series inputs. Unlike classical numerical methods, it evaluates free-form textual explanations. Unlike prior LLM-judge studies focused on textual consistency, we explicitly investigate numeric faithfulness, hallucinated statistics, and partial correctness in time-series explanations. Further, we introduce a multi-experiment framework that enables systematic analysis of both generation and evaluation behavior across diverse reasoning tasks. 

# 3 Methodology 

3.1 Overview 

We study the evaluation of time-series explanations using a rubric-guided, reference-free framework. Given a time series, a question, and one or more candidate explanations, the goal is to assess whether the explanations are factually consistent with the underlying numerical data. Our approach combines structured prompting with a multi-task evaluation setup that probes different aspects of model behavior. Rather than relying on reference explanations, evaluation is performed directly against the time series by verifying patterns, numerical claims, and logical consistency. The framework is designed to evaluate both generative and evaluative capabilities under a unified setting. 

3.2 Input Representation and Evaluation Setting 

Each evaluation instance consists of a univariate time series T = {t1, . . . , tn}, a natural-language question q about the series, and a candidate explanation e. We assume no reference textual explanation is available at evaluation time. Instead, correctness is determined by logical and numerical consistency between e and the observable properties of T.We formulate evaluation as a ternary classification task: 

y = fθ (T, q, e), y ∈ { 0, 1, 2 }

where y = 0 denotes an incorrect explanation, y = 1 denotes a partially correct explanation with numeric inaccuracies, and y = 2 denotes a fully correct explanation. 

3.3 Rubric-Guided Evaluation 

Although the final output is a single label, evaluation is guided by an implicit rubric that captures multiple dimensions of correctness. The model is instructed to reason over the following aspects: 1. Data Faithfulness: Whether the explanation correctly identifies patterns, anomalies, or trends present in the time series. 4Preprint. Under review. 2. Numeric Accuracy: Whether numerical claims such as magnitudes, indices, and percentage changes are computed correctly. 3. Question Relevance: Whether the explanation directly answers the question. 4. Logical Coherence: Whether the reasoning is internally consistent. 5. Unsupported Claims: Whether the explanation avoids statements not grounded in the data. The model is explicitly instructed to verify all numerical claims against the raw time series and not rely on values stated in the explanation. These dimensions are not scored explicitly but are internalized through structured prompting to guide the final classification decision. 

3.4 Evaluation Tasks 

We evaluate model behavior across four complementary tasks, each designed to probe a different aspect of reasoning. We provide a complete end-to-end example of all four tasks, including dataset samples and evaluation traces, in Appendix A. 

Explanation Generation. Given a time series T and question q, the model generates an explanation ˆe. The generated explanation is then evaluated using the ternary rubric to assess correctness. This task measures the model’s ability to produce factually grounded explanations. 

Relative Ranking. Given a time series T, question q, and a set of three candidate expla-nations {e1, e2, e3}, the model selects the best explanation. Each set contains exactly one correct, one partially correct, and one incorrect explanation. This task evaluates the model’s ability to compare competing explanations and identify the most accurate one. 

Independent Scoring. Given a time series T, question q, and a single explanation e, the model assigns a ternary correctness label. This task evaluates the model’s ability to assess explanation quality in isolation. 

Multi-Anomaly Detection. In this task, the model is given a time series and asked to identify all anomalies present in the sequence. For each anomaly, the model reports its index and the percentage change relative to the previous point. No explicit thresholds are provided for anomaly detection; instead, the model is expected to infer its own distinction between noise and anomalous deviations. This task evaluates the model’s ability to directly reason over numerical data and identify multiple deviations without predefined criteria. 

3.5 Design Choices and Justification Why a rubric-guided approach? A rubric provides structured guidance for evaluating mul-tiple aspects of correctness, including numerical accuracy and logical consistency. This en-ables the model to move beyond surface-level textual similarity and perform data-grounded reasoning. 

Why reference-free evaluation? Reference-based metrics require ground-truth explana-tions and often fail to capture numeric inconsistencies or reasoning errors. A reference-free setup allows evaluation to be grounded directly in the underlying data. 

Why multiple evaluation tasks? Different tasks probe different capabilities. Generation evaluates the ability to produce explanations, while ranking and scoring evaluate the ability to assess correctness. Multi-anomaly detection tests direct reasoning over numerical data. Together, these tasks provide a more comprehensive view of model behavior. 5Preprint. Under review. 

# 4 Experimental Setup 

4.1 Models 

We evaluate three open-weight large language models: Qwen-3 8B, LLaMA-3 8B, and Gemma-2 9B IT. These models are selected to represent comparable parameter scales while differing in training data, architecture, and instruction tuning strategies. Each model is evaluated in both roles within our framework: as a generator of explanations in the generation task, and as an evaluator in the ranking and scoring tasks. This enables direct comparison between generative and evaluative capabilities under a unified setting. 

4.2 Datasets 

We evaluate our approach on TSQueryBench , a synthetic benchmark of 350 time-series instances spanning seven query types: linear spike, seasonal drop, structural break, multi-metric consistency, relative extremum, mean shift, and volatility shift. Each query type contains 50 instances, with time-series lengths distributed across 100, 200, 300, and 500 time steps. The overall structure of query types is loosely inspired by prior time-series QA formulations, though all instances and evaluation tasks are constructed independently. For each instance, we generate three candidate explanations corresponding to correct, partially correct, and incorrect reasoning using a rule-based construction pipeline. Faith-ful explanations are derived directly from ground-truth statistics (e.g., anomaly location, percentage change, z-score, or distributional properties), partially correct explanations intro-duce controlled numerical perturbations, and incorrect explanations consist of plausible but factually inconsistent reasoning patterns such as shifted indices, incorrect trends, or denial of anomalies. This results in 1050 total explanation instances for evaluation in the independent scoring task. The multi-anomaly detection task is conducted on a separate dataset of 100 time-series instances containing between 1 and 10 anomalies each, evenly distributed across sequence lengths of 100, 200, 300, and 500. Representative samples from both datasets, along with full evaluation traces for each task, are provided in Appendix A. 

4.3 Evaluation Protocol 

All tasks are performed using structured prompts that instruct the model to reason directly over the underlying time series rather than relying on values stated in the explanation. For evaluation of generated explanations in the explanation generation task, we use annota-tions from human experts to ensure label quality and consistency. For explanation generation, model outputs are evaluated using the ternary rubric described in Section 3. For relative ranking, accuracy is measured as the proportion of instances where the correct explanation is selected. For independent scoring, accuracy is computed against ground-truth labels across all candidate explanations. For multi-anomaly detection, performance is evaluated using two metrics: count accuracy, which measures whether the predicted number of anomalies and their indices matches the ground truth, and F1 score, which evaluates the overlap between predicted and true anomaly indices. Predicted indices are matched to ground truth indices within a tolerance of ±2 time steps. 

4.4 Implementation Details 

All experiments are conducted in a zero-shot setting without task-specific fine-tuning. Generation and evaluation prompts are standardized across models to ensure comparability. Outputs are constrained to structured JSON formats to enable deterministic parsing. To reduce stochastic variability, experiments are conducted with fixed decoding settings across models. All evaluations are performed on the same set of instances, ensuring consistency across experimental conditions. 6Preprint. Under review.

5.5 Discussion 

The results reveal a clear distinction between generation and evaluation capabilities. While generation performance is highly dependent on query type and exhibits systematic failures, evaluation performance remains stable across both ranking and scoring tasks. A key observation is that models are often able to identify correct explanations even when they fail to produce them. This suggests that structured prompting enables more reliable verification than open-ended generation. Additionally, models perform well on localized patterns but struggle with higher-order statistical properties such as volatility. The anomaly detection results further highlight limitations in precise quantitative reasoning, particularly in threshold calibration. 

# 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we introduced a rubric-based LLM-as-a-Judge framework for evaluating time-series explanations in a reference-free setting. By conditioning evaluation directly on raw numerical data, our approach enables structured assessment of explanation correctness without relying on ground-truth textual references. Through a series of controlled experiments, we show that model performance differs signif-icantly between generation and evaluation tasks. While explanation generation is highly dependent on query type and exhibits systematic failures for certain patterns such as volatility shifts, evaluation tasks such as ranking and independent scoring remain compar-atively stable across settings. Notably, models are often able to correctly identify or score explanations even when they fail to generate accurate ones. Our results further highlight that models perform well on localized and explicit patterns, but struggle with higher-order statistical properties and precise quantitative reasoning. In the multi-anomaly detection setting, models demonstrate moderate ability to localize anomalies but fail to accurately estimate their count, suggesting a tendency toward over-sensitive detection. 9Preprint. Under review. Overall, these findings suggest that separating generation and evaluation is critical when assessing data-grounded reasoning capabilities. LLMs appear to be more reliable as evalua-tors than as primary generators in such settings, particularly when guided by structured prompts and explicit rubrics. Future work includes extending the framework to real-world datasets, improving robustness on statistically complex patterns, and exploring hybrid evaluation systems that combine model-based and symbolic verification methods.
